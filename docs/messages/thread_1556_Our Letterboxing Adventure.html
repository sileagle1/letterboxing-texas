Our Letterboxing Adventure

From: (kacmbc@aol.com) | Date: 2005-12-30 22:01:13 UTC-05:00
Today, the Frye Team girls and I went on a letterboxing adventure!  It was my last day off before school starts up again next week, so we went in search of several letterboxes! 
Ones we found:
San Jacinto Letterbox- walking through waist high to shoulder high weeds with one of the Frye girls on my back was my strength workout for the day!  We did find the letterbox, although it was opened and the contents were strewn about.  The logbook was totally waterlogged so we weren't able to stamp in.  We dried off the stamp and replaced the bag, cleaned out the box and put it back where it was supposed to be.
 
Peanuts: Tickling the Ivories Letterbox - Anything involving the Peanuts characters is right up my alley!  Cute stamp and a nice place to sit and stamp in!  While walking back to the car, we saw dozens of Roseate Spoonbills flying around.  Awesome!!
 
Jerry Letterbox- great hiding spot!
 
Strawberry Fields Forever- the girls liked the stamp!
 
Don Key- since we had already eaten lunch, we stopped in for chips and salsa to go!  Fun place and we will have to go back!  The stamp was in good condition but the logbook was waterlogged and very dirty to the point that the paper was crumbling inside the microbox.  Looks like the back lamp post where the letterbox resides is also the dumping grounds for the plastic plant containers used for landscaping!
 
Long Tall Texan- First Finders and a WAY cool stamp!!
 
We were unable to get the Battleship Texas letterbox.  It seemed to be missing from it's location.
 
Tomorrow, my husband and I are going out to Austin to run, plant a letterbox and search for letterboxes in the Austin area.
 
Enjoy the rest of 2005!
 
Runs for Chocolate :)

Dewberry,

I always like the points you make - and I think it
would be good to get a wider range of views on what
you mention.

I've been thinking about regulatory type officials
quite a lot lately. We are very fortunate that we
have virtually no regulation, but I have to wonder how
much longer we have to enjoy that privilege.

I think that if we as a community present ourselves in
a responsible, considerate manner, we'll go further
without rules than if we consider ourselves as "just
folks out for a walk". I think it's really important
that we are environmentally aware both as we plant and
as we find, and that we have a broader view of the
effect of our hobby beyond just what we ourselves as
individuals are doing on a day to day basis. I think
we are basically there, that most of us know we don't
want to cause harm and we want to be smarter about how
we go about letterboxing. I'm still not sure we are
thinking "big picture" though.

Can we prove to outsiders that we think along
conservation-related lines? If someone were to hold a
hearing tomorrow to determine whether letterboxing
should be regulated in state parks, how would we as a
group defend ourselves against regulation? What
evidence could we use to demonstrate our care for the
environment and our efforts to minimize our effect?
How many of us know the principles of Leave No Trace -
and follow them?

Just a few questions to throw out on a Saturday
morning...and now I'm off to clean house for an hour
before I reward myself with getting out and about and
maybe even finding another box or two. I got a new
logbook for Christmas from SJZ, but I promised myself
I would use the one I have until the end of the year,
so today is the last day!

Lucy

*****The LNT organization has a pretty good website
and several very good training programs. I'm
considering getting qualified as a LNT trainer, if I
can ever find a course for it. The website is
www.lnt.org

I think most of us think of LNT as a restrictive list
of don't's, but actually, it's a set of principles
that help us to enjoy the outdoors harmoniously. It
reminds us to take care of ourselves when we get ready
to go out as well as preparing ourselves to take care
of the environment.

The six principles of Frontcountry LNT are:

1) Plan Ahead.
2) Stick to Trails.
3) Leave what you find.
4) Manage your pet.
5) Respect other visitors.
6) Tote your trash.

(And FYI, I've been working on a program for LNT for
our Cub Scouts this Spring, so I've made a sentence
out of the first letters of each element - Proper
Scouts Leave More Respectable Trails)

Frontcountry is what most of us do. We visit parks
that have established trails and are intended for
heavier traffic. Backcountry is geared to backpackers
and wilderness hikers and overnight primitive camping,
so the principles for that are a little more
complicated.

The Backcountry principles are:

1) Plan ahead and prepare.
2) Travel and camp on durable surfaces.
3) Dispose of waste properly.
4) Leave what you find.
5) Minimize campfire impacts.
6) Respect wildlife.
7) Be considerate of other visitors.

If you check the website, you'll see that some of the
suggestions they put forth include:

1. Know the regulations and special concerns for the
area you are visiting.
2. Schedule your trip to avoid times of high usage.
3. Visit in small groups.
4. Use a map and compass to eliminate the need to use
marking paint, etc.
5. Walk single file and stay in the middle of the
path, even when wet or muddy (won't our kids love
this???)
6. Avoid places where impact is just beginning.
7. Pack out all trash, leftover food, and litter.
8. Examine, but do not touch, cultural or historic
structures and artifacts.
9. Do not build structures, furniture, or dig
trenches.
10. Avoid wildlife during sensitive times like
breeding, nesting, feeding.
11. Observe wildlife from a distance.
12. Let nature sounds prevail. Avoid loud noises and
loud voices.

Don't these things all sound like things we try to do
as letterboxers? But do we know how to verbalize it
if someone were to ask us? What if a park ranger
approached us and got a little bristly about what we
are doing? Could we explain to him on his own terms
that what we are doing is not harmful, and that we
are, in fact, often helpful?

The deeper into Scouting that my husband and I have
gotten, the more convinced I've become that we really
need to practice LNT, so for awhile I struggled with
letterboxing while embracing LNT. I've eventually
come to the belief that I CAN letterbox and not
materially violate LNT principles. The biggest reason
I can justify the two together is that once I plant a
box, I'm committed to checking on it fairly often and
I know that as a regular visitor, I'll be picking up
trash and generally looking after the area it is in.
I considered for awhile going to micro boxes only, but
one thing I've wondered is if going smaller really is
more LNT, or if tiny boxes just enable animals to
handle them more easily. I'm not sure about that -
I'm still thinking it one through.

I really don't see myself as a militant
environmentalist, more as a conservationist. I once
heard someone talk about the difference between
preservation and conservation. Preservation is the
maintenance of resources exactly as they are now.
Conservation is the wise use of resources while at the
same time ensuring the continuity of resources. I
sometimes wonder where the parks services see
themselves - as preservers or conservers.

Lucy
