From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-04-21 09:03:17 UTC-06:00
I think it's so easy to get around that particular requirement that it doesn't make sense to do it, and in fact, it encourages dishonesty. All someone has to do is log enough finds to reach the threshhold, and then all those false finds skew other boxes and create other problems. And yes, this DOES happen!
If you are wanting to limit access to a particular box, I think it's far more effective to list the box, then ask that finders to contact you for the clues. A little sleuthwork on your part andsome polite conversation with the potential finder has the same effect as hiding a box behind an F count requirement. You can even diplomatically write in your listing that your box is intended as a reward for boxers who have found at least "xx" boxes, and invite them to contact you when they reach that mark. One thing this does is actually let them know that box is there (using the AQ minimum find feature, they can't even see it) and gives them something to shoot for. It's also a more upfront and honest way to do what you want to do.
I realize it puts the burden of trusting someone on you, the planter, but the same people who would not honor your request to wait to ask for the box until they've found the number are the ones who would log a bunch of falsefinds so they could get boxes that have a minimum F count requirement, so what are you gaining or losing by being upfront about it?
Also, using the minimum F count feature forces people to log their finds on AQ if they are going to find your hidden boxes. There are several notable finders who don't log finds there, and some who don't even use AQ. Would you really want to miss out on some nice stamps in your logbook?
I've had a few boxes I've wanted to protect by limiting access, and it has worked very well by havingfinders contact me directly for the clues. I've declined to share my clues on a few occasions for a variety of reasons, but I've always managed to find a way to be kind in doing so and the finders either understand and come back later at a more appropriate time, or they've dropped out of the game before the timing became better. I'm also able to emphasize to the finderwhy aparticular box is importantto me and ask that they be especially careful when finding it. I believe they have been far more receptive to my direct request than they would have been on a box that suddenly appeared for them to find.
My .02

From: Christine Velez (christineauger@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-04-21 10:08:54 UTC-05:00

While usually I think this practice is a bit exclusionary, I have used it myself once. The reason is that I have a box in a place that is very public and very well-tended by groundskeepers. In order to limit traffic to the box and also prevent a foot trail from emerging (one was starting to show before I changed the clue to reflect an F-count), I decided to make it available only to letterboxers who had a little more experience and were therefore more likely to respect the whole idea of being stealthy and leaving no trace.

As far as using an F-count to restrict a "regular" box though, I wouldn't do that myself.

I've been tempted to use this feature from time to time when boxes I know are well-hidden start disappearing (like a few have recently), but it just seems so elitest to me. Yep, yep Sandi. See - that's another way to do it. I just think there are more letterboxer-friendly ways of accomplishing box restrictions - and making them challenging is one of them.

I have to laugh when I think of Boots dangling clues in front of someone and saying "neener, neener, neener" - that would be like Santa peeking in windows at Christmas time and showing kids what he THOUGHT about giving them!

I've been enjoying reading everyone's responses to this well-posed question and I've been thinking on it as I've had time throughout the day. I find it very interesting that at least two folks used the word elitist in the course of this discussion, and both used it in the context of the F or P count minimum, but not with respect to the idea of holding boxes a little closer by restricting the clues. I think I realized why there is a distinction between the two - the arbitrarily set minimum implies that "YOU can't find this box until you've met this standard." Restricting box clues by making them into puzzles or asking finders to request them directly says "I consider this box to be special, so I'm putting a little extra work into it to be careful with it." It leaves control over the clue with the placer, while also allowing the placer the leeway of cutting someone some slack if they want to, whether that means giving them a hint to the puzzle or handing them the clue even if they've only found 44 boxes and not 50, or because they pay the best bribe - whatever.

One of the things I have always appreciated about letterboxing is that there is a variety of ways to deliver clues. It means you always have to be on the lookout for angles and options. It allows the finder and the placer to interact creatively while still allowing for friendly civility.

I don't log my finds on-line, but we DO always contact the placer of every box we find. The two actions aren't mutually exclusive. When we started letterboxing, contacting the placer was what you did if you were a conscientious sort - nice but not necessary. I also log my finds (and attempts) on LBNA, mainly because it helps me exclude boxes I have already looked for when searching clues, but also because it helps create a historical record for that box. This feature was added by the webmasters mainly for that reason since so many people requested it. Of course, like everything else in letterboxing, there were many with opposing views, and it made for some pretty heated posts several years ago. Basically, some people wanted to be able to see a historical record of attempts on boxes, like on the Geocache site, so they would have a better idea if the box was still there and worth looking for. They started creating databases on LBNA to hold this kind of information, which led to complaints from others not wanting their boxes in these databases. That is why in some clues (e.g. Funhog's) you see disclaimers at the bottom about not putting information in any database. I think logging is a pretty good compromise, as long as you take it with a grain of salt. Just because somebody didn't find a box (log=attempted) doesn't necessarily mean it is missing. However, if several people log it as attempted, then that gives it more credence.

I still try to send people emails about their boxes, especially if there is a problem, and I rely heavily on emails about my boxes to update their status. If I go on a big trip with lots of finds, I still like to do a sitrep if possible, and I enjoy reading about other's trips, too. Bottom line, logging is another tool to be used, or not, at your discretion.
