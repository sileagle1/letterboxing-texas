From: Pats (votremerci@yahoo.com) | Date: 2007-11-02 10:59:16 UTC-07:00
I'm glad you decided not to change your name, Stargazer.  I've got a history of looking for, being frustrated for not finding (esp. FWAS), delight in finding some of yours on this year's trip to Texas, being astounded by the fact that some them were left standing, sealed but out in the open (Whelk especially since it was a group project) without a single leaf, twig, doggie doo or anything (not that I'm recommending covering a box with doggie doo) to conceal the letterboxes that should be hidden to the casual or interested eye.
 
Up here where I live, crowds hitting the boxes have resulted in many losses, due to attention getting activity and by poor replacement.  Parents of some of the crowds have bragged, despite having "Be discreet" stated 4 times in the clues, "Discreet at the mall, we were not!!" followed by laughter.  The kids chimed in, "We don't know how to do that!"
 
I take that to mean that they won't do that.
 
Hence, some of the better boxes - usually those donated by others for me to plant, which is one of my favorite parts of this hobby - now have a P5/F50.  I feel badly that I'm excluding people, but by the same token, I won't come across one of my boxes sitting right on the middle of the trail with a note in the logbook "Too many spiders!" but no note via email that they didn't rehide it.  This was not a case of an animal dragging, before you guys start to look for possibilities.
 
In this latest bit here, I can certainly see both sides and agree with both.  It's tough when you're new and you think you're being judged, especially unfairly because you haven't done anything yet.  On the other hand, a lot of time - more than many people would believe - goes into the choices of image, carving, placement, and upkeep.  Somes its hard not to be objective when trying to give judicious advice.  I'm one of those that people think I'm bring rude when I ask them to rehide well, in terms I think way too soft, but evidently, is taken much meaner that I wanted it to.  Really though, don't leave corners of boxes sticking out or sticks sticking out and such to 'help the next person.'
 
Anyway, I'm being verbose, which rhymes with my last name, incidently and probably Barefoot Lucy is casting her moderator wary eye at this message, shaking her LB finger at me.  ;-)

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-11-02 21:00:23 UTC
I have some boxes with a F-500 count. But that is because they are
basically my WOM boxes, it's just easier for me to have the clues
stashed somewhere where I can get to them easily to email them to
someone else. I do plan on having some upcoming ones with
P-restrictions. The vast majority of my boxes have no P or F
restriction of any kind. I occasionally do boxes that have some sort
of condition on them, like I did one that required a P-25 (but you had
to email me for clues).

You may want to play around with them a bit, to see what level of
finders you're comfortable with.

If you put P & F restrictions on your boxes at AQ, it is better to
remove the link if they're linked from LBNA. People get a weird error
message otherwise. With restrictions on AQ, if they don't qualify,
they'll never know the box is there.

dewberry

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-11-02 19:41:44 UTC-05:00
I personally like the idea of making boxes more difficult via puzzles and mysteries because it is a natural way of eliminating dabblers without being a snob, and for inexperienced finders who have the will, they will strive to elevate themselves to the level required to get those boxes.  And once a finder goes to the trouble of solving a mystery or a puzzle, they are far more likely to show their appreciation by properly handling the box when they find it.
 
And since there are far more easy boxes out there than hard ones, even those dabblers have plenty of fodder to try out before they get down to the puzzles.

I personally like the idea of making boxes more difficult via puzzles and mysteries because it is a natural way of eliminating dabblers without being a snob, and for inexperienced finders who have the will, they will strive to elevate themselves to the level required to get those boxes.  And once a finder goes to the trouble of solving a mystery or a puzzle, they are far more likely to show their appreciation by properly handling the box when they find it.
 
And since there are far more easy boxes out there than hard ones, even those dabblers have plenty of fodder to try out before they get down to the puzzles.
