We may all be hiders and seekers, but Geocachers and Letterboxers are not the same, and if you don't believe that then you are more of a Geocacher than Letterboxer.  Geocachers want the public to know about their hobby and actively advertise it.  Letterboxers are secretive and don't want their hobby advertised.  Because of this basic difference in philosophies, Geocachers have developed a rapport with State Parks and feel obligated to get permission before placing.  That is great for them, but not for us.  Even though State Parks may have legitimate reasons for requiring permits, it will never fit with our hobby.  To paraphrase Patrick Henry:   "Give me freedom from permits (liberty), or give me cemeteries (death)".

Names aside. Whether you are a Geocacher, letterboxer or both, we are ALL hiders and seekers. Scavenger hunters extraordinaire.  Not only do the Parks want to put safety of their visitors in top priority, but they are concerned about the protection of our parks. They have the right to know where things are in the park. Will it's placement cause a "geo trail" from future finders tromping through the brush? Is it in an area with endangered plants? Not everyone is familiar with the flora and geology of the parks in all seasons the way the staff are.  Maybe they know that the area is prone to flooding in wet seasons or will be completely overgrown by poison ivy in summer. If you're placing a container, is is something that a squirrel could destroy in 2 seconds and spread the contents all over or choke on the plastic baggies?  If they plan to do controlled burns in an area, they can warn hiders to temporarily remove their boxes, but only if they know they are there.

I know there is a lot a badmouthing of Geocachers in certain letterboxing circles. But, there are some good qualities of them as well. The first thing you will hear from most caching podcasts, websites, blogs, etc. Is to get permission before placing a container. It is courtesy and establishing good relations - not micro-managing.
