From: Boots Tex (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2013-08-09 18:30:05 UTC
Most people hide boxes wherever they go, even knowing they won't be back that way. You have to be willing to depend on others to provide maintenance for your box and when it goes missing, you must be willing to let it go. I don't generally place boxes without a logbook because I think that's an important part of the game. Many people will stamp into a logbook when they won't log a find online. So, if you ever do get to see your logbook, it's really fun to see who has been there that you never knew about. That being said, some people do place boxes with stamp only and no logbook. Since there are no rules about that, you are free to do whatever you want to do. What area do you plan to visit? There are places in Texas where boxes are very scarce.

My problem with the "fire and forget" mentality is what happens when no one in the foreign area steps up to do any maintenance. There ends up being boxes that are in disrepair or missing and they just stay on the "active" lists. New boxers might find them (or not) and think that's the best this hobby has to offer. I know I have found a few and some have turned out to be a waste of time on a search for missing boxes and/or were nothing more than geo-litter. When I was new at this, my first thoughts were that the owners were not very good and they were sorry for not doing upkeep on their boxes. Depending on others can backfire and it doesn't reflect badly on the area boxers, but on the owner on the listing.

Plus, don't you get enjoyment from seeing other peole's stamps in your logbook? I know I do. I personally don't plan to plant anything that I'm not going to take responsibility for maintenance. But as this is everyone's game, each to his own.

From: Boots Tex (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2013-08-10 23:16:22 UTC
Since there are few rules in letterboxing, you are entitled to your opinion. If you don't want to plant letterboxes outside your home county, that is your privilege. However, if everyone did that, there wouldn't be very many letterboxes around. I know that's the way geocaching works, but to me that would be boring. One of the things that I like most about letterboxing is placing boxes wherever I go. It's my way of saying that I was here and I found this interesting place that I would like for you to see, or here's a bit of history that you might like to know about and it happened in this spot. Some places I will never visit again and sometimes, the need to repair or replace a box brings me back to a place. The best letterboxers are the ones that travel all over and place boxes as they go. I think it's exciting to go to California or Alabama and find a Silver Eagle box, or to go to South Texas and find a Wisconsin Hiker box or to North Texas and find a Safari Man box. That's part of the allure of letterboxing.

Boots Tex

Yes, even though we have yet to meet many of you Texas boxers, we know your names and in May we were happy to find some of the boxes you planted in Arkansas & Oklahoma.  We found boxes by Silver Eagle, Viewfinder, Lucy Locket and BlueButterfly.  We've found letterboxes in all 50 states and when vacationing we usually seek out boxes planted by people we have met, especially if the boxes aren't in their "home state".  That way we can give them a status update on how their far-flung boxes are faring. 

 

Way back  in 2004 we adopted a box that we found in Hawaii.  The box had been planted in 2001, but the initial planters were no longer active.  Since that time the box has survived a fire and over the years several people vacationing in the area have done maintenance by replacing the container, logbook and/or baggies when needed.  I'm happy to report that the box was still active in March of this year.  The box is now 12 years old! 

 

So far we have planted boxes in 45 states and hope to plant in the other 5 remaining states within the next year or two.  We've also planted in Puerto Rico, Mexico, New Zealand and Finland and it is very exciting when we get emails that these boxes have been found.  (Still waiting for someone to find our 5 Puerto Rico boxes...)

From: Christine Borgfeld (christine@borgfeld.net) | Date: 2013-08-13 20:35:38 UTC-05:00

Maybe it’s about expectations.  If you start in one hobby you get used to things being a certain way and then when you start in the other it doesn’t seem right.  Those starting in letterboxing and moving to geocaching would probably find the rules too restrictive - someone has to review and approve your plant before it’s listed, and you have either live close to the cache or prove you visit a location often enough to maintain it.  That would probably give letterboxers fits, but geocachers start out that way and don’t think twice about it. 

 

The attitudes of the communities are also different which causes some friction.  In geocaching, the planter is expected to follow the rules and keep their boxes in good shape, and cachers are expected to provide lots of feedback on box condition to help the planter and others.  In letterboxing the boxer is expected to respect the wishes of the planter, help with maintenance if they can, and be cautious about logging attempts (since this can discourage future boxers).  The differences are subtle, and if you move from geocaching to letterboxing it’s likely you won’t catch on before you offend someone – I know I did!

 

Maybe we need a letterboxing guide for geocachers that we post every few months for people just discovering letterboxing?
