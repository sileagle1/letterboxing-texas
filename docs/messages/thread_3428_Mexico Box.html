From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2008-03-21 16:54:04 UTC-05:00
There are two sides to that issue (like there are to most things). Whereas it's true that you probably won't be able to return and maintain your box, often people who live nearer to the box will volunteer to check on it from time to time. Just last week, Barefoot Lucy was kind enough to check on my box at Rusk (former) State Park. I personally think it's nice to hide a box or so when you're traveling, because people in that region get to experience boxes hidden by someone other than those whom they may already be familiar. I love it when our friends from Houston or east Texas or wherever  hide a box in our area (DFW). I consider it to be a real treat. I had a box in Reno that was steadily visited for a couple of years before it finally went missing. I received such nice notes from people who enjoyed finding the wonderful location (a hill with a great view of Reno) where it was planted. When it went missing, I simply retired it.

Ditto what PuddleSplasher said - it's a two-sided issue.  It could be said that placing a box where you can't care for it is the equivalent of leaving behind what will eventuall become trash, but there is a lot of residual pleasure in leaving a box somewhere that you enjoyed - i.e. a vacation spot.
 
When I first started out (I'm feeling like an old lady because I've written this now twice in the last hour in responding on this list), there were so very few boxes in my area, and those were thanks to the kindness of Silver Eagle and Baby Bear, who diligently plant everywhere they go.  I was so desperate for boxes to find that I told letterboxers that if they would only plant in my area, I would be glad to maintain their boxes for them - and to my immense pleasure, they came through like gangbusters.  Most of the boxes in my area now are planted by local planters, and it works out that all of us locals take care of those boxes by distant planters. 
 
I am still ever so grateful to all of y'all who fed my need way back then...

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2008-03-22 12:15:22 UTC-04:00
About those long-distance letterboxes:I've planted a few boxes in far-away places that I'm unlikely to revisit, at least not anytime soon, and I probably get more excited when someone contacts me about finding one of those boxes than my local boxes because it's like getting a postcard from a traveling friend.  At least briefly, I mentally revisit the place and remember carving that special stamp for that location and finding what I hope to be a good safe location, since I won't be there to check on it.  I have boxes in Connecticut, Arizona, Colorado, far West Texas (Marfa), East Texas (Rusk SP), and South Texas (Somervell SP) -- all far from here in the DFW metroplex.   I'm also trusting in the letterboxing community to let me know if a logbook needs replacing and hoping someone in the area will be kind enough to help me out there if I send a new one, as Lucy did with my Rusk box recently.  It's sort of like a souvenir in reverse - leaving just a small personal part of yourself in a place you really enjoyed, and knowing it's still there, waiting to be discovered. 
    But I also admit that my first intended vacation-spot letterbox, a moose stamp I carved and took with me to Canada and Montana with full intentions of planting somewhere there, came back home with me because I just couldn't bear to leave it so far away, and it finally found a home at Lake Grapevine (The Lost Moose, the first of the now-famous Grapevine moose population), and sadly, it washed away in the summer floods last year.  Had I left it in Canada, who knows, maybe I'd still be hearing from finders!  All letterboxing is faith and chance - you  plant it out there and see what happens, depending on acts of Nature and other Letterboxers for its fate.
