Thanks, Joy. The article (minus any pictures) showed up just fine -
very neat, not messy at all. I had been searching all over the
internet and couldn't find anything, so came back here to check, and
saw that you had copied and pasted the article. Thanks. :)

So, the idea in the article, I guess, is: "Money left at graveside
goes back to the coins left with the dead in ancient Greece to pay
Charon, ferryman over the River Styx -- the price of transport to
the Underworld. And then there's the Jewish tradition of putting a
small rock on the gravestone to mark one's visit. Leaving a small
remembrance is common in many cultures." But I don't know... This is
an old Catholic Polish Cemetery, and looking around as best I could
on my rush out of the cemetery, I didn't see any coins or anything
unusual on any other graves. I just don't think the 'River Styx'
thing would be observed there. So, I am still baffled. And the money
was not really, really old from what I saw on top of the pile - just
ordinary, modern day nickles and pennies....on a really old grave.
Still, maybe it was just a simple 'remembrance' in sympathy for the
loss of a child.

I didn't have a minute to spare today, but what I should have done
was stop in the Panna Maria museum/visitor center and ask the ladies
there. So if anyone else remembers to do so when you go for that
letterbox, maybe you'll check with them. If anyone does, please
report back to the 'inquiring minds' here. ;)

Also, now that I think about it, the last time I was at the old
Helena Mexican cemetery (very near Panna Maria), I explored all
around and looked at all the headstones and didn't see any coins at
all there.

Busy Bee

Re: Solidarnosc (Coins on tombstone)

From: Wanda (wsthm@hotmail.com) | Date: 2005-04-02 04:48:57 UTC

Curiousity got the better of me, and I called the visitor's center
this morning, but the only lady who was there, said she had no idea.
She had never seen such a thing, although she did say that she had
heard of 'little children leaving a teddy bear on a brother or
sister's grave'. She said that she visits the cemetery quite often,
but had never seen that, and only attends to her family's
gravesites. She said that she would ask the other visitor center
volunteers when she sees them.

So then today, I asked a few ladies I was working with, and they had
never heard of it either.

Part of my puzzlement comes from the fact that it is modern day
money (I think.) on a very old grave. I don't remember the date on
the tombstone, (1800's???) but I wouldn't think there would be
anyone alive now who would have known the child. So to me, that
somewhat rules out the 'remembrance token' thing.

Busy Bee
