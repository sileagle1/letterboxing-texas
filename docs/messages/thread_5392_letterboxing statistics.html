Take a look at this link and type in letterboxing in the search term box…

 

http://itools.com/tool/google-trends-topic-popularity

 

this is a tool for tracking popularity of keywords in google searches over time.  Basically it shows that searches on letterboxing have been in steady decline since it peaked in 2006.  This prompted me to go look at the AQ stats page.  Messages on the forum peaked in 2009 and were at half of the peak volume last year.  The peak number of boxes planted was also in 2009, but that is dropping more slowly.  Finds peaked in 2010 and are still not far off the high.  I’m curious what do you think this means for the hobby?

 

You can also enter geocaching and to compare it to letterboxing…it’s much bigger but also leveling off.  It’s growth spiked up in 2010 but seems to be leveling off and starting a bit of decline also.  Both are very seasonal with most interest in the warmer months (no surprise there).

From: Vicki Bell (vicki@bellmotel.com) | Date: 2014-09-13 17:10:30 UTC-05:00
The hobby seems to be in decline in Dartmoor as well.  Generally, the members of the Dartmoor 100 club whom we met no longer box, mostly due to age.  The enthusiastic "godfather of modern-day letter-boxers" who popularized the hobby in the 1970s, Godfrey Swinscow, is in an assisted-living facility now.  Many boxes on the moors have been stolen, and newly-planted boxes are being stolen almost immediately, which makes active boxers leery of planting any new boxes.  Finding boxes on the moor is no longer like an Easter-egg hunt.  Not sure what will happen next there.  -- Bell Motel

From: (sileagle1@yahoo.com) | Date: 2014-09-14 19:52:17 UTC-07:00

This confirms a suspicion I have had that interest in letterboxing is declining, but I wasn't sure if my view was tainted because I don't use AQ, where most new boxers would probably go.  The fact that it is declining also is sad news indeed.  Participation on this List is way down, too, probably because all us "old timers" are talked out, and no new people are joining.

At the risk of sounding morose, I think this doesn't bode well for letterboxing.  Most letterboxers don't actively participate after a few years, preferring to lurk on Lists rather than actively pursue the hobby, so without an influx of newbies I'm afraid it will die a slow death.  LBNA already has one foot in the grave.  I guess I am an aberration in that I still actively participate, albeit not as much as a few years ago, but even I am getting tired of losing stamps and boxes to Geocachers.  Through the years I have battled nature, muggles, park rangers, critters and even other letterboxers to keep my boxes safe, but I have no remedy for Geocachers since they will ultimately find most letterboxes unwittingly while looking for the millions of Geocaches out there, and it only takes one taking the stamp to ruin a box.  The only way for us to coexist is if they change their rules to no longer take things out of Geocaches, but I don't see that happening.

So what is the solution?  Well, I still don't believe in advertising like Geocaching does, but it would be nice if we could recruit new members in a subtle way.  Or, maybe the hobby has run its course and people are only interested in the next new thing.  Slow growth is good, fast growth is bad, and no growth is probably fatal.  The good news is that many of you still come to TALE, and that makes it worth continuing on for me.  Cheers to those of you still hanging in there!

Silver Eagle

We are new members to the letterboxing community and are enjoying it! And it's all thanks to girl scouts for letting us know about it. From: Vicki Bell (vicki@bellmotel.com) | Date: 2014-09-15 20:10:26 UTC-05:00
Yes, we attended the meeting of the Dartmoor 100 Club on the Wednesday evening of the trip.  Roger and Stephanie Paul, also in the Smithsonian article, had come to where we stayed the night before to give a presentation on landmarks used for letterboxing on the moors, and to give us all our Lifetime Membership Cards, and the opportunity to buy patches.  So, we arrived at the meeting as full-blown members of the club!  It was quite delightful to laugh and talk with everyone, and trade Traveler images.  (They don't trade signature stamp images, so I was glad I brought my one Personal Traveler.)  They often collect images on 5x8 cards to be filed, rather than in a logbook.  However, some have put together logbooks grouping stamps from various series together, since a series of boxes in Dartmoor may be planted all over the moors, and is collected over time.  There was also a Dutchman in attendance at that meeting, who comes to the moors for three weeks, three times a year, to letterbox.  We ate and drank and talked and stamped, and had a great time!
-- Bell Motel
