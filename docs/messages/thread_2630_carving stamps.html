From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-03-08 11:36:05 UTC-06:00
I've taught carving to Girl Scouts before, as has Thimble Cats...
 
For my part, I gave gauges to the younger girls and exactos to the older girls, and they did very well.  I did, however, give them ALL very careful directions on how to use the knives - 1) hold the blade still and turn the material, 2) keep the cutting edge DOWN, 3) keep the material on the table - don't try to cut with it in your lap or anywhere else unstable.  Counterintuitive is to make sure you have very sharp exacto blades - in Cub Scouts we teach "Sharp is safe, dull is dangerous".  A dull exacto blade is much more likely to slip and slice than a sharp one is. 
 
I made sure they knew I was giving them zero tolerance rules, and that I would take a knife away from anyone who wasn't being safe with it.  These girls had obviously had pretty good knife training though, and I never once felt they were incapable.  There were also plenty of adults to go around.
 
Remember also, they can use gauges without handles.  I've done this myself, holding the gauge in my fingertips.  Don't underestimate the bite of a speedball gauge though - my worst cuts have come from the gauges.

Re: carving stamps

From: ThimbleCat (inklaura@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-03-09 14:40:46 UTC
Lucy gave some great tips. I have not had my 3rd graders carve yet,
but my own kids and my nieces (6ys to 11ys) have been able to do it
WITH supervision. They used the speedball tip and carved a
negative image (where the design is the part that's cut away).

Other options you might want to do:
1. Carve a single stamp for the troop (let the girls help design it)
and have the girls just sign their names to the logbook.
2. Have the girls design their own stamps and have someone else do
the carving - they feel like it's theirs because it is their own
artwork.
3. Have them cut a simple design from funfoam and attach it to a film
canister with a square of double stick framing foam. This is what I
teach to my letterboxing GS leader class and you would really be
surprised at the great foam stamps that are possible. I think there
is a good demo about this at AtlasQuest.
4. If your tools are limited, have a few come early and a few stay
late for a couple of meetings until all the carving is done.

My troop has one troop stamp with our mascot and troop number carved
into it and we use that to stamp the letterbox logbook. Then each
girl stamps the box stamp into their own logbook. So far that seems
to be fulfilling their stamp "fix". Plus it makes it much
easier/faster for the entire troop (I have 12 girls) to stamp in.
After someone has stamped into the box logbook, we pass it around for
them to look at.

Good luck in whatever you decide to do. With enough supervision and
strict safety rules, kids CAN carve. But the carving is not always
necessary for them to enjoy letterboxing (even some grown up boxers
don't carve and that's ok).
ThimbleCat

From: sandramilfeld (studiousmom@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-03-09 17:48:49 UTC
I teach stamp carving, linoleum carving, and wood carving every year
in my art classes, and I just require that parents be present for
those classes. I explain how to safely carve with the tools (keep
your hand behind the direction of blade travel, etc.) and leave
enforcement up to the parents. The only one that ever got cut in clas
was me. :-)

Also, sharp blades is a must. Dull blades are dangerous...no matter
how old or young you are.

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2008-07-25 00:20:23 UTC-05:00
Hi, Fran. Thanks for finding that series. Since it's on a rather long trail in a fairly isolated park, that series isn't found all that often, It is a nice hike though, with a variety of landscapes! When I planted that series, my husband and I decided to follow that trail on around the loop after I placed my last box. I thought I was going to die before we got back to the parking lot. The next time, I turned around and retraced my steps back the way I came, and it was much more manageable. It helps when it's not 100 degrees out!
 
I use Staedtler carving tools which I ordered several years ago from Mister Art at http://www.misterart.com/store/view.cfm?group_id=7883&store=001. The set is around $18. I primarily use the 1V for finer details, and the 5U for removing background and larger spaces. Recently I've used an xacto knife from time to time (depending on my mood) and trace along the outline of my image, then go back with the blade at a slight angle and cut out a trench along the original line. There are so many really good carvers out there, and they all seem to develop their own technique.
 
You can order the Speedball pink stuff from Mister Art also ($4.39 for a 4 x 6" block). I used to buy it at Asel Art supply in Dallas in big blocks, but they've not had it the last few times I stopped by there, and the "guy" can't tell me if they'll reorder it or not.
 
More than the carving tools, the thing that has helped me most when carving details is to use a magnifying light. I have one mounted on my work table with a swing arm. Before I got that, I used one of those magnifying glasses that you hang around your neck (sold in craft stores for doing needlework), but I love that the lamp illuminates my work and isn't hanging around my neck. Some of them are quite costly, but mine wasn't too bad, plus I used a 40% off coupon from Hobby Lobby when I bought it.
 
I'm so glad you're having fun letterboxing. I can't wait to get up to Commerce and find your new box (or maybe boxes)!
Puddle Splasher
