From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2011-07-18 06:42:20 UTC-06:00
Good morning.

Still recovering from jet-lag - but settling in at home now. We had a GREAT
time. In many ways, Germany has not changed. In other ways - it has
changed completely. Progress?

I never even thought of a letterbox over there. We got up at 0600 every
day, and pretty much stayed awake and moving until we couldn't keep our eyes
open every night. And we were busy nearly every minute. On the family
front, it was perfect. My wife's mom and dad are getting old - mid-70s.
But they are in good health and are doing just fine. Her sister Gerda is
just as I remember her, except her English is much improved (as is my German
again - now). Gerda is the manager of a supermarket across the street from
a U.S. Army housing complex, she has plenty of time to use her English
daily. We had not met her husband Helmut ever (27 years) but we hit it
right off perfectly. Gerda and Helmut are both ham radio operators, so a
lot of our activities centered around radio (as it was 27 years ago when I
was there).

The base I was assigned to is still there. The building I was assigned to
is now the post office. That is about the sum total of the time I spent
looking over the military side of my life over there.

About half the days the weather sucked. Some days it was so hot and humid
we were uncomfortable (from the humidity, not heat). Other days it was so
cold-windy-foggy-raining that we had rain coats over winter jackets. We did
not let that stop us. Helmut has a bad leg, motorcycle accident when he was
young. That doesn't stop him. I and Helmut climbed 7 summits to operate
ham radio. 3 of them the ladies accompanied us. I have operated with rain
dripping off my nose, and with sweat dripping off my nose. 4 of the summits
had a restaurant at the top. 3 summits were in formerly enemy occupied
territory, East Germany. This was behind the Iron Curtain, it was the
reason I was there for 7-1/2 years. We never approached the border with
under a squad of riflemen, fully armed - locked and loaded. This time there
was nothing to stop us.

The first time we crossed the border I was very anxious. Old feelings are
hard to let go of. I didn't know what to expect. Well - we spent a total
of 4 days in the formerly occupied area. At least half a dozen times men
stopped me on the streets and shook my hand and thanked me for standing on
the border and eventually forcing the fall of the curtain and causing them
to be free. I got to tour the border check-point in the Meiningen Gap,
where I was stationed. It is abandoned now, and somewhat vandalized. I
only wanted to spend a few moments reflecting. We spent over two hours and
it was pretty emotional for me. You need to understand that border was more
meant to keep them in than to keep us out. It was obvious by the placement
of the (now disconnected) electric fence.

In one restaurant in the city of Meiningen we were not allowed to pay for
our dinner. The restaurant owner had tears in his eyes as he shook my hand
and told me of how he was forced to work the border as a Young Pioneer" of
the state, forced to learn and speak Russian and guard the border. By the
time he had finished, I too had tears in my eyes. We slept one night in a
motel - this was too cool. It is owned by a widow lady named Angelina. She
is your typical East-European/Russian, well-built with short cropped blond
hair and muscular arms. She and her son are slowly converting a former
Russian Army Barracks into a hotel, room by room. The rooms are nice, but
it is fully obvious what the building was. We paid about $15 per person.
These people are converting from Communism to Capitalism. Every place we
could get away with it we left a huge tip and a big thank-you. That wasn't
often.

I used to belong to a radio club in Schweinfurt, B-14 for Bavarian Club #14.
Sadly, they are all old men now and very inactive. I only talked to one
member, and he said about half the friends I asked about are dead now or so
old they are inactive. Helmut belongs to B-10 in Bad Neustadt. I made lots
of new friends there. I also made many friends in X-34, the city of
Meiningen in the state of Turinga. They were extremely interested in my
radio equipment and my portable antenna.

So we are back. Next summer we are sending my son and his new bride (as of
30 July) to visit Germany for a wedding present. When they come back they
will escort cousin Andrias to El Paso for a vacation. Two years from now
Gerda and Helmut hope to visit us. When will we go back? Not sure, but I
won't wait another 27 years this time.

I will be posting stories of our mountain outings on the web - I will let
you-all know here so you can read it if you like.

Oh - when I got home - poor River nearly pee'd on himself for joy. I missed
him too.
