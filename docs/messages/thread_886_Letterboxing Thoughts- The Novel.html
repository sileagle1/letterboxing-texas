Letterboxing thoughts- A Novel

Forgive me as I lapse into 3rd person for a minute.

The other day MonkeyToes decided to check on one of his local
letterboxes. The Riverside Duck Pond Box was a crowd favorite and
he wanted to make sure it was okay for the summer vacation season.
There was a small family at the pond, but they were busy pestering
the ducks with stale bread.

He hopped onto the rail and reached into the eaves to grasp
nothing. His eyes widened as his head screeched, "what the hell!"
Peering into the eave, all he saw was old mud-dauber nests and
nature lint. "Nonononono how could this happen? That was such a
good box" he says.
Trying not panic, he looks into the next section of rafters and
there it lies! MonkeyToes quickly grabs it and hops back down to
the cement floor. A quick glance around shows him that no one is
around. The ducks, having tired of moldy hot dog buns, must have
eaten the small family that was there. He opens up the box and
inspects its contents.

What's this? A folded sheet of notebook paper is lying in the
bottom. "Read if you want. I was bored," is written on one side.
He opens it up to what appears to be an essay on his frustrations
with capitalism. It's written by a kid named Corey Something.
MonkeyToes cannot decipher the last name. He reads the letter and
sees that it's pretty good and can tell the kid was trying to work
out some important issues. He writes "Thank you, Corey," on the
note and puts it back.

The logbook had several entries by people who obviously have no
idea what letterboxing is. The first odd entrant has no signature
stamp, just a little bible verse and a date. Then follows another,
similar entry offering words of wisdom. There are now at least four
or five encouraging phrases or words of advice mixed in with the
stamps of the letterboxing public.
"Interesting," thinks MonkeyToes. "My box has turned into a
support group for teenagers. That's kind of neat as long as they
don't mess anything up. I should probably check on this one more
often. I wonder if anyone else has had this happen?" He then puts
the box back in it's correct place and carefully returns to his
car. He goes around the long way to avoid the carnivorous ducks.

*end third person*

Well? Has anything like this happened to anyone else? It's a
little bizarre, but it's kind of nice. I wonder how they found it
in the first place since it's invisible from the ground and the
rail. You really have to stick your head up in there to see it.


-MonkeyToes, director of letterboxing psychology

Well that is indeed verrrry interesting!

1) How did they find it? Teenagers can find anything,
anywhere because they literally HANG around.
Sometimes upside down, sometimes sideways, sometimes
even hanging by their eyeballs you would think. They
are a curious lot with a constant need to learn more
AND the means to go exploring on their own. Spaces
that most folks would never notice are considered to
be alternate universes to teenagers (hehe...and
letterboxers). I know all of this because I am the
proud owner of a teenager.

2) Will they mess it up? Most of them won't,
especially if they've adopted it as a forum for
philosophical discourse. Of course, there will be the
occasional one who MIGHT make a mess of it, but if the
rest of them respect it as a safe place to write their
thoughts, they will police themselves. And actually,
they probably won't tell troublemakers about it,
because they don't want to expose their thoughts to
folks who will take it lightly. I would, if I were in
your shoes, check it more often and make sure it is
amply supplied with paper and perhaps write occasional
notes of encouragement back to them, as you did. I
might leave a letterboxing information sheet in it for
them, but frankly, it doesn't sound as if it is your
box any more - it sounds like it has taken on a life
of its own.

I think it's really neat that has happened with your
box. I spend a lot of time with and around teenagers
(remember, I have one of my very own and I also work
with them in scouts and formerly in soccer). I have a
great deal of respect for them. They are taking a
world that has become all too small and working out
how they will fit into it. They are actively
examining themselves and the world around them and, I
think, digging deep to find a way to make themselves
meaningful in ways recent generations haven't had to
do. I attended my daughter's baccalaureate (yeesh - I
KNOW that's spelled wrong!) a couple weeks ago, and
one of the speakers pointed out that these graduating
seniors were freshmen at the time of 9/11. They were
pretty much fully cognizant of what was going on at
the time, and they are old enough to remember pre-9/11
and understand how things are changing in post-9/11.

As with any generation, there are the good, the bad,
and the ugly, but I believe today's teenagers will
make great leaders of the future and I believe that if
they are taking an opportunity to express themselves
in your letterbox, you are honored.

It reminds me of the fact that we started getting our
boats out and ready for the summer last weekend, and
when we uncovered one of them, we found a very large
hatch of baby ducks staring up at us. We realized
that for the moment at least, we don't have a boat, we
have a big metal duck nest.

Lucy

P.S. I guess we will just hope they aren't
carnivorous ducks!
