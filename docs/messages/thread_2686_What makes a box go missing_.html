What makes a box go missing?

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-04-01 18:52:58 UTC-05:00
I recently had someone ask me why boxes go missing.  They were bummed over the lost of their box, which they thought was well-hidden and would last forever.
 
I don't know about the rest of y'all, but when I have a box go missing, I can say things like "an animal must've gotten it" or "it was muggled" or "it wasn't rehidden well", but the long and short of it is sometimes (if I'm honest with myself) that I could've done a better job of hiding it in the first place.
Take as an example - Go Jacks.  I'm really bummed I lost that box.  But I'm frankly surprised it lasted as long as it did.  It was 1) in a high traffic area, 2) hidden under a rock that someone was eventually going to move, even if just for curiosity's sake, and 3) in a place that was very clean.  I think the only reason it lasted as long as it did was because I had to be emailed for clues so anyone who went and looked for it was keenly aware of the need to rehide well. 
 
SO - what about y'all?  Have you ever visited a box and said "This one won't be here long" and sure enough it wasn't?  How did you know?  What were the tell-tale signs?  What are flags for you that a box won't live a long life?
 
Hopefully all our answers will be helpful to new planters or planters who are having trouble with boxes that disappear!

Yep.   I agree with that one.  I once came upon one of DrooP's like that - it looked like a funeral pyre in the middle of a tree.  I pared it down quite a bit, but even after I got rid of about half the stack, it still looked really obvious.  I hated to do any more because I didn't want it to be noticeably missing to anyone who might frequent the trail. 
 
I think there has to be a good balance between plenty of fodder in the area for hiding and overuse of fodder and sticks and rocks and such.
 
I went out to my Killough Monument box today - I love the way that one hides.  I turn the rock over and find the box, then turn it back and it looks as though the rock hasn't been turned for months.  It's hard to find good hiding places like that.

I'm always afraid of boxes washing/floating away in one of our torrential downpours.  The airtight boxes float well.  This is one reason I like to use rocks to block a box on a side or sides and the top, esp. if it's on an incline.

And boxes in holes - if the hole is the type that drains slowly and therefor fills with water - the box might float up and out.

As far as high traffic - a tree cavity in the woods could be a good spot, but a tree cavity in a busy area is tempting to look into...  :)

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-04-03 20:39:52 UTC-05:00
I always worry about flooding too - I try to make sure to look for waterlines on trees when I plant, so I know how high the water gets.  Of course, a water line doesn't tell the story in unusual rains, so it doesn't hurt to play it safe and use a better grade container and a big rock if the water lines are even close to the level where you will be planting.
 
I have a box hidden in a hole - I thought when I planted it that I might regret it, but apparently the hole does drain well because the box has been in place for a good while now.
