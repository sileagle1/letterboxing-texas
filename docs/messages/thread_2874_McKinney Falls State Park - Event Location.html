From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2007-06-24 17:47:51 UTC
Sorry to hear about this, especially since I was planning on finding
them soon. I wonder if Baby Bear got the same message?

This is a situation that I have seen coming and dreaded. It might be
a State-wide mandate, but not all parks have been enforcing it
equally, but that might be changing. I'm afraid placing boxes in
State Parks without permits is just not going to work in the long
run.

Now normally, I'm all for following the rules and getting permission
when appropriate, but this is one time it just doesn't seem worth it
to me. When I was requested to complete a permit for one of my boxes
that was confiscated, I tried to comply, but the requirements are
just too stringent. They require you to pick a spot that is
acceptable to them, and re-register again every year. As ya'll know,
many of my boxes are in State Parks scattered all over TX (and
beyond), and it just isn't feasible for me to re-register or visit
all of them every year. If I only had boxes in a State Park near me,
I probably would do like Mike and cultivate a relationship, but then
I would only have boxes in that one park. However, I also feel
uncomfortable claiming responsibility for anything found in the box,
whether I put it there or not, so not sure I would sign a permit even
then. I guess as my boxes get discovered they will be confiscated,
and I will avoid State Parks for future plants, since I don't want to
break the rules or follow them either.

As far as future Gathering venues, we may have to accept the fact
that no boxes will be in the park, but be satisfied with many boxes
placed nearby, and Austin meets that criteria. It might not be so
bad since the main purpose of Events is to meet other letterboxers,
and finding boxes is just an added bonus. The only alternative would
be to find a non-State Park with camping, a shelter big enough for
us, and plenty of trails for boxes. If anyone knows of such a place
in Central TX, I can look into it.

Which alternative do ya'll prefer?

Silver Eagle

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-06-24 14:18:28 UTC-05:00
I can't remember which state park (I thought I would never forget this, but apparently I have, at least temporarily) in my area freely allows letterboxes without permits.  When I approached the ranger three or four months ago about their permit requirements (I thought I would try the ask first approach), he said they don't require permits and they had decided to deal with problems on a case-by-case basis.  The ranger went on to say that they had found geocachers and letterboxers to be strong advocates of their park, found us to be responsible users, good payors, and frequent visitors.  Given the current state of the budget for TPWD, I would say that park is doing a particularly good job of picking its battles and instead joining with us to see how we can mutually benefit each other. 
 
They've been trying to close Rusk State Park for two years now, and since that began and I became more mindful of the budget issues, I've made it a point to write anyone and everyone as high up the chain as seems relevant and who might care, to let them know how much I have enjoyed a particular park and why I found it to be pleasant.  Only once have I sent in a bad review, and I sent it to the lowest level guy NOT in that park so that maybe he could work on it.  I do also try to spend a little time talking to rangers and getting a feel for their philosophy, for future reference boxing-wise.
 
I can't get real excited about planting in state parks anymore myself though. As I see it, the risk is the existence of a policy (which specifies geocaching, and presumably means to cover other treasure hunt games even though it doesn't have any "or other" phraseology) coupled with inconsistency in applying the policy.  The park I described above is only as friendly to us as its head ranger, and if the next guy comes in and wants permits, he may well pull every box in the park and treat them as suspect even though there was a "don't ask don't tell" sort of local approach previously.

I wonder if each park could have a designated letterbox manager or
volunteer. For instance, since I live pretty close to Huntsville
State Park, I could "adopt" it for letterboxing. I could check on
each box periodically and, when a new box is placed, bring the clues
to the attention of the ranger and get his approval. I would be
authorized to move the box to his satisfaction if necessary and
notify the placer of the new location. If the box is objectionable
for some reason, I could pick it up and notify the placer. I would
return it to the placer if he/she so desired. Of course, this would
have to be worked out with the Parks and Wildlife folks. Just a
thought. As for McKinney Falls SP, I still think it is a good
location for an event. McKinney Roughs, which is a Lower Colorado
River Authority park, would be a good place to hide boxes and it's
about 12 or 13 miles away, but there are no camping facilities or
facilities for a gathering that I know of.

Boots Tex
