From: Mike Olbrisch (mike2004@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2006-10-24 03:47:09 UTC
Well - a lot of my feelings are biased in a different way.  Let me try to explain - although many will not understand it anyway.
 
Let me preface it this way.  Military service changes everyone.  I can think of no person I know who came out the same as they went in.  Combat changes you even more.  Some people are destroyed by it, some come out stronger.  Those that survive it that is.  I got stronger.  My best friend was destroyed.  He lives up in WA-state somewhere.  Will not answer calls or E-mails.  He asked to get out, and they wouldn't let him go.  So he ate his way onto the fat-boy program and got tossed out that way.
 
Example-1.  We are hiking along a trail together.  You see a beautiful arroyo with lots of green plants and rugged breaks.  You think - what a pretty scene.
 
I am hiking ahead of you.  I am super-alert - head swiveling and eyes constantly moving.  I am thinking - what a terrible place to be caught in an ambush.  I may be sub-consciously holding my hiking stick at high-port.
 
Example-2.  You and I are standing in a parking lot talking.  A car going by back-fires.  You are now standing there talking to yourself.  I am under a car somewhere.
 
My father is a perfect example.  He was a policeman in Chicago for 25 years.  He saw the raw guts of the city so many times he now has a very hard time seeing the good in anything.  In some respects I am much the same.  I am not complaining.  It comes with the job.  I am blessed with a wife who can almost read my thoughts and always says the right words to bring me back to the real world and sets me at ease.  And 10 years of retirement has dulled the reflexes a bit.  I can almost stand watching a fireworks display and see the beauty in it again for instance, as opposed to imagining seeing a combat vehicle blowing up or something like that in my mind.
 
The things I saw of other countries were NOT the touristy things people go to see.  What I did get in years of travel is a very good understanding of the world.  I will say America isn't always right.  But, I will also say that I am certain I don't want to be anywhere else.
 
On the AMUSING end of this - when I would return home my son James would often make fun of my reflexes.  The family was walking thru a park one evening.  A pack of firecrackers started popping and I dropped to the ground, rolled to a big tree, and shouted for the family to drop.  They laughed at me.  OK.  But now the shoe is on the other foot!  <grin>  He don;t think it is funny any more.  I am enjoying it more and more.  PAYBACK - for real this time!!!!  <grin>
 
I am not against travel.  I think everyone should experience other places.  But you come to a time when you decide for yourself - where you really belong.  Maybe had I been introduced to the touristy places as a civilian I might appreciate those places more.  But then again, that might mean I would appreciate the USA less.
 
Let me end it this way.  I love the USA.  I am ready to pick up a weapon and step forward to fight again if needed.  I don't fear dying, only cowardice and dishonor.  I know to some it sounds hokey.  But you get it or you don't.  If you don't that's OK.  We will fight to protect you too - anyway.
 
As to TEXAS.... I wasn't born in Texas.  But I got here as quick as I could.
 
I hope you get it - it is more a feeling inside than words can express.  I am not the boy that left Chicago so many years ago.  My son is not the boy that left El Paso after 9-11.  I am curious what my grandson will be like - if I ever get one......
 
Mike.
