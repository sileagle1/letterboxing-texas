Boxing in the North East

From: Christine Borgfeld (christine@borgfeld.net) | Date: 2014-07-19 22:51:05 UTC-05:00

Since it’s been so quiet here…I thought I’d share some recent letterboxing adventures.  Four of us from Texas attend the Grateful Letterboxer’s event in Massachusetts last weekend.  Bell Motel, Screamin Meemie, Wry Me, and I went up before the weekend and three of us actually camped!  The theme this year was Dark Star and all the boxes were space themed.  Star Wars, Star Trek, NASA, Astronauts, Moons, Planets, Constellations…and lots of space ships and aliens.  The hikes are beautiful.  Imagine an east Texas forest with ponds, hills, and rocks to climb over and around.  Some of the clues were a little challenging (the organizer responsible for writing them didn’t have time to double check things…he was too busy sailing through a hurricane!).  But we preserved and managed to find everything we went after.  There were two night boxing series, and tons of fun boxes all over the campground.  (can you say alien autopsy?)

 

The event is in early July and has such fun and creative boxes and clues.  The weather is more like late spring in Texas than summer – complete with some cool evenings in the 60s.  This is my second year attending this annual event (I first went in 2012) and I highly recommend it if you have a chance to go next year.  You can get tickets on sale from Southwest for about $200 to Boston and share a rental car.  Several people camped by borrowing most of what they needed from local boxers (tents, coolers, etc).  There are also several hotels within about 20 min of the park.

From: Wry Me (wry_me@yahoo.com) | Date: 2014-07-20 09:25:15 UTC-07:00
Ahhh, yes.  The hills.  I still twitch in my sleep.
 
Several thoughts on our adventure.  First of all, we had awesome campsite decorations, thanks to OS lugging them along and being all sparkly and creative.  The other thing I noticed, (because I'm the slow one and had plenty of opportunity for contemplation from WAAAAAYYYY in the back there, muttering, "Texas doesn't have HILLS, y'all!!"  Yeah, I know.  Some of you do, but still!  OS's fitbit said we did like the equivalent of 40 sets of stairs.  OMG.  I think I made a muscle.  Somewhere I don't need one!!!!!! Buns of steel?  Buns of aluminum foil -- everyone has to start somewhere, right?  But I digress.) is that TX carvers can hold their own!  It was fun seeing names of people from the boards and seeing carves from one of our own at an event. Open Space did us proud with her contribution. I was also impressed with how kind and generous the locals were in taking care of boxers from out of town.  I liked that they had a singalong in the evening that many of the longtimers were rushing to get to.  During the day, the kids gathered at the group campsite where they played all day under the supervision of several adult LB'ers who forewent their time on the trails to hang out with the kids and let them soak each other with squirt guns and basically tire themselves out being hooligans. 
 
I know that Bell Motel managed to get every, single box!  Go, you!  Many, many, had boxing at their actual campsites, which was fun since you got to sit around and chat or just sneak in while they were on the trails.
 
The generosity of spirit seems to be common to all letterboxers, so you still feel like you're part of a huge family you haven't even met yet.  But the really interesting thing is that letterboxers all over the country seem to have an odd underwear fetish.  While boxer shorts with hearts are preferred in one area of the country, it seems that tassled bras are the standard up there, even for men!, which apparently matches Bell Motel's preferences perfectly.  Whooo --WOO! as we say in my family when you drop your towel.
 
The other thing I learned was that it was a good thing I sucked it up and actually shelled out the big bucks for hiking shoes.  As much as it pains me to pay for anything, it really does matter.  One blister, but no crippling, thanks.
 
I also learned that in the northeast, they have a "camp coffee"-induced disability.  They cannot recognize faces and instead call you the name of the person to your left.  Consequently, Screamin' Meemie became Mosaic Butterfly, and I became no one, having no one to my left ever.  To help them with this disability, you must wear a hat.

From: (sileagle1@yahoo.com) | Date: 2014-07-21 13:31:47 UTC-07:00

Now this is the type of post I like to see:  letterboxing adventures!  It sounds like y'all had a great time and as an old Dead-Header I can appreciate the theme of the event.  I may have to check it out.  Thanks for posting about it and for representing Texas so well.

I just returned from a trip to Colorado with the family and though I didn't get to find many boxes, a couple were interesting.  When planning my trips I decide roughly where I want to go, then look at letterbox clues to see if there are any interesting places nearby that I have never heard of.  If so, I research them and add to the itinerary if time allows.  It is a tedious process, but has enabled me to go to places I never would have known about.  This time, while looking for boxes near Rocky Mt. NP, I found one called The Strangest Bird in a place called Windy Gap Watchable Wildlife Area near Grandby.  Even though the box is not currently there, I decided to add it to the itinerary since my wife loves bird watching.  Then, while researching the place, I found out it is on the Colorado Headwaters Scenic Byway, which I have never been on, so I added that drive, too.  It turned out to be quite scenic and I was so glad to have discovered it all from one box that I didn't find.

Another box, Rafting Teddys in Buena Vista, introduced me to a whole bunch of trails along the Arkansas River and gave me quite a surprise when I stamped in.  Two Texas boxers had stamped in a few years ago:  Dewberry and Viewfinder.  It is always fun to see fellow Texans in logbooks!  BTW, that area of Colorado is known for river rafting and I highly recommend it.  We did The Numbers with River Runners and it was exhilarating!  We stayed at Mt. Princeton Inn where you can soak in hot springs after rafting in the cold water.

I've had a few big physical struggles in my adventures, and I would classify swimming against the current in the Royal Gorge to get to shore from our overturned raft to be the third biggest struggle I've had to contend with (but definitely one of the scariest). Number two was reeling in an Amberjack on a deep sea fishing trip off the coast of Florida (took every ounce of energy in my body), and NUMBER ONE: Drum roll....... climbing those mountains on the Appalachian Trail. In fact, that was so hard, I'm planning to do another section next summer including McAfee Knob and Dragons Tooth, which requires hand over foot rock climbing with a 30 pound pack on my back. I'll have to carry a lot of days of food for that section because there aren't resupply places close to the trail, but at least there should be plenty of water along the trail. I'm not really an adrenaline junky, but I do enjoy a good challenge - especially when it's over!
