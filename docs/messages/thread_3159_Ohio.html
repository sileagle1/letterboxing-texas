From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2007-11-04 14:06:46 UTC-06:00
Oh that's funny. I used to get cabin fever when I lived in CT, so on sunny winter days, I'd do a little letterboxing (hiking) in the snow... even had to wear my snowshoes a couple of times. Truthfully the snow has so many places where it is disturbed from ice and snow falling from tree branches, deer and animals rooting around and other hikers, I never actually considered disturbed snow to be an issue. I had a small fold-up shovel in my backpack (used in the summer for burying my Lab's gift back to nature). It was a necessary tool for digging out a letterbox in the winter. The big problem is the depth of snow. If it's too deep, you may not be able to find the landmarks listed in the clues. I remember one hike in deep snow, uphill for nearly two miles. By the time I got up to the location where the letterbox was supposed to be (a firetower on the top of a mountain), I couldn't find the rocks under the snow where the box was hidden. The strangest thing though... I enjoyed that challenging hike so much that even though I couldn't find the box, it was one of my favorites! Guess you don't need that stamp to validate the box every time!
Puddle Splasher

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2007-11-04 15:47:10 UTC-05:00
My memorable snow experience was a letterbox on the Fremont Pass in Colorado in January two years ago.  We found the place, with the landmarks just visible in a real blizzard, and stopped at the side of the road trying to figure out if it was even possible to get through those deep drifts to find the box in the snow - I really really wanted to try it, but good sense prevailed - that, and the big snowplow that was bearing down on me while I stood there  weighing the possibility of climbing through drifts over my head to get to it.   But luckily, we were back there again in September this year, and, even without the clues, we recognized the exact spot, found the box, and it was all the sweeter for having been a deferred gratification!  It really is the journey, not always the stamp, that counts.
