From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2007-10-24 14:48:15 UTC
You should be certain, first of all, that you are in the right
cemetery. The size of the cemetery is important. If it's a small
cemetery, you can walk up and down the rows fairly quickly and find
what you're looking for. The death date may be important. There
are older areas and newer areas of cemeteries. You shouldn't look
in the old part of the cemetery for a new stone. Larger cemeteries
usually have an office and good records, and are generally very
willing to locate a grave for you on a map. (In Sparkman-Hillcrest
in Dallas, they chauffered me to a Texas governor's gravesite in a
black limo and escorted me to the marker). If there is no office, go
to the nearest funeral home. They also keep records of grave
locations for local cemeteries. Just tell them you are doing
genealogy or looking for the grave of a relative. There are many
genealogical websites that have cemetery records. The USGenWeb
Project is a good one (www.usgenweb.org). Replace the US with the
state abbreviation, (i.e. TXGenWeb), hosted by www.rootsweb.com, to
find a particular state. www.findagrave.com is a good place to find
burial places of famous people and, sometimes, anscestors. Most of
the time they don't give you the actual location in the cemetery,
but I have found some that do. Some even have gps coordinates.
Good Luck.

Boots Tex
