From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-07-22 16:20:26 UTC
Box sizes do vary greatly. It depends on the area where you are going
to be planting the box. For urban areas, you see a lot of micros or
minis, because the surrounding areas usually do not offer that great
of cover. I like, when possible, to plant a slightly bigger box, and
have, even in urban areas, because they will carry a hitchhiker and
offer more room for stamping bigger signature stamps.

However, our first box was only hidden 7 months ago, so I don't have a
good sample on if the bigger ones go missing more frequently (we have
only lost one box in that time--a normal sized one, but one we chose a
spectacularly bad hiding place for). Someone who has been hiding
boxes longer might have a better feel for if there is a true
correlation between box size and missing boxes.

I think for boxes you plant on vacation or far from home, the most
important thing to do is to plant it in a very sturdy, waterproof box
that's easy to seal, with more pages in the log book than you think
you'll need. That is not the time for the cheapest box. We have only
included a stamp pad one time, because we thought the stamp needed to
be a particular color. From what I read on other boards, stamp pads
often cause more trouble than they're worth, and most finders bring
markers or their own inkpads.

Hope this helps,

Dewberry

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-22 10:42:50 UTC-07:00
Don't worry about re-asking something that's been
covered - we all know how difficult it can be to
search yahoo archives.

I plant in a sandwich sized box myself, although I'm
having an internal debate about needing to go smaller
to minimize the impact on the natural environment.
The box I use is about as large as anything I've
found. I wouldn't go with anything taller than a
sandwich sized box because the higher the profile of
the box the harder it is to hide. One thing I've
found that seems to have worked very well is a small
nalgene bottle. It's a little awkward to squeeze the
stamp and log into, but it's really just a matter of
rolling them like you would a film canister box.
Also, the top tightens down really well so it seems to
be impervious to leakage.

I used to put ink pads, but I had one that looked like
it had exploded in the box. Fortunately, I had
everything bagged so nothing was destroyed. After
that experience, and after listening to everyone else
and realizing that most boxers carry either stamp pads
or markers with them, I decided I could forego the
stamp pad - the nice thing is, it also cuts the cost
of placing a box!

Lucy

Re: [letterboxingtexas] Box sizes

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-22 12:33:11 UTC-07:00
In my opinion, stamp pads in boxes are truly, truly
more trouble than they are worth! You're right they
DO dry out, as well as puke all over the place as was
my experience. If you opt not to leave a stamp pad,
but you do want to have your stamp stamped in certain
colors, include the suggested colors in your clues.
Silver Eagle's Sam I Am has just that and I was glad
to know in advance what colors to bring. Otherwise,
someone is going to come along who only has a black
stamp pad and they're going to really muddy up the
stamp for everyone who follows!

As far as bagging, it doesn't take long to figure out
that double bagging is a really good idea! I try not
to use just regular baggies anymore, although I did
for awhile - I prefer freezer baggies now. Some folks
bag their boxes inside and out, but in my opinion,
putting a box in a baggie makes it more susceptible to
discovery because the outer baggie will fold and bend
and the edges catch light if it happens not be
completely covered. Also, the outer baggie is bound
to keep some air and it makes the box slightly larger
than it needs to be. I can see why some boxers do it
though - it's an added layer of protection from the
elements.

More of my .02,
Lucy

From: gheningt2003 (gheningt2003@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-23 09:26:24 UTC
It is true that many urban boxes are microboxes, using film canisters, however, I have
found a delightful range of containers on the hunt. There is no standard - you make
the rules - that is what makes it fun. I assure you that we will enjoy any box you
place. Think about what you want. Will you return to check on your box, perhaps to
enjoy the variety of stamps and comments, or is this a one time visit to the area? If
you are going to "maintain" the box by revisiting, perhaps a bigger box would be
more satisfying. Of course this means that more thought should surround its
placement. The main thing is to follow your bliss - whether the number is many or
few, we welcome you.

Tall Texan
