LOL...I know what you mean.  You just can't stop from reading sometimes.  This particularly one was actually pretty humorous.  It always amazes me how folks are willing to run with any new idea, no matter how odd or impractical it is. I guess the only way to find really good new ideas is to have lots of stinky ideas too.  For the record, this flame war seems to have gone out now.
 
The thing is, in the midst of a big flame war some kernel of particularly good information comes out that folks keep missing or choose to ignore.  The original poster said she wanted help "burying' t-shirts, and talked about digging them up and wanted to get them buried in all 50 states (and I did get a kick out of the fact that she said she was planning to email them out at first until she corrected herself).  The original premise was that folks would dig them up and sign them and rebury them.  I got this vision of someone wanting to hide the t-shirt really well, so they dig a hole six feet deep on some university's grounds. A few folks sent emails poking fun at her and a few folks volunteered to help, but finally someone sent a very nice message saying "please reconsider digging as digging isn't part of letterboxing and it would be best not to associate digging with letterboxing."  Someone else suggested putting the t-shirts in letterboxes and using them for logbooks, which seems like a more workable way to make her idea work (ain't my thing, but whatever).  She finally said she didn't mean "digging" but "planting" and thought she could use the two terms interchangeably.  In the midst of all this was the standard flame war verbiage of someone with a thin skin posting to the Big List and others responding in their normal very direct way.
 
This is a good example of an idea that could have reflected badly on letterboxing and was just basically ludicrous to an idea that is workable as a letterboxing experiment (although the logistics of mailing 50 t-shirts to 50 different states would preclude me from carrying it out - I can barely remember to buy stamps, much less mail packages!), and I'm going to be interested to see how it develops.  I have no idea who she is although she says she has been letterboxing "for a long time", so I am guessing she's from another part of the country. 
And the offshoot thread is "How to keep t-shirts from smelling musty".  Yuk.

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-01-13 12:56:08 UTC-06:00
I'm really glad it isn't like that too!
 
BUT - there is a lot to be gleaned from the Big List.  That's where the long-term folks hang out, the ones who were in it from the beginning, the ones that can give some depth to the game for those who are just coming into it.  And therein is the rub...
 
You may or may not have noticed that there is sort of a generational thing in letterboxing.  The generations are a lot shorter now than they are in real life.  I visualize the scene as a big ol' family farm, with lots of dogs. 
 
The newbies are the puppies.  They are very excited and romp around, yapping along and tugging on the tails of the bigger dogs.  They think they discovered the stick.  Every new game is a great idea and they jump in with all four feet to play.  Without puppies, the farm will eventually run out of dogs, but goodness, are they ever obnoxious sometimes.
 
And puppies get older - the young dog is above all that romping around and silliness.  He envisions himself much wiser than the puppy, although occasionally he can be caught playing with the stick too.  But he knows the puppy didn't invent the stick - after all, HE invented the stick.  He knows there is a lot he doesn't know, but he wants to hang with the old dogs, so he pretends.  If he's a smart dog, he learns not to howl with the big dogs, but to listen to them howling and notice what makes them howl.  Somewhere in there, he vows not to become like those old grouchy dogs though.
 
And the young dog becomes the working dog.  Who knows what job he has, but who cares.  He doesn't have time for that stick, and he gets really irritated watching the younger ones playing with it - after all, he is now a working dog and he has to worry about what must be done and how it must be done and who cares if it gets done.  He's gotten it all figured out, but he's too busy working to realize it.  Occasionally at the end of the day, he kicks back on his haunches and howls away with the old dogs, but he tries not to howl as loud as them because he feels like he hasn't quite earned the right.
 
And finally, there are the old dogs.  Retired they are.  They've done their due and it's time for them to relax on the porch.  They sleep most of the day, dreaming of their glory days.  They are pretty happy, until one of those young yappers accidentally slaps him with the stick, and then, oh then, he comes alive and all but eats the pup for lunch.  By now he realizes the stick has been there all along and dogs long before him had the stick too.  He looks at those puppies and realizes how silly they are, how they have all these silly games they play, but he knows they really don't have a point in the end.  He has a little respect for the working dogs, he tolerates the young dogs, but those puppies?  They really, really get on his nerves.
 
That's how I see the big list and why I think there are so many flames there.  It is, after all, the original letterboxing talk list for the US and that's where the first boxers wound up.  Now as new folk come along and start trying to use the Big List for a sounding board for their new ideas or thoughts or concepts, the porch dogs get cross and shoo them away because they have already been there and done that. 
 
Just my .02,
--
Barefoot Lucy (who occasionally likes to howl on the Big List)

I'm really glad it isn't like that too!
 
BUT - there is a lot to be gleaned from the Big List.  That's where the long-term folks hang out, the ones who were in it from the beginning, the ones that can give some depth to the game for those who are just coming into it.  And therein is the rub...
 
You may or may not have noticed that there is sort of a generational thing in letterboxing.  The generations are a lot shorter now than they are in real life.  I visualize the scene as a big ol' family farm, with lots of dogs. 
 
The newbies are the puppies.  They are very excited and romp around, yapping along and tugging on the tails of the bigger dogs.  They think they discovered the stick.  Every new game is a great idea and they jump in with all four feet to play.  Without puppies, the farm will eventually run out of dogs, but goodness, are they ever obnoxious sometimes.
 
And puppies get older - the young dog is above all that romping around and silliness.  He envisions himself much wiser than the puppy, although occasionally he can be caught playing with the stick too.  But he knows the puppy didn't invent the stick - after all, HE invented the stick.  He knows there is a lot he doesn't know, but he wants to hang with the old dogs, so he pretends.  If he's a smart dog, he learns not to howl with the big dogs, but to listen to them howling and notice what makes them howl.  Somewhere in there, he vows not to become like those old grouchy dogs though.
 
And the young dog becomes the working dog.  Who knows what job he has, but who cares.  He doesn't have time for that stick, and he gets really irritated watching the younger ones playing with it - after all, he is now a working dog and he has to worry about what must be done and how it must be done and who cares if it gets done.  He's gotten it all figured out, but he's too busy working to realize it.  Occasionally at the end of the day, he kicks back on his haunches and howls away with the old dogs, but he tries not to howl as loud as them because he feels like he hasn't quite earned the right.
 
And finally, there are the old dogs.  Retired they are.  They've done their due and it's time for them to relax on the porch.  They sleep most of the day, dreaming of their glory days.  They are pretty happy, until one of those young yappers accidentally slaps him with the stick, and then, oh then, he comes alive and all but eats the pup for lunch.  By now he realizes the stick has been there all along and dogs long before him had the stick too.  He looks at those puppies and realizes how silly they are, how they have all these silly games they play, but he knows they really don't have a point in the end.  He has a little respect for the working dogs, he tolerates the young dogs, but those puppies?  They really, really get on his nerves.
 
That's how I see the big list and why I think there are so many flames there.  It is, after all, the original letterboxing talk list for the US and that's where the first boxers wound up.  Now as new folk come along and start trying to use the Big List for a sounding board for their new ideas or thoughts or concepts, the porch dogs get cross and shoo them away because they have already been there and done that. 
 
Just my .02,
