This is just a reminder that our 5th TX Letterbox Event is less than
2 months away and if you haven't signed up yet or gotten your
campsite, now is the time to do it. It is listed on AQ:

http://www.atlasquest.com/events/event.html?gEventId=508

Details are on the Event website:

http://www.geocities.com/sileagle1/Events/TALE05/

Last year we had 2 demonstrations of letterboxing techniques and I
was wondering if there was any interest this year, and for what? If
you want to see a demo on something, mention it here and hopefully
someone will volunteer to give it. If there is no interest in demos,
maybe some of you could just do a "Show & Tell" of your letterbox
supplies that you really like (e.g. markers, carving tools, logbooks,
etc.).

Also last year, we played a letterbox trivia game with prizes for the
winners. This year we will be playing a TX Letterbox Match game,
where you match a TX letterbox with its placer. So start looking at
the almost 2000 boxes listed on LBNA, because no cheat sheets will be
allowed!

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2008-01-11 14:36:23 UTC-05:00
I'd also like to suggest a discussion about what makes a good letterboxing plant location, and why.  Recently, I've walked away from a few boxes because something about the location made me uncomfortable, and sometimes I've wondered why anyone would choose to put a box in a particular place for others to come to.   I've found boxes that I know won't last long because the location is in an obvious flood area or sitting in the middle of a well-groomed garden where it's sure to be discovered and probably trashed as the landscape is maintained.  I've found boxes covered only in a handful of leaves or grass, which is sure to blow away and leave the box exposed.   But mostly, I've wondered what there is about some locations that would possibly attract a finder's interest, why someone planted a box to invite finders to discover and explore this spot, or why a planter would put others at possible risk -- and wondered if they were they just looking for a place to throw a box! 
    As the number of letterboxers -- and letterboxes -- increases exponentially in Texas, I think we all have a responsibility to choose carefully where to plant:  a safe area (and I don't necessarily mean "easy," but a place where a letterboxer would not feel threatened), a unique or scenic or historic or just plain interesting place that you'd like to lead others to discover through letterboxing, and a well-camouflaged, well-placed, well-hidden box that isn't likely to be considered road litter.   I think, along with how to carve good stamps and make good logbooks and write clever clues, maybe a little more attention to this important aspect of letterboxing is due.  After all, putting yourself or your kids at risk to find a piece of rubber in a plastic box isn't what the game is all about.
Well, that's my 2 cents worth.

Trail death disturbs local hikers

By AMY RILEY - 01/12/08
http://content.times-herald.com/367576700025810.bsp

News this week that 24-year-old hiker Meredith Emerson was found
dead in the north Georgia mountains hit the Newnan Hiking Club part-
icularly hard.

A group of 18 from the club, which is based out of Robbie and Mary
Hinely's Hit the Trail shop in downtown Newnan, just hiked Blood
Mountain, where Emerson was last seen alive, in November.

"I'm deeply disturbed by individuals that choose a trailhead to target
people, especially women," said Craig Fowler, Newnan Hiking Club
organizer. "The state and national trails are some of the last refuges
for people who just want to enjoy nature and don't want to have to
worry about crime."

Fowler's first recommendation is always to hike in pairs or in small groups.

"That why I joined the [Newnan Hiking] club," said Tricia Mahoney, a P
eachtree City resident who first met Craig and Lisa Fowler through the
Hinelys at Hit the Trail, where she was shopping for some new hiking
gear.

"I just feel like there's safety in numbers," said Mahoney. "I'm hoping
that the Emerson case is an isolated thing," she added. "You should be
able to go out and take your dog for a walk without having to worry about
going missing."

Gary Michael Hilton, 61, has been charged in the case and is currently
being investigated in connection with other missing and murdered hiker
cases.

"I think the man they arrested was an opportunist," said Craig Fowler. It
is likely that Hilton targeted Emerson because she was hiking alone, he
said.

"People who are willing to make a climb like Blood Mountain aren't out
there to cause trouble," said Fowler. "You strike up conversations with
people, and the overwhelming assumption is that they're out there doing
the same thing you are," added Fowler, which may be exactly what
happened to Emerson.

Having to worry about people on the trail "really dampens the spirits of
people who want to hike," said Fowler.

Fowler recommends that hikers take along a whistle, something to
make some noise. He also recommends that hikers carry along a cell
phone, though battery life and signals are not necessarily sustainable
on hikes that span several days.

Fowler occasionally carries along a hand-held Global Positioning System
(GPS) device, which triangulates with satellites to identify a smaller
search region in the event that a hiker does not return on schedule.

"These cases are still very rare," said Fowler. "They tend to be sens-
ational in nature because of the brutality of the crime and the heartache
of someone disappearing. It's still relatively very safe to be on the trails.

"About the biggest threat on the trail is running across a bear," he added,
but even that can be prevented in most cases.

"You're supposed to take normal precautions, such as not cooking
where you plan to sleep," said Fowler. "Animals are generally more
afraid of you than you are of them.

"If you see cubs, stop, back away if possible — because if you come
between a bear and her cubs, she's likely to be more aggressive. In most
cases you can make a little noise, and they're generally going to try to get
away from you," said Fowler.

"I'm 52, and I've been hiking on and off since I was 17," said Fowler,
"and I've encountered a bear twice, and that includes hiking out west.

"I try to stay away from gaps that are accessible by road," added Fowler,
"because there is more of an opportunity for someone who intends to
do harm in those locations. I try to stay at least a quarter to a half mile
away from places like that."

Lt. Col. Charlie Raines, who retired after 20 years of Army service —
much of that in Special Forces or Infantry assignments — currently
teaches science at East Coweta High School and is one of the adult
advisers to Venture Crew 46, a coed Boy Scouts of American program
that specializes in teaching young people outdoor skills and outdoor
safety.

"You should always hike with someone you know and trust, never
alone," added Raines, "and buy or borrow the best equipment you can
afford and make sure it is in good shape. If a crisis does arise, it will
only be made worse if your gear is not in proper condition."

"Tell people where you are going and how long you expect to be gone,"
said Raines. "Carry enough emergency food, clothes and shelter to
handle the weather and the terrain conditions you might encounter."

"Be in shape for the trip, and know your limitations," said Raines.
"Watch the current weather," and check predictions for several days
out.

"If you are new to the sport," said Raines, "join a club or group," to
benefit from the experience of those who can teach you the ins and
outs of hiking.

Venture Crew is open to all young people between the ages of 14 and
20. Interested individuals must have completed the eighth grade. For
more information about Venture Crew, contact Peggy Murphy, the
Coweta County Boy Scouts of America Commissioner, at
678-603-8445 or by e-mail, pmurphy@bsamail.org.

The Newnan Hiking Club welcomes new members. For those interested,
Craig Fowler can be reached by e-mail at cfowler@numail.org.

"People who are new often want to go with people who know what
they're doing," said Fowler. "They are usually interested in the day
hikes," he added. "We also offer more strenuous and demanding
trips for more experienced hikers."

"In proportion to other hobbies, I don't think it's any more dangerous
than anything else," said Mahoney, who has been hiking with the Newnan
Hiking Club for three years but first began hiking with her sister back in
college.
