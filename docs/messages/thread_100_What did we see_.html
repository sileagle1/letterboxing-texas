From: carrascoruth2000 (a.carrasco@earthlink.net) | Date: 2004-04-12 03:52:13 UTC
We spent the weekend hiking and letterboxing (I'll tell you all later
what we did and didn't find) but I have a pressing question that
maybe someone can help me answer first. We were at the Confederate
Reunion Grounds near Mexia, and at the entrance to the park we saw
eight or nine animals that were either wild pigs or Javelinas. We
watched them for quite a while, they were varing colors...some more
reddish and some more brownish. They were smaller than javelinas I
have seen in other parts of Texas, but they were most certanily some
sort of pig. They were sitting in a clearing, so we got a really
good look at them, but I've checked the flora/fauna sections of
nearly all the state parks in this area and I dont see anything about
wild pigs. There wasn't another soul in the park to ask. I promise
we didn't imagine them. Anyone out there have any idea what they
were? My kids are crazy to know. Thanks! Mother of Four

I looked up javelina on internet and found this site that
compares them with wild pigs:

http://www.desertusa.com/magnov97/nov_pap/du_collpecc.html

Where I live in Sugar Land near the Brazos River we currently
have a problem with wild pigs rooting up yards at night, so
I know they are around. The few I have seen appear more
black than reddish brown. If the ones you saw had sharp
tusks and smelled bad, they were probably javelinas.
Either way, I bet it was fun to see them, and you now
have a reason to place a "Mystery Pig" Letterbox.

Silver Eagle

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-12 08:15:18 UTC-07:00
We live in Cherokee County, and we have quite a few of
what I assume to be javelinas here too. It's a big
thing to go hawg huntin' (not hog hunting) around
here!
My husband was on a bent to kill one a couple of years
ago, and learned that they are apparently somewhat
shy, but agressive when confronted. He never actually
killed one, or even saw one while hunting, although
the guys that took him out hunting swore by their
large population (my husband has the same effect on
fish - they could bite all day, but the minute he
throws a hook in the water, the fish are nowhere to be
found). I've been shown evidence of their rooting
several times and have seen them dead in the back of
pickups, but have only caught fleeting glimpses of
them. I know we would've loved to see what you saw!

Lucy

From: Susan (ssmith043@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-14 18:00:05 UTC
We live in South Texas and my DH is a hawg hunter. I go out with
him and I enjoy going with him, but don't like to watch the kill.
But, skin it, clean it, and I'll eat it, the ribs are to die for
when bbq'd!!! Just a young one, tho. Less than 100 lbs. Wild
ferral hogs can come in any color...just like domesticated hogs. We
hunt in the fields here with the farmer's blessings. A band of hogs
can ruin by rooting a field of milo, corn, onions, spinach,
watermelons literally over night!!! Hogs can be very agressive. I
have seen men in the ER that tangled with a hog when hunting. Wanna
see the pic of one hanging on my wall? DH won a contest with this
boar with the length of the tusks and it's weight. Email me
privately.

Javelina's are a rodent of sorts. Their babies are cute...yellow
striped. But don't get near them!!! Mama will eat your lunch!

Wanna see javelinas and deer and turkeys? Go to Choke Canyon State
Park Calliham Unit. http://www.tpwd.state.tx.us/park/choke/
And while there check out the Lb's there and in Three Rivers.
Dancing Ducks and Southern Magnolia. I think I read that Ali's Lb
is missing.

Check out this website for some info on hogs. Has pics. You can also
do a search and check out some of the hawg hunting sites. But
beware some of these can be pretty graphic.
http://www.rw.ttu.edu/sp_accounts/feral_hog/default.htm

I apologise for being so long-winded this morning.
