Supply list for Tuesday's class - stamp carving

From: Eibhlin/Aisling (eibhlinm@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-07-29 08:56:57 UTC-05:00
Hi,

If you're taking the free class on Tuesday night, we're going to keep
the supply list very simple.

If you plan to work on a stamp or two in class, you'll need something
to carve and something to carve with, plus your usual letterboxing
kit.

THINGS TO CARVE:

Erasers - Most of the smooth-surface ones work well. I haven't tried
the Target $1 specials recently mentioned on the LbNA list. Some
smooth erasers have a slightly oily surface that can repel ink, but
you can try a corner of it at home and see if it picks up ink okay,
before you carve it.

I have used Sanford's "Magic Rub" erasers and they're okay but not
fabulous. But, when you're starting out, you may prefer to invest in
the least-expensive erasers that will do the job. So, the Magic Rub
or other smooth-style erasers are fine to learn with, and also for
boxes that may vanish in a hurry anyway.

I've heard that some people carve traditional pink erasers. In my
opinion, they crumble too easily and absorb ink irregularly, but that
could be a quirky experience. For all I know, they work fine. If you
already have some on hand and want to try them, go for it.

Pink stuff - At Michael's, Hobby Lobby, etc, there are thickish sheets
of pink rubber and white rubber that are sold for block printing and
making rubber stamps. I've used both happily. Usually, they cut well
and hold detailing nicely. One sheet will make lots & lots of stamps,
but they're usually more expensive than erasers.

THINGS TO CARVE WITH

Some people love to cut with a small Xacto knife... the kind with the
pen-style holder. I have one in an Xacto Gripster handle, and the
blade is standard... I think it says #11 on it. It's not my favorite
tool, but I know many, many letterboxers and stamp carvers who swear
that it's the best possible tool.

I have also heard of people who can actually carve well with plain ol'
utility knives, kitchen knives, and so on. I'm not one of those
people.

I generally use a traditional block cutting blade (in a holder) that
are sold in art supply stores. You may want a couple of different
sized blades, but one holder is probably enough if you're just testing
this to see how it works.

I have two Speedball block cutters. One is a #2 v-style blade that I
use in a good holder, and I have a smaller #1 v-style blade in a
cheapo wooden holder.

All of these supplies can be purchased at Michael's, Hobby Lobby,
Texas Art Supply, and so on.

TRANSFERS

I'd like to keep the transfer process simple. Bring a small sketch
(the size of the finished stamp) or two or three. You can use a
photocopy of a simple Dover design if you like--the public library has
Dover (and other) clipart books (sometimes in the children's books
section)--or any very simple design.

Also bring a pencil, preferably one with a soft lead (4B or 6B, from
the crafts shop) but a regular pencil (the kind you sharpen, not a
mechanical pencil) will be fine for the class.

STAMPING SUPPLIES

Please bring your own rubber stamp pad. Ink or pigment... either is
fine, but ink dries faster. I recommend one very dark pad (black, for
example) and one that's very light (grey, tan, pale pink, etc.). But,
if you are simply bringing your usual stamp pad, that's fine too.

You'll need a few sheets of paper for testing your stamp as you carve
it. Junk paper left from misprints at Kinko's or your home printer
are fine. The back of envelopes from junk mail are fine. Any paper
that's smooth and light-colored will work.

Also bring your usual letterboxing kit so that we can exchange stamps
during the class. If you're new, this means a stamp, stamp pad, and
logbook... unless you're making the stamp and logbook in class, of
course.

CLASS NOTES

I'm running very late after an exhausting (but productive) research
trip to NOLA this week... I'll have to see how it goes. However, the
class notes aren't vital and if I end up posting them after the class,

IF YOU SHOW UP WITH A SMILE AND THAT'S ALL...

When I teach, I always say, "If you show up with a smile and that's
all, you'll still have fun in my classes."

I always try to have extra supplies on hand, and teach an
information-packed class anyway. So, if all you show up with is a
smile, you can still take notes and perhaps do a little hands-on
experimenting with my own supplies.
