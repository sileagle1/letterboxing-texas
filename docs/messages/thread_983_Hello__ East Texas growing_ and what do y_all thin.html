>and some yet undecided), but they probably won't make

>
>As most of y'all know, I split my time between cub
>scouting and letterboxing.  And as I mentioned on the
>big list, the topic of discussion last weekend on the
>national scout leader list I belong to was geocaching
>and letterboxing with scouts.  And as has been
>discussed in the past on various letterboxing lists
>(this one included), letterboxers are often a little
>leery about scouts and other groups heading out to
>letterbox because sometimes leaders aren't diligent to
>make sure that boxes are cared for and rehidden well.
>Because of the recent discussion, several local
>scouters have asked me to help them introduce
>letterboxing to their scouts.
>
>Soooo...I'm considering setting up a "training course"
>of sorts, several boxes in a single park, with clear
>solid directions so that a group (or an individual)
>can come out and find several boxes geared toward the
>beginner and get the hang of interpreting clues,
>practicing compass skills, using good outdoor skills,
>and being responsible boxers.  Although it would be
>geared toward scouts (and specifically cub scouts),
>anyone could try it out.  I'm thinking that instead of
>posting the clues on the website, I would list it on
>the website and direct those interested to email me
>for clues and information.  I realize that this would
>be a high risk series, so I'm thinking I'll plant it
>somewhere near to me, like Jim Hogg State Park, so I
>can check on it more easily.  I would like to place
>nice stamps, but nothing too intricate since I may
>well have to recarve from time to time.
>
>So what do y'all think?  Is this a lame idea?
>Thinking back to your newbie days (especially if you
>have kids), what would have been helpful to learn
>early on?  As planters, what would you like finders to
>know?
