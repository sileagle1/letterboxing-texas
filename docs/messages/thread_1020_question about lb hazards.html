question about lb hazards

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-07-10 15:44:09 UTC
I got an email this week from an out-of-towner who found our
Labyrinth box. She was very emphatic that I needed to update the
clues to say that there might be poison ivy in the area. I didn't
see any when I planted, and believe me I check as I have a newly-
developed PI complete intolerance. I did see thorns, lots o thorns,
so I put a specific warning about being careful when reaching in the
clue.

How much handholding does there need to be in the clues? I feel
that finders need to be vigilant and assume responsiblity for risk.
They need to look for PI, snakes, muddy areas, ponds to fall into,
hills, non-boxers, spiders, thorns, nasty critters, etc. I can't
possibly warn about every hazard. Are you careful when you LB?
Don't you pretty much assume that every box you go to there's going
to be something that you need to watch out for?

And of course, this time of year you can't get away from PI. I had
to give up looking for Tweety because I couldn't safely get to the
tree where it was hidden. I'll go back in the winter. Hopefully
the PI is guarding it and keeping it safe.

What do you all think?

From: Semalee (Semalee@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2005-07-10 10:53:57 UTC-05:00
I think the finder is responsible for their own safety.  It is quite frankly impossible for the placer to know all of the hazards that will be there.  I usually write to watch for thornbushes, but honestly if you are there you can see them, so the warning is kind of pointless.  I guess it would help to know to bring some leather gloves, but I plan on adding those to my boxing bag anyway, cause you just never know what you will run into!!  If you are going to be out in the woods, you are probably going to run into something on your journey...  We have heard a lot about poison ivy and snakes lately.
 
Now that said, I do think it is a good idea if you go to a particular box that is being guarded by a snake or poison ivy to go ahead and post it here so others are aware to be cautious and plan accordingly.  However putting it in the clues I don't think is neccessary because in a few months there won't be any poison ivy and that snake may have moved on.....  There may be an entirely different thing lurking near the box, this is just the way nature works and part of what makes being in nature so fun!!
 
That's my 2 cents...
 
Semalee of MAGS

From: Eibhlin/Aisling (eibhlinm@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-07-10 11:03:18 UTC-05:00
Hi,

In my opinion, if it's an unusual hazard, it should be noted in the clues.

For example, my Bridget Bishop box (Salem, MA) was near a fairly steep
and unexpected drop-off, so I mentioned that for letterboxing families
with unruly toddlers.

I also post a notice if a park doesn't allow pets on leashes,
because--in my opinion--that's unusual and can affect a letterboxer's
plans.

However, outside of urban areas, poison ivy is common. Snakes,
sometimes poisonous ones, are the norm in warm-weather states. Ditto
black widows and, in Florida especially, scorpions.

It's all relative to your own experiences. My sister-in-law (from
sunny California) had to live in snow country for a couple of years,
and I thought she was going to have a nervous breakdown when she
discovered that frostbite can be a hazard in winter.

Having lived all over the U.S., I feel that anyone who is going to
explore a new area should spend some time at that state's Official
Welcome/Visitors' Center. Many of them have a free brochure listing
what hikers should watch for.

If the site has unusual hazards or challenges, I think that we need to
list them with the clues. If it's something that every hiker should
expect on a trail in that particular area, I don't feel that we should
clutter our webpages with warnings.

That's my two cents, anyway.

Cheerfully,
Ais
http://www.aisling.net/

From: Lois Rutherford (loruther@ev1.net) | Date: 2005-07-10 11:22:24 UTC-05:00
AMEN!!!  Forgive me ahead of time if I step on toes...
I feel that Letterboxing (and all that goes with it) appeals to certain types of individuals/groups that are looking to enhance their daily lives with knowledge of history, geography, art, nature, etc. (the list of virtues could go on and on).  I choose to believe that people who dedicate themselves to this enriching pastime are able-bodies who think on a different plane than the masses.  Part of the problem today is that everyone expects their hand to be held rather than figuring things out on their own.  I have had two uncomfortable rounds with PI since I have begun LBing and even though it is frustrating and painful, I wear it as a badge of courage that I was out in my beloved Houston finding virtual forests that I never knew were there.  What challenge is it if we are told about every single thing that might be encountered out there?  Knowledge is power, but the power is stronger when obtained through first-hand experience.  While I don't wish to exclude anyone, I think we should make people step-up their abilities rather than lower the hobby down to a lackadaisical level.  I feel strongly that this is such an enriching experience--why clutter the clues with the obvious precautions?
Pardon me for staying on the soap-box too long....
Lotus Blossom
(formerly know as LoRuther)

Re: [letterboxingtexas] question about lb hazards

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-10 11:17:27 UTC-07:00
I can see that if you posted about the thorns, someone
would think you should have posted about the PI as
well. Kind of a "legal-ish" interpretation of "if you
warned me about one hazard, you accepted
responsibility to warn me of all hazards."

I tend to think that posting about ANY hazards might
tend to make the finder more complacent about other
hazards not mentioned. I can see, as Ais mentioned,
that it might be important to post about deadly
hazards like a steep drop-off. In a case such as that
though, I might write it into my clue more than
drawing special attention to it by saying "this is a
hazard".

I can see possibly writing a disclaimer such as the
one you are supposed to read from the LBNA site before
you go hunting and putting it at the bottom of all
your clues - maybe something that pertains more
directly to Texas letterboxing that includes poison
ivy, rattlesnakes and fire ants. I don't think I'M
going to do it, but I could see where someone might
want to.

I guess I'm fed up and frustrated with folks who want
everything spelled out (in life in general, not on
this board). If YOU are allergic to PI, then YOU
should know to watch out for PI. My daughter is
dreadfully allergic and believe me, she avoids it like
the plague - I think the girl can smell it. I, on the
other hand, am not the least bit allergic (I
occasionally get a bit of a blister, nothing more),
but I do like to trip over roots. Does that mean that
other boxers should warn me if there are exposed roots
in the area? I think not - what I think is that I
should know to be cautious of roots.

I mean really, why wouldn't we write something into
our clues warning about wearing sunscreen too?

I think that when we become too focused on getting the
stamp, we find ourselves not giving proper heed to
hazards. Sometimes, you just have to walk away and
leave the box behind until the circumstances are
better. If that is the case, count the trip as your
reward and be happy with that.

While I'm all in favor of making boxes
finder-friendly, especially in areas where there are
lots of new boxers, I frankly don't care to see folks
who aren't willing to show some common sense and
awareness get involved in letterboxing. It probably
isn't their kind of thing anyway and they won't be an
asset to the activity.

Lucy
***Nodding vaguely in the general direction of the
sense-free zone and apologizing in advance.
