From: Vicki Bell (vicki@bellmotel.com) | Date: 2015-01-15 00:28:14 UTC-06:00
I have to say that this wonderful, creative, fun-loving, outdoorsy, zany group of letterboxers is one of the most welcoming groups I have ever joined.  My second day of letterboxing was spent attending TALE5, and while my first day of letterboxing had been a blast, I was quite ignorant of all the ins and outs of this game.  I was immediately taken in by a fun, funny group of people, who put up with my questions and over-enthusiasm, and laughed at my glee.  Gryzzled Gryphon set the example for me at events of approaching every letterboxer I hadn't met, introducing myself, and asking for an exchange.  Letterboxers have been there to hug me when I found out my Mom had cancer, and to stand by me during other tough events in my life.  I have hosted letterboxers in my home, when all I knew about them were their trail names.  As I have attended events around the country, letterboxers whom I didn't know and had never heard of have provided me with a tent in California, a sleeping bag and ice chest in Massachusetts, and even a can opener so I could eat my tuna fish.  Letterboxers work so hard to make each carve better than the last, to find interesting hiding places to bring us to, and to sometimes make clues ever more diabolical so as to exercise our minds as well as our legs.  I've said before that I am so grateful to be part of a worldwide club that asks nothing from me, and never stops giving to me.  I haven't responded before when people have said letterboxers are unwelcoming, because that experience was so far from my truth, that words failed me.  My experience with letterboxers in Texas, across America, and even in Dartmoor, England, is that they are the most welcoming, clever, creative people around.  I remain grateful to belong to such a group.
-- Bell Motel

I have to admit that the pranks have piled up over the years, and if you see us giggling over what appears to be a shared memory, ask us!  We love to regale people with stories of past silliness.  Most of the pranks involve letterboxes, so after you hear the story, if you go out and find the letterbox -- Voila!  You are now in on the prank!

From: Wry Me (wry_me@yahoo.com) | Date: 2015-01-15 19:20:48 UTC
I think that's possibly why events are a much better gauge of the hobby.  As my friend used to say, "Computers don't have eyebrows."  Much is missed due to loss of tone and inflection and some people are more sensitive to criticism than others.  It's just a completely different atmosphere.  Coming to my first event as a socially maladaptive person entering a room of complete strangers was one of the hardest things I ever did. I am not worthy of most of the people who tolerate me, that's for sure!  I'm not always grateful for the input of those who disagree, but often it's something that wouldn't have occurred to me otherwise and I have to own up to that.  Eventually. :) Every online "community" has different tacit rules and atmosphere.  That's why it used to be standard procedure to lurk for awhile before offering comments.  I guess I'm old school in that I still kind of believe that the oldtimers are due respect and when one tells you you've gotten it wrong, you fix it, suck it up and move on.
 
Tonight, I'm meeting friends I wouldn't have known otherwise.  I've never been to an event yet where the newbies weren't taken care of, or someone boxed alone other than by choice.  Just like anything, not everyone is going to get along, but it's wrongful thinking to believe that that means no one is ever going to get along with you.  The online community is far smaller than the actual event community, which in turn is smaller than the entire boxing community.

I've resisted responding before now but I'm going to chime in. We started out letterboxing and interacting a lot. Through the years we've attended several events but never a TALE (poor timing for our family and no hotels near the sites - we REALLY are not campers). There have been some people throughout the years who were not nice (or any semblance of cordial) in various emails. In truth, I think they are they type of person who thrive on negativity and being right or doing things only one way - their way. That's ok, I just don't need them in my life. Because of some interactions, both on the board and on AQ, we've stopped interacting as much and made boxing what we need it to be for us. We still box occasionally, but we don't log anything anymore except in our personal logbooks. That being said, we've met and had great interactions in person. I loved the yearly Tomatofest meets and the Dia de Los Muertos meet at Bell Motel's house was fabulous!

My advice is to try some smaller meets to check out the people behind the names. There are good people out there in the boxing world. Don't let some gruffness put you off, because you stand to lose the opportunity to meet some amazing people and artisans.

From: Maribeth (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2015-01-15 17:11:41 UTC-06:00

Well said, Ms. Motel! And I agree with you, Blue Butterfly, that I too miss the campfire where we used to sit around and rehash old pranks and start new ones! And I also like Wry Me's comment about the internet not allowing for raised eyebrows. So often what is said in jest, just doesn't reach the reader with the same meaning. (Guilty!) I too am looking forward to TALE: seeing old friends and meeting new ones.

But I do feel a need to mention this about the Circus Box or any other special traveling boxes. Anyone who has ever been fortunate enough to find one of those traveling boxes realizes what a special treat it is. So many boxers put their best effort into carving a stamp and creating a neat page in the logbook. Many of us have made a special effort to find one of those boxes when they land in our area (or even sometimes... not in our area). In the past, one of those great boxes went missing when a non-boxer received it, and we were all very upset as we all treasure those boxes and look forward to the opportunity to find it and participate. We used to have an "unwritten rule" (since we don't have rules in letterboxing), that you should use the same urgency in replanting the box that you did in seeking it, because many people want to be able to look for it. Obviously, situations happen in people's lives that delay them from being able to re-hide it quickly, and I'd suggest if that happens in the future, we contact the "owner" of the box listed in the front of the logbook, or post on this list that we have the box but circumstances have occurred that prevent us from being able to re-hide it. (That's happened before.) I can almost guarantee that some local boxer will be able to help you out. I'm not scolding anyone, but just suggesting that in the future, hurt feelings may be avoided if we have a system in place to allow this type of box to stay active and in circulation because they really are wonderful boxes and are treasured by any Texas letterboxer who finds them.

Puddle Splasher

Obviously, situations happen in people's lives that delay them from being able to re-hide it quickly, and I'd suggest if that happens in the future, we contact the "owner" of the box listed in the front of the logbook, or post on this list that we have the box but circumstances have occurred that prevent us from being able to re-hide it. (That's happened before.) I can almost guarantee that some local boxer will be able to help you out. I'm not scolding anyone, but just suggesting that in the future, hurt feelings may be avoided if we have a system in place to allow this type of box to stay active and in circulation because they really are wonderful boxes and are treasured by any Texas letterboxer who finds them.
