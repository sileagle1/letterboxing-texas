Letterbox-friendly

From: sileagle1 (sileagle@windstream.net) | Date: 2013-01-30 14:12:55 UTC
The other day I got an email from a ranger at the North Little Rock Parks & Recreation in Arkansas saying that a controlled burn in Burns Park (what an apt name for the park) was scheduled and since I have a letterbox in that area I should remove it. At this point I figured it was just another way for a park to get rid of a letterbox, but he went on to say that I was welcome to replace the box after the burn or place it in a new location if the burn ruins the original spot. I thanked him for the heads-up, but explained that I did not live close by and could not comply. He then volunteered to do it for me if I would give him the clues to the box. Since it was one of my game boxes (20 Questions Game 19-Person) I couldn't just give him the link to the clue page, so I solved it, cut and pasted the clues, and sent them to him. He then responded:
"Thanks. I will look for it this week. Just an FYI - the arch is still there but the trail has been blocked due to a change in our trail layout. Once I find the letterbox I will try to get you an updated description to the letterbox when it gets put back". Now that is what I call a letterbox-friendly park, and it makes me wonder why this park and ranger are so helpful when others are not.

Silver Eagle

I think that's awesome. I could only hope that more Rangers are aware of, and considerate of, boxes and caches in their parks. From: Christine Borgfeld (christine@borgfeld.net) | Date: 2013-01-30 19:09:03 UTC-06:00

Yes, they do seem to be more aware and flexible toward letterboxing lately.  However they haven’t always been, so there is some controversy on permitting boxes in Texas parks.  Many parks (primarily those where past events were held) have numerous boxes planted without permits.  And many of these parks are oblivious to them or let them slide under the radar.  So the risk is exposing existing boxes to parks that may be unaware of them and having them pulled.  Other parks have a reputation for checking for and removing boxes without permits – these seem to be in the mostly in the hill country area.  So any boxes there are either permitted or well “hidden” (clue restrictions, puzzles, mystery boxes etc).  There are also a handful of parks that have permitted all the existing boxes and ask that new ones be permitted (Rusk maybe?).  On top of all of that, Boots Tex tried to work with the parks at the state level to come up with a consistent policy and was pretty much told we are too small to worry about.  So it’s all something of a mess.  Personally I don’t permit my boxes and don’t plan on worrying about it until they get more consistent.

From: Denise Vajdak (denise.vajdak@gmail.com) | Date: 2013-01-30 23:54:37 UTC-06:00
I think some of you might be looking at permitting as a bad thing. This is the Park's property we are talking about and I think permission should be a given. And...in the case that started this thread, how else would the Ranger know to alert a box holder of a pending burn unless there is a contact name and number? If boxers have the "I'll put my box where I want and no one can tell me different" and it goes against Park rules (or creates geotrails or other disturbances) then it could just cause a Park (or more) to ban them altogether.

As far as letterboxing being so much of secret? Seriously, I just don't get it. It's not like the Park rangers are going to post your permit on Facebook for everyone to read. It's just for their own records in each Park. I like to work WITH the rangers. A couple of them have even pointed out good locations for me to put boxes/caches. I don't want this hobby being so secretive that there's no new boxes to find. That kinda defeats the whole purpose now, doesn't it? Tell a friend and take them with you. Grow the hobby.

From: sileagle1 (sileagle@windstream.net) | Date: 2013-01-31 14:20:15 UTC
I don't want to get into this whole debate that we have hashed over many times, but I disagree with almost everything you said. Parks are OUR property, too, and I don't think a few well-hidden boxes are going to cause any more of a problem than allowing hunting, scout troops, or other groups to use the park. Also, letterboxing IS supposed to be secretive. That is one of the main things that separates it from Geocaching and is what I like about it. Growing the hobby too fast will just result in more boxes being badly placed by people not really interested in letterboxing other than as a passing fad. The box I placed in the park that started this thread was not permitted, nor was it required to be, yet the Ranger was able to contact me via LBNA email from my clue page.

Now that being said, I do respect the wishes of Texas State Parks wanting all boxes to be permitted, so I have stopped placing boxes in them rather than comply. For those that have the time and desire to go through the permit process, more power to you.

Silver Eagle

P.S. If you want to look at previous arguments on this topic, look in the history of this List about 2 or 3 years ago.

From: borgfeldc (christine@borgfeld.net) | Date: 2013-02-01 15:20:37 UTC
I'm sorry if our responses seem harsh. It seems obvious that we should "just follow the rules" if you just look at the current forms. The issue is that the rules are evolving and slowly becoming relevant to letterboxing. Our attitude toward the state parks comes from long well thought out discussions and feedback from those here who have tried to work with the parks with mixed success. The rules are changing positively as a result of their work.

Three years ago the permitting form looked very different and was clearly intended for geocaches only. There are some subtle but big differences in the two hobbies that make a one size fits all process difficult to use, but it is getting better. If I were to plant a cache today I'd permit it, but with a letterbox it depends.

By the way, I'm hiking a portion of the Appalachian Trail this summer with a friend, and probably carrying a 40 pound pack. We'll probably be changing our hiking philosophy as we go along! Hike your own hike, friends. See you all in a month.

I don't see a link, just a mention of an old thread 2 or 3 years ago. That's a long time and things change (hopefully for the better). I personally haven't had a problem working with TPWD, so I don't have a tainted view. Actually, it was on the TPWD's website that I first ever heard about geocaching. These games are all just a scavenger hunts to me, with just a few differences between them, and I don't discriminate between them.

This is just my opinion (and everyone is entitled to their own, of course).

Geocaching is a scavenger hunt that uses a GPS and clues. Most (other than virtuals and earthcaches) contain logs. Some also contain stamps and those are my favorite. Geocaching sites have reviewers. As a geocacher, I don't have bad feelings toward boxers as a whole.

Letterboxing is a scavenger hunt that does not use a GPS, but instead uses just clues. Of course, they MUST contain a stamp. The stamps is the part I like best...and not having to follow a GPS. Letterboxing has no reviewers. As a letterboxer, I don't have bad feelings toward cachers as a whole.

You'll find lock N locks, film canisters and the like in both games. Both are good for people of all ages. Both hobbies have good and bad containers - and good and bad hiders/finders. It's all just hiding and seeking and I enjoy the adventures that both bring. I have met some great people through geocaching (and unfortunately some that were not so nice) and I can't wait to meet some of you great people through letterboxing at TALE. I hope any not-so-nice ones stay home. :)
