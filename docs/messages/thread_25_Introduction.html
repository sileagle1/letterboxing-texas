Well – as a retired soldier with lots of desert experience, I can say that snakes are truly rare in the late fall through early spring.  Not gone completely, but usually.  I speak for West Texas, the habits in the east may differ somewhat.

 

Study up on them – respect them, but do not fear them.  And above all, do not mess with them.  Or kill them.

 

Rattle Snakes do not want to encounter you.  If given the chance they will warn you with a buzz of their tail.

 

If forced into a human encounter, they would rather flee than bite. 

 

If cornered, they will strike.  Most snake bites happen in two ways.  The snake is found suddenly in an unexpected place and startled, or people do not listen and try to mess with the snake.

 

If a snake bites you, sometimes they do not inject venom.  This is more true with older and larger snakes.  But you cannot count on that.  Smaller snakes are more dangerous than the larger ones for several reasons.  First, they are not as frightening, so people will be more likely to mess with them.  Second, when they strike they nearly always dump all the venom they have into the bite.

 

Death from snake bite is a rare thing for normal healthy people.  The very young, very old, and those with reactionary troubles (like bee stings etc) need to be aware of their situation.  Keep the bite victim calm, and seek medical aid ASAP.  Cutting the wound and sucking out the venom is for TV.  Study up on proper first aid for snake bite.

 

Knowledge is power.  Most people fear what they do not understand.  Kind of like hoplophobic people, what they do not understand frightens them.  Knowledge and experience usually conquers those fears.  So it should be with snakes.  Knowledge and precaution is all you need.  Use a stick to poke – not your hand.  Use a grabber to reach the box, not your arm.  If you see a snake, remember it belongs there, you are a visitor in its land.  Give it the respect it is due by yielding the trail to it, and you will be fine.  Most snakes cannot quite strike as far as they are long, but give it as much room as you can.

 

In 20 years I only had to evac one soldier for snake bite.  It was just a cute little baby snake.  He had heard that the baby snakes didn’t bite.  Heh heh heh – just like all guns are loaded, so too are all snakes.  Give them their due, and all will be well.

 

Hope this is helpful.

Well – as a retired soldier with lots of desert experience, I can say that snakes are truly rare in the late fall through early spring.  Not gone completely, but usually.  I speak for West Texas, the habits in the east may differ somewhat.

 

Study up on them – respect them, but do not fear them.  And above all, do not mess with them.  Or kill them.

 

Rattle Snakes do not want to encounter you.  If given the chance they will warn you with a buzz of their tail.

 

If forced into a human encounter, they would rather flee than bite. 

 

If cornered, they will strike.  Most snake bites happen in two ways.  The snake is found suddenly in an unexpected place and startled, or people do not listen and try to mess with the snake.

 

If a snake bites you, sometimes they do not inject venom.  This is more true with older and larger snakes.  But you cannot count on that.  Smaller snakes are more dangerous than the larger ones for several reasons.  First, they are not as frightening, so people will be more likely to mess with them.  Second, when they strike they nearly always dump all the venom they have into the bite.

 

Death from snake bite is a rare thing for normal healthy people.  The very young, very old, and those with reactionary troubles (like bee stings etc) need to be aware of their situation.  Keep the bite victim calm, and seek medical aid ASAP.  Cutting the wound and sucking out the venom is for TV.  Study up on proper first aid for snake bite.

 

Knowledge is power.  Most people fear what they do not understand.  Kind of like hoplophobic people, what they do not understand frightens them.  Knowledge and experience usually conquers those fears.  So it should be with snakes.  Knowledge and precaution is all you need.  Use a stick to poke – not your hand.  Use a grabber to reach the box, not your arm.  If you see a snake, remember it belongs there, you are a visitor in its land.  Give it the respect it is due by yielding the trail to it, and you will be fine.  Most snakes cannot quite strike as far as they are long, but give it as much room as you can.

 

In 20 years I only had to evac one soldier for snake bite.  It was just a cute little baby snake.  He had heard that the baby snakes didn’t bite.  Heh heh heh – just like all guns are loaded, so too are all snakes.  Give them their due, and all will be well.

 

Hope this is helpful.

It is ok to be afraid of things.  I am somewhat afraid of heights – but I regularly jumped out of aircraft (not since I retired).  I am afraid of electricity, but I work in a power plant.  Some jobs cause me to sweat and make my hands shake.  But I get the job done.

 

As I posted earlier – my experience is in DESERT snakes – you may encounter slight differences in east Texas.  And you might encounter a snake at any time, although there is a lesser chance in cold weather.

 

Check out this site as a primer.  http://www.desertusa.com/mag98/mar/stories/rattlesin.html

 

Being afraid is OK.  It is when you allow that fear to paralyze you into unreasonable avoidance that it really affects your life.  Like a person that has been bitten by a dog, and now wants to have all dogs destroyed.  Knowledge, thought and caution are usually enough to keep you safe.  Don’t lock yourself inside your house just because there might be snakes outside.  <grin>.

 

As to bears – I just sent something to a ham-radio hiker friend….  Let me see if I can find it for you.

 

 

We have bears in the NM mountains just north of me.  There are bear warning signs, and even precautions to take right on the trail-head signs.

 

They warn that black and brown bears are frequently seen, and sometimes even grizzly bears in the more northern mountains bordering Colorado.  They warn of approaching bears, or even coming upon them and startling them.  They recommend wearing brightly colored clothing, making noise while hiking, and even sewing little bells on your clothing so that the bears know you are coming.  They also recommend carrying bear repellant spray.

 

Another thing they mention is identifying what bears are in the area.  If you see them - of course you can tell the difference.  Brown bears are brown.  Black bears are black, and about the same size as a brown bear.  Grizzly bears are also black, but much larger and meaner too.  Although they are cute - DO NOT approach any bear cubs under any circumstances!

 

If you don't see bears, you can still somewhat identify them by their scat (poop) found on the trail.  Black and brown bear scat is very similar.  It will contain bits of twigs, leaves, and sometimes rabbit fur or bird feathers.  Grizzly bear scat will usually contain bits of brightly colored clothing, little bells, and will smell like bear repellant spray.

 

Really - if you want to know....  http://www.bear-tracker.com/blackbearscat.html

 

Good luck on your next hike.....

 

 

As you can see – part of it was a joke, as it is generally accepted that Grizzly bears do not come this far south, being predominantly in Idaho and Washington St.  Doesn’t mean they can’t walk the continental divide and come south.  But generally, the last grizzly sighting this far south is generally accepted as being in the mid 1940s.

 

So don’t let the idea of Texas snakes stop you from enjoying the out-doors and finding lots of letterboxes.  I guess what I am saying is – don’t let fear control your life.

 

There is a rock-rattler that lives in a crack in the rocks of a trail that I frequently hike.  I see him often, every summer for three years now.  I named him Rocky.  It is a little-used trail, I could very well be his only human contact.  While I carefully step around him, I do sometimes spend a little time checking him out.  It has been interesting to see him growing year by year.  BTW – the number of buttons on the rattle has nothing to do with the age of the snake, only with how many times it has shed its skin.

 

Hope you enjoy your boxing adventures.

It is ok to be afraid of things.  I am somewhat afraid of heights – but I regularly jumped out of aircraft (not since I retired).  I am afraid of electricity, but I work in a power plant.  Some jobs cause me to sweat and make my hands shake.  But I get the job done.

Check out this site as a primer.  http://www.desertusa.com/mag98/mar/stories/rattlesin.html

Being afraid is OK.  It is when you allow that fear to paralyze you into unreasonable avoidance that it really affects your life.  Like a person that has been bitten by a dog, and now wants to have all dogs destroyed.  Knowledge, thought and caution are usually enough to keep you safe.  Don’t lock yourself inside your house just because there might be snakes outside.  <grin>.

As to bears – I just sent something to a ham-radio hiker friend….  Let me see if I can find it for you.

We have bears in the NM mountains just north of me.  There are bear warning signs, and even precautions to take right on the trail-head signs.

They warn that black and brown bears are frequently seen, and sometimes even grizzly bears in the more northern mountains bordering Colorado.  They warn of approaching bears, or even coming upon them and startling them.  They recommend wearing brightly colored clothing, making noise while hiking, and even sewing little bells on your clothing so that the bears know you are coming.  They also recommend carrying bear repellant spray.

Another thing they mention is identifying what bears are in the area.  If you see them - of course you can tell the difference.  Brown bears are brown.  Black bears are black, and about the same size as a brown bear.  Grizzly bears are also black, but much larger and meaner too.  Although they are cute - DO NOT approach any bear cubs under any circumstances!

If you don't see bears, you can still somewhat identify them by their scat (poop) found on the trail.  Black and brown bear scat is very similar.  It will contain bits of twigs, leaves, and sometimes rabbit fur or bird feathers.  Grizzly bear scat will usually contain bits of brightly colored clothing, little bells, and will smell like bear repellant spray.

Really - if you want to know....  http://www.bear-tracker.com/blackbearscat.html

Good luck on your next hike.....

As you can see – part of it was a joke, as it is generally accepted that Grizzly bears do not come this far south, being predominantly in Idaho and Washington St.  Doesn’t mean they can’t walk the continental divide and come south.  But generally, the last grizzly sighting this far south is generally accepted as being in the mid 1940s.

So don’t let the idea of Texas snakes stop you from enjoying the out-doors and finding lots of letterboxes.  I guess what I am saying is – don’t let fear control your life.
