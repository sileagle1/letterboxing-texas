Option B: Fasten the container securely in place. I have several
letterboxes in locations that might flood, and they are tied down
with metal chains. I use a clip to attach the chain to the container
so finders can unclip it and carry it to somewhere convenient to
stamp in.

In some cases, I tie a bit of stainless steel wire around the
container to form a loop to fasten the clip to. It's gotta be
stainless, I've found regular steel wire rusts away in only a couple
of months. I've also considered using copper wire, or perhaps
insulated copper wire with black, brown, or dark green insulation.

I've got tons of chain laying around, light-duty stuff intended for
hanging plants or whatever. I typically connect it to the clip with
a key ring.

I usually fasten the chain down to a tree or something with a big
screw. Sometimes I just wrap it around a tree or root and fasten it
with another key ring, but that doesn't work too often.

Needless to say, if the place floods you're gonna be hoping that the
container is truly watertight! Lock&Locks reportedly work, and
probably the imitators, but it's not easy to fasten a chain to them.
Some just use duct tape, which will probably work for a good long
while.

Most everything else leaks. I'm using big-mouth screw-top jars (like
peanut butter jars) and adding a bit of vinyl under the lid as a
gasket. Seems watertight, but I haven't tested them in an actual
flood yet.

-- Kirbert

SandiBox wrote:

> My concern about using chains is damaging the tree, especially if a

> inhibiting grown. Both could potentially cause damage.

I own 73 acres of woods with about a billion trees on it. Let me put
your mind to rest: Putting a screw into a tree doesn't hurt it at
all. Over the past 100 years or so, the trees in my woods have had
big spikes, fencing nails, barbed wire, No Trespassing signs, you
name it attached to trees. The trees happily grow around all of it.
In one place on a neighbor's property a spike was put into a tree and
a wrapped-up garden hose hung from it, and the tree grew around it
all, so there was a garden hose sticking out of a tree.

It'd do the same thing to a chain wrapped too tightly, I expect, but
there is some chance it might choke the tree. That's why I never
wrap the chains tightly at all. In fact, I generally avoid wrapping
them around a tree, although that's primarily because they're usually
plainly visible when you do. I'm more likely to wrap the chain
around one root of the tree -- and I'll still do that loosely.
