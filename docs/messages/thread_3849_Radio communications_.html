From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2008-11-09 18:39:41 UTC-07:00
This information applies to VHF and UHF radios.  HF radios (short waves) work differently.
 
Well - you are likely using FRS radios.  They are limited to 1/2 watt output.  Most handheld ham radios are rated at 5 watts, with some lower output switch-selectable.  So you could expect some better performance simply because there is more power available.  There is another factor.  More output power means more battery power needed or shorter battery life expected.
 
That said, when there are thousands of tons of rock between the two stations, even 100 watts may not make it.
 
HOWEVER.
 
If you all are in sight of a high point, the same high point...  and there is a repeater on that high point...  no problems!
 
 
 
The technical info of how a repeater works would be lengthy.  But this simple diagram gives you the idea.  It works both ways.  Many can receive, but only one may talk at any time. 
 
Any questions?

On that note, Mike, let me ask a question that might be of interest to everyone, given that letterboxers often find ourselves out of cell phone range.
 
My family often carry pretty good two-way radios with us in case we get separated (or if I'm rendezvous'ing with FF and L to pick them up after a weekend hike).  In flatllands they work fairly well, but in mountains like we run into in Arkansas specifically, unless we are calling down a valley or a ridge, they aren't worth a plug nickel - we can't get a signal until we are almost within visual range.  How do mountains affect ham radio?  I'm guessing they are much more reliable, but what exactly would we expect if we move from two-ways to ham? (And now I'm sitting here realizing we would now have to look at buying THREE handhelds instead of one...)
