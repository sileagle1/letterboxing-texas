Mike - 2007 <mike-2007@elp.rr.com> wrote:
Not knowing your abilities, I cannot say any specifics.  But I will tell you a secret.
 
Repetition is the key.
 
Example.  Lift a calf every day, pretty soon you can lift a cow.
 
Do not train for the event.  Start training slowly - but push yourself a little bit.  Some days you will feel like you can do more - so do more.  Some days you will not have it in you - but try to get at least 15-20 minutes of good heart-rate for that day anyway.  Get on a schedule of training.  Like Mon-Wed-Fri or Tue-Thu-Sat.  Do your best to keep to it.  And try to do a little more each day.  It will get easier.  Also - there is only one way to get trained for climate.  That is that climate.  This is why our soldiers stage in Kuwait before going into Iraq.  They don't just sit around waiting for an assignment.  They train every day - and a little more every day - till they are ready fir the daily duty.
 
Try to do some training in the gear you will be using for the event.  The LAST thing you want to do it put on new shoes just before you walk 20 miles.  My rule of thumb - 50 miles in short walks with a new pair of boots before you do a road-march (military hike) with them.  Lighter shoes might not need as much break-in, but the day of the event isn't the time to learn that there is a glue-spot on the tongue that gives you a sore spot every time you flex your ankle. 
If you need to carry your own gear (water - lip balm - sun screen - etc) consider a Camel-Bak.  I like the military models - they are rugged.  I hike a lot of rugged places with heavy gear.  Just carried a 55 lb power supply up to a radio site a few days ago.  But they are also heavy.  The civilian models are light-weight.  They won't hold up to my abuse, but this one works for my wife just fine - the Camel-Bak M.U.L.E.  It carries nearly 1 gallon of water, and had enough pockets to carry bug spray etc.....  It was made for biking, but also works out just right for light-weight hiking.
