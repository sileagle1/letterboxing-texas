I am glad we're re-emphasizing the need for discretion and stealth.
It is possible, even with children. Mine are careful, partially
because they know how they want others to treat their boxes.
However, they're still noisy kids, so we have some excuses at the
ready--like a "snack break" or a posed photo.

We choose not to participate in Scouts, but Scouting makes a great
excuse if ever questioned when you're out with the kids. Just
mumble something about an orienteering badge and people remember
their own scouting days.

And finally, as we're going over some of the basics. Please make
sure that you rehide a box as well if not better than when you found
it. Particularly for urban boxes, that is the key to survival. I
ALWAYS double check the re-hide if the kids do it, just to make
extra sure. I find box maintenance a bore and am not likely to
recarve and replace one. Most likely, except for in a few
instances, one of ours that goes confirmed missing will be "retired."

Hope you're out enjoying the weekend. We found Crimson Roar last
night when we (the adults) stopped in for a pint after dinner.
Asterix says forget the coffee series, he would like all the boxers
to place boxes at decent pubs!

DB

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-03 10:22:58 UTC-07:00
That reminds me of a funny story that happened to us
last year.

We have a box planted in Tyler State Park. When we
planted it, we had gone up past TSP to place one at
Tiger Creek Refuge, so we wanted to be sure to get
double benefit by getting the TSP one placed also.

Well, it was a busy day at TSP and it was very busy on
the trail itself, but we didn't realize how busy until
we were actually on the trail and in the area we
wanted to plant. As I've mentioned recently, I really
don't like the heat, so I wasn't about to waste a hot
sweaty walk just to turn around and not do what we
went to do! We hung out and ate a snack, read a book,
made notes in our nature journal (our what?), rested
on a log, looked at bugs, blah blah blah. We
meanwhile had been scoping out the spot and knew where
it needed to be, just needed to get it in place and
document it.

We thought we finally had a chance to place the box
and were zeroing in, when a group of several teenaged
boys started down the trail. Now bear in mind, I box
with a teenage girl, so this wasn't a good situation.
I suspected they were going to have to stop and check
her out, so I quickly got her and my 8-year-old boy to
work with me on a compass lesson. Now - bear in mind,
using a compass does NOT come easily to me, and I've
only really started working hard to get good at using
one in the last year or so (I mean really, what
self-respecting scouter/letterboxer doesn't know how
to use a compass?), so me giving a lesson was a little
ironic. Sure enough though, the guys stopped to say
hello and see what we were doing, so I wound up giving
them a compass lesson as well. I was really
stretching and I thought I was gonna have to launch
into my weak understanding of declination before we
finally got them to go away by boring them to death
(and thankfully, they weren't my daughter's type).

I was never so glad to get a box slapped into it's
hiding place!

Lucy
