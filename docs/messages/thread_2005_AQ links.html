What ever happened to that box you could check on AQ to have boxes automatically listed on LBNA, anyway? Thanks! MO5

When Ryan was experimenting with the "Hide boxes from Search Engines" feature, he instituted a F1 requirement for those boxes, meaning you had to have logged one find to see the clues for these boxes.  It upset a number of people and the automatic link was removed as a result.  Ryan eventually removed the F1 feature (as an automatic option if you are looking to hide from search engines), but the link was not reinstated and I don't expect it to be.

It was much easier for me when it was there.  As a result, some of my boxes have not ended up being listed on LBNA.  AtlasQuest is my personal preference, because of the mapping and search capabilities, so I turn to AQ first if I'm going to be listing the clues anywhere.

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-07-24 04:38:45 UTC
I like how if you're planning a trip, you can use the trip planner to
search within XXX miles (you decide) of the Interstate and it'll find
the boxes for you. I also like how if you search with a given city as
your centerpoint, you will find all the boxes within a X radius
(again, you decide), even if they're in another county, another LBNA
region designation, or another state. I loathe looking up boxes by
county--because my geography skills are not that precise--and because
I just don't think like that. I want the boxes closest to where I'm
going to be. I find flipping back and forth between regions on the
LBNA map, figuring out what cities are in which county, etc. to be a
huge time drain. And, then, to find out that you missed a box you
were within 2 miles of, simply because it was in the "other" county.
Just Arrgh.

So, with the recent upgrade of the mapping function on AQ, all you
have to do is put in an intersection or an address if there is one.
Sometimes it doesn't take the exact address, so I try to put in the
closest intersection. Then, when someone clicks on the map link,
they're in the general area at least. This is particularly helpful
for a sprawled out city like Houston, where boxes can be in the same
"city" but be 60 miles apart. In a rural area, where the cities/towns
aren't so big, just using the closest town is good enough in my
opinion. When I'm planning trips, I just want to know how far out of
my route a given box is, and the closest town pretty much tells me that.

The funny thing is, people always say that AQ has more bells and
whistles, but I'm not really a bells and whistles techno-freak kind of
person. What I am unabashedly is is a map geek. I love maps, I like
to look at them, I study them, etc. Thinking graphically is so much
more helpful (for me) than reading linear directions--take this
highway here, turn onto this one for .6 miles, etc--I'd rather see the
map myself and figure out how to get there.

I would love it if there would be a little map that would pop up
showing you the locations of all the letterboxes. I've seen that on
the geocaching site and I think it's great. Ryan keeps upgrading AQ,
so maybe one day that site will get there.

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-07-24 09:31:51 UTC-05:00
I agree with you about the region thing on LBNA.  As an example, here in East Texas, Cherokee County is in one region and Anderson County is in another.  We have several natural groupings of towns and counties, these two counties being one of them.  We share quite a few resources, but they are split down the middle on LBNA.  Silver Eagle had devised a new means of outlining the regions that made a lot more sense community-wise, but the powers that be never have gotten to it.  The side benefit, to me at least, of being forced to use LBNA for the first year or so I letterboxed was that I learned counties really well and I've developed a minds' eye view of where each county in my area is and what towns are in them.  Still though, it IS frustrating to have to try to figure out
 
I guess what I've found to be the problem on the trip planner on AQ is that I rarely go near an interstate.  I know that sounds odd, but even on longer trips we just don't "do" interstates. I cross over them and I ever so occasionally actually travel on one, but using interstates to plan a trip isn't a practical approach for us, although I realize it makes perfect sense for most folks!  Maybe if Ryan would add US Highways....

I do agree with you about being able to plug in a town and get letterboxes within "xxx" miles.  THAT's a nice feature.  The only real problem with it is that there are still quite a few boxers who don't list their boxes on AQ, but that will come with time and then that will be a great feature. 
 
I'm like you with respect to maps.  I really need the visual orientation.  If you try to tell me how to get somewhere, I'm a lot less likely to get there than if you show me where I'm going on a map and let me figure it out.  Consequently, I love maps, even though it wasn't until I started letterboxing that I learned that.  Quite honestly, I don't know that I'll give up paper maps even if we eventually get a site that works perfectly - I do think I like to be able to run my finger along the route and I also like being able to pan out and in visually instead of having to wait on the computer to do it. 
 
I'm going to have to play a little more with the mapping on AQ, but one thing I realize - I need to go back and make sure my address information is as good as it can be on AQ for those folks who rely on it.  I've kind of glossed over the address lines in the past.  And that's the big problem with either LB site - they rely on people to put in good data in the first place! 
 
Lucy

I agree about the regions being illogical. Another example: I have
an interest in West Texas because Midland is my hometown. The "West
Texas Letterboxer", El Lobo, has placed boxes in Midland (Midland
County), Odessa (Ector County) and Stanton (Martin County). Odessa
is 20 miles west of Midland and Stanton is 20 miles east of Midland
and the three counties are adjacent. According to the LbNA map,
Ector County is in West Texas, Midland is in Central Texas and
Martin County is in The Panhandle. Another interesting thing is
that his Midland box is listed in the West Texas region (which is
logical), even though the map shows Midland County to be in Central
Texas. It does get confusing and you have to be on your toes when
you're planning your letterboxing expedition, don't you?
