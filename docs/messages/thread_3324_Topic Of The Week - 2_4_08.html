I have an idea for this week's topic: What is the worst thing that
has happened to you while letterboxing? Don't think of it as a
depressing subject so much as a learning opportunity that might help
others from suffering the same fate. If you don't have a "worst"
story, then insert "most interesting" or "strangest".

I have several, which is probably not a good thing. My worst has to
be the time my car got broken into while letterboxing. It was in
Houston at the parking lot for Terry Hershey Bike Trail off
Memorial. I was with my family and we were only gone for about 15
minutes, but the crooks had time to break a window and steal my
wife's purse with everything in it. Not much money, but what a pain
getting new credit cards, drivers license, locks on house doors,
etc. Getting your car broken into is always bad, but having it done
while enjoying nature is especially galling. My wife now feels like
she has to take her purse on hikes, which is a pain. Guys definitely
have it easier with wallets. I have been back to that trail head
since, but I park near Memorial so my car is less secluded. I have
to walk a little further, but I feel a little less nervous.

My worst physical thing is the time I got bit by a scorpion. Now you
are probably thinking "what a bozo, doesn't he know to wear gloves or
move a box with a stick before grabbing it", and you are right, but I
did do this. I don't wear gloves, but I always move a box with my
boot or a stick before grabbing it and I did so this time, too.
However, the incident occurred WHILE PUTTING THE BOX BACK. I had
never thought of being careful returning the box, but I sure am now!

My worst fright came while retrieving a large letterbox (the original
TX Tour Bus) from inside a hollow tree. It was up inside about my
entire arm length, so I couldn't see what was up there. I used a
stick to try and get it out, but it was stuck too well. After poking
it for a while, I figured any critter would have moved on by then, so
I reached up and grabbed it. It finally came loose and as I brought
it out I noticed something big on top of it; a giant snake! Now I
don't mind admitting I don't like snakes, so I just about had a heart
attack. Luckily it was winter and the snake was sluggish, so he just
slowly slithered away and I was able to stamp in after awhile. But
now I had to put the box back! Putting my hand back up that tree was
probably the hardest thing I have had to do in a long time. You can
be sure I poked a stick up there for a long time before doing it,
though.

Luckily, none of these incidents was life-threatening, but I did
learn valuable lessons that made me a safer letterboxer.

Silver Eagle

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-02-04 11:31:03 UTC-06:00
Well - I have to say that because I have heard you tell about that scorpion incident before, I DO always wear gloves when sticking my hand in a hole, and I will only stick my hand into a blind hole if there is no other way to get the box.  I'm just not into stings and bites!
 
I think the car break-in thing is truly a bad thing to have happen and thankfully I can't share a story like that.
 
The worst thing I've ever had happen - and it IS a lesson to all - was when I went to plant SJZ's birthday box August before last.  I left everyone at the swimming area at Ray Roberts SP and went to quickly hide the box.  I knew where I wanted to hide it and I thought I would only be 15 minutes at most.  I wanted to travel light so I didn't carry anything but the box and a piece of paper and pen with me, not even a bottle of water.  The place I wanted to hide it was a little further down the trail than I remembered, and the place I wanted to hide the box wasn't as good as I remembered it being so I had to find another place, which made me take longer.  I had been in the water all day, so it was hotter than I thought it was, and the return trip to the car was uphill with no shade, so it took a little longer too.  By the time I was halfway back to the car I was feeling the heat a little too much (y'all know my aversion to heat anyway, but this was more than my standard "I hate heat" gripe) and I had to stop pretty often whenever I could find a scratch of shade along the way.  When I got back to the last trail junction, I was literally hoping I could at least make it back to the car.  All of our cell phones were in the car, so even if I reached the car I couldn't call Fearless Fox, and I was not at all sure I could drive.  When I reached the car, I managed to grab a bottle of Gatorade before I cranked the car up and started the A/C.  I sucked down the first bottle and started on a second, being mindful by then not to drink it too fast.  I finally cooled off enough to drive myself back to the swimming area and over to where the rest of my family was, and while I thought I was feeling a little better it was obvious I didn't look good when I saw the alarm on FF's face.  He wanted to take me to the ER and I probably should have gone, but I convinced him to give me an hour or so to rehydrate and cool down, so he went to the concession and got me some popsicles. 
 
Heat exhaustion is nothing to play around with!  Unlike the cold or flu, it isn't a suck-it-up sort of condition.  I never go anywhere without a bottle of water now, even if it's cool.  DRINK!

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2008-02-05 10:51:06 UTC-05:00
I guess my worst-thing-while-letterboxing was getting bonked on the head hard enough to cut a gash and have blood pouring all over me,  all the while trying to remain invisible in the trees just above a very busy Barton Creek trail in Austin on a Saturday afternoon.   Like SE, I was putting the box back, bending over to get it way back into the hole, and didn't see the big sharp stub of a branch sticking out until after I bonked my head hard enough to make me see stars.  There I was, leaning back against the tree holding my hand to my head, and when I removed it, my hand was covered in blood and blood was dripping off my head all over the place.  Of course, the only paper I had handy to staunch the bleeding were the pages of clues for the rest of that trail, so I crumpled those and pushed them to my bleeding head with one hand while scrambling through my backpack with the other, trying to find something like tissues or paper towels (which I usually have but couldn't find).  I dug out a bottle of Purell antibacterial lotion and poured it over my head, all the while worrying about all those joggers, strollers, bikers, and families passing below me on the trail - if they saw me they'd be sure to think I'd been a mugging victim and I'd be in the ambulance before I could say anything, so I was trying to be quiet and invisible.   I had no hat, of course, but I managed to pull my hair over the bleeding places enough to cover it with my sunglasses on top of my head, wipe the worst of the mess off my hands and face with leaves, and cautiously made my way back to the restroom at the trailhead and washed up as best I could.   I had to throw all the clue sheets away - they looked like evidence from a crime scene.   What a headache - for days!  Probably should have gone for stitches, but the thought of explaining myself was worse than a hole in the head.   I learned some lessons, too:   look where I'm going, carry extra paper towels and antiseptic, and wear a hat!

From: Puddle Splasher (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2008-02-05 16:34:22 UTC
It seems I've made about every letterboxing and hiking mistake known
to man (running out of water, not having bug spray, wearing the wrong
shoes, bonking my head on a branch, reaching into a hole without
checking it first), but I think the worst situation I put myself in
was hiking a very icy trail. It was an unusually beautiful day in
late winter in Connecticut, and I decided to go find a box that was
hidden about 20 miles from my house on the Appalachian Trail. I had
hiked that portion of the trail many times and was quite familiar
with it. In fact I knew a backtrail that would cut through the woods
and get me to the section where the box was hidden faster. So I
decided to take that trail going to the box. It meandered through the
woods and along the back edge of a farm. When I got to the box, I had
to use my hand-shovel to retrieve it since it was buried under a
couple of feet of snow next to a rock fence. No problem. Then I
decided to take the official part of the Appalachian Trail back. The
birds were singing, the sun was shining, there was a gentle breeze,
and I had just found a great letterbox. It was a gorgeous day in New
England and life couldn't be sweeter. This part of the trail hugs the
edge of a cliff, high above the rushing waters of a beautiful river
far below. What I had failed to take into consideration is that it
was winter and the trail is steep, rocky and frozen. There were many
places where I actually had to sit down and scoot along on my rear
end, just to keep from possibly sliding off the side of the mountain
and plunging into the fierce, icy river below. Other places, the
trail was wider and I could "ice skate" in my hiking boots without
fear of sliding off the trail. It took me about 30 minutes to get to
the box, and about two hours to get back. I certainly had visions of
being the lead story on the six o'clock news of the fool-hearted
hiker who plunged off the trail and had been the subject of a massive
rescue operation. I could just see some perfectly coiffed news
anchor, clucking her tongue and shaking her head. However, I arrived
safely back at my Jeep, no worse for the wear. I stopped at the local
general store in Kent for a cup of coffee and there were other hikers
huddled there who asked if I had just come down from the north (far
steeper than the part I had just slid down). It must have been the
muddy stain on my butt that gave it away. Lesson learned? Nothing
really.... but then I'm a slow learner.
Puddle Splasher

I get into the general vicinity of the cache, and my GPS starts going bonkers due to tree cover. That'll leave a mark...

I picked up my GPS, Phone, and Bag and walked back out to the paved trail and back to my truck. I'm in shock. Once back at the truck, I realized that I hadn't opened the cache. Hmmm, I'm not going back IN there, not without some Artillary and Heavy Hardware!

A little later in the day, I was exhausted and called my wife to let her know I was on my way back early. I hadn't told her this story. When I got home, she had my dinner ready ..Pork Chops! My wife ...I gotta love her!
