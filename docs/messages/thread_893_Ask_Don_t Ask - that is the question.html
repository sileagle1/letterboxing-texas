Ask/Don't Ask - that is the question

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-06-05 20:08:17 UTC-07:00
This is all based on my understanding - you'll
probably get a wide range of responses to your
question...

National Parks state that they are off limits to
letterboxes and geocaches, but if you have also been
reading the big list, you know that Choi received
permission to place a box in a National Park last
week. That's no small feat! National Parks, then,
are an "always ask" place, and go in with a rational
explanation of letterboxing and your specific
response. and why you want to plant on their park, do
a little research, and hope for the best. There ARE
clandestine boxes in National Parks, but there are
also lots of them that get confiscated. If the Park
finds them and confiscates, it's my understanding they
aren't very kind about it.

National Forests, on the other hand, are more
amenable, although I noticed someone recently
mentioned that they tend to frown on LB'ing too. I
think that's probably more a regional thing. I
wouldn't ask in a National Forest in Texas, I would
just plant.

State Parks in Texas are very friendly to letterboxes
and I wouldn't worry about asking permission in them.
Local parks - same.

I've never heard anything about State Forests, but I
would imagine they would follow along with National
Forests. I've got a state forest adjacent to my town,
so maybe I'll try it out here soon.

Cemeteries are a judgment call. Some folks feel
strongly that boxes shouldn't be placed in cemeteries
at all. Others feel that if the box pays proper
reverance to the site and isn't invasive of the burial
area, it's okay. Personally, I think it's okay to
place certain boxes in certain cemeteries, but I
wouldn't just go plop a cute little teddy bear box in
a cemetery because that's the one I had on me when I
got the urge to plant one - it would be specifically
carved for the location and the site thoughtfully
selected. As far as asking, that would be a judgment
call also.

Private property is an "always ask" thing.

And I think you've been reading the thread about zoos
- the reason it's a good idea to ask in zoos is
because many zoos are operated by private foundations,
hence private property. Even still, zoos (regardless
of who owns them) are usually kept pretty clean, so a
letterbox placed in any zoo would be at high risk of
discovery, so if you ask and have permission, staff at
the zoo knows it's there and is more likely to return
it to its hiding spot instead of throwing it away or,
heaven forbid, make a federal issue out of it.

That list should cover the basics, and as I said,
let's see what everyone else can add to it!

Lucy
