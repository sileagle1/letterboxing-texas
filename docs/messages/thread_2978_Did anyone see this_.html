Tourism Department Adopts Geocaching Hobby - The Arkansas Department of Parks and Tourism has adopted geocaching as a way to encourage travelers to explore the state this year. Cache in With the Natural State provides the coordinates to a different cache each month. Trade items in the caches may include tourism incentives such as certificates for lodging or restaurants. Geocaching is an international hobby in which caches are hidden by individuals or groups. The location of each geocache is identified by global positioning system coordinates that are shared via the Internet. Hobbyist use hand-held GPS units to hunt for the caches. For coordinates and details, see www.arkansas,com/geocache.

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2007-08-10 14:50:32 UTC
I contacted Mr. Good at TPW again and received the following reply:

Hi Charles,

We are in the process of reviewing and updating most of our
operational policies for State Parks. I am confident that our
present policy regarding geocaching can be expanded to cover
letterboxing as the activities have similarities. I believe the
policy update will be completed by September 1.

Thanks for checking,

Kevin Good

I'm not sure this is the answer I was looking for, so I replied to
him:

Dear Mr. Good,

I am hoping letterboxing can be considered on its own merits. The
policy for geocaching is restrictive in that the box must be
maintained quarterly. If a family from Houston, for instance,
visited Davis Mountain State Park and placed a letterbox, they
wouldn't be able to go back there every 3 months to check on it,
which would violate the policy. I respecfully ask that you consider
this point. Thank you.

I'll keep you posted.

Boots Tex

First of all, BT, one point I think you can make to differentiate gc'ing from lb'ing is this (Mike, I'm relying on you to correct me if I'm wrong):

 

Most gc's give you cords and possibly an encoded "hint" to help you find it, but most gc'ers consider looking at the hint as wimping out.   There are a few gc's that give you coords, then give you clues to bring you on in those are good.  However, by and large, gc's give you coords only (with the aforementioned encoded hint maybe).   Then - every GPSr has some margin of accuracy 10 sq ft is pretty darned good (yeah, I've heard stories of folks who buy the el cheapo at Walmart and it gets 'em within a foot or two, and I just don't buy it) and when you add the margin of your unit to the margin of the planter's unit, you are within 20 sq ft on a good day, and then you start looking.   And if you aren't good at thinking like a geocacher, you wind up overturning everything until you find the cache, creating a mess and disturbing all the things park rangers don't like to see disturbed.

 

With letterboxing, GOOD clues bring you right on in to an exact location.  This isn't to say that the same thing can't happen with a LB, but it is less likely, especially if the clues are well-written.

 

Here's another difference between letterboxing and geocaching.  We talk.   Yes, they talk too, but they talk more face-to-face and their means of disseminating information is not as efficient as ours.  We have the national list, the newboxers list, the state list, and in DFW and Houston, we have regional lists.  

 

If we play on our effective communication, and if we do "education" on the lists sometimes (and since we are talking about state parks, I suggest using the state list this one), we can point to the fact that we keep our members up and running on best practices i.e. not placing boxes in such a way as there is only one way in and one way out.  Perhaps we can make a list of critical topics and discuss one a month, or have one person lead a discussion of them ( i.e. since I teach LNT, I would be willing to lead a discussion of LNT). 

 

THEN if we had one park or one person who agreed to be the letterboxing liaison, so that if they could not reach a box owner they could contact that person who will try to get in touch with the owner through other means, or will come get the offending box, or will arrange for someone else to pick it up, they might be more amenable to loosening the rules for us.   I think though, that we cannot put too much on the liaison it has to be a "TRY" thing, not a "WILL" thing.  But if the liaison is unable to reach the owner and unable to handle a box or arrange for handling a box, he can contact the park and say "no luck here either chunk it" and we can do some spot education to prevent the same sort of problem from happening again.

 

It's a bit of a jump, but these may be the sorts of measures that allow us freedom.  We are in a sense agreeing to self-police, which means we all have to be accountable to each other.   The problem here is that the folks who are planting but not on the lists may not be meeting the standards we agree to, and we have to account for that.
 

So what do y'all think?

Most gc's give you cords and possibly an encoded "hint" to help you find it, but most gc'ers consider looking at the hint as wimping out.   There are a few gc's that give you coords, then give you clues to bring you on in – those are good.  However, by and large, gc's give you coords only (with the aforementioned encoded hint maybe).   Then - every GPSr has some margin of accuracy – 10 sq ft is pretty darned good (yeah, I've heard stories of folks who buy the el cheapo at Walmart and it gets 'em within a foot or two, and I just don't buy it) – and when you add the margin of your unit to the margin of the planter's unit, you are within 20 sq ft on a good day, and then you start looking.   And if you aren't good at thinking like a geocacher, you wind up overturning everything until you find the cache, creating a mess and disturbing all the things park rangers don't like to see disturbed.

ALL TRUE IN A GENERAL SORT OF WAY.  SOME GEOCACHERS WRITE GOOD CLUES.  SOME LETTERBOXERS COULD USE LESSONS.

ACTUALLY - I REALLY DON'T SEE A LOT OF DIFFERENCE HERE.  IN MOST CASES THERE ARE ONLY SO MANY ROCKS TO HIDE IT UNDER.

Here's another difference between letterboxing and geocaching.  We talk.   Yes, they talk too, but they talk more face-to-face and their means of disseminating information is not as efficient as ours.  We have the national list, the newboxers list, the state list, and in DFW and Houston, we have regional lists.  

I HAVE TO DIFFER HERE.  THERE ARE PROBABLY MORE GEOCACHERS IN THE GEOCACHING FORMS AND MORE GEOCACHING FORUMS THAN YOU CAN IMAGINE.  THERE ARE STATE AND LOCAL GROUPS AS WELL AS MORE THAN ONE NATIONAL GROUP.  NOT ALL CACHERS BELONG OR PARTICIPATE.  NEITHER DO ALL BOXERS.

IMPORTANT POINT.  NOT EVERYONE IS ON THIS LIST.  LIKE I HAD ONE CACHER DIG HOLES AROUND MY FAKE CACTUS - CAUSE HE DIDN'T KNOW AND DIDN'T PARTICIPATE.

THEN – if we had one park or one person who agreed to be the letterboxing liaison, so that if they could not reach a box owner they could contact that person who will try to get in touch with the owner through other means, or will come get the offending box, or will arrange for someone else to pick it up, they might be more amenable to loosening the rules for us.   I think though, that we cannot put too much on the liaison – it has to be a "TRY" thing, not a "WILL" thing.  But if the liaison is unable to reach the owner and unable to handle a box or arrange for handling a box, he can contact the park and say "no luck here either – chunk it" and we can do some spot education to prevent the same sort of problem from happening again.

MUCH AS I DO IN EL PASO NOW.

So what do y'all think? 

ANYTHING IS BETTER THAN NOTHING.  PRO-ACTIVE APPROACH WILL AT LEAST GET YOU AN UNDERSTANDING WITH THE RANGERS.  DOING NOTHING WILL NOT GET YOU ANY NOTICE AT ALL.
