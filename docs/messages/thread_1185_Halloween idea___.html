Hi,

I'm about to babble, in case anyone else wants to run with this idea...

I was just thinking about how cool it could be to start kids on
letterboxing by having a local event where people swap stamp imprints
instead of candy treats at Halloween.

Here's one way to work with really little guys, to create their own
rubber stamps:

Use styrofoam trays from the produce and meat departments at the
grocery store.

As a negative print, kids can use a pencil (broader/duller points are
better) to impress a design onto the styrofoam. The wider the
line/impression, the better. But, this will create a short-term
rubber stamp.

(The foam--like all plastic--has a "memory" and will slowly fill in
the area that was pushed down with the pencil. Experiment to find one
that's very slow to remember, before you do this with kids.)

As a positive print, even kiddie scissors usually cut styrofoam.
Children can cut out cool shapes (think Matisse) and glue them onto a
wooden cube (from Michael's, etc., wood sections). You may need to
build up a few layers, or use double-stick foam insulation tape (a
thin version is made by 3M and sold at office supply stores, or you
can get the thicker kind at Wal-Mart, etc.) and this will raise the
cut-out shape above the wood support.

These will usually print with pigment-type rubber stamp pads, if the
stamps resist the ink on the dye pads. Worst case, you can use
water-based block printing ink. (Saturate a square of felt from the
fabric store, to make your own ink pad for this.)

There is actually a foam sheet that is ideal for this kind of project,
if you can find it at art supply stores. I haven't used it in
forever, and I've forgotten what it's called. They usually keep it in
the section for that's for grade school teachers.

Of course, you can work with regular rubber stamp supplies, for older kids.

Taking this ahead to spring, wouldn't it be fun to hide letterboxing
stamps in big (L'eggs-type) plastic eggs and hide them for the annual
Easter Egg hunt!

Am I reinventing the wheel? Are other people already doing this? I
can see this organized by the children's room at a public library, at
a church, by a Scouting group, homeschoolers, etc.

Anyway, that's what's running through my brain this sunny Saturday.
