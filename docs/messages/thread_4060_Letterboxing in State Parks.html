Letterboxing in State Parks

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2009-04-20 16:22:27 UTC
As most of you know, there has been an ongoing problem with placing letterboxes in Texas State Parks. Part of the problem has been the growth of letterboxing and the corresponding larger number of boxes being both found and placed in the parks. I know of letterboxes that were placed in State Parks 7 or 8 years ago and that are still there and have never had a problem. Others seem to be found soon after placing, prompting an email from the Park Ranger asking that the box be permitted or removed. Some people think that some Rangers find their way onto LbNA and seek out the boxes, simply not wanting them in their particular park. This could be true in some cases, as each Ranger seems to interpret the rules differently. Yesterday I got an email from the Ranger at South Llano River State Park about my letterbox there called "Mr. TexasTexas Governor Series". He was very nice and understanding and asked if I would mind filling out the permit application so that the box would be properly registered. I thanked him and told him how much we enjoyed spending time in his beautiful park, promised to fill out the required form and asked, out of curiosity, how he happened to find my box, since I thought I had placed it in a safe place. Here is his reply:

"Your letterbox was brought to my attention from one of our campers who was looking for the letterbox and wasn't able to find it. She asked me if I knew it was still there or not and I went and found it to confirm it was still there.

Your letterbox was in a great place and is causing no problems at all. I wish that we had more letterboxes like it in the park. I think that letterboxing is a great way for people to get out and enjoy the outdoors and with the history that you provided with the letterbox it makes it even better."

There are a couple of lessons to be learned here, in my opinion. Number one is that you should never jeopardize someone else's box by asking the park staff about it. Number two is that, maybe some parks are okay with letterboxes and might even, under the right circumstances, embrace them. This Ranger would probably have never found this letterbox if it hadn't been brought to his attention, so maybe he's not actively searching for boxes. One thing is becoming obvious to me, based on contact that I have had and that Barefoot Lucy has had with state park Rangers in the past month or so: there are rules in place, originated for geocaches, but now adapted to include letterboxes, in our Texas State Parks and the Rangers are becoming aware of them. Consistency is still a problem, as each Ranger is apparently free to interpret the rules as he sees fit. My intention is to ask for a permit application when I go into a park to place a letterbox. You need to decide what you will do, but, please, never do anything to put an existing box in danger. I feel sure that the camper mentioned by the Ranger was not an experienced letterboxer or they would have first, found the box (it isn't difficult to find) and, they wouldn't have brought it to the attention of the Ranger.

Boots Tex

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@windstream.net) | Date: 2009-04-21 13:35:59 UTC
I know this is like preaching to the choir since most offenders are probably not on this List, but this is a great point, and is more important than ever now that officials are starting to crack down on boxes. Never assume Rangers know about boxes unless it is explicitly stated in the clues, and even then you should exercise caution since things change and future Rangers might not be as sanguine. Another tactic that can endanger boxes is referencing other boxes in your clues. This can cause extra boxes being found by Rangers when they happen to find one box. I have been guilty of this in the distant past, mostly in parks where Baby Bear and I had the only boxes for a long time, but I have been removing the references as I find them. I must admit it goes against my nature to not advertise boxes and the parks they reside in, especially since I'm so used to doing that from the early days when I was trying to recruit more letterboxers, but we live in a new world now, and maybe it's time to be as stealthy with clues as we are with the boxes themselves.

Letterboxing in State Parks

From: Boots Tex (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2010-09-03 20:05:03 UTC
I was doing a search for the Texas Parks and Wildlife Department policies in regard to geocaching and came across an article in the Dallas Morning News about geocaching in state parks. It ended with the following note:

"If you don't own a GPS but want to join the search? Texas Parks and Wildlife is developing a non-GPS version of the game for people who want to follow clues the old-fashioned way. Clues are provided on the TPWD Web site."

Huh?! Finding out about this will be my next project for sure.

I also found a Q/A section on the TPWD website:
Q: Is geocaching allowed in Texas State Parks?
A: Yes, geocaching can be a great way to enjoy parks and geocaches can also add to the educational experience of site visits.
Q: What should I do if I want to place a geocache in a Texas State Park or Historic Site?
A: First, visit with the park or historic site superintendent and discuss the location where you intend to place the cache. Some parks or historic sites may be entirely off-limits to geocaching due to the sensitive resources contained within the sites. The superintendent may limit placement of caches in some areas due to archeological or historical resources, endangered species or concerns for visitor safety. If the placement is approved, obtain a copy of a geocache permit in order to complete the contact and placement information.

Here is basically what I have found to be true about letterboxing in Texas State Parks:

First of all, you have a right to place a letterbox in a state park. The policy is in place, thank to our friends, the geocachers. The rules that apply for geocaching also apply to letterboxing. Here is a link to the geocaching policy and application for a permit:

http://www.geocachingadmin.com/info/Texas_State_Parks.pdf

I have found that virtually none of the park personnel have ever heard of letterboxing, so I have adopted the following procedure:

I take a letterbox with me that I want to place in the park and I walk into the office and ask for a permit application to place a letterbox in the park. After the blank look, I say, "It's a treasure hunting game similar to geocaching, except that you don't have to use a gps." Then, I show them the letterbox, opening it up and taking out the stamp and the logbook. Almost invariably, the reaction is something like "Wow, that's neat! So what is it that you wanted?" I explain that if they would give me a geocache permit application, I could use that. When they find it and hand it to me, I ask them if there are certain areas that I should stay away from because of park rules." If there are any, they'll let you know. After I place the box, I write the clues down on the permit application and fill out the other blanks. I leave the place for gps coordinates blank and at the heading, I mark out "geocaching" and write in "letterboxing". I take it back to the office and they look it over and approve it. I haven't had one disapproved yet. Getting my existing boxes at Huntsville SP did take a long time, but I think one park employee was responsible for that because he "just didn't have the time" to go find the boxes and approve the location. He's not there anymore. The permit is good for one year and can be renewed by email. If you don't renew it and they happen to notice, you'll receive an email request to renew it. One park, Cedar Hill SP, renewed my permit automatically and sent me a confirmation. I don't believe a letterbox will ever be confiscated and disposed of without some effort to contact the placer and try to get it permitted or, if it's in a bad location, moved. In short, Texas State Parks are not "anti-lettetboxing" and a few have even expressed the opinion that they were good for the park because they bring in visitors. I think the big problem is that we are "below the radar" in contrast to geocaching, but that's not necessarily and bad thing. The more parks we approach, in a respectful manner, the more they'll get to know us as a legitimate recreational activity. Even though there is a policy in place on the state level, each park manager has the authority to manage our hobby as he sees fit, so we just have to educate them, one park at a time. I encourage you to follow this procedure the next time you visit a Texas State Park and report your experience to us.

Boots Tex
