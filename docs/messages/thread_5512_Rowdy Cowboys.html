Rowdy Cowboys

From: SandiBox (sandibox@windstream.net) | Date: 2015-09-07 08:42:31 UTC-05:00
Soooo... Those rowdy Cowboys have been taking a breather for the summer, but on this official end-of-summer holiday weekend, they were itching to go!

My trusty 4-legged sidekick and I followed them to the appropriately named "Lone Star Trail" trailhead #4, on FM 149, 6.5 miles north of Montgomery, TX.
(That's at N30 28.898 W95 41.838 for you specific types)

They were raring to go, and Meekah and I chased them east on the trail for 182 steps to a large 3' stump on the left side of the trail. We were dog-tired from the chase, but sat a spell on the stump and rested. But darned if those crazy Cowboys hadn't also taken a rest behind the stump. So much for all that pent up energy! We left them and enjoyed some nature therapy of our own.

I think y'all got the wrong idea about how those rowdy cowboys get around.  They don't come to you; you have to come to them!  They just lie around in the woods somewhere until somebody takes pity on them and invites them into their home, so if you want to see them, you have to take pity on them and find out where they're hanging out, go get them before someone else does, and take them home with you.  Then, you don't want to keep them around too long or they'll start to wear out their welcome, then you have to take them out and drop them off somewhere to wait for the next kind-hearted soul to feel sorry for them and go pick them up.  That's just how they are.

From: (mjrekedal@gmail.com) | Date: 2015-09-14 18:57:16 UTC-07:00

Wow! That was fast! I was sure those rodeo folks would stick around for a few weeks, at least. But they were so pleased with the change in the weather and couldn't see hanging around inside any longer. Plus they picked up another member and said they needed to give him some road experience.  I was headed to south Austin, so they asked if they could hitch a ride. They wanted to visit an old friend Mary Moore Searight. She used to ranch in the area, and I guess some of these older cowboys knew her. Anyway, they wanted to see where her ranch house used to be. It's now a metropolitan park It was easy to drive in off Slaughter and park in the spot farthest to the left, right in front of the sidewalk heading past the pavilion. Those guys sure were interested in seeing where her house used to be, by the creek. So I followed them along that sidewalk. After looking  at the house foundation and reading all those info plaques, they thought they'd head on into the woods, taking the trail just to the right of the plaque about What's Growing Now?

So off we went. After a bit they saw a nice bench right where the trail turns sharp right. But everyone was too interested in the trail to stop and rest. So turn right we did, for 85 steps to a gnarly oak tree on the left, with a broken trunk stem pointing right at us. From there we continued another 32 steps to a Y in the trail, taking the right branch with an oak tree on the right with branch over the trail and a larger oak to the right of that oak. (Lots of oaks in this woods.) We kept going on the trail. There was a volleyball net on the right thru the trees, but no one was interested in playing volleyball. Kept on another 110 steps or so to the next Y. We could see a picnic table off to the right. But the fellows wanted to stop and turn around. They walked back 15 steps. There was a medium sized oak tree on the right about 5 steps off the trail, with a large broken branch hanging down. Just beyond that tree another 5 steps was a double trunked oak, with the bark stripped off the front of one trunk. They decided they wanted to rest a spell between those two trunks. They were careful about the barbed wire on the ground, I thought it was pretty overgrown with bushes, but they all thought it would be a good place to rest  awhile and orient the newest member of the troupe. So we said our good-byes and they were really very polite and said to tell Bird Dog thanks for the coffee.
