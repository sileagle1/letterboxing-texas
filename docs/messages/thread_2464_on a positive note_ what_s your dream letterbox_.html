on a positive note, what's your dream letterbox?

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-01-05 02:21:10 UTC
Let's flip this discussion around to the positive. Letterboxing in
some ways is very individualized--some people like long hikes, some
people short. Some people like drive-bys, some people don't. Some
people like puzzles and mystery clues (me!), some people don't. Some
people care more about the stamp, others about the location. . .some
people are all about quantity, others focus on quality.

and. . .

That's only natural, because we're all individuals, with different
likes and dislikes.

So, what's your dream letterbox find? (and don't say I love them all,
because that's NOT what this question is about--it's more about what
would be your ideal.)

For me, I adore a medium-long hike (about 4 miles round trip) with
great mystery or clues that are crystal clear once you've solved them
that takes you to some special, unique intriguing place that you
didn't know about before you found the letterbox. Bonus points if the
hide is clever or the stamp is really well-carved. That's the box I
would give a 10.

My goal is to get my plants closer to my dream 10, because they're not
there yet. But I strive.

Now, I live in Houston, and our lack of sweeping vistas is sad. So, I
can focus more on mystery clues, challenging myself in my carving,
improving my logbooks, finding unique hikes, and the like. I could
definitely use some improvement in actual hiding place selection.
That's what I'm working towards.

Now, what about you?

Dewberry

From: (kacmbc@aol.com) | Date: 2007-01-04 22:13:01 UTC-05:00
My dream letterbox find would be the one that, as I approached the area in which the box was placed, Lance Berkman would be standing there!  :)
 
I won't say that I like them all, because that would defeat the purpose of the post, but I do like different boxes for different reasons.  Sometimes it is the stamp image that brings a smile to my face as I make an imprint in my logbook. Other times, it is the people I letterbox with and the memories of our journey.  Letterboxing in different states is always fun because you get to see different approaches to letterboxing ( the boxes, the logbooks, the stamp image, other letterboxers who have stamped in the books etc...)  but with that said, there are probably three things that would make a letterbox a "dream" come true! 
 
1.  Location of the box- two examples that come to mind are The Easternmost Letterbox in the USA which is near Quoddy Head Light in Maine.  Talk about an absolutely beautiful climb up a mountainside.  Not only was there an awesome stamp, the views from a nearby bench were worth the climb alone!  The second example is the  Black Beans letterbox by Baby Bear.  Had it not been for his box, I would have never learned the story of The Black Beans - and I have lived in Texas my whole life!  I think that might have been the only time I cried letterboxing- to date, anyway!  :)
 
2.  Letterboxes that require solving a puzzle.  Don't get me wrong- I LOVE straightforward clues, but there is a special feeling of satisfaction in knowing that certain stamps were obtained because I made my brain work for it!  ( Please be kind this year, DB!!)
 
3. Letterboxes that are a play on words.  It is really fun trying to guess what the stamp image might be like based on the name of the box, and even more fun actually seeing if I was right or not.  I love how different people interpret phrases, names, images and ideas for stamps!
 
Runs for Chocolate  :)

My dream letterbox would be in an off-the-beaten-path location.  The clues might revolve around the history or folklore of the area.  The box would be cleverly hidden (as opposed to being under a stack of carefully arranged sticks that scream “Here! From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2007-01-04 22:17:32 UTC-05:00
I think, for me, it's usually the place - the hike, the discovery of a new place or the discovering something new about a place I thought I already knew, plus the amazing fact of finding a small box containing someone's original idea of a letterbox, following good or funny or clever clues successfully, even if it takes me several tries to get it right, and then the bonus of the stamp, the logbook, and reading what others have noted or looking at the signature stamps of whoever has been there before me, unless, of course, I'm the FF!  And if there's a bonus, piggy-back, or HH, that's even another plus. 
    So my perfect letterbox would be all of the above:  a great hike, great views (hence, my trailname!), something interesting or new to discover, clever clues - but not too hard because I'm not as great a puzzler as some of the rest of you are, and I do want to be able to solve it! - and then finding the letterbox that contains a nice, dry, interesting logbook filled with stamps and notes (or not!  and being FF and snagging that Certificate!) and an interesting, original, carved stamp.  And finding that my markers or ink is all in good shape and I can get a good impression of the stamp to keep in my logbook ever after.  And contacting the placer to let them know of my wonderful day and to say thanks, and having them respond.  That's my perfect letterbox.
    -- Viewfinder

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2007-01-05 16:03:37 UTC
My dream letterbox is not one I find, but one I place. If I can pick
a great place that even some locals don't know about, carve a good
stamp, hide the only box at that site, then get tons of emails from
satisfied letterboxers, that is a better feeling for me than any box
I have found. The best example of this is my Nubble Light box in
Maine. It is a beautiful place that my family visited this last
summer and I could not believe no box had been placed there. I
carved a decent stamp (good for me) and placed my box last June, and
so far the log has been replaced twice already. Luckily a local
boxer has been kind enough to replace the logs for me and send the
old ones back. It is so satisfying to see all the stamps and
comments from visitors expressing their thanks for taking them to
this new place.

Silver Eagle

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-01-05 23:58:20 UTC-06:00
My dream box to find on the other hand...
 
I like a good hike, not too long, not too short - 2-4 miles is nice.  Long enough to get me out to some peace and quiet, somewhere with a view to enjoy - some that come to mind are Baby Bear's and Silver Eagle's boxes in Cedar Hill State Park, Rebel's Cameron Park Zoo #2 (in spite of the Lance Armstrong wannabe's that nearly ran over me at the top of a wildly careening trail), and The Frye Team's box, The Jig.
 
I like a box that has an unexpected image - I especially like Baby Bear's most recent boxes with that Far Side twist that allow a glimpse at his personality - Northern Exposure and Udder Madness come to mind.  I also like Delivery Girl's Chick boxes.  Quirky and unexpected.  I love humorous stamps!

A box that tells a story - TLB's A Family Affair I think is my all-time favorite in this respect. 
 
Outstanding carving is nice - Cactus Licker has done some of my faves, like Shawnee Trail (which I also like for its clever hide and the fact that it took me somewhere I probably never would've tarried at).
 
And a box that is wickedly difficult to solve - like Ruby Tuesday's Scarlet Pimpernel.  I cursed that box and yet was drawn back to it over and over again for six months, slowly whittling away the solution with the collaboration of much smarter PuddleSplasher and Gryzzled Gryphon.  That sense of accomplishment?  Priceless!  (and by the way, the key to a good puzzle box is not in making it ambiguous in the hunt, but making it ambiguous in the solution)
