Strange message from beyond

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2007-06-15 17:47:04 UTC
We received a strange (as in creepy) message through LbNA about one
of LSQ's Quilt Block letterboxes. This box was placed in Oakwood
Cemetery in Austin the first week in April. It was reported missing
only a few weeks after that by experienced letterboxers, so was
marked as missing. Here is the text of the message, sent by "the
ghost of oakwood (Kasper)":

Message:
I look after the graves of oakwood cemetery. Your letterbox was
found disturbing the spirits of the Blanton family. We are
currently looking for a good haunting. If you want the contents of
your letterbox contact Kasper the not so friendly ghost guardian of
Oakwood cemetery so we can make arrangements to trade my new
letterbox for a piece of your soul or something of equal value.

This person (spirit?) was able to contact us through LbNA, which
means they know how to find clues to other letterboxes. They
mention "my new letterbox". Do you think this is a new boxer with a
weird sense of humor? Has anyone received any messages like this?
What do you make of it?

Boots Tex

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-06-15 13:32:14 UTC-05:00

Eesh!

 

My first blush is scary.  Yikes!

 

My second blush is let's think it through.  What are the possibilities?

 

1)      literate, net-savvy ghosts who want some of your soul

2)      a muggle found your box and didn't much appreciate it being there, and is now wanting to return it to you for "some" reason.

3)      a muggle found your box and did a little research and would like to return it and get more information, and thought it would be funny to write such a message.

4)      a letterboxer found your box and is letting you know they have it, and are willing to return it to you and thought it would be funny to write it as a ghost.

5)      a caretaker found it and is offering it back to you.

6)      someone (anyone) found it and are just going to have a little fun with you by email but don't intend on returning it, only scolding you for placing it in a sacred place.

7)      someone (anyone) found your clues and are going to have a little fun with you since the box you lost is in a cemetery.

 

I think we can safely rule out #1 (last I checked, Oakwood wasn't a wi-fi hotspot).

 

Although I think it's funny that they wrote it from the perspective of a ghost and the humor potential there is great, I think if it were a letterboxer or a "friendly" person, they wouldn't have written "Kasper, the not so friendly ghost" and what they asked for would be something innocuous like a letterboxing patch, or a personal exchange.   Asking for a piece of your soul is a little gruesome and not Bible-beltish at all.  HOWEVER, you could just be dealing with someone who doesn't get the nuances of humor and thought it would be funny to make it ominous.

 

I also doubt it is a grieving family member, who would not as likely frame it as humor, more as "how dare you disrespect my dead great-uncle Blanton".  

 

And I also doubt it was a caretaker, because they would probably either write a formal "not on my turf" letter, or they would re-place it surreptitiously and keep an eye on it to make sure it doesn't cause a problem.

 

So that pretty much leaves someone who either has a bone to pick with you over what they may perceive as disrespectful placement (lots of folks don't like cemetery placements!) or someone just wants to jack with you as a joke.

 
It also seems more likely than not that the person in question has your box. 
 

I don't think it would hurt to email them back and ask them how they would like to go about exchanging the box.   You might get a little better insight.  However, I wouldn't ask them to mail it to me - at least not to my home address.  If you have a PO box, that might work.  If you happen to know anyone on staff at a cemetery it would be really funny to give them the cemetery's business address as the place to send it back to.

 

Just out of curiosity, did you have information in your box about how to find out more about letterboxing?   Do you think that's how they found the website or did they do research?  I've always been pretty gun-shy about putting "Learn more at www.letterboxing.org" information in my boxes because it could potentially lead a ne'er-do-well to a goldmine of fun and entertainment at our expense.

is too weird.
