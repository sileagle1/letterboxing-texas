We are the Key Note Sneakers of the Houston Area.  We learned about LB by doing some research that was prompted by a relative sending an email suggesting geocaching.  After reading about GC, we thought that

 


   1) the high tech element conflicted with the natural environment we hoped to spend more time in

 

Hmmmm… OK. I say maybe. At what point to you call something “high-tech”? At some point in the past a compass was high-tech. So was an “accurate” map. Original letterboxers way back in history didn’t have automobiles to carry them to the letterbox, it was done on foot or horseback. Clues were WOM or hand written, not compiled for you on a web site to be printed out on a computer. Today’s high-tech is tomorrow’s common tool. GPS was high-tech for Gulf War I. It is in many cars and even cell phones today. Likely your pizza delivery guy uses one to find addresses. Using a PDA for paperless caching/boxing – that is pretty high-tech too. I don’t own a PDA…. <grin> I print my clues…… using a high-tech laser printer.


   2) we didn't want to buy a GPS for a new hobby not knowing how long we'd be involved with it

 

THIS - Good call.

 

If I owned a GPS just for Geocaching, it would be a waste for me too. But I am an outdoorsman. I hike way more than I letterbox or geocache. (See localhikes.com, look for TX-El Paso) So for me the first GPS unit (a gift in 2001) was a high-tech toy. Now I use it in so many facets of my life that I own 3, and I almost always have one with me.

 

BTW – the GPS only does the same thing as the map and compass of letterboxing. It gets you close. That is all. Actually finding the item still is exactly the same in both cases. Ya gotta search.

 

I actually use GPS to help me letterbox. I look at all the clues on Google Earth. I try to get in as close as I can with Google Earth, then I waypoint that location and load it into the GPS. I then allow the GPS to pick the order in which we go to the letterboxes – because the GPS will auto-route the most efficient way from point to point. The GPS in the van can even keep you from exploring a street that looks good, but ends up in a dead-end.

 

END RESULT – more letterboxes per day while on the road.

 

I have more examples, but you get the idea.


   3) technology changes so fast that a GPS purchased today is obsolete in 6 months (we think)

 

NO! Absolutely NOT!

 

There have been very few “QUANTUM LEAPS” in GPS technology. Like anything – MP3 players for example – technology advances and things improve. They miniaturize too.

 

A few years ago a second generation GPS chip came out. It was more sensitive, allowing the unit to function better under tree cover AND extended battery life. That was a QUANTUM LEAP in GPS technology. Having a handheld unit with a color screen large enough to read maps, and battery life far longer than I can hike – that is great! Later units allow you to add memory sticks to hold more maps. But other than the new chip, the improvements have been small, and mostly what the consumers (like me) asked for.

 

But it doesn’t mean the older unit is obsolete. The new one just works better.

 

I have been through 6 GPS units. I still own #2, because it serves a purpose. #5 is the one I wish I had bought from the start. But like you, I was unsure and didn’t want to spend the $$$$. GPS #6 is in the car. And I wasn’t stupid about the one in the car. I did my research and paid the $$$$ up front, and got one that is very satisfactory. Amazing is a better word. It got me home an hour quicker yesterday by finding a better path around Fort Worth. It finds detours around traffic jams. Until you really use one, you just can’t see it.

 

BIG ONE – COST. You can shop on the E-place, and find the old E-Trex Yellow for $50. Don’t know about you, but I would rather get a GPS unit and skip the Starbucks! OTOH, you can be smarter than me. BORROW one from a friend for a while. Play with it. The look at it again, and see of you don’t want a better one.

 

The unit I recommend to most people goes for about $300 new.


   4) there is nothing worse than being out in the woods trying to do something with high tech gear and running into a dead battery or other tech glitch which ruins your outing (dewberry can vouch for this one (see below))

 

Well – I guess stuff can happen. You can fall on your compass and break it or drop your map in the stream and ruin it too. If you use an ink-jet printer do not let your clues get wet! Some GPS units have a 30 hour battery life. That is about 3 days worth of outings for me. If you can wear out a set of batteries in one outing I am in awe of you.

 

I don’t know about others, but I carry several battery items along when I go out. I also carry a few spare batteries. The one thing I do that most others do not is that I try to keep all items in one common battery type. Nearly everything I carry is AA-battery. Ham radio, digital camera, flashlight, GPS, laser pointer, rescue strobe , MP3 player – all AA-battery. I use MAHA-Powerex Mi-MH batteries, they last forever at 2700 mah.

 

I guess if you didn’t check your gear before leaving you could end up with some dead batteries. But even if you did, how hard would it be to stop in a 7-11 and get two more? I keep a box of 4 spare Alkaline AA batteries in just about every back pack I own.

So, after learning more about GC on Wikipedia and such, we stumbled upon LB which seemed to be a better match for us.  We read about it and within a couple days carved a stamp, went out for our first box and never looked back.

That was over 50 finds ago already (since Oct. 24th) which we are excited about (not quite 2 months).

 

One last note. MOST (but not all) of my letterboxes are co-located with a Geocache. Geocachers call them Letterbox-Hybrids. I don’t know that letterboxers have a name for them yet.

 

So there you go – my take on high-tech.

 

Merry Christmas.

 

Mike – El Paso.

down this road and can save us several headaches if we heed your advice.

you use to add LB coordinates to your GPS DB.

Systems that work well are worth their weight in gold. Systems that
HALF work are usually a WHOLE pain! So, if adding that information to
the GPS unit is tedious or time consuming, we'd probably be remorseful
having spent the money on the unit.

For example, we find that using Plucker to put clue data on our Palms
doesn't work well when trying to "sync" the data. However, it is
actually easier and more dependable to simply drag a couple files over
to an SD card and put the SD card into the Palm. This method doesn't
require a "sych" or a conduit to be properly configured.

Is the unit you use in your car removable for hand-held use also? I
know of people that have just mounted a hand-held unit (using a
ram-mount (ram-mount.com)) in their car. That way they can take it
out of the car whenever they need to.

We really like the idea of being able to plan an outing more
efficiently. Can you load different routes so that you only look at
one route at a time?

Surely many more questions will follow. Our biggest concern at the
moment is not wanting to part with the cash (but wanting to have the
unit).

END RESULT – more letterboxes per day while on the road.

But it doesn’t mean the older unit is obsolete.  The new one just works better.
