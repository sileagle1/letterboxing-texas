From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-01-22 22:57:05 UTC
Hi!

I think we have some of the most interesting people on this list.
Your artistic background sounds very interesting, and I can't wait to
see what you might come up with.

There are some other sites you might be interested in that would help
you learn about letterboxing. Silver Eagle has a file of links in the
section on the right, but here are some of my favorite:

www.atlasquest.com
www.letterboxing.info

and then of course the new boxers yahoo group, where newbies and
oldies from across the country meet to answer questions (any question)
about questions letterboxing-related. Questions are definitely
encouraged!

http://groups.yahoo.com/group/newboxers/

And, because you're a paper artist, you might want to learn more about
postal letterboxing, as I bet it would appeal to you. It's not really
letterboxing in the same sense as following clues and finding a box in
the wild, but it's a postal stamp art exchange, where people carve
stamps and share them with others, either singularly or as part of an
organized exchange ring. There are a number of others who do postal
letterboxing who are into bookbinding and altered books as well.
Here's the link:

http://groups.yahoo.com/group/postal_letterboxing/

Anyway, this is a friendly group and we're here if you have any
questions. Welcome!

Dewberry

Welcome. This is such a fun, family friendly sport/hobby. The get started kit should include your sig stamp, a log book to log your finds, an ink pad or markers for stamps, a compass (trust me on this one, even a cheap one), and a recently printed clues (don't hold on to clues, they could change).
 
Try to find a few before planting. It will give you lots of ideas of how to plant. If you can find some boxes by different planters, that's good too. It gives you a wider range of perspective (did I say that right?).
 
And, most importantly, HAVE FUN!

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-10-31 07:16:13 UTC-05:00
Hi Channing,
 
You will find lots of great advice on this list, but I would like to take a moment to put things in perspective for you!
 
You mentioned vandalism and that it doesn't discourage you - and for that I'm glad!  I'm not sure what exactly is "local" to you, and whether there is actually a lot of vandalism going on in your area; however, in the grand scheme of things what probably seems like a lot of vandalism when you read it on the boards is a very small percentage of the whole game.
 
What comes across as vandalism is often just plain bad luck, or sometimes bad planning, or sometimes lack of understanding, and sometimes it's nothing more than animals!  I guess I think of vandalism as someone willfully destroying a box and that actually happens very rarely. 
 
Sometimes a finder has not rehidden a box very well and it is discovered by non-boxers (muggles) and thrown away or removed.  Other times the finder might not reseal the box well enough and it gets wet inside.  And sometimes groups box together and the person who removed the box from its hiding place isn't the one to put it back, and the one who puts it back places it in the wrong place and the box was gone.  Sometimes the placer picks a place to hide a box without taking into account the "what might happen here", like the 100 year deluge that a lot of us lost boxes to this year!  Sometimes muggles stumble across boxes and don't know what they have, and they take the box away, or a ranger finds it and confiscates it because it wasn't supposed to be where it was (either in truth or based on his/her perception).  Sometimes someone handles a box while they have food on their hands and when they return it to its hiding spot, animals sniff out the food smell, steal the box and gnaw it and claw it into oblivion. 
 
On very rare occasions a not-so-nice muggle finds a box and they strew it about or destroy it, and maybe leave ugly messages in the logbook or write the placer a nasty email.  This happens much less often than any of the other scenarios - and thankfully so!
 
So anyway, yes vandalism does happen and it concerns each and every one of us because we know that it could as easily be our own box as the boxer it happened to.  But the vast majority of the time when a box is lost or destroyed, it could have and should have been prevented with a little care and thought on the part of letterboxers.  Sad that it happens that way, but thankfully it is something within our control....

 After Action Report - Hueco Tanks Letterbox #2.
This message is the second part of the continuing story of our search and recovery efforts for the Letterboxes known as Hueco Tanks #1 and Hueco Tanks #2.
 
To recap the previous operation, my wife (Dane Lady) and I applied for and were granted permission, and enlisted the help of a park guide to search for both Hueco Tanks #1 and Hueco Tanks #2 Letterboxes. We successfully recovered Hueco Tanks #1 at N 31 55.195 W 106 02.910. The letterbox was removed to be given to the park superintendent.
 
With the clues for Hueco Tanks #2 in hand, the guide and I did a whirlwind recon of most of Hueco Tanks North Mountain. Dane Lady did her best not to slow us down too much. We identified and did a fast scan of several promising areas. We did not recover Hueco Tanks #2 that day, but did identify and GPS-waypoint several areas to concentrate on. This operation all but did my wife in. She is a real trooper, but not up to that kind of strenuous recon activity. I on the other hand loved it!
 
After discussion through E-mail, the park superintendent reviewed the clues and I was granted permission to continue to seek recovery of Hueco Tanks #2 on my own, within the park rules of course. At that time I was also offered the opportunity to repair Hueco Tanks #1 and place it somewhere (To Be Determined later) in the park. This is great news. It may be difficult to find a good hiding place that will be approved. But one thing is certain. It will not happen without trying. My ranger-friend Danny Lee says it should work out. I am hopeful and will continue to press forward on this as time permits.
 
The original Hueco Tanks #1 stamp is not in good shape. The original logbook is ruined. The plastic container is crumbling. I intend to make a new stamp in the image of what I believe the original stamp was intended to be. I also intend to re-mount the original stamp and leave it in the Letterbox. And I have a good supply of ammo cans. One small one will be painted to match the approved location. Ammo cans last forever.
 
Everyone knows I am also a Geocacher. All my letterbox hides to date are Letterbox Hybrid Geocaches. I have decided to keep Hueco Tanks #1 a Letterbox exclusively. I am not comfortable having hundreds of Geocachers searching for the box. A few respectful Letterboxers will be enough. The park must be protected first and fore-most. I may request to place a Geocache out there in the future. But that is beyond the scope of this report.
 
The search for Hueco Tanks #2.
 
************************* Hueco Tanks #2 This box is Unclaimed. Placement date: Jul 11 2000 State: Texas County: El Paso Nearest city: El Paso Number of boxes: 1
 
Clues HUECO TANKS LETTERBOX #2
 
If one climbs the south side of North Mountain; follows one of the easy routes at the end of the paved road (all 5.4 to 5.6 stuff) they lead to a large amphitheatre that has a huge oak in it. The amphitheatre is accessible from all directions. In a hueco (natural hole-as is the area) 14 paces from the old oak is a box covered by a rock.
 
Note: access to this area requires some rock climbing.
 
Brooks / Devere, 12-28-2000
*************************
 
On Saturday, 24 June 2006, a second attempt was mounted to locate the Letterbox known as Hueco Tanks #2. This attempt was made with the help of my son James (Army Scout) who was home on leave from the Army. We hit the gate as early as was possible. After his orientation briefing, we were on the trail. My ranger-friend Danny Lee asked us to keep a watch for sign of some Bighorn Sheep that had moved into the park. We promised to do so.
 
To get to the locations I GPS-waypointed on the last mission required us to hike over North Mountain. Although the primary point of interest was lower on the south end of the mountain, the ground approach on the south side is closed, open only to guided hiking. We were unguided on this mission, so our approach was limited. The western approach is nearly a shear cliff. Not impossible, but certainly more dangerous. Crossing the mountain was the safest allowed approach. And so we did. I thoroughly enjoyed that hike. We were out early and only had to share the mountain with a pair of foxes, some prairie dogs, and an uncountable number of lizards. The sky was lightly overcast and the temperatures were cool. Cool for summer in El Paso anyway. The mission was ahead of us. We were optimistic. We had proper gear and equipment. We were on the hunt. Two warriors headed out to do what they do best. Life doesn't get better than this! I was loving it. W.E.T.S.U. <grin>
 
While we were hiking across the mountain we discovered several places with sheep droppings. I GPS-waypointed and photographed the first few. The first location was at N 31 55.340 W 106 02.783. But soon it was obvious that the droppings were not difficult to find. So I stopped marking and photographing.
 
James and I arrived at the first and most promising point for Hueco Tanks #2 within an hour of hiking. It is a 30 foot deep steep-walled hole with a large oak tree on the south west end of the mountain. It is located at N
31 55.242 W 106 02.860. We set out a safety rope, and James went down. I lowered our gear to James, then followed. James - being a Cavalry scout, always seems to take point. As I was climbing down he remarked that this secluded hole was full of sheep droppings. I got to the bottom and we began our search.
 
For about 1.5 hours we checked every possible hole, crack and crevice. We found two very promising places that fit the clues perfectly and had rocks covering the holes in the east wall north of the tree. One hole was empty. The second had broken glass in it. Neither had anything resembling a letterbox inside. In the process of searching for the letterbox we filled two shopping bags with trash. After exhausting every possibility, I climbed out, pulled our gear up, and threw the rope down for James to get out. I enjoyed every minute in that spot. But I was disappointed we came out without the letterbox.
 
The search of the other areas went about like the first. None was as promising as the first. Some places required us to climb in and out carefully. In order to search many of the likely places we needed to carefully look behind the plant life. Thorns and scratches were the rule of the day. At one point we had to remove our gear and crawl through a series of cracks and crevices with the gear tied to our feet dragging behind to get to the spot. Each time we went in hopeful, but came out empty. After nearly 6 hours on this mission we had exhausted every possibility. I was not ready to give up. But there was nothing left to do. We crawled out and shouldered up our gear to start back. About 200 yards into the return trip we realized we had forgotten about 3 bags full of trash. So we returned to collect up the bags.
 
On the way back, I took James to see some of the rock paintings. If you visit Hueco Tanks State Park - be sure to see these paintings. They are located on North Mountain at N 31 55.422 W 106 02.796. The coordinates will put you at the base of a rock face to your south. Crawl in the crevice at your feet and move west to the large room. As your eyes adjust to the dim light, you will see some of the finest Indian paintings in the park. Don't forget your camera.
 
The return trip was uneventful, almost somber. We had failed. Failure is not a word we accept easily. On the other hand, I had a GREAT outing with my son. He has grown into a man. The kind of man I was used to working with when I was a hard-charging Sergeant so long ago. The kind of man you want to have by your side when everything else around you is going wrong. Cavalry Scout. Sniper. Paramedic. He can use his hands as weapons, and his weapons as though they were his hands. His hands can heal or kill with equal skill. Who wouldn't be proud to stand on line with a warrior like this?
 
By the time you read this - and sadly for me - he will be gone. But some of you will have the opportunity to meet him at Fort Hood. He will be doing some letterboxing, scouting out the good ones for me when we visit him in late September. I hope you-all will welcome him to your part of the state.
 
This concludes the report on Hueco Tanks #2 Letterbox. I will continue to work with the park staff to rebuild and replace Hueco Tanks #1 in a location worthy of letterboxing, but fully acceptable to the park staff. This will not happen quickly. Perhaps by Christmas.
 
I would like to once again point out that not a word of this report would have been possible without the cooperation of the staff at Hueco Tanks state Park. Thank you Rangers! And it is a shining example of why I believe having an open dialog with the park staff about your letterbox is better than placing a letterbox secretly and hoping the rangers do not find it.
 
I also thank Dane Lady and Army scout for participating is parts of this adventure. Having them along made it all the more enjoyable.
 
Last - I will send a final report when Hueco Tanks #1 is repaired and replaced. This has been a great adventure for me. I would also like to thank Silver Eagle for asking me to take on this mission. I wish the 50-% successful report I bring to the list was a 100% successful report. I can only say we did our best.

Select your connection speed and turn on your sound. Sit back and enjoy. The photos do not do the park justice. Still - what a great adventure!
