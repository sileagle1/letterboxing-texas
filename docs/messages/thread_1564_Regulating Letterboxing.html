> I think that's a hard thing to prove, that we

****Well first of all, I said "unduly" regulated. The
problem with poor regulations is that they are often
written by people with clean hands - folks who don't
understand what they are regulating and who don't see
the bigger picture themselves. I spent several years
as the CFO of a state-funded (NOT state-operated, just
state-funded) mental health facility. Every
legislative year I would spend the majority of my time
in Austin trying to protect our funding so we could do
what needed to be done in our community. I saw it
happen over and over again, that someone who had no
idea what the needs of mentally ill or retarded people
were would set themselves up to dictate through legal
code and funding how it should be done. Regulatory
type folks sometimes like to think that if they make
enough rules everything will be all right, when quite
often the reverse is true - create minimal regulation
to address broad issues and it will be enough. I
think Mike's idea to make friends with people like
rangers and get them to see the value of having us
around is a valid approach - win them over and they
will lobby with us instead of against us when the time
comes.

I suppose we could refer

> stress the Leave No Trace guidelines, tell them to
> look at some of the smaller area lists (NOT the big
> one for pete's sake or they'll think we''re all a

> concerned with not harming the surrounding area. We
> could invite them to gatherings so they could meet
> some of the people involved in boxing. Though I
> suppose that might not be practical....them coming I
> mean.
*****While I can sure see that letting them know us en
masse could be a very good approach, I fear that they

in the whole bunch - or does the fruit loop gravitate
to them? How does that always seem to happen? LOL

*****Exactly.  To an outsider, we look like
litterbugs! 
 
As a matter of fact, I had a meeting with the Site Superintendent out at Hueco Tanks today.  At first, it WAS looked upon as litter.  Somebody left litter in their park!
 
Fortunately - the head ranger is a good friend of mine from a previous park.  He spoke up, and defended Geocaching and by the fact that Letterboxing is much the same - letterboxing was defended too.
 
So as I stated in a previous message - what the rangers go along with is rarely reversed by the boss.  And so it went today.
 
I gave the boss a short presentation about Geocaching and Letterboxing.  I then notified her that there were two abandoned letterboxes on site.  It was my intention to recover them and either move them to good (read SAFE) areas, or remove them completely.  I produced the clues.  She, I , and the head ranger studied the clues.  It appears that both letterboxes are now in restricted access areas.  The park was too busy today for a ranger to personally guide me in those areas for locating the boxes.  But the Superintendent promised to work with me as soon as things returned to normal.  I (and my wife) was put through the orientation video and given a registration card.  I was asked for and provided personal contact information.  And I was promised that arrangements for me to come back and complete this task would be worked out.  I was to have a ranger assigned to work with me in completing this task.  I am looking forward to it.
 
I believe that had I not had a previous friendship struck up with the head ranger there, it might not have gone as well.  There are over 3500 Indian pictures on the rocks out there.  They are VERY serious about protecting them.
 
If a few people would like to E-mail the superintendent a note of thanks - I can provide her E-mail address.
 
I will say this again.  Proactively working with the rangers is far better than waiting for them to discover letterboxing accidentally.  But in the end - you must do what you think is right for your area.
 
This is how my Sunday went!
 
Mike.  El Paso, TX.
