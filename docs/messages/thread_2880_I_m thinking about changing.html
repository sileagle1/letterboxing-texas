I'm thinking about changing

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2007-06-25 13:09:53 UTC-05:00

I'm thinking about changing my trail name. Puddle Splasher suits me too well right now and I'm not sure that's a good thing. I live within a mile of a nature trail in McKinney with a letterbox which I'd not found as yet. This morning, I was restless and decided that although we had already had a pretty good rain, the sun was shining and the radar indicated the next storm was south of Dallas, at least an hour or so away. So I grabbed my backpack and headed out to find Wolf Pack 4. A short distance up the trail, I passed a young mother with three small children covered with mud and carrying bug nets. She told me that she hadn't realized until it was too late that the trail ahead was submerged under a huge mud puddle. I thanked her for the advice but continued.... just in case my perspective was different. Soon I came to the puddle and she was definitely right, and there was no way around it. I could see the tracks her kids had left in the mud as they obviously went sliding down the slippery incline into the mud puddle. So I turned around but was not daunted. I knew another way to get around to the trail I needed to take - much longer, but at least I didn't have to give up just yet. It was a beautiful morning... birds singing, a soft breeze blowing through the leaves, peaceful, and I had the trail all to myself.
 
Finally I reached the trail head from the far side. As I started up the nature trail I found quite a few muddy puddles, but I was able to bypass all of them by leaving the trail and finding my way through the woods until I could rejoin the trail in a dryer place. Following the clues, eventually I arrived at the right location and found the letterbox. The woods was full of huge, fallen trees from all the recent storms we've had roll through the area, so I found one nearby to sit and stamp into the box. The problem was, when I opened the box, it was soaked. Inside was a sheet of paper from a family from Houston who had stamped in last week and said the logbook was wet and slimy so they used a sheet of paper instead.... and that was soaked. So I looked in my backpack and found an extra logbook I carry for such occasions and a new baggie to put it in and was just about to stamp it when a huge, monstrous, evil, loud clap of thunder exploded just over my head. As I looked up, I saw a streak of lightning immediately followed by another clap of thunder. I didn't have to think too long and hard to know I was a long way from my jeep and in a pretty dangerous situation.
 
Needless to say, I was soaked through and through by the time I got back to the jeep.... heart pounding, and still clutching the Wolf Pack 4 letterbox in my hands. So Wolf Pack4, I've got your box, I'm going to try to dry the logbook, but it's really pretty soaked. I'll put a new logbook in it and replace the bags and box (it was leaking too). The stamp is fine, but I think the ink pad looked like the ink may have bled out of it. I'll replace your box to it's nice little hidy hole  within the next day or two... after the storms really do pass through!
 
Advice to anyone thinking about finding this box... just wait a few days until the trails dry out, but it's a beautiful area so I definitely recommend you add it to your list of boxes to find in the McKinney area.
 
And me... I'll keep the name Puddle Splasher. In fact my plan is to dry my hair, treat the wounds, and go back out this afternoon because I still have a couple of boxes I want to hide!
 
Puddle Splasher

From: Silent Doug (silentdoug@letterboxing.info) | Date: 2007-06-26 15:41:00 UTC-04:00
At 02:09 PM 6/25/2007, you wrote:
>Finally I reached the trail head from the far side. As I started up
>the nature trail I found quite a few muddy puddles, but I was able
>to bypass all of them by leaving the trail and finding my way
>through the woods until I could rejoin the trail in a dryer place.

According to the Leave No Trace rules of outdoors ethics, you're
supposed to wear waterproof (not water-resistant) hiking boots and
walk through the mud puddles, not around them. Walking around the
puddles contributes to trail erosion, widens existing trails,
possibly enlarges puddles, and/or contributes to the creation of social trails.

http://www.lnt.org/programs/lnt7/index.html

> I'm thinking about changing my trail name. Puddle Splasher suits me
too well right now and I'm not sure that's a good thing. I live
within a mile of a nature trail in McKinney with a letterbox which
I'd not found as yet. This morning, I was restless and decided that
although we had already had a pretty good rain, the sun was shining
and the radar indicated the next storm was south of Dallas, at least
an hour or so away. So I grabbed my backpack and headed out to find
Wolf Pack 4. A short distance up the trail, I passed a young mother
with three small children covered with mud and carrying bug nets. She
told me that she hadn't realized until it was too late that the trail
ahead was submerged under a huge mud puddle. I thanked her for the
advice but continued.... just in case my perspective was different.
Soon I came to the puddle and she was definitely right, and there was
no way around it. I could see the tracks her kids had left in the mud
as they obviously went sliding down the slippery incline into the mud
puddle. So I turned around but was not daunted. I knew another way to
get around to the trail I needed to take - much longer, but at least
I didn't have to give up just yet. It was a beautiful morning...
birds singing, a soft breeze blowing through the leaves, peaceful,
and I had the trail all to myself.
>
> Finally I reached the trail head from the far side. As I started up
the nature trail I found quite a few muddy puddles, but I was able to
bypass all of them by leaving the trail and finding my way through
the woods until I could rejoin the trail in a dryer place. Following
the clues, eventually I arrived at the right location and found the
letterbox. The woods was full of huge, fallen trees from all the
recent storms we've had roll through the area, so I found one nearby
to sit and stamp into the box. The problem was, when I opened the
box, it was soaked. Inside was a sheet of paper from a family from
Houston who had stamped in last week and said the logbook was wet and
slimy so they used a sheet of paper instead.... and that was soaked.
So I looked in my backpack and found an extra logbook I carry for
such occasions and a new baggie to put it in and was just about to
stamp it when a huge, monstrous, evil, loud clap of thunder exploded
just over my head. As I looked up, I saw a streak of lightning
immediately followed by another clap of thunder. I didn't have to
think too long and hard to know I was a long way from my jeep and in
a pretty dangerous situation.
>
> Needless to say, I was soaked through and through by the time I got
back to the jeep.... heart pounding, and still clutching the Wolf
Pack 4 letterbox in my hands. So Wolf Pack4, I've got your box, I'm
going to try to dry the logbook, but it's really pretty soaked. I'll
put a new logbook in it and replace the bags and box (it was leaking
too). The stamp is fine, but I think the ink pad looked like the ink
may have bled out of it. I'll replace your box to it's nice little
hidy hole within the next day or two... after the storms really do
pass through!
>
> Advice to anyone thinking about finding this box... just wait a few
days until the trails dry out, but it's a beautiful area so I
definitely recommend you add it to your list of boxes to find in the
McKinney area.
>
> And me... I'll keep the name Puddle Splasher. In fact my plan is to
dry my hair, treat the wounds, and go back out this afternoon because
I still have a couple of boxes I want to hide!
>
> Puddle Splasher
>
