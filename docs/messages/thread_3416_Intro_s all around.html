Intro's around are probably a good idea, especially since there are now so many interconnections.
 
I'm BarefootLucy and I also live in East Texas not too far from Blue Butterfly (in Texas "not too far" terms).  My son, Lightning, is 12 and has been my #1 boxing partner-in-crime for the 4.5 years we've been at it.  My husband, Fearless Fox, is an LSS (long-suffering spouse) and a great sport about letterboxing, even if not an avid participant (which, by the way, is pretty much the definition of LSS).
 
My daughter, SJZ, is 20 and lives in Denton.  She's not terribly active, but you'll see her stamp around if you are in the right areas, and you may well run across some of her HH's - we did a bug series that has been floating around very actively for about 3 years.  Her b/f, RXKaffee, doesn't understand us but goes with us on occasion, so if your box has a RXKaffee image, consider yourself lucky!  Their son, Jonah, is 7 months old, and Gryzzled Gryphon suggested the trail name, Trail Whale, which I thought was highly appropriate but I haven't yet gotten a thumbs up from his parents (and if they don't decide soon, we're gonna roll with it - I do, after all, have his sig stamp!).  He, and maybe his mom, will likely attend the Denton gathering with me.
 
Nac Trail Cat, from Nacogdoches, is my mom.  She has been letterboxing for a year-ish.  Her husband was known as Mr. Nac Trail Cat until the Bastrop gathering we had recently, when I had devised a plan to have him trail-named by the group.  He quickly came up with Cat Nac. 
And lastly, my sister-in-law lives in the Corsicana area (and her daughter takes/used to take band from Lucy Locket - I'm sure which at this exact moment).  She occasionally goes boxing and she uses the trailname TX Flutterby.  You might run into her stamp along the I-45 corridor.
 
Of these folks, only Nac Trail Cat is likely to post her own intro.
--
Barefoot Lucy

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2008-03-21 17:00:40 UTC-05:00
I'd suggest you back your stamp with whatever is economical for you. Many stamps are not backed at all and they do just fine. I believe I found over a hundred boxes before I ever dared to place my first box, and I doubt if many of them were backed at all. Don't be in a hurry to start planting boxes. There are so many boxes in your area of Texas now. Just enjoy finding them for awhile; that's probably the best way for you to see what you think works or doesn't work in your region. Keep reading the posts, keep finding boxes and just have fun!
Puddle Splasher

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-03-21 17:51:47 UTC-05:00
Looks to me like you have it down just fine!  I'm in the East Texas area too - Cherokee County. 
 
I've been letterboxing for four and a half years and one thing I have noticed is if folks take it slow getting started, they usually last a lot longer too.  There are plenty of people on this list that prove that statement wrong, but my thinking is that for every one of them that got off to a fast start and are still here, there are at least ten who started fast too and dropped out.  Perhaps you could say the quick starters who are still with us are exceptional...;^)  Big thing is - don't worry about how fast or how slow you start out.  The great thing about letterboxing is there are no first, second and third place trophies - everyone wins if they just get out and go boxing.  If you letterbox and you enjoy it, you have accomplished the whole sum purpose of the game.
 
One thing I WOULD suggest though, before you plant very many boxes, is that you find bunches first.  As you go find them, take note of how they were placed, what sort of container they were in, how the pieces of the box were packaged, what kind of logbook did they use.  Also take notice of the clues and how they were written - were they clear or did they seem vague to you?  Did you have fun following them or were they boring?  And as you think about all these things, think also about what you liked and disliked about a particular box (now this information is for your own use and it is probably best not shared) and what elements you would like to include in a box. 
 
A case in point would be one of my own early boxes.  I can't remember which one, but I went and checked on it recently after a very long time.  It was in a disposable container, which I've since decided isn't the best sort of container for long-term use - they get brittle, The baggies inside were thin and easily ripped - I now prefer freezer bags because I've seen how much better they last in other peoples' boxes.  I used an index card notebook for the logbook - which I learned I don't like because the spiral rusts and folks feel like they should stamp on the lined side as well as the unlined to conserve space in my logbook for me, and it takes away from their stamps.  I still use index cards but I get unlined ones, or I use cardstock, and I put it on a binder loop.  So you see, if your first box to find had been my box, and it was the example you were using to make your box, you would be following a very poor example.  (I really cringed when I saw that box!)  Anyway, find quite a few boxes and boxes by several different placers.
 
I wouldn't worry too much with all the debates - there are a hundred of them!  Mount/Don't Mount, Hand-Carved/Storebought/Custom Made, Exacto/Gouges, Journey/Stamp, AQ/LBNA.  That's just a few!  What most of those debates come down to is personal preference. It really is that simple.  What works for you?  For the record, I'm a Don't Mount/Hand-Carved/Exacto/Journey/LBNA.
 
If you practice the Golden Rule and rehide well, and over time leave us some boxes to find that take us to places that you are fond of, you will be greatly appreciated in our community!
--
Barefoot Lucy
"It's not about footwear, it's about philosophy"

I agree with PuddleSplasher and Barefoot Lucy's advice to find as many boxes as you can before you begin to plant your own, because that's the only way you'll really discover all the wide range of possibilities!  It's so exciting to plant that first box and hear from finders, but after investing time in carving your stamp and creating your logbook and all the rest of it, it's really disappointing if the box goes missing quickly because of something about the planting you'd not anticipated, such as poor coverage when the wind blows the leaves away or being washed away by rising water when the location looked perfectly dry at the time, or having the box become trash as a result of over-exuberant landscape workers -- all things that can't always be helped, but with experience, you can begin to anticipate some of these things in your choice of planting locations.  Also, finding boxes by lots of different planters will expose you to a wide variety of styles of stamps and logbooks and containers for the letterbox, as well as locations and clues.  You'll definitely be a better planter after finding more boxes! 
Happy letterboxing,
