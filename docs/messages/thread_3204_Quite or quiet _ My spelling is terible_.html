Hi Chrispy!
 
Viewfinder eloquently expressed some of what I've been feeling as well!
 
Let me assure you that for my part (VF will speak for herself, as perhaps others will as well), I know full well that there are some great newbies coming into the game and it is a pleasure and an honor to get to know those of you who "get" what we do and want to be a part of it.  As with anything, the worst of the group somehow manage to get the most airplay and some of those folks really do get me down.  I could write about what it is about those folks that bugs the snot out of me, but instead I could mention what attributes makes me eager to meet new boxers...
 
If you:
1) Are eager to learn more about how letterboxing is and has been before you start trying to change it.
2) Enjoy the journey AND appreciate the treasure at the end of the journey.
3) Enjoy letterboxing enough to ensure that the next finder will enjoy themselves as much as you did by rehiding and resealing well.
4) Want to find a way to help take the game to a higher level instead of dumbing it down.
5) Want to learn to do it right, which doesn't always translate to doing it fast.
6) Empathize with how other letterboxers would feel if their efforts were not appreciated.
7) Would rather not ask for help finding/solving, but if you find you need to, you will ask for a hint, not for the whole answer.
8) Have a can-do, not a why-don't-you, attitude,
 
If you are most of these, you are the kind of new letterboxer that encourages me in the game and keeps me going when I get discouraged about the direction things are going.  I'm not the sort of person that hates change, but I do like to see traditions and practices kept when they make sense.  But I'm also all for trying something new if it keeps the spirit of the game.
 
As an interesting sidenote, I think what we see is that if someone doesn't have most of the above attributes, they really don't stay around very long anyway, because letterboxing is just not well-suited for the weak-minded.
 
Take care!

Animals certainly are the culprit with some, but usually those boxes appear to be chewed or clawed badly, and often they are dragged away.  It's when you find a box that has baggies poorly resealed, or when the container is left ajar, or when it is not rehidden well, or in the case of Viewfinder's box, finders had been through just a few short hours before she happened upon her box open and exposed, when it is evident carelessness.  Carelessness has an appearance of "we sort of tried", whereas animals aren't in the least bit subtle and they leave the box contents in chaos.  Obviously these are general thoughts and don't apply universally - the exception proves the rule!
 
As another note about animals, often they will ignore our boxes unless they smell like something they might like to check out.  That's why, as tempting as it is, it's not a good idea to bring lunch and eat it on the trail while you are letterboxing.  Baby wipes are also a big no-no for cleaning stamps!  Anything that has any sort of an odor to it might be animal-appealing.  We try to remember to rub our hands in the local dirt when we go letterboxing so our hands smell like the local surroundings.
