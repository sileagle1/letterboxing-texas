I was just thinking about how letterboxing has changed in the nearly seven years that we have been involved.. This week, The Hoppers found my box called Capt. Davis Boots at Fort Boggy State Park. This box was placed in May 2011 at a nice little park maybe a mile from Interstate 45 and yet they were first finders. Not a mystery, not a long hike. The only problem with finding it may be the fact that some of the landmarks used in the clues were overgrown from lack of use and the recent rains. I can remember when a box, any box, placed under those circumstances probably wouldn't go a week without someone finding it, even if they had to drive all the way from Evant, or Johnson County, or even Coryell County, or wherever. (not picking on anyone, just saying) Why do you think that is? I know we don't get out as much as we used to, and that there are so many boxes available that you can't get them all, so maybe that explains it, but I wondered what your thoughts are?

With the economy the way it is and the current price of gasoline, I find that I am a lot more circumspect about running out after every new box.  Now I wait to see if maybe another box or two might pop up in the same general area or if I happen to be going that way for some other reason and can combine letterboxing into the trip.

Everyone who stays active long enough is going to reach a point where all the "easy" boxes have been found. The remaining ones are either tough puzzles, or in a part of town you are not particularly interesting in visiting, or something else.

I think that is part of the reason why events like TALE are growing in popularity - it brings out a whole crop of new boxes and there are a whole bunch of like-minded people to visit with as well.

It seems that there is consensus about how and why letterboxers interests change over time.  Over time the need to find every box diminishes and those who stay interested do so because their interest broadens into other aspects of the hobby.  Carving stamps, planting, LTCs, attending events etc.  But question from this thread hasn’t been discussed.  Where are the new boxers?

 

So in light of this discussion I’ve been thinking about a few thingss…

Most of the newer boxers I know are not on the yahoo group (either don’t know about it, don’t care for the format of the email group, or are just not comfortable posting)

There is very little activity on the Texas board on AQ – basically we just answer the random questions that come up

We have very few events compared to areas where boxing is more popular (and I suspect compared to a few years ago in TX)

 

I had the chance to attend an event in MA last weekend and I was impressed with the vibrancy of their letterboxing community.  The event had a great mix of old and new boxers, not to mention some incredibly creative series that everyone raved about.  It’s also pretty well known that letterboxing there is incredibly popular with plenty of new boxers to replace older boxers who slow down.  If you look at boxes in the area they tend to have dozens of finds.  There are also lots of events – people were talking about the various ones and who was hosting/attending etc. 

 

So as I ask myself what is different it comes down to community.  I think for a new boxer in Texas it is fairly difficult to get a sense of the Texas community. You pretty much have to go to TALE or you don’t get it.  Until you go, half of the discussions and references on this board don’t even make sense LOL! 

 

Texas should be able to support many more boxers and we could easily have a couple of events a year in each major area.  That would let people get to know each other and help with the driving and traveling issue.

 

So my thought is that we need more opportunities for letterboxers in Texas to meet and communicate.  We also need ways to include and welcome new boxers.    What do you think?

There are three major differences between letterboxing in New England and in Texas: summer climate, hiking trails, and geography. I could drive from the eastern boundary of Connecticut to the west boundary in the time it takes me to drive from the DFW area to Tyler! However in the six CT counties, there are more than 30 state forests filled with gorgeous and diverse hiking trails plus my favorite American trail, the Appalachian runs through the state as well. I could hike everyday in the summer and not even begin to cover all the trails within a two hour drive from my house (not to mention upstate NY which was 18 miles away). Many of the trails could be very challenging, yet many were easy - something for everyone. My biggest challenge was winter hiking when snow covered the trails - and if you've ever letterboxed in the snow, you know that finding the landmarks can be another adventure when there's a bunch of snow covering everythingl. I used to take a hand shovel with me to dig in the snow for a box! New Englanders HIKE! Maybe it's the long, harsh winters, but come springtime, everyone gets out on the trails which are beautifully maintained. I was hiking for several years up there before I'd ever heard of letterboxing, and once I started letterboxing, I was surprised by how many people didn't know about it. By the time I left Connecticut, I'd introduced my doctor, dentist, antique dealers, etc etc to letterboxing and some of them are still going strong. It's a lot harder in Texas to be motivated to leave an air conditioned home when it's 100+ degrees outside and go hike on a hot, parched trail. However, winter hiking? That's the ticket!
