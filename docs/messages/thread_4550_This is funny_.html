From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2010-05-15 10:06:23 UTC-06:00
I just got a log report on a Geocache I have hidden in the Franklin Mountains.
It is called The Lost Aztec Treasure. This cache is still reported to be the
toughest cache in El Paso. Not because it is so well hidden, but because the
hike is of epic proportions. There are 5 points to find, the last one being the
cache. A number of people have taken more than one outing to complete the
adventure.

BTW - as an explanation - the word MUGGLE is a word from the Harry Potter movies
- it refers to a person who is a member of the non-magical community, the
Muggles are simply ordinary human beings rather than witches and wizards. As
for the Geocaching meaning, it is a person who doesn't play or understand
Geocaching, and hence is likely to destroy or steal the cache.

The common cache clue theme is WOOD. All the clues are hand-printed signs
screwed into WOOD. The first one is at the trailhead to Mundy's Gap - screwed
into the back of the trailhead kiosk. It points you about 1.5 miles north
cross-country to the Aztec Cave trailhead kiosk. That one sends you up the
Aztec Cave trail. Here is where it gets tricky. So far the hiking has been
relatively easy, mostly the alluvial fan around the base of the mountains or
actual trails. http://www.uoregon.edu/~millerm/fan.html

Most searchers go to the EXACT location on the GPS and look around. And they
fail. Remember the WOOD theme? 200 feet straight below - inside the cave -
there is a wooden bench. Screwed to the bottom of the bench is the third clue.
You have to lay on your back and look at the bottom of the bench to read the
clues. Hey - did I mention the clues are not written in English? They are
written in the Aztec Alphabet. So on top of finding the clue, you have to
translate it. One team forgot to print the page with the alphabet translation,
so they worked each clue as a puzzle. And they got it!

Anyway - this gives you coords to clue #4. Number 4 is easy to find, it is a
bench right in the saddle of Mundy's Gap. But getting from Aztec Cave to
Mundy's Gap isn't an easy trek. After that hike, they deserve an easy find.
You have to lay on your back and look at the bottom of the bench again, but the
rest probably feels good.

Clue #4 sends you to West Cottonwood Spring. The cache is a good bit away from
and above the spring, hidden in a natural hole in the rock with more rock stuck
in to hide it. If you take a compass heading and start walking, it is a tough
hike. If you have a map or slow down to look a little, you find there are two
trails to take you from Mundy's Gap to W. Cottonwood Spring.

The final part - the ammo can is locked to a chain. It is a combination lock,
and the combination is engraved on the lock - in Aztec numbers. Yeah - you got
to decode the combination too. . There are nice cottonwood trees around
the spring, a nice place to sit and rest after the rugged hike.


You can see the cache listing here:
http://www.geocaching.com/seek/cache_details.aspx?wp=gcabd4

Just above the logs there are two photos. One of a young Parker proudly wearing
his Park Volunteer patch and patrolling the trails. The other photo is of the
Aztec Alphabet. Look it over. If you find it interesting - there is a line to
clock on the bottom for more logs - There are more logs. View Them All on One
Page.

This cache has only been found 24 times! This is the lowest number of visitors
to any of my caches. OTOH - the highest is 192.

Anyway - some amusing reading for a Saturday morning.
