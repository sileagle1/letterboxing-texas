I’ve always been interested in the whole LbNA/AQ split, but never knew that much about it.  I post my clues to LbNA out of respect to the boxers who use it ex-clue-sively, as a nod of reverence for the founders of boxing in TX and as a nod to our history here. I will continue to do that, and I don’t think it’s unreasonable to honor that unique part of local, as opposed to national, standards, however inconvenient you find it.  But I confess that I always found AQ to meet more of my needs, but rarely questioned whether they were “needs” or preferences, and thought I was humoring those who used LbNA, a bit patronizing in retrospect.  So I always kind of looked at LbNA as the red-haired stepchild, and like I was doing the old-timers a favor, out of respect and consideration.  That is, I did until I thought, for a brief moment, “If Baby Bear is posting all his clues on AQ, does that mean LbNA is going away?” and had a little panic attack.  That gave me pause; I wouldn’t have expected that.  I realized that most of the old, old history and boxes that are iffy, and many of the boxing coups I’ve had, have been finds on the old, “abandoned” boxes people are always complaining about on LbNA.  I realized I didn’t want, not really, AQ to have cornered the market, or for the whole thing to be mindless and foolproof, and that ferreting out old clues, wile PIA at times, is part of the set-up to the adventure.
Most of the time, I suspect I’m viewed on the AQ boards as somewhat old-fashioned and possibly even crabby.  Ahem. I think the hats & stars are a little silly, but there’s just no getting around the ease with which we can communicate, at least superficially, and with which we can share, sort and categorize searches and clues and that it fosters the exchange of ideas, even stupid ones. 
 
To me, hybrids, for example, don’t educate cachers, they just enjoy them as a subset of caching, and all the unnecessary permission-seeking they do has not helped our hobby one bit, but outed it to muggles and left the impression that there are no standards.  Though accustomed to rules and their enforcement in their own hobby, they enjoy the relative freedom they have letterboxing to break what minimal standards there are.  God forbid you say that, though.  Hybrids are listed on caching sites, with co-ordinates and locations approved by moderators.  These things completely disregard the most basic tenants of the hobby.  I say this not to start another war or even a discussion, but merely as an example of my “old-fashioned-ness,” because it helps illustrate where I’m going next.  This geocacher/letterboxer dichotomy illustrates perfectly what I’m talking about. So grit your teeth, Dusty, corral the voices, and hang in there.  My views on this are well-known, and I’d like to be able to discuss them freely here with the understanding that this is not a personal attack, just a discussion of values, on a letterboxing forum started to foster the hobby of letterboxing. I'm just discussing the standards of our hobby, or what I consider to be a lack of them.
The more I started posting about things the mattered to me, the more out-of-step I felt. I've been deleted once and moved to Dead Lemurs twice! -- my personal red badge of (dis)courage. I believe in keeping the location of boxes secret.  It aggravates the hell out of me that boxers are intentionally seeking out the input and permission of non-boxers before planting, that boxes are being placed in scrapbooking stores, etc., where we have to out ourselves or shop, and I’ve felt many times that oldtimers are dropping the ball when they don’t say something about these and other things.  It seems to me at times that by their silence they are encouraging the deviations that the newer boxers are starting without really thinking about their relative merits.  I kept thinking, if they understood a little about the history, things would make more sense to them. And it never once occurred to me that no one else gave a @#"* about this stuff; I just thought they were too polite to say anything!  THEN I ran across some of Wanda & Pete’s posts about letterboxing that make me seem like a poster child for the digital age! What a shocker! 
So while I’m moaning about hybrids and longing for the good old days of say, 5 years ago (!), ironically, many of my practices would utterly appall them.  They have 30,000 finds and they were boycotting events where hitchhikers weren't hidden properly in boxes, because that's how it's supposed to be DONE, and they despise AQ as a money-grubbing, well, I’ll post links at the bottom.  And here I couldn't care less about the little blighters! Ok.  So now I've been thinking about all the ways my own attitudes would have annoyed early boxers and it has been enlightening.  I haven't been around that long, so this is entirely based on my impressions.  Feel free to correct any misconceptions I have.

 

These attitudes have been gleaned from the earlier post I linked, and also from other poking around in the archives, etc.

 

Firstly, I've long held that it was best, not required, just best, to learn a little about carving before you planted your first box.  Many early boxers felt the stamp was beside the point, and bemoan the later tendency to equate the letterbox with the stamp and little else, that the carve has become the point.  They were just happy to have a box to find.  Driving awhile to find a box was not uncommon. So drive-bys just for the sake of finding a stamp would have been a little weird?  I'm assuming.  I support them, but for a different reason.  My best friend was in a wheelchair.  Had she still been alive when I started boxing, these would have been the only ones we could have done together.  I think about her all the time, and she has shaped my perspective on many things.  ANyway. I understand their point and the feelings behind it.

 

I have a strong aversion to store-bought stamps.  Many of the early boxers had no such feelings.  Eoghan's boxes come immediately to mind.

 

Many times, I've whined a little about stamping in, particularly multiple times on long series.  Early boxers loved the logbooks.  Boxes were few and far between and were visited by their owners.  When you stamped in, you took a few minutes to enjoy the logbook, looking to see who'd found your boxes and the comments they'd made.  People took the time to LEAVE comments, IN the logbook, not about the fabulous carve, per se, but about their experiences finding it.  This point was driven home to me when we found a box in Austin that had been found my many, many muggles, all of whom had added comments and then rehidden the logbook, instinctively realizing the essential qualities of the secret find.  It was awesome.  I'd love to post the photos we took, but I don't own the box, so you'll have to take my word for it that it was an intriguing find.

 

Technically, a letterbox had to have a logbook.

 

In a related concept, no one logged finds online, so you HAD to visit your box to see who'd been there. It was exciting not to know until you got there.

 

Non-traditional boxes are considered by many not to be a true part of letterboxing: drive-bys, LTC's, postals, etc.

 

Some old-timers don't even bother to log finds.  This of course puts them out of the running for any and all P- and F-count restricted boxes on AQ.

 

Some are actually hostile to the idea of online logging in the first place and prefer to keep track of things in their own, personal logbooks.  This no doubt is also part of the aversion to the AQ way of doing things.

 

Many believe strongly that "real" letterboxes must be unrestricted and freely available to everyone.

 

The idea of sitting around the dining room table at an "event," and collecting stamps wouldn't be considered letterboxing by many.

 

Hitchhikers were fun little treasures, and not PIA's.  Although I check the HHH close to home and take/leave HH's there, many more times I leave them in the boxes where I've found them, especially if they're (forgive me) "crappy carves."

 

I have often said that if I wanted to be a "hiker," I'd join a hiking club.  But I'm not, I'm a boxer, and for me, finding a box is the entire point, and the hike is just gravy.  With lots of boxes WOM, it seems that a long, rambling trek without having a clue whether or not the box was actually there, was more the norm earlier on, and gratitude for the entire experience was the focus.  Um, oops?

 

Some oldtimers feel that the launch of AQ put the focus on finds, rather than the experience.  They especially resent the fact that AQ is a money-making entity.  This also relates to the free and unrestricted access to boxes.  (This is a variation of the game I don't believe was a part of Dartmoor boxing thinking.  The stealth portion, however, has been assimilated as an essential component, but is rapidly disintegrating as more and more boxers have only the online experience boxing to get them started. Non-stealth boxing is fairly tolerated on the boards.  I would have at least fit in here with the long-timers on this one.)

 

There was an emphasis on the location of the box, rather than the snazziness of the logbook or thef skill of the carver.  Having known nothing else, I'm thrilled with the art I'm seeing now.  I'm not sure this is a bad trend.  It just seems we can afford to be a little discerning now.

 

I found Wanda & Pete's information about the hats and how it relates to the premium hats on AQ to be a very interesting bit of letterboxing historical trivia.  I can also see, under the circumstances and in consideration of their feelings for AQ, how it infuriates them.

 

Many of these oldtimer voices are without representation on the boards, especially the LbNA/AQ split, which is danced around exquisitely by anyone who brings it up.  Ryan is careful not to say anything negative about LbNA, for one thing, as well he should.

 

Some of my attitudes, I think, would have served me well had I started boxing in the beginning, like the stealth part, for sure, and my vocal belief that Ryan is not the first nor last word in letterboxing etiquette, but merely manages the site that he has created.  There is a difference.  But for the most part, I would not have fit in much, and that has given me pause.

 

I'm sure those of you who've been around can correct any misconceptions that I have and perhaps add a few as well, especially since many of my sources aren't local to us.  It was an interesting experience being the newbie side of things, in terms of boxing style and attitudes, as I read this older stuff.  It's also fun to look at photos from some of the early events, where there were comparatively few people in attendance!

Wow, some heavy conversation while I was out of town this weekend.  Before I respond, let me first applaud your desire to learn more about letterboxing history and what letterboxing meant to early boxers.  Even if you disagree with the views held by them, it helps to know where they are coming from and I urge everyone to seek them out at Events to discuss in person.  Unfortunately, many early boxers no longer participate so it is hard to pick their brain, but there are a few left and I have been fortunate enough to speak with some of them.  At the "Back To Our Roots" event in 2009 and "Live & Breathe XV" event just 2 months ago, some of my favorite memories were getting to know some of the legends of letterboxing like Der Mad Stamper, Jay Drew, and Brian of Team Green Dragon.  Their stories of the early days were quite entertaining and gave me a whole new respect for what they did to get this hobby started.  Another good source for information is the Interview Series by Mark Pepe.  He took the time to interview 11 early letterboxers and posted it on his website:  http://interviewseries.blogspot.com/


As a moderator and owner of this Yahoo Group I usually try to keep the peace and don't always post exactly what I'm feeling, so I have been guilty of "dropping the ball" by not complaining about things I don't like about the current state of letterboxing.  I have occasionally voiced my opinion about things (e.g. State Park permits) in the past, but unless you go back and read all the posts of this Group you won't know about them.  I don't post too many opinions any more because I don't want to bore people who have been around a long time who have heard it all before, but that means newer people never hear it.  I think this is why you don't hear other oldtimers speak out much any more, along with the fact that we have come to realize it does no good.  Most people are going to do what they want to do no matter what they hear, and I guess in some cases this is ok.


It is often mentioned that letterboxing has no rules and I guess that is true to an extent, but it does have a set of norms that early letterboxers were expected to follow (e.g. stealth, non-commercialization) and it is the flaunting of these norms that gets them upset.  As the hobby continues to grow and new people with creative ideas come along, the norms get stretched to the point that new norms are created that irk early boxers but seem perfectly fine to the new boxers.  I think this is the crux of the discord and to help older boxers get past this feeling that letterboxing is "going to hell", we need to look back at our own history.  When letterboxing was first "discovered" by America, those early boxers took norms that had been around for decades in England and stretched the hell out of them, probably pissing off British boxers in the process.  To the Brits, posting clues on an Internet site was sacrilege, but to us it seemed like a great idea whose time had come.  So, even though I bemoan some of the changes that are taking place in letterboxing, I have come to realize that it is evolving just like it did 15 years ago and that it is a different letterboxing world now.  I can either take my stamps and go home or accept it and continue letterboxing the way I want to.  However, I do agree that learning the early history of letterboxing is still important, and I will try to share what little knowledge I have.  It is hard to write down so maybe the best way is to just talk about it next time I see any of you interested at the next event.  In the mean time, you can see a little of my history in the document I wrote during my 5th anniversary of letterboxing and placed in the Files section of this List:  http://groups.yahoo.com/neo/groups/letterboxingtexas/files, SE-BB_lbanniversary5.doc.


Now for my confessions:


Even though I admit AQ is nice for some things (e.g. Event listings, box searches) I will never accept it because I disagree with Ryan's commercialization of letterboxing.  It also doesn't help that I don't like Ryan personally, either.


I don't like Government entities (like State Parks) interfering with my life or knowing too much about me, so I will never accept permits.


I don't like Geocaching because it negatively impacts letterboxing.  If they would just eliminate their need to take things from caches, I think we could all get along much better.  Letterboxing was first, so I think they need to change their rules, not us.


Even if I disagree with y'all on some things, I still enjoy being part of this group and I hope we all continue boxing together, even if we do it differently.


Silver Eagle
