From: Boots Tex (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2010-12-08 03:26:18 UTC
We've been gone for a week and just got back home this evening. I have been reading the postings on this board while we were gone, but haven't had much time to reply, so I thought I would expand just a little on my meeting with TPWD. I noticed that Adams Trail tried to permit a box in a SP but met with some frustration. I discussed the lack of consistency with Ranger Owen and we are attempting to come up with some standard procedures. At this time, most park managers should at least be aware of letterboxing, but since there are no procedures in place, each is pretty much on his own as to how to carry out the permitting process. We are having to use the geocaching policy and procedures at the moment, but apparently they lack consistency as well. We're trying to do something about that. We should be at least getting our own application form. I don't think anyone should expect our policy to differ much from the one in place for geocaching, but we should be recognized as a separate entity from geocaching, so over time, I think the park personnel will come to appreciate the differences in the two activities. We have to prove to them that we can police ourselves before we can discuss changes in the policy. We are also discussing the possibility of "grandfathering" letterboxes that were placed before the TPWD policies were put in place. No promises have been made, but Mr. Owen is considering the request. I would like to say that I appreciate the encouragement that I have received from Barefoot Lucy and Silver Eagle in my endeavor, in spite of Silver Eagle's opinion that we shouldn't have to permit letterboxes. There is no one whose opinion I value more than Silver Eagle's. I even agree with him that asking for forgiveness is sometimes better than asking for permission. I, too, believe that letterboxers are capable of policing ourselves and should be allowed to do so. However, I don't believe that will happen and I think that, if we work with them and continue to place our boxes responsibly and according to their rules, we will be better off in the long run and may, with our good example, be able to convince them that we are causing no harm to the parks. I don't want to talk negatively about our cousins, the geocachers, but they didn't set the best example and we are helping to pay for their mistakes. Okay, maybe I do feel some bitterness, but that does no good. I believe, from Mr. Owen's response to my appeal, that we will not only be welcome, but that we will be embraced in Texas State Parks. Until we have our own policy in place, I encourage you all to test the waters by placing boxes in State Parks, by the rules as interpreted by each park manager. I think, in this way, we can prove our right to exist in their parks.

Boots Tex

From: Boots Tex (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2010-12-09 19:38:11 UTC
I can present it to him as a suggestion. Maybe I'll ask if they already have park maps showing the protected and historic areas that we can use for this purpose. I'm sure I could get copies of those if they exist. I think, for the time being, if we ask the park manager, when we pick up the permit application form, if there are areas that are sensitive or protected that we should stay away from, they would tell us. I don't think there is any way we'll be able to avoid the permitting process completely. I have pointed out to Mr. Owen that some letterboxes have been in State Parks for years without causing any trouble and, in fact, without any park official even knowing about them. That tells me that they have been responsibly placed and he had to agree with me. All these little points add up and maybe as we go forward, we'll be able to take over more control of our boxes. They have stressed that they don't want to add more paperwork, so I will at least make the point that we would be willing to handle the paperwork ourselves. Let's think about how we could do that in a way that they would consider. Understand, now, that they don't want to give up the right of approval, and that they can't go out and check on your box on a moments notice when you drop it off at headquarters. They want to reserve the right to delegate that task to someone on a "when we get around to it" basis. I can understand their position. But, that leaves you, as the placer, hanging. You would have to leave the box as you place it and leave the application for their approval. If, when they get around to checking it out, it doesn't meet with their approval, you would either have to go back and move it or abandon it to their descretion (which they don't want, because then they'd have to pick it up and dispose of it). This is the dilemma that we're trying to resolve and so far, we haven't negotiated a solution. Does anyone have a plan that would satisfy both them and us?

Boots Tex

You are right - and I did say it wouldn't be easy. But it is one suggestion. I
do understand your point.

Unpermitted boxes? That would depend on each park. As said - if it is
well-hidden, and nothing brings it to their attention, it might go forever. If
it is placed poorly, or a ranger either finds it or sees a finder going to it,
or a patron complains, it may be gone right away.

OTOH, I have known of rangers who have actually registered, checked out the park
on-line, and then contacted placers and had them pick up the offending box.

I know of at least one instance where this has happened to a letterboxer too.

I have three state parks within 50 miles of my home. I help out in all three,
although as protected as it is, Hueco Tanks doesn't take a lot of work. There
are about 400 letterboxers on this list. Do you think there are 400 state parks
in Texas? Some of the more remote ones might have to do without a volunteer.

One suggestion. Find a POC for each park, work it out with a ranger in the park
ahead of time for a few hours of visit with that ranger on the day you intend to
visit that park. Perhaps that ranger might even have a known hiding place to
share with you. You could fill out most of the application and E-mail it, then
complete the final bits in person. I know - still not a perfect plan - but it
is the best idea I have had yet.

A secret - you are never going to make everyone happy. No matter what happens,
someone will not like it. You can blame the permitting on geocaching - it was
their exploding growth that caused this. But eventually it would have happened
with letterboxing too. It may still have been in the future.... or maybe not.
Truly - it doesn't matter now. It is what it is and it is something to be dealt
with.

I have quite a few thoughts on this issue, but at the moment I am drawn away by a personal matter that is distracting me away. I apologize for not speaking up sooner. I'm going to answer the question first, and then throw out some random disconnected thoughts. Each of you is free to agree or disagree with me, as sometimes I disagree with myself!

Let me just say - I am willing to go through the permit process, and have done so at Tyler State Park. I completely understand where the parks are coming from - they want to know what goes on in THEIR park so they can monitor activities and understand why things happen.

I am also willing to be a park liaison - a single point of contact a park can use to contact to facilitate for further communications and actions with letterboxers.

Now, random comments:

I don't think this IS the first TPWD has heard about letterboxing. They've known about us for quite some time - take Fairfield SP, Huntsville SP (in the past - not currently), and it seems like another park or two - have apparently pulled boxes as they have found them without the benefit of notification. We have found out when someone has gone to look for boxes in a particular park and all of them were gone. Some parks, like Mission Tejas, have known about us and have turned a blind eye to our activity. This inconsistency of policy application is what alarms me the most - Mission Tejas may like us today, but give 'em a new head ranger and they may go militant on getting rid of unpermitted boxes.

I believe that while Boots is working from a statewide level, each of us who wants to box in a SP needs to be ambassadors to our area parks - volunteer a little perhaps (as Mike has promoted strongly), be good stewards when we are there, and let them know who we are, even if to say "Hey, I'm a letterboxer and I would really like to place a box here because I enjoy this park so much, but it's just too darned complicated to place one". This is a big enough issue that we can NOT rely on a single method of attach to get the problem resolved to our satisfaction.

On a similar note, whether YOU intend to place a box in a state park or not, it IS a matter of concern for YOU - you being the global letterboxer. When YOU go to visit a state park to letterbox, YOU are on deck to be the latest contact the park has with letterboxing. Rehide well, rehide well, rehide well. Watch not to contribute to social trails, don't leave trash (in fact, pick up trash). Be especially conscientious about NOT outing boxes that are there while we are transitioning into the age of permitting.

IN ANY SITUATION, Don't ask for help from rangers or strangers. If you can't find it on your own, don't ask for help from non-letterboxers. This is about one of the stupidest things letterboxers do and regardless of the permitting situation, it's a good way to get a box to go missing.

Having permitted and unpermitted boxes in a single state park worries me too. I don't want to permit my box and draw attention to letterboxing, knowing full well that Silver Eagle or Baby Bear has a box there. Although I disagree with them on the permitting issue, I consider them to be conscientious objectors of sorts and respect them for their position. I don't have an answer for it, I just wanted to say it concerns me.

As long as we have the mentality of "More Is Better", I don't believe we have the ability to police ourselves. Even if we get them to give us a map of off-limits areas, stacking boxes in on top of each isn't good even in hardy areas.

Mike mentioned the idea of legacy boxes. That came from me. I happen to believe that not every box should stay in place forever. There are some, a few, boxes that should be cared for as long as possible, but I believe a responsible placer will consider whether a box should not be retired and removed to allow other boxers to place boxes and share with us of their creativity without creating a situation where there are too many boxes in too small a space.

And a personal note to Baby Bear and Silver Eagle, I realize some of this discussion touches a raw nerve for y'all and I hate that. Many, many of us - and not just on this list - consider you to be personally responsible for our getting a start in letterboxing and then sticking with it. I will do anything I can to protect your boxes because of the impact they - and each of y'all - has had on my family's enjoyment of the hobby.

I'm sure I have other thoughts, but I'll move on for the moment.

Silver Eagle, and I'm sure others, have issues besides the complicated permitting process with getting permits. However, it seems like the biggest hindrance for most of us has been the time involvement in getting permitted. Many times, we go to a destination and want to be able to leave a box behind, not come back again (and hopefully ONLY once more).

When I permitted three boxes at Tyler State Park about a year ago, they were well aware of geocaching, vaguely aware of letterboxing, and haphazard about it all. I was permitting two boxes that were already in place and wanted to place a third. They emailed me the permitting form, let me mark through it to make it more box-friendly (i.e. gave them the link to the clues rather than coords), and let me scan it in and email it back to them once I had checked on the two boxes and placed the third. I asked them if there was anywhere off-limits, they told me not to place the boxes anywhere dangerous (well heck - WHY NOT???). Clearly, they didn't consider themselves to be eco-sensitive or historically important, so pretty much anywhere was okay as long as someone wasn't going to get hurt. I emailed the permits back and never heard from them, so I got in touch with them to get a copy sent back to me and they were a little surprised I was concerned about whether they were approved - more like they just wanted the paperwork completed.

In retrospect, I think that one question was pretty telling - "Is there anywhere off-limits?" If they pull out the map and start pointing out areas that wouldn't be box-friendly, it's probably going to be more difficult to get permitted than in a park that is pretty hardy.

Can we see if we all have the same idea of a park liaison? This is what I would think would be "ideal" - but because of the time and distance factors it would probably be something less than this - so lets just say that if we could have everything we wanted in a park liaison, this is what it would be, in my mind:

The liaison would make an appointment to go out with the permit contact at the park and take a view of the park. Get a good view of where would be off-limits, where would be acceptable. Get an idea if they would like to see quantity limits placed. Find out if the park has other expectations from boxers. Give them an example of a box (perhaps this could be a courtesy box - a box placed at the counter at the park office that would be there for the asking?) This gives the permit contact an opportunity to get familiar with the liaison and to get some assurance that the liaison (can I call this the PL? I keep getting the "i"s out of order) Placers could call on the PL for help in placing a box, not necessarily in person, but in general to make sure the placer is going to propose a successful permit. The PL sort of walks the placer through the process. Later, if the park runs into a problem with a box (i.e. someone finds it and brings it into the office), they can contact the PL instead of going on to the website to track the box down and figure out which one it is, if it isn't obvious from the box itself for whatever reason. The PL can get in touch with the placer and let them know they've been contacted and coordinate a fix. Maybe the PL could even place a series in coordination with the park like Boots mentioned that they might be interested in having a letterbox challenge in some parks. And maybe even the PL could do an occasional letterboxing program for park visitors (we would secretly call this a gathering, but they could call it a program because they like to say they have programs for guests). If someone places a box without permitting (i.e. someone who isn't on this list and doesn't know about permitting), the PL could contact them and offer to help them get "legal" with their box.

This would truly be a volunteer position, and it COULD be rather time consuming, although I think once the PL was in place and there was a meeting of the minds with the park, it wouldn't be too bad. It has features of self-policing that could really be workable and would probably be welcomed by parks to take the burden off them for the whole permitting process. I think the biggest reason the permitting process is so cumbersome is that the central office folks wrote the policy and the park staff has to enforce it and they don't really understand it, so they make it more difficult than it has to be and they are hesitant to quickly approve and instead shuffle it to the bottom of the stack to handle when they get inspired to do it. If they had a trusted volunteer to help them through it, they would be more willing to hustle through the paperwork. This would be especially workable if they would be willing to handle things via email as Tyler was.

Anyway - just trying to brainstorm to help us get through this mess. Anyone who wants to run with this idea or change it or propose something different is welcome to do just that.
