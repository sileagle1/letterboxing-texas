Stamp Size

From: Puddle Splasher (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2009-04-26 14:27:11 UTC
I imagine we all agree with Granny Butterfly that we're grateful to find a letterbox, no matter what size the stamp, but I posed this discussion question because it's my nature to always want to tweek things to improve them when possible. And I wasn't clear - I was asking about stamps FOUND in letterboxes, not signature stamps - that's definitely none of my business! Perhaps it's not actually the size that confounds me as much as the large areas of uncarved medium. Try as I may, and careful as I may be, I just can't seem to get a good image of large stamps with lots of uncarved areas and it ends up being a "blob" in my logbook. Maybe I need to add a brayer to my backpack supplies so I can roll the back of the stamp and get a better image transfer into my logbook, but then we'd have to be super careful not to crack or damage large stamps. I try to ink the largest areas last on a stamp so they don't have time to dry out. I replaced my markers and stamp pads often, I make sure to use plenty of ink, I huff, I carefully place the stamp on my page with the logbook on a flat surface, I press evenly all over the area, paying special attention to large uncarved areas, and I still come up with missing areas.
Sooooo... just for the sake of discussion, assuming we all appreciate the effort all letterboxers make when placing a box, what would be the IDEAL size or style of stamp.
A few years ago, I found a stamp carved by Beneficus Rex which was mounted on a small wheel. It was too cool! Celticlions also had one like that at the Moose Meet. I do love an original idea!
Puddle Splasher


Re: Stamp Size

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2009-04-26 15:24:49 UTC
Can you carve an image on that brayer? That might be an idea. I guess you're talking about those stamps that are carved with a negative image, leaving a large area uncarved? I have at times used a marker and just put enough ink around the carved image to get an impression and then put pressure only on that area. Sometimes it works. I try to use only as much carving material as I need for my image, no matter what its size. That way, I can get more stamps out of a block of material. Sometimes I'll even use my irregular-shaped scraps for a stamp if I can make the image fit, then cut away the rest. You could always carry an xacto knife and .... just kidding!

Boots Tex
