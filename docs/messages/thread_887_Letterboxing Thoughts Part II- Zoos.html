From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-06-05 11:34:34 UTC-07:00
I have a box in a zoo. I didn't ask permission and
it's been awhile since I've been there, so I'm not
sure it's still there (it's one of the first couple we
planted and we've learned a lot since then). Looking
back, I should've asked permission and if it's still
there, I think I'll take it up, then ask permission
and replant it. I did a great stamp of the zoo's
mascot animal, then planted it within view of that
animal's enclosure. If I ask permission, I will do it
on the premise that it is an artistic nod to the zoo's
mascot, will be placed where there will be no
destruction, and that the clues are online and
encourage visitors to the zoo. I think the mascot
thing will be enough to make it fly because the mascot
isn't just an animal chosen by the zoo to represent
it, it has personal historical significance for the
zoo and its founders.

I also have a box at Tiger Creek Refuge, north of
Tyler. TCR, while not a zoo per se, is a wildlife
sanctuary that rescues big cats and cares for them, so
it is zoo-ey. I did ask permission to plant there.
When I told them about letterboxing and showed them my
awesome tiger stamp, and explained that I wanted to
plant it there because I believe in what they do and I
wanted to share that with other people by bringing
them to TCR through my box, and when I added that
LB'ers are by and large naturalists and
conservationists, they were enthusiastic. I visited
there a month or so ago and there were a fair number
of stamps in the logbook, several of whom noted that
if it hadn't been for the box they would never have
known about TCR. The director also told me that the
folks who had come out to look for my box were the
nicest group of folks they could ever hope to have as
visitors and that they seemed to really care about
learning about the TCR mission. It appears that
everyone wins by my having placed that box there.

Does that help?

Lucy
