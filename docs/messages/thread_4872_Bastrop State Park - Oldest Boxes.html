Re: Bastrop State Park - Oldest Boxes

From: sileagle1 (sileagle@windstream.net) | Date: 2011-09-09 12:56:47 UTC
Well I guess by some definitions it is the oldest active letterbox in TX and the Lost Pines LB is the oldest original active box. Just like a lot of things in letterboxing there can be several interpretations. For example, there is a debate about which is the first letterbox in America. Some claim Max Patch was first, others say Prayer Rock. But it gets even more complicated because the original Prayer Rock box was lost in a flood several years ago. Jay Drew re-carved the stamp like the original and replaced the box in a slightly different place, just like Mike did for Hueco Tanks box. So if you consider that box the same as the original, then you must do the same for Hueco Tanks box. Either way, the Hueco Tanks box is definitely worth finding and I thank Mike for replacing it. My family had a great time exploring that park after finding the box.

BTW, I had the privilege of finding the replaced Prayer Rock letterbox two years ago and even though it wasn't quite the same as finding the original, it was still quite a thrill.

Silver Eagle

Re: [letterboxingtexas] Re: Bastrop State Park - Oldest Boxes

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2011-09-09 08:59:17 UTC-05:00

I have long thought that we have certain legacy boxes that we as a community need to maintain for as long as possible. In my mind at least, these boxes are few and far between. They are boxes that are momentous like first box in the nation, first box in the state, or boxes that create a shift in the game, or significant boxes by pioneers of the game.

Unfortunately, the true first box in Texas (Duck Creek, which was really a series of 4) was in place very briefly (planted 09/28/98, first ones disappeared 01/07/99) and the placer of that series, Julie - also known as ex posto facto in the mail art world, has long since moved on. It was found by three of Julies friends, who have also moved along. These ladies were all artists first, who discovered letterboxing as a venue for their art. I believe, in reading the archives on the Big List and other related information, that they werent anticipating the risky lives that letterboxes lead and were heartbroken to see their art disappear so readily and so they moved on to other venues. It would be nice to be able count the Duck Creek series in what I have in my mind as a list of legacy boxes, but they were fleeting, as were the finders and the placer, so that they really are lost forever. As a side note, Ruby did briefly make contact with Julie and invited her to our second TALE, but she wasn't able to attend, and if I remember, Ruby's impression of meeting her was that although she found it curious that we still talked about her and her boxes, she was over letterboxing. That was a first foray into letterboxing in Texas and it would be awhile before it was tried again.


Hueco Tanks and Lost Pine are also legacy boxes in my little list. It's true that by letterboxing definitions, Mike's Hueco Tanks should be counted as a new box, but when you consider that the original box was the first box planted on the second venture into Texas letterboxing, I think maybe the legacy factor transcends definitions. After all, Mike's effort was to keep the fire going, so to speak - to maintain our history as best we can. Until Silver Eagle spoke up and compared Prayer Rock and Hueco Tanks, I was struggling with how to reconcile the whole idea of "firsts" and "news" with legacy boxes.


Lost Pines is important too - it was placed by Eoghan, who was the first letterboxer who really got established in Texas. Even though he eventually moved away and he has (if I remember correctly) moved on to other activities, he was one of our pioneers. If in the end it turns out that Eoghan's Lost Pine is lost to the fires, I would hope that "someone" will step up and keep that fire going too - I think it would be most appropriate if it were Silver Eagle or Baby Bear since they most closely represent the walking history of the game for those of us who letterbox in Texas today.


I wish I had really, really understood the significance of Lost Pine when I found it. I think I sort of had the idea, but I found it in the first year that I participated in letterboxing and really didn't have a grasp on the history of the game. When I have the opportunity to find Hueco Tanks, if I ever get to find Prayer Rock, and - if I ever get to letterbox on Dartmoor - I believe I will pause at times to consider how the box I have in my hands paved the way for letterboxing as it is today.


Really - for those of you who have never read of the early days of letterboxing, it is exciting, sometimes breathtaking, to read the writings of those early boxers in what we now call the Big List. There were some big challenges to overcome to get the game going in the United States! It's exciting to read the words of the early boxers in Texas too - when this list was begun, we could hardly stop talking about letterboxing and everything in all of our lives somehow reminded us of letterboxing in some fashion or another. If we ever get rain again, I suggest you spend a rainy afternoon in front of your computer screen, reading the archives of the Big List and of this list.


Y'all have a good day! Stay cool and - please - don't play with fire!
BarefootLucy
"Not about footwear, it's about philosophy"

Several years ago I walked some trails at Duck Creek (Garland, Texas) so see if it would be possible to plant a new letterbox in that area in honor of the first boxes placed there.
