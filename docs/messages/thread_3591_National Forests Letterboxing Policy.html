From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-05-30 14:13:16 UTC-05:00
That's always the confusion - there are several different agencies that govern federal land. 
 
The policy has always been that you can't box in National Parks, but they are operated by the National Parks Service (NPS) and they are truly agressive about enforcing that.
 
National Forests, on the other hand, are governed by USDA and they have never had a policy on stashing games until 2006.
 
I have two concerns with the policy:  1) every cache has to have exact GPS coordinates.  Many letterboxers don't have GPS's.  2) there is a fee.

LOL...sometimes it's hard to know who's ground you're walking on!  (Although as Don, of Don and Gwen fame, is always quick to point out - it's MY land!)
 
If the temporary special use permit is pretty much the same for every NF (I can't seem to find the one for our region, but I've looked at Rocky Montains' and Superior Forest's), this will also be a one time/one year only permit, so that we would have to pick our boxes up after one year and let them know we've retrieved it.  Hopefully they will review the box and allow us to re-place it if there isn't evidence of undue traffic near our box. 
 
I don't have so much of a problem with this particular requirement because it is intended to avoid social trails and wear on the land, and by making it a one year only thing it allows other boxers to get in and place their box for a year.  Thing is - they aren't only looking at boxes, but also at caches and any other stashing games that are out there, so we're going to be competing for space with geocachers as well as other letterboxers.  Some regions actually have a spacing requirement of 1/2 mile between stashes.  Eesh!
 
Depending on how this all plays out, the cooperative group we have in Texas is going to be our strength - we can place and rely on other boxers to help out when it's time to pick up.

Re: [letterboxingtexas] National Forests Letterboxing Policy

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-05-30 15:12:19 UTC-05:00
The state parks have their own form and I don't imagine it would be the same as the federal forms.  I have a state form somewhere - they're available online.  When I get off work, I'll hunt it down and put it out on the group homepage or email it to you directly.
 
The problem with State Parks (and we may wind up seeing a similar problem with National Forests) is that they have just started pushing the statewide enforcement of the policy, so the parks themselves aren't really up to speed on the policies and some park managers aren't really interested in enforcing it either, especially since they've been suffering from budget cuts the last several years and are on tight staffing and reduced hours of operation.  You can go to one SP and they will welcome you with open arms and never mention a form, and then go to another and get treated like the plague.  This particular problem makes me really nervous because they aren't being consistent - how are we to know when a new park manager comes along who hates stashing games and goes around confiscating all our boxes because the old park manager didn't care if we submitted permits requests or not?
 
I'm a little more leery of the NF's GPS rule because quite often the staff for a forest aren't as accessible as SP staff are (assuming you aren't there during the week at many state parks), so they may rely more heavily on that GPS reading.  Personally, GPS's have gotten cheap enough that I think it's not a bad investment for a boxer to have, but even so, it's a piece of equipment that you shouldn't have to buy just so you can comply with park rules.
 
This should all be very interesting as it unfolds, and with any luck it's a short-term push that will result in a little more trouble for us, but no horrible woes.

I think most letterboxers do something similar, but we have never tried to quantify it so we can demonstrate our benefit to the parks.  It isn't really asking any more of boxers than we are already doing, just wanting to get it on paper so we can show them. 
 
I have to confess that I often get too caught up in something else while I'm on the trail to take notice of trash.  It's usually on my trip out as I get tired and I'm not stimulated by other things that I start noticing trash on the ground and pick it up.  It always makes me wonder how much I've walked by already.
 
I went out to Fairchild State Forest this morning and found Baby Bear's A Good Day for my Wife.  I decided on my way out there that once my feet hit the ground I would pick up any trash I saw, and that I would focus on finding trash.  That forest isn't a hugely trafficked area, so you would expect not to find a lot of trash, and I didn't, but I did find enough to cover the bottom of a kitchen trash bag.  Since there was nothing notable amongst it, I tossed the bag into my trunk and figure I can grab the same bag the next time I'm out.
