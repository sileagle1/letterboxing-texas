Silver Eagle & Baby Bear Annual LB Trip Report - Part 1

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2007-04-03 14:40:48 UTC
Well, we finally got home late Sunday after 4 days of intense
letterboxing in East TX, and it has taken me this long to post
clues, log finds and type this trip report. Of course, spending
time with my neglected family had something to do with it, too.
As some of you know, Baby Bear and I try to make at least one
letterboxing trip each year and it has become sort of a tradition
ever since our first "seeding" trip back in January, 2003. On
that trip, the main objective was to place boxes and see the sights,
since there weren't many boxes placed at that time. Now our trips
are more about Finds, but we still place a few along the way.
We usually go in late Fall, but our 2006 trip kept getting delayed
until it was now or never, so just last week we got the green light
to go and hoped for good weather.

Since we have gone to the Dallas area the last 2 years, we decided
to go somewhere else this time, and East TX looked good to us.
Even though we had just been there for last year's Event, there
were many new boxes to find, plus, I wanted to place a box in
Sabine county, one of the few virgin ones left in that area. I
planned the trip way back in November of last year, assuming we
would go in early December. I plotted a route and printed all
the clues, but then we waited, and waited, and waited. About the
time we decided to go this weekend I saw BL was setting up a
non-gathering near where we would be. Alas, since I had already
plotted the route and made camping reservations, I knew we would
not be able to attend. While they were flirting with Green Shirt
Guy, we were prowling through cemeteries in the Jacksonville area.

We got an early start Thursday morning to beat the Houston traffic
(4:00 wake up for me) and headed up Hwy 59 toward Coldspring. The
first few boxes were on the Lone Star Trail and it was still dark,
so we put on our trusty headlamps and headed out. It was pretty
eerie walking in the woods with just a small beam of light and
all kinds of animal noises around us, but waiting for daylight was
not an option since we had a schedule to keep.

Now, a quick word about our trip philosophy. Even though it is
usually more enjoyable to take your time and find a few boxes in
a given area, our trips are the complete opposite. We find as
many boxes as we can, as fast as we can. We still take the time
to appreciate the area and perform the appropriate letterboxing
rituals, but stopping to smell the flowers too long is frowned upon.
The main reason we do this is because we so seldom get to letterbox
together that we want to take full advantage of it. We hate to
"Burn Daylight". Our trips are not for the average letterboxer
and I sometimes feel like I train for them as much as I used to
train for races. Running on trails is not unheard of.

Anyway, we continued boxing in the Coldspring area, finally getting
to the town itself. After finding one in town, we made a wrong
turn and at the outskirts of town we finally realized it. As we
were turning around, I noticed a big red building off the road that
said The Big Red Barn, and I remembered that Boots Tex and Lone
Star Quilter had a retreat nearby and figured this must be it. We
thought about stopping to say Hi, but there were no cars visible
and since it was a weekday I figured they were out letterboxing.
It would have been fun to see them, especially since it was their
boxes we were finding. Also, I have become a big fan of their
stamps. His Governor series is great!

We then headed up the west side of Lake Livingston where I placed
a new box in San Jacinto county for Boots & Quilter, and continued
around the Lake to Lake Livingston SP. After finding a few there
we headed east on Hwy 190 to the LPC Dogwood trail where we placed
boxes. There were only a few dogwoods blooming but they were
beautiful. After a stop at Coushata Reservation, we made it to
Martin Dies SP, our destination for our first night camping. The
place was deserted because the lake had been drained (no fishing),
and there was still considerable damage from Hurricane Rita. After
setting up the tent, we decided to try for the boxes hidden there
while it was still light. I did not have much hope of finding them
because of Rita, but we found all 4, though some had wet logs. It
was quite a challenge climbing over downed trees and looking for
landmarks mentioned in the clues that no longer existed, but we
did it. If you decide to go, here is a helpful clue for Wading
Bird: go 190 steps from the big bridge, then look for the large
tree with no top. Since I don't know if Droop still participates,
she might not change her clues, so this should help. Ironically,
the only box I could not find was my own. It was getting dark
so I might have missed it, but everything looked so different it
will be a challenge to ever find it, if it still exists.

The next morning we broke camp and were on the road by 6:30 heading
east to Newton. We kept hearing things hitting the windshield and
it got hard to see out. Finally I realized it was thousands of
June Bugs kamikazeing our car. I have never seen such carnage on
a windshield! Not sure what it means when June Bugs are already
out in March, but we wiped them off and kept going. We couldn't
find the box at Caney Creek Park since it looked like a flood got
it, so we continued on to Burkeville where we both placed boxes at
the Canyon Rim Woodlands Trail. I wish we could have taken more
time at this place and if you go for our boxes I highly recommend
spending some time here.

Next up was Willow Oak Recreation Area in Sabine County. Now I
had looked online for a place to put a box in this county since
it did not have one yet and decided on this place. I carved a
specific stamp for it and was committed to going there. Well,
imagine my surprise, and dejection, when we saw that the park was
closed because the bridge was unsafe for cars. This is the problem
with carving a stamp for a specific place; it makes you want to
place it there no matter what. So Baby Bear and I parked the car
and checked out the bridge. There was a huge tree laying across
it and the far side had some erosion, but it looked ok for foot
travel. We climbed over the tree and scampered across with no
problem, feeling like Huck and Tom. I placed the box pretty close
so if you decide to go it should not take too long. It doesn't
look like they will fix the bridge any time soon so you can enjoy
the same experience we did!

After that is was on to San Augustine for some interesting history
and a box, then Nacogdoches. We had already gotten many in this
town last year, but we did a few new ones. Unfortunately, we did
not find several and it turns out it was because my clues were
outdated. Remember I had printed them out last November and even
though I did check to see if the clues still existed before we
left, I did not read every line to see if subtle changes had been
made. Sure enough when I got back I noticed slight changes in
some of the clues where the boxes had been moved. Oh well, we
still had fun in this great town. And the Azaleas were just awesome
everywhere! We knew the non-gatherers would really enjoy it the
next day and we thought about leaving notes to them in logbooks.

We then worked our way through a few back road cemeteries before
arriving at Rusk SP, our home for the next 2 days. We wanted to
arrive before 5:00 so we could register with the ranger, even
though we had already reserved and paid for a site. Last
time someone took our site before we got there and we didn't want
to go through that again. We just made it as she was locking up
and we had no problem. After setting up the tent we headed out to
do the boxes west of town in I.D. Fairchild SF. We found them all
and placed some ourselves just as it was getting dark. Time to
go back to camp and cook dinner, then retire early for the big
day tomorrow in the Jacksonville/New Summerfield area...

Silver Eagle
