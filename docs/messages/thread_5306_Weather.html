Silver Eagle wrote: Hope all of y'all in northern TX are ok after the cold and ice. We were spared the worst of it down here.


Thanks, Silver Eagle. We're going into our fifth day of ice in McKinney, but school reopen (late) today. It's so unusual for it to last this long, but hopefully the temperature will start to rise a little so it will melt today. We love McKinney because of all the large trees, but when the ice came, many of the trees were still holding onto their leaves. The ice coating was too much weight and many of them came crashing down, taking power lines with them. We were without power for 41 hours at our house with temperatures fallng into the 20's at night with only a gas fireplace to heat our house. Thank goodness for my camping equipment (making coffee on my backpacking stove), our Jeep to get us down and up our steep driveway and the hill we live on, my Kindle for entertainment, my headlamp to read by and get around the dark house, and my cellphone to keep us in contact with the outside world. (Did I mention my 20 degree sleeping bag and thermal underwear?) When the electricity finally was restored, it was a shock to our system to have lights, tv, heat and noise again in our house.... but all welcomed. it was an adventure, and one I hope we don't have to face again!

Glad to hear you are ok, but sad to  hear about the trees.  Sounds like your camping experience and tools helped make the situation tolerable, and it brought back memories of when hurricane Ike left us without power for 6 days and we had to "go primitive" also.  That was in the summer so staying cool was the concern instead of staying warm, but cooking, lights and entertainment was the same.  With the power out that long we had to move everything from the refrigerator and freezer into ice chests with ice, but ice quickly sold out in stores so we just started eating all we could.  I used the gas BBQ grill to cook meat and propane camp stove to boil water to cook vegetables and we feasted!  Some people bought gas generators but I didn't want the constant noise.  We learned several things from the experience:  without microwave everything takes longer to heat, you need a light source in the bathroom even during the day (just close the door and see), cans of food are worthless if you don't have a manual opener, and life is much quieter without electricity.  Though it was tough, in many ways it was also a good experience.  We talked with neighbors more than we ever had, and since we had nowhere we had to be, we bonded as a family by playing games and talking.  After the electricity came back on we rejoiced, but part of me missed the "primitive" way of life.  I've suggested we have a "primitive day" every year, but nobody wants to do it, so I take them camping instead.
