Local woman catalogues those buried in old Jacksonville cemetery

By Cristin Ross

cross@jacksonvilleprogress.com

    Jacksonville resident Bindi Caveness isn’t letting her home town’s history stay buried in the past.  Caveness and several of her friends recently catalogued names, dates and other pertinent information included on the headstones located in Jacksonville’s City Cemetery on Kickapoo Street. 
    “I’ve always been a big cemetery buff,” Caveness, who teaches science at New Summerfield Independent School District, said. “My mom used to tease me about it, but now she’s sort of into it, too, since she helped us on this project.”
    After transcribing the data into a coherent list, Caveness plans to donate the list to the city of Jacksonville.  “The city has names of those who owned the deeds to the cemetery’s plots,” Caveness explained. “That doesn’t necessarily tell you who’s buried where or even how many people might be buried there.”  Caveness said she’s also planning to provide her lists to the Web site http://www.cemeteries-of-tx.com/Etx/Cherokee/ListCherokee.html, to help make local genealogy searches a little easier.
    “This is an immense benefit to the city,” Jacksonville City Secretary Betty Thompson said. “And we especially appreciate them all volunteering their time and efforts. This kind of documentation is priceless.”
    Caveness began the project after getting involved with another project regarding local famous people.  “I was researching Fred Lowery, the world-famous whistler, and wanted to find his grave,” Caveness said. “After walking the cemetery for a couple of hours and not finding it, I called the city to see if they knew where it was. That’s when I found out the city only had a list of plot deed owners — no lists of who’s actually in the cemetery.”
    Barry Autry, a local funeral home director, helped Caveness find Lowery’s grave, but the idea of Caveness’s mission had already taken root.
    “It’s important,” Caveness said. “If we lose these names, then we lose part of our history.  There’s so much local history right here in our cemeteries. It’s interesting to me to see the names of our streets attached to something as personal as a headstone. It links those everyday names to the people who had them.”
    City records show the cemetery was first incorporated in 1872 and is still in use today. Three or four of the oldest graves contained people born in the 1700s.
    “There were a lot of unmarked stones,” she said. “We weren’t sure if they were actually graves or just graves that have been reserved for someone who isn’t there yet.”  There are even some graves that are marked with a simple iron ore rock.  “Some of those stones are very hard to read,” Caveness said.
    It took Caveness and a group of friends — some of whom traveled from as far away as Corpus Christi and Ben Wheeler to help — 10 days to cover the entire cemetery. The group catalogued 5,006 recognized grave markers. The last stone was catalogued Monday morning.
    “At first, I was like, ‘Where do I even start and what have I gotten myself into?’” Caveness said with a laugh. “We started at Mother Templeton’s plot, since I’ve always liked it.”
    Legend has it the statue used as a Templeton family grave marker talks and even moves around on occasion.  "I’ve never seen or heard it do anything,” Caveness said. “But there have been people who say they have.”
    Other historic names found at the cemetery include Jacksonville’s founder Jackson Smith, Bolton, Nichols, Alexander, Whitehead, Pearman, Slover, Newburn, O’Keefe, Tilley, Fry, Trantham and Troutman.
    One personal discovery Caveness made while completing her project concerned her great-aunt’s grave.   “She was buried here and when we found her marker, we realized no one had ever come back and added the date of her death on her marker,” Caveness said.
    Caveness said she’s especially glad to have found something she could give back to her home town.  "Jacksonville’s been good to me,” she said. “I’m proud to be able to do something for my hometown.”
