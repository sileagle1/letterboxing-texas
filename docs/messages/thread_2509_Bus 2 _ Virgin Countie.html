Barefoot Lucy <barefootlucy@gmail.com> wrote:
I'm game to work on this too - in fact, Blue Butterfly and Lucy Locket and I had tried to set a day awhile back to get some boxes up into the state parks to the far northeast of the state, but it didn't work out.  I still have a couple of boxes for that trip out.  In thinking about this, what I'm noticing is there are a LOT of tiny counties up there! 
 
I've not proven this will work yet, but an idea I have is to use the atlas of Texas historical markers ( http://atlas.thc.state.tx.us/) as a guide to finding places and box ideas in areas I don't know.  You can do a county search of markers and the descriptions include a transcript of the marker itself in a lot of cases.  It might be a way to at least get an idea for a stamp in a particular town or county, even if you can't plant the box AT the marker.  Otherwise, I wind up doing a crazy amount of research on a town and getting so involved in learning about it that I forget what I'm supposed to be doing!
 
Now would be a good time for us to count how many counties have boxes, and how many we have to go, so we can measure our accomplishments at the end of the year!

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2007-02-02 17:33:58 UTC
I've had the book "Why Stop?) for several years, but a few months ago
I ordered the latest edition (number 5, I think) and discovered that
they had taken out all the markers that weren't on a state or national
highway, so if you are looking for something off the beaten path, like
on a county road or in a town, you're out of luck unless you can find
an older edition. Their reasoning was that if you were on a major
highway, you wouldn't have time to stop, but if you were driving out
in the country, you could just stop and read the sign. I would like
to write to the publishers and tell them that I look for places in the
book, then go find them, usually not the other way around. Anyway,
thought you might like to know.

Boots Tex
