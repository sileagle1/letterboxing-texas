Circus Today

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2009-01-24 05:28:55 UTC
No, it's not what you think. I don't know how many of you live in a
small town, but you don't always see the kind of things that may be
commonplace in the big city, so when something does happen, it can be
a big deal. I drove in to town this morning, and that doesn't take
long where I live. I was just going to haul off a little trash, but
I had to go way over to the other side of town, which is about 4
miles from the house, and I began to see these little hand-written
paper signs, tacked to a stick and stuck in the ground on the side of
the road. "Circus Today", they all said, and that got my
attention. 'Course, I was thinking it might be the Traveling Texas
Circus, so I followed the signs a bit, without getting too far off my
intended path. I followed them past the courthouse to the grounds of
old Jones High School. Thought it might be there, since the school
is not used anymore, but didn't see anything going on. I thought
about my childhood in West Texas when the circus train would come to
town and we'd go down to the tracks and watch them unload the
elephants and set up the tents on a vacant lot. But we don't have a
railroad in Coldspring, so that was out of the question. I guess
they hauled everything in on trucks, but I doubted there would be
elephants. Went down to Old Town Coldspring, but nothing was
happening. I finally had to give up and get rid of the trash. I
guess Silver Eagle was right: the circus train must still be holed
up in Dewberry's garage, waiting for spring.

Boots Tex

From: (vicki@bellmotel.com) | Date: 2009-01-24 15:01:30 UTC-06:00
Carson & Barnes Circus plans their route to play the Houston area every other year.  Only Ringling Bros. Barnum & Bailey and the Shrine Circus play in Houston proper.  The smaller circuses play in the suburbs or nearby towns.  Travelling circuses play the south in the early spring and late fall, and move to the northern half of the U.S. in the summer.  Carson & Barnes used to play Texas City, every other March.  When I hear of them coming your way, I will let you know.
 
My favorites are also the small, one-ring tent shows.  If you look closely, you'll find the performers being "candy butchers"-- selling popcorn and "floss" (cotton candy) at the small shows in the U.S. as well, including Shrine circuses.  Performers also run the pony "sweep" (ride) and inflatable rides during intermission and after the show.  The circus is hard work, and everybody pitches in.  A circus contract usually contains the requirement that the contracted, as well as performing his acts, must be "generally useful"!  I actually got to run the pony sweep for a week once, with a circus travelling near Houston.  The ponies were owned by the ringmaster.  I learned how to scoop all the pony poop up in one swift motion with a huge shovel.  It was a great week!
 
There are some Texas circus connections.  Texas used to have it's own Circus Museum -- the Hertzberg Circus Museum in San Antonio.  The fabulous collection is now part of San Antonio's Witte Museum.  Several members of the Flying Valentine family -- a family of trapeze artists and circus owners -- live in the Houston area.  The local Houston chapter of the Circus Fans of America is named after the Valentine family.  Another favorite Texan in the circus world was the late, great Bobby Gibbs, an elephant trainer who (at 370 pounds) was almost as large as his elephants, and had a quick wit and a huge heart to match.  He retired from the circus to live near Dallas, and had a second career driving a truck hauling bananas, which he called "monkey pickles".  If anyone is interested, as I think of more Texas circus connections, I will post them.
 
-- Bell Motel
