I gotta throw in. And yeah, this is gonna be one of my adversarial sorts of posts - no offense intended - I just think it's time for a little self-examination.

If I remember correctly (Mike, correct me if I'm wrong), Mike worked his way in to the state parks by volunteering for them, first and foremost. He did trail maintenance and whatever other sorts of things Mike is good at that made park rangers like them. He did things for them that they needed to get done but didn't have the time to do, so when they saw him show up, they were glad to see him.

SO - FIRST QUESTION. How many of us who feel the state parks aren't responsive enough to letterboxers have actually gone and done non-letterboxing volunteer work for the state parks more than once in the last year? I have to confess - not me.

So let's say you are a Park Ranger, and every day you have dozens of demands on your attention - the bathroom near Site 15 doesn't work, someone saw a snake in the road near the fishing pier, Ranger Rick didn't show up for work, the Park Host had to pull out for a family emergency, there are two campers fighting over the last spot, someone just checked out and their campfire hasn't been extinguished, someone reported a tree across the trail on the perimeter trail but they couldn't tell us where, someone said an alligator ate their poodle, there's someone who calls herself BarefootLucy waiting for you needing a permit of some kind, and Mike just showed up and needs something to do. You, the Park Ranger, quickly evaluate all that - and first off, you know you can send Mike off to check the tree on the trail and maybe he can put the fire out at the campsite on the way, and you've instantly solved three pressing issues with one. Which of the remaining are safety-related? Which ones have a monetary impact on the park? Which ones affect staffing? Hmmm....well BarefootLucy is just going to have to wait until I get all these other things under control. And the poodle will have to be digested...

If you were a ranger faced with all of that, wouldn't you look first for the friendly face that you recognized as a volunteer and respond to them first? And wouldn't you be more likely to listen to that person's special request when things settled down?

We really don't need a state liaison because a policy is now in place. The work has been done at the state level and we were too late for it. Admittedly, the policy doesn't perfectly fit letterboxing, but it doesn't perfectly fit geocaching either. They didn't pass the policy for our (or geocachers) benefit, they passed it because they saw that we were creating an issue for them and they needed to address it. The best place for us to be is side-by-side with state parks, making ourselves personally known to their staff personally because what is now important is how the local park interprets and exercises the policy.

One reason geocaching has caught on and letterboxing hasn't, is that geocaching has certain rules in place that make them more responsible. They have the 1/10 rule. We do too I suppose - but where theirs is a 1/10 mile spread, ours is if 1 box is good, 10 is better. They have a stated Cache In Trash Out policy. We talk about it, but we don't as a larger group actively promote it and have special days for it.

As much as I hate to bring this up, one thing that always concerns me is the sheer volume of boxes we infuse into a park after a gathering. Is that cool? Is it okay? Are we taking into consideration the bigger picture of how we affect the park, or we only interested in our own entertainment? This is not accusatory - again, I'm looking for some self-examination.

Maybe instead of a gathering, we should have a Local State Park day where we all go to our nearest state parks and volunteer. Maybe we should get in the habit of volunteering at our state parks.

Dunno. I think instead of complaining that they don't respect us, we should begin respecting ourselves a little more and doing things that make us better stewards of the facilities where we box. It's not gonna happen overnight that they'll embrace us, but maybe once we get ourselves noticed, they'll take an interest in us, and maybe once that happens, permitting will get easier and we can have an impact on the policy itself.

So I guess I'm done. I fully expect a range of disagreement, and I'm good with that. My purpose in writing is not to get everyone to agree with me, but to get everyone to think beyond the fact that the rangers won't play with us.

So I guess I'm done.  I fully expect a range of disagreement, and I'm good with that.  My purpose in writing is not to get everyone to agree with me, but to get everyone to think beyond the fact that the rangers won't play with us.
