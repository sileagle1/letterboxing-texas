From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-29 08:09:05 UTC-07:00
I recently started working on a field guide of Texas
letterboxes for my own use (although if you . I've
found it frustrating at times to not have a hard copy
guide to grab and go with, so I thought I would
download clues and put it into a backpack friendly
format. It wound up being a bigger project than I had
thought it would be when I started it (I'm thinking
now - DUUUHHH!), so I had set it aside for awhile, but
Jeff's great website has spurred me back to action on
it.

Here's what I've been doing - I've copied clues from
the LBNA website into Word and stripped the graphics
off. For the sake of tackling one challenge at a
time, I've not worried about organization yet - just
started a file for each region and left them listed by
county. I've copied the clues for each region into
the same file. I'll page break between each box so it
will be one box per page.

Now then - y'all think about if this were for your
purposes (and I'd be willing to share the file after
it's done, once I make sure I comply with LBNA's
information usage policy - whatever that is!). If you
were inclined to use a field guide, what would you
really like to see in it? Lines for notes on each
box's page? Sorted differently? Do you like the
regions? What size? What format?

Personally, I have had in mind a 9x7 inch notebook,
although I'm not sure how conducive this size is to
printing or copying. This is the size I keep my bird
guide in and it carries very well in a backpack (for
day hiking anyway). Copied front and back, one
letterbox per page. Plenty of margin space for notes
(or as an alternative, lines on each page, but I
prefer margin space). I don't have a problem with the
regions, and I think I would tab the guide by region
and put an index at the front of each section and a
copy of the map showing how the regions are broken
down at the front of the book. I'm kind of lukewarm
on the counties, but I don't know how else I would
sort it - maybe by town? I think I would put a
section at the back of the guide with some handy
information that isn't clue-specific. And the all
important disclaimer.

I'd love to hear what y'all think would be good to put
into a field guide, what sort of information would be
handy to have on the fly (you know, when you can't
just pull the website because trees don't have
internet connections????).

Lucy

From: Birds of a Feather Family (birdsofafeather_lbox@yahoo.com) | Date: 2004-04-29 22:43:11 UTC-07:00
Lucy,
 
 
My wife started out printing clue pages for boxes in the nearby area for towns that she recognized.  She put them in a 3 ring binder with 1 inch rings.
 
 We are in the Gulf Coast region which is not a compact region.  Not being native Texans, we have found that we are having to go look at a map to see where counties and towns are located.
 
I have started just printing out the clue pages before we leave for a trip. After missing out on two letterboxes because we left the second page at home :-( , I now just copying the text to reduce the size so each box's clues are on one page. 
 
In terms of sorting, my tendency would be to use the regions on LBNA as most of us are already familiar with it, county and then city.   I would still want to look at a map since sorting cities alphabetically does not match their physical relationship to each other.
 
Another posibility would be to group letterboxes by themes ( Texas History, Famous People, Cartoons, Wildlife, etc.).
 
I have enjoyed Silver Eagle's suggested itinerary for letterboxers going to an area.  That may be worthwhile capturing. 
 
Silver Eagle and I were visiting about when clue pages are removed from LBNA. One thing about capturing the clues in a field guide you maintain a record of the Texas letterboxes.  Even if a box is missing, the fact that someone place a box at a location is a recommendation for a trip to visit.
 
Jeff (Sea Dove)

Copy and paste the clues to a word document. I put all the clues
for a particular trip onto one page. I'm going to goliad soon and I
have everything on a single page. It's easy to keep track of if not
too pretty.
