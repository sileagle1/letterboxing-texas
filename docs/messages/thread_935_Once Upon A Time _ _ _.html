Once Upon A Time . . .

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2005-06-23 02:39:39 UTC
It all started for me back in April, 2002 when I read
an article in a magazine (I can't remember which one)
about letterboxing. I thought it sounded interesting,
so I went to LBNA website and read all about it and was
further intrigued. However, when I looked on the Texas
clue page, there were less than 10 boxes in the entire
state and none near me, so I promptly lost interest and
did not think any more about it for several months. Then
in August, Baby Bear (my brother) and I flew to Spokane, WA
to go backpacking in Glacier NP, so I looked on the LBNA
site for Spokane and found one box that looked close to
where we would stay the last night before heading home.
I talked Baby Bear into looking for it, even though we did
not have stamps or logbooks or inkpads. We were both
skeptical until we actually found it, then a change came
over us and we knew this was something we would enjoy. The
Finch Arboretum Letterbox became our first find, and luckily
it had cards with the stamp image on them so we were able
to bring back the image and eventually paste it into our
newly purchased logbooks. As luck would have it, there was
a Hitchhiker also in the box, so not knowing exactly what
to do with it except to move it to another box, we took it
with us back to Texas.
Our next step was to pick trail names and get a personal stamp
and logbook. At the time, I was in Indian Pricesses with my
daughter and my Indian name was Silver Eagle, so I went with
that. I bought some silver ink and an eagle stamp, since I
did not think there was any way I could carve one, and was
all set. But, since there were still no boxes near me and
I had this HH to get rid of, I realized I would have to place
a box of my own. My family was about to go to Zube Park, so
I bought a train stamp and created my first box, Zube Zephyr,
where I placed the Hitchhiker (it was the Hitch HH, shaped
like Alfred Hitchcock) on 09/21/2002.
After reading on the Big Talklist about how hand-carved stamps
were much more desireable than store-bought, I decided to try
one. Now understand, I have no artistic ability, so this was
a major undertaking. I had this huge, green, rubber eraser
on my desk, so I decided to create an alligator and put it in
Brazos Bend SP. This became my first hand-carved stamp box
and first box in a series (TX Bird Trail) on 11/09/2002.
Soon Baby Bear was placing boxes of his own and we started
finding each other's and going on Letterbox Placing trips
when our families would let us. I had decided early on
that the best way to get letterboxing going in Texas was to
spread new boxes over as wide an area as I could, so that is
what I tried to do. I thought of them as little letterboxing
seeds and I was the ever-hopeful gardener. I guess it worked,
just look at us now!
As I got better carving, I decided I should create a hand-
carved personal stamp, so I created my current Silver Eagle
and stamps for both my children. Unfortunately, they still
remain uninterested in letterboxing and it is the one regret
I have about the whole thing.
I often wonder what would have happened if Baby Bear and I
had not found that box in Spokane. Would we have eventually
started letterboxing after a while or forgotten all about it?
Only the Letterboxing Gods know for sure.

Silver Eagle
