Re: Replace or not to replace (boxes)?

From: Puddle Splasher (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2008-11-15 17:59:01 UTC
I think it's great when you replace a lost box (even if you put it in a
new location). There are always people who haven't had an opportunity
to find it yet and would love to. I've recarved and replaced boxes
before. One of my Artist Series boxes went missing last summer and I
recarved it and placed it in the exact same location as before. It had
been visited well over 100 times before it went missing, so I couldn't
entirely blame the location. It is in a busy location (Ft Worth
Botanical Gardens) so it's likely that it was observed being found or
replaced and someone went to investigate.
On the other hand, after my Wooly Booly box went missing, I chose not
to replace it. I think it was my very best carving effort and the box
was hidden in a location where it should have been safe. It was in
place for well over a year or so before it went missing. For some
reason, I just couldn't bring myself to make the effort a second time.
I think that loss just took the wind out of my sails a bit! (It
happens.)
Puddle Splasher

Re: [letterboxingtexas] Re: Replace or not to replace (boxes)?

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-11-15 19:25:14 UTC-06:00
I lost my SFA box awhile back, probably to custodians.  It was a tribute box to my dad, and the stamp in it was positively excellent, if I do say so myself.  After much thought, I decided not to replace it.  It was a very good box for the time it was there, but I knew when I planted it that it was a high risk location (albeit the place it HAD to be) and that's why I made it a WOM box.  It gave pleasure to the folks who found it, and to me, but I had the feeling that working too hard at keeping it there would take some of the pleasure of it away.  I came to think of it as a passing serendipity (how's that for a good word Janet?), something that gave a lucky few something special. 
 
One of my first boxes was a box planted at Killough Monument - a local historical site that moves me every time I'm there.  I recarved that stamp three times, changing locations once, before I finally let it go.  I had become concerned about the safety of the location because it was extremely isolated and nearly every time I went out there, I ran into "someone" doing "something", and they never seemed too pleased to see me.  The last time the box disappeared - or was destroyed actually - I toyed with the idea of replacing the box near the monument instead of at it, but it just wouldn't be the same, so I decided that box had seen its day and I retired it.  About a month ago someone was murdered at the site.  Although the victim was presumably a part of a drug deal gone bad, it could have easily been a letterboxer who stumbled upon the drug deal gone bad. 
 
I also recently lost a box that was placed purely to plug a hole in the map box-wise.  When it went, I shed no tears and never considered recarving.  I was almost relieved not to have it sitting out there with my name on it, because it wasn't anything special and nothing I was particularly proud to call my own.  It haled back to the days of few boxes in East Texas, when something was better than nothing, so there it was.
 
My Spring Hope box is disabled at the moment, but it has been in place for quite awhile.  I will always continue to replant that box, although this time I'm going to change the image up.  Why is it important to me to replace it?  Because Ruby Tuesday and Stillwater came boxing in my town one weekend, and she was sick enough at that point that Stillwater (an LSS mind you, not himself a boxer) would get out of the car and go find the box, walk back to the car with it and let her stamp in, then take the box back to its hiding spot.  He didn't just do this with a box or two - they found lots of boxes that weekend. I happened upon them that Saturday in the park near my house, and it touched me deeply to watch him do this for her.  Now that's LOVE!  When I met up with them later for coffee, they laughingly told me the story of finding my Spring Hope box.  It's a puzzle box, and Stillwater actually figured it out before Ruby did - something that was really hard to do.  Stillwater told me to never let that box go, that it was his favorite they had ever found, probably because he bested her.  And Ruby mentioned that box and their experience with it every time I saw her after that.  So that's a box I will always recarve and replace.  It's always been a favorite of mine anyway, and Ruby will always be one of my favorite letterboxers, and Stillwater loved Ruby enough to do her boxing for her when she couldn't manage it for myself, something I hope Fearless Fox would do for me if it ever came to that (and I think he would).  It holds a lot of symbolism for me.
 
So you see, I think that the decision to recarve and replace has to be a case-by-case thing, and only you can know what you should do.  Over the years, I've come to the decision that - for me - planting a box shouldn't be a haphazard thing in the first place.  I try to think what I want to accompllish by placing the box, who I'm aiming it toward, what I expect to get out of it.  So when a box goes missing that was planted with forethought, it is much easier to make that decision whether to replace/recarve because I can ask myself if my mission was accomplished.
 
BarefootLucy
"Not about footwear, it's about philosophy"

Re: Replace or not to replace (boxes)?

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2008-11-16 03:59:44 UTC
In the beginning, I used to always replace boxes with the same stamp
recarved, sometimes up to 3 times, but I soon got tired of doing this.
Since most of my stamps are related to the location, changing the idea
of the box was not an option in most cases, but I soon realized that
except for unique stamps I could just modify them a little and put a
number on the end of the name (e.g. box 2) and it would be a new box.
This way even people that had found the original box would have another
one to find. This is especially nice when I send a box to someone to
help me replace a box far away since it gives them a new find for their
trouble. The only down side to not replacing is that the historical
place date is lost, so if you placed the first box in a particular
place, but then it went missing and you did not replace it, future
boxers would never know about it. Not a big deal, but worth
considering.

There are a few special boxes that I will always replace or some that I
will just retire. I guess it just depends how you feel about a
particular box as to what you do, but there is no wrong answer.

Silver Eagle

From: Baby Bear (babybearlb2002@yahoo.com) | Date: 2008-11-17 21:29:01 UTC
My thoughts are pretty much in line with Silver Eagle's. I use to re-
carve and go hide in same place, until twice I recarved stamps that
were reported missing, then when I got there I found the original in
place. I decided in the future if I did re-carve same idea, then
would give it #2 addition, since it was not exactly the same as the
orignal, and the log book would be new anyway (with new hide date).
Some I just retire, as find it hard to motivate myself anymore to
recarve the same image. I no longer get upset when a box goes
missing, just part of the game, and I try to keep my box status
updated so others don't waste time looking for missing boxes (but
gets hard to keep up). I did decide after losing quite a few to
create a master book for all my carved images (in addition to my log
books) so I would have all of them in one book.

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2008-11-18 17:15:33 UTC
Baby Bear said, "My thoughts are pretty much in line with Silver
Eagle's. I use to re-carve and go hide in same place, until twice I
recarved stamps that were reported missing, then when I got there I
found the original in place."

I've had that happen more than once, where a box would be "attempted"
or even reported as missing. I'd carve a new stamp with the original
image and make a new logbook and box, only to find the original box
exactly where it was supposed to be. I tend to ignore the
first "attempt", especially if I don't know the letterboxer. If a
favorite box really goes missing, though, or if one in a series, like
the Murder at the Arboretum, I'll recarve and replace. In fact,
everytime one of those boxes are attempted, but not found, I go check
on it. It would't be fair to know one of those boxes were missing
because I believe if someone is going to take the time to look for
them, they should all be available to complete the story line. One
finder of that seven-box series reported finding the first 3 boxes
and reported the last 4 as "attempted". I went and checked and found

The group of really regular letterboxers here in Houston is small. We
all get excited (still) about new boxes and we want new boxes to find.

So, rather than replacing old ones, I generally decide to create an
entirely new box so that the letterboxing friends who give me so much
fun & adventure may have a new one of their own.

That said, there are special boxes that I will always try to replace.
For instance, I have had a replacement for my Pirate's puzzle riding
around in my car for almost exactly one year...some day, some day.
