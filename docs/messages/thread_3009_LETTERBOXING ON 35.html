From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-14 00:02:45 UTC
This won't work for letterboxes not listed on AQ, but my favorite
Atlasquest feature is the trip planner. Just go to that feature, and
search by Interstate and the distance you'd like to travel from the
interstate. You can even map the results to see the stops graphically.

My dream would be if everyone who uses AQ would enter the correct
address (not just the city name) when they list their boxes. This
mappable feature helps those of us who are addicted to maps. I really
find the LBNA way of having to look up every city in a county manually
very cumbersome.

dewberry

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-13 19:56:43 UTC-05:00
I know what you mean about addresses on AQ - they DO make it a lot easier to map!
 
BUT - (and we've had this discussion before) - I really relate to the County approach on LBNA.  I was thinking about this on my way home from Denton last night as I crossed from Dallas County to Kaufman County.  It was funny because as I read the Kaufman County sign, I immediately got a little inventory in my head of the boxes in Kaufman County and a mental map of where the towns with letterboxes were in the county.  I also find I have a much easier time relating large areas together because in my mind I have towns placed in specific counties, so that all I have to do is know how the counties relate to the each other geographically - jigsaw puzzle style.
 
I know much of this has to do with how people process information, so I'm once again reminded how glad I am we have both sites to use for those who process information in different ways.  I also wonder if this has to do with rural vs. urban?  Being a country girl, I have to think beyond the boundaries of my town and county sometimes to get what I need AND there is a lot less to remember in one county than there is in an urban area.

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-08-14 12:20:39 UTC
I think it's so interesting how there are so many different
approaches. I'm not a big person on having it all spoonfed (I don't
want hints on clues), but I do like to "see" where the town/park
is...especially when I travel. This helps me decide whether a
particular box is do-able on a road trip. There are a zillion Texas
counties, and I don't always know (like Lucy) off the top of my head
which ones are next to the others. That information does not stay in
my brain (like why Rusk is in Cherokee County, not Rusk County--I
don't get that).

I think part of my attitude is colored by where I live. Houston has a
large surface area, and most of it is in Harris County. If someone
were new or traveling, trying to figure out what box is where is just
an overwhelming & daunting task. For a box that is not a mystery, I
use the correct address as the location to help someone ease that
task. Often, my clues are hard enough that I don't have to provide
the additional challenge of figuring out the streets too.

I know some people like the mapping feature because they want to get
every box in an area--that's not me, I know I'm not going to be able
to detour that much, even if I wanted to the rest of the car would refuse.

Hello. My name is dewberry & I am addicted to maps. Paper, Key Maps,
Google Earth, Mapquest--I use them all every day.

dewberry

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2007-08-14 12:18:41 UTC-04:00
Don't miss Silver Eagle's Salado letterbox.  It's right along the way!  Also in Austin, the Oakwood Cemetery is right next to I35, and it's a nice cool shady place, too, and that's a really cool box.  If you expand your range about 5 - 15 miles either direction from I35, there's a plethora of boxes out there to be found.  If you have time for a little hike, Georgetown has several boxes not far from the highway, but you'll have to make time to walk a ways to find them.  Still, it's a good break from the traffic.  Same is true in Austin - several good boxes not a long distance from I 35, but will require some time to walk to find them along the way, plus the additional time to wait for all the busy joggers, walkers, and others to pass by -- letterboxing stealth is an issue in Austin!
