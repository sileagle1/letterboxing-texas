> Being a ghost sometimes has its perks; you can meet and chat with
> other ghosts, some of whom were famous and important mortals. Just
> the other day I was thinking about Davy Crockett and wondering what
> was up with him, so I took a little trip over to the Alamo and
found
> him (more properly, his ghost), sitting there looking a little
> dejected. He was still wearing his old buckskins and that coonskin
> cap which was getting a little scraggly looking, if you ask me, and
> he was cleaning his old Tennessee long rifle. Anyway, I asked him
> how he had been doing and he allowed as to how he was doin'
tol'able,
> except that he missed his favorite pastime, which was hunting and
> asked if I knew if there were any bears around, any old kind would
> do. I told him that there were very few bears left in Texas
because
> people like him had killed them all. He said he was sorry about
that
> and would like to make amends if he could. Maybe he could find a
> bear, just any old bear, and apologize for killing so many of them
in
> his lifetime. I told him that I knew of a bear out west of
Houston,
> but that he was just a Baby Bear. I asked him if he would like to
> take a trip on the Texas Tour Bus and I'd show him where the cub
> lived, if he would promise that he only wanted to apologize. He
> brightened up at the prospect and said he'd just take me up on it.
> We got on board with all the other well-known Texans, a few of whom
> he remembered, and some he had heard of, and headed for a place
> called Cypress, where I knew the little bear lived. Pretty soon,
> Davy (or more properly, his ghost), took over the wheel and drove
> that bus all the way to Cypress. I don't know how he learned to
> drive, but ghosts can do about anything they want to if they try
hard
> enough. We found the house of the Baby Bear and he parked out
front,
> surveying the neighborhood, which was a very nice one, indeed, for
a
> Baby Bear. Then, he turned up the walk and, just before he got to
> the front door, he thought he heard something, so he turned to the
> right and ducked under some bushes. The last I saw of him, he was
> sitting on top of the bus sighting in that long rifle, with a grin
on
> his face and a gleam in his eye. I have no idea what he had in
mind.
>
> The Ghost of IRA Boxer
