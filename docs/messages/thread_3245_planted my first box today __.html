From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-12-30 09:36:53 UTC-06:00
Hey Chrispy,
 
I would suggest you also list it on www.letterboxing.org since there are quite a few Texas letterboxers who don't use AQ. 
 
Also, it isn't terribly unusual for one or the other site to go down for a little while and it really sends everyone into a frenzy when they can't get to clues.  If your box is listed on both sites, then folks can get to your clues on the site that isn't down. But the downside to this logic is that it means you have to enter clues independently on both sites and do maintenance on both sites, because if you link to one site from the other, the whole idea of your clues being able to be accessed flies out the window.
 
Anyway, there is nothing that says you have to list on both sites, but sometimes new planters get attached to one site or the other and don't realize there are benefits to having boxes listed in both places.
 
My .02,

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-12-30 10:17:14 UTC-06:00
You will find that the older (as in longer in the game, not age-wise) letterboxers tend to use LBNA more (although there are a few notable exceptions like Dewberry, who is an AQ wiz) and the more rural letterboxers like LBNA. 
 
In the beginning, there was nothing but a list like this one where folks posted their clues, and that became cumbersome very quickly.  Then there was LBNA, but you had to submit your clues to be added (ask Silver Eagle and Baby Bear about this).  Finally, they got LBNA going so that boxers could add their own clues, but there was no tracking and it was extremely planter-centric.  There really wasn't a reason to have a log-in unless you were going to add a box (that's about the time Mother of Five and I came in).  Folks started clamoring for things like tracking capabalities and status notes.  There were many, many discussions and a few flames on the Big List about these ideas and some folks were very much in favor and some folks were very much opposed to lots of bells and whistles.  Finally, LBNA added some rudimentary features.  Somewhere in there, Ryan started stewing about for a new approach and kicked off AQ.  AQ was definitely more user-friendly, which I think was his goal.  He has always also been very responsive to what folks ask for and makes lots of changes and updates regularly.  It is great for newbies because of the user-friendliness.
 
So why would LBNA continue to exist if AQ is doing it better?  There are quite a few folks who don't think it IS better.  These are usually the same folks who would like to see clues passed WOM only.  For myself, I like the sleek elegance of putting clues out there so other folks can download them - nothing more, nothing less.  I get terribly distracted by all the extra features that don't enhance letterboxing itself (okay, in my opinion, the mapping AQ has does enhance the game) - only add to the tech-y side of letterboxing.  Some of the folks who prefer LBNA say that AQ spoon feeds folks, so that they don't WANT to do the hard stuff.  Sometimes I feel that way, but I remind myself that I am generalizing when I get that mindset. 
 
On the other hand, LBNA and it's lack of user-friendliness actually drives some folks off because they can't get the meat of the game quite so easily.  Some boxers would say "Good.  They didn't belong anyway."  I'm betting we lost some pretty good boxers along the way because of that.  I think the LBNA database was created to go hand-in-hand with the talk list and was never intended to be a tutorial for letterboxing.  The founders hoped that boxers would participate on the list and learn from more experienced boxers and the database would be a mere repository.  Over time, the Big List became very unfriendly to newbies, so the learning element virtually went away.  Other lists were formed to help folks learn, and it was during the rethinking of the lists I think that Ryan jumped in with his alternate way of looking at a database.
 
And then there is the rural vs. urban thing, and it all comes down to what types of searches work best for whom.  For me, out here in the sticks, a county search works great and that's what LBNA offers.  In Houston, a county search isn't such a great idea and AQ works far better for locating boxes because it is city-based.  However, in Houston, or DFW for that matter, even a city search doesn't work very well if AQ users don't enter at least a rough address in their box descriptions. 
So anyway, I thought you might be interested in a brief history of the two databases as I remember it, and now this LBNA user is off to pick up my husband and son who have been backpacking overnight, and I will hunt some boxes along the way with clues pulled and mapped from an AQ search of my blue tags.  I may like LBNA better, but I can't resist some of those neat little features on AQ!

--
Barefoot Lucy
"It's not about footwear, it's about philosophy"
