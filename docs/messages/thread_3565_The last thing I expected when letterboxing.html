The last thing I expected when letterboxing

From: Anna (afbradford@austin.rr.com) | Date: 2008-05-19 17:23:43 UTC-05:00

Today I went out to find a new series of letterboxes that had been planted over the weekend.  The subject of the series is really interesting to me, and the greenbelt where they are planted is close to my home.  So off I go….

 

While hiking in the woods it occurred to me that it probably wasn’t the safest thing to do – going out alone in a really large wooded area where there may or may not be other people around.  What if I fell down, got hurt, ran into a mugger, whatever.  I couldn’t find the boxes and kept hiking further and further into the wood, all the time letting my imagination get the better of me.  I had pretty much worked out every calamity I could think of and a had plan of action for most of them.

 

I was walking along a main trail and noticed a person coming toward that trail from a spur trail to my right.  I could just make them out through the trees.  It turns out that they got to the main trail at the same time as I got to the entrance to the trail they were on.   I looked up to say hello and noticed that the guy was all alone and was carrying a chainsaw.

 

He said hello and went on to find the camping co-eds or whoever he was after… but it sure scared me.

From: Abbie Beck (abbiembeck@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2008-05-19 16:38:30 UTC-07:00
This is interesting...my husband and I were just having this particular conversation at the beginning of the weekend.  On Thursday night, I was eagerly awaiting his home coming from work, hoping he would agree to me having a little while to go letterboxing by myself.  A friend from church was going to have a small get together with some of her friends and introduce them to her newfound hobby of letterboxing.  Not wanting them to get easily discouraged, I thought I would go hunt the boxes prior to the meeting and make sure all was well.  My husband thought I was nuts!!  He DID NOT want me to go to the park alone...now this is a park I frequently go to with the kids and I really couldn't understand the problem.  I did try to call a friend (who wants NOTHING to do with lb'ing, but was the only person I thought available on short notice), and I guess when he saw the downcast look on my face after she said no, he caved. 
 
So, as I am trying to "enjoy" myself...I felt like I was hearing people hiding in bushes, thinking, "Here I am, off the main path with only a cell phone for protection."  I'm sure some of his fear had gotten me a little nervous, but I guess it was a good kind of nervous that kept me alert.
 
That all being said...what were the ideas about being safe on the trail?  I guess I wasn't in on that conversation.  I really think the usual 5 kids are a great deterent!!  Maybe I could start bringing the dog...she could lick them to death!!

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2008-05-19 19:05:32 UTC-06:00
Well - the idea of self-defense is a very personal issue.  There are several levels of force available.  the problem is, you really do not get to choose - it gets chosen for you by the attacker.  But there are a few things you can do to help.
 
The #1 thing is to be alert and aware of your surroundings at ALL TIMES.  This isn't just in the woods - it should get to be a life-time thing.  Just knowing what is around you and sensing danger early is a great start, it allows you to avoid the danger.  This is especially important if you travel alone.  TRUST YOUR GUT!  If something doesn't feel right - it ain't right.  Screw making people feel hurt.  Get out now!
 
#2 - distance is your friend.  If you let someone near enough to touch you - you have already lost half the battle.  In most places the "21 foot rule" applies.  If someone is within a 21 foot circle from you, and is acting threatening, you would be justified in acting.
 
#3 - make a fuss.  The first moments of an attack or kidnapping are your best chance to escape.  Do not cooperate - FIGHT!  Immediately and hard.
 
#4 - everything is a weapon.  Keys poking out between your fingers and punched into the attacker's eyes will usually end the fight.  But - remember #2?  Stand-off weapons are best.  MACE brand Pepper FOAM is a good choice if you will not consider a handgun.  Don't get the cheap spray.  Pay the $$$, get the foam.  It has some advantages.  NOTE:  In Texas, pepper foam is simple assault.  If you would be justified in punching, you are justified in the use of pepper foam.
 
#5 - I am licensed for and DO carry a handgun.  ALWAYS.  So does my wife.  As do most of my friends.  No, I do not expect trouble.  If I expected trouble, I would carry my rifle - remember #2?  I do not expect to have a flat tire, but I have a spare.  I do not expect my house to burn, but I have a fire extinguisher and insurance too.  I usually also carry pepper foam.  But most attacks are swift, surprise is what the attacker is counting on.
 
Here is the COMBAT COLOR CODE.
 

White - Oblivious to your surroundings. You should never be in white while awake.

Yellow - Alert to your surroundings, no particular threat(s) or danger.

Orange - Possible threats or danger nearby. You are alert and ready but action is not eminent.

Red - You are in danger. Take appropriate action to ensure survival for you and your family

Black - Action taken - threat(s) or danger neutralized.

I have been accused of being more in ORANGE than YELLOW.  So be it.  But just realizing that you are in white and trying to develop the mindset to get into yellow is a good start.
 
You really deserve a better reply than this.  Perhaps someone can dig in the archives for my old message.  But my dog is very ill.  I am afraid he will not be with us much longer.  8 years.  5 show titles. A wall full of ribbons and photos.  Thousands of miles travelled.  Hundreds of miles hiked.  Damn bone cancer.  So I am going to sit with him for a while.  And is all 175 pounds of him lays on top of me, I guess I won't complain.
 
Y-all have a good evening.

Today I went out to find a new series of letterboxes that had been planted over the weekend.  The subject of the series is really interesting to me, and the greenbelt where they are planted is close to my home.  So off I go.
While hiking in the woods it occurred to me that it probably wasn't the safest thing to do going out alone in a really large wooded area where there may or may not be other people around.  What if I fell down, got hurt, ran into a mugger, whatever.  I couldn't find the boxes and kept hiking further and further into the wood, all the time letting my imagination get the better of me.  I had pretty much worked out every calamity I could think of and a had plan of action for most of them.
I was walking along a main trail and noticed a person coming toward that trail from a spur trail to my right.  I could just make them out through the trees.  It turns out that they got to the main trail at the same time as I got to the entrance to the trail they were on.   I looked up to say hello and noticed that the guy was all alone and was carrying a chainsaw.
He said hello and went on to find the camping co-eds or whoever he was after but it sure scared me.

Well - the idea of self-defense is a very personal issue.  There are several levels of force available.  the problem is, you really do not get to choose - it gets chosen for you by the attacker.  But there are a few things you can do to help.

 

The #1 thing is to be alert and aware of your surroundings at ALL TIMES.  This isn't just in the woods - it should get to be a life-time thing.  Just knowing what is around you and sensing danger early is a great start, it allows you to avoid the danger.  This is especially important if you travel alone.  TRUST YOUR GUT!  If something doesn't feel right - it ain't right.  Screw making people feel hurt.  Get out now!

 

#2 - distance is your friend.  If you let someone near enough to touch you - you have already lost half the battle.  In most places the "21 foot rule" applies.  If someone is within a 21 foot circle from you, and is acting threatening, you would be justified in acting.

 

#3 - make a fuss.  The first moments of an attack or kidnapping are your best chance to escape.  Do not cooperate - FIGHT!  Immediately and hard.

 

#4 - everything is a weapon.  Keys poking out between your fingers and punched into the attacker's eyes will usually end the fight.  But - remember #2?  Stand-off weapons are best.  MACE brand Pepper FOAM is a good choice if you will not consider a handgun.  Don't get the cheap spray.  Pay the $$$, get the foam.  It has some advantages.  NOTE:  In Texas, pepper foam is simple assault.  If you would be justified in punching, you are justified in the use of pepper foam.

 

#5 - I am licensed for and DO carry a handgun.  ALWAYS.  So does my wife.  As do most of my friends.  No, I do not expect trouble.  If I expected trouble, I would carry my rifle - remember #2?  I do not expect to have a flat tire, but I have a spare.  I do not expect my house to burn, but I have a fire extinguisher and insurance too.  I usually also carry pepper foam.  But most attacks are swift, surprise is what the attacker is counting on.

 

Here is the COMBAT COLOR CODE.

 

White - Oblivious to your surroundings. You should never be in white while awake.

Yellow - Alert to your surroundings, no particular threat(s) or danger.

Orange - Possible threats or danger nearby. You are alert and ready but action is not eminent.

Red - You are in danger. Take appropriate action to ensure survival for you and your family

Black - Action taken - threat(s) or danger neutralized.

I have been accused of being more in ORANGE than YELLOW.  So be it.  But just realizing that you are in white and trying to develop the mindset to get into yellow is a good start.
