SE,

   I have really enjoyed all these history lessons you have been producing.  I remember when I first started letterboxing that LbNA was very rudimentary. You could not keep track of your finds online so I would print out the Central Texas Letterbox list from LbNA and then take different colored highlighters and mark up the list for boxes that I had found or tried to find but couldn't.  Every few months I would print out a new list as boxes were added to LbNA and I would have to copy over all my personal data to the new list. When I returned to letterboxing after wandering away for several years I was very happy to see that I could now keep track of my finds online and the list could be filtered to not show boxes I had already found. Other features seem to appear every so often and I appreciate most of them. More details about where a box is located if the planter chooses to provide it, maps, personal notes, etc.

   I don't go to LbNA website quite as often as I used to - I get most of my clues now using Box Radar on my Android smartphone - but I do applaud all the efforts that have gone into the site and the volunteers that continue to add boxes, data, and features.

PI Joe

From: (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2013-11-16 08:09:48 UTC-08:00

Yes, I don't think anyone ever found the one in Crane County.  Just last month, Lionsmane found one of those that LSQ placed near Knox City on that 2009 trip and he was the first finder.  A few of those have never been found and are probably lost, so there may be some counties without active letterboxes.  It was a great adventure, though, and a couple of those boxes have interesting stories.  One in Baylor County only had one finder before going missing, but the finder was from Great Britain.  One of them in Throckmorton County has been adopted by the man who owns the ranch that it's placed on.  He takes his friends out to visit the box and they all sign their names, even though they are not letterboxers.  One box in Benjamin was in honor of the Texas Official photographer, Wyman Meinzer, who lives in the renovated jailhouse in Benjamin.  He and his wife found the box in the local cemetery and signed it and sent me a message of thanks for saying nice things about his photography.  Sometimes, "remote" can be a good thing.
