From: Michael P. Olbrisch (mike2004@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2006-05-10 03:31:57 UTC
My story is simple.  I ran in to a Letterbox Hybrid Geocache on the Geocaching.com website.  I had no idea what a Letterbox was, so I looked it up.  Google took me to LBNA.  I thought it was a cool idea.  So I had a stamp made.  This is my stamp.
 
 
It was made from a photo of me and the dog hiking in a field of yellow/orange poppies.  So the next weekend my handgun instructor and I hiked to the Letterbox Hybrid Geocache on Indian Peak.  This was 18 Sep 2005.  It was a tough hike.  So much that my partner wanted to quit and go back when he saw the final approach.  I agreed, it looked tough.  But I am not a quitter.  So I told him to set up under some shade, I would be back.  Well, he isn't really a quitter either.
 
The letterbox part of the Geocache was as cool as I expected it to be.  It was a store-bought Kokopeli stamp, in red ink.  I stamped up, took a few photos, and we started back down.  We were both sore for a few days from that one.  But I was hooked.  If you look at the photos you can see how steep the last peak was.  The photo doesn't really do it justice!  Here is the hike:
 
http://www.localhikes.com/Hikes/Indian_Peak_2320.asp
 
My next letterbox was Silver Eagle's Mogollon Mask.  This was a REAL letterbox, not a hybrid geocache.  This was 24 Sept 2005.  It wasn't a tough hike, so my wife could come along.  We took two of the Texas Chihuahuas with us, Parker and Sammy.  Parker is the black one, Sammy is the pup.  The directions were a little confusing.  After this weekend, I am beginning to think that Silver Eagle is 8 feet tall, because his estimate of distance is a lot longer than it should be.  I bet he has 5 ft legs and one pace is 48 inches on him!  <grin>  But find it we did, and stamped up.  Then walked the dogs around the park for a while.
 
http://www.vonbonehenge.com/ourdogs.html
 
The very next day - 25 Sept 2006 - we did a Letterbox Hybrid called Baby Baylor, at Baylor Pass east of Las Cruces NM.  It was a nicely done one also.
 
And again the very next day, I placed my first Letterbox Hybrid Geocache.  It is called the Highpoint of El Paso. 
 
http://www.letterboxing.org/BoxView.php?boxnum=18163&boxname=The_High_Point_of_El_Paso
 
This name has a special meaning.  There was originally a Virtual Geocache up there, owned by a cacher called HighPointer.  When I asked him if he would consider deleting his cache so I could put a physical letterbox Hybrid Geocache up there, he agreed.  So up it went.  Here is the stamp.
 
 
It has a multi-color ink pad.  The cloud is blue.  The mountain is dk green, and the bottom is orange.
 
I have introduced my wife to Letterboxing.  She plays along - just for me.  Don't think she cares one way or the other otherwise.  My daughter has a stamp kit, but isn't doing much and not likely to plant a box.  My son the Cavalry Scout/Sniper/Paramedic in Colorado Springs......  just too far.  But a few weeks ago I got some friends interested.  Perhaps they will soon plant a box or two for me!
 
Sooo... that is my start.  5 Letterboxes placed, and 22 found.  Kinda hard to do when you own 70% of the boxes in your immediate area!  <grin>  There is still one box left here.  I have touched it.  I have seen the stamp.  But I didn't stamp in.  I am saving it for a rainy day.
 
Hope Y-all enjoyed the story.
 
Still need to give an After Action Report on the weekend at Abilene.  But we only now got the gear dried and put away.  Look for it soon.
 
Mike.  El Paso, Texas.

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2006-05-10 07:36:41 UTC-05:00
My introduction to letterboxing was serendipitous. I was an antique dealer in Connecticut and spent most of my weekends at "tag sales" (garage sales) hunting for treasures. One Saturday, I bought a lamp at a tag sale and the lady wrapped it in an old yellowed copy of the Danbury Post newspaper. When I got home and was unwrapping it, an article in that old paper caught my attention. It was a reporter's account of his weekend letterboxing adventure with some friends. I immediately went to the internet sites listed in the article (Connecticut Forestry Department Letterboxing and LBNA). Two hours later, my husband and I had located our first box. Five weeks later, I had found all thirty boxes hidden by the Forestry Department in each of their thirty forests in CT and "earned" my walking stick. Then I moved on to the LBNA site. Hiking has always been my favorite outdoor activity, so letterboxing was a natural fit! I've always been amazed by the incredibly interesting places I've discovered by letterboxing, but the most amazing part is the wonderful and fascinating people I've encountered through letterboxing.
Puddle Splasher
