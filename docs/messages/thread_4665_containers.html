just for what it's worth, i generally use the cheapest plastic thing i can find, usually a zip lock - brand food container.  at first i worried they would be too thin - but now with at least three boxes still in place three years later, no animals bit them, no rotting, no nothing, it's worked out very well (and cheap) for me, and I've stopped worrying about them

as another option, you can get the hard-core very strong plastic old-style plastic food containers that could resist anything, I think, very cheaply at the thrift store or yard sales

i think peanut butter jars would work great, myself, but i have never tried them

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2010-11-07 07:06:11 UTC-07:00

Heh heh heh – out here in the west – the sun would eat them alive.  At 4k ft + elevation, the UV rays destroy plastic.

 

I have a love for ammo cans.  Almost indestructible.  You can shoot them, and you can blow them up.  But they last for years otherwise.  Many of my plants are ammo cans.

 

Otherwise, I use the loch-n-lock boxes.  Not as long-lived as ammo cans, but with care they hold up.  Painting or covering with cammo duct-tale helps.

 

The other side of the equation is the finder.  A lot depends on the finder being careful to re-seal it and re-hide it.  Unfortunately, not all are as good as they could be.

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2010-11-08 11:27:07 UTC-05:00
Please, please avoid anything with any possible peanut contamination (even going through a dishwasher doesn't always remove all traces) -- peanut allergies can be deadly, and being exposed while in a slightly remote place increases the danger.  Jars break when they freeze.  Other plastic jars might work fine, but please be thoughtful about creating a potentially dangerous situation for someone.
