I know everyone is still sad over Blooming Flowers, but I would like to acknowledge a huge milestone in TX LB history. This last weekend, El Lobo placed letterboxes in the last 2 virgin counties (Crane & Terrell), thus allowing us to color in the entire state! I sent the webmasters an updated TX Clue map with all counties colored green and they have already posted it. I also uploaded the final map to the Files section of this List showing counties added by year since we started this quest in late 2006. You can also see an old TX Clue map in the Files section from that time period and marvel at how far we have come.

For those that don't know, in late 2006 we set a goal to color in all the counties of TX by placing at least 1 box in every county that did not have one. We set no time constraint, but I never dreamed we would complete it in less than 3 years. Thanks to all that contributed!

From: janetsredbarn (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2009-09-17 22:41:41 UTC
Because some of these boxes are very remote and don't get visited much, they tend to vulnerable. Many of them are the only box in the county. I have some fairly remote boxes that have been planted for almost a year and have never been found. If you get the opportunity to go find any of the boxes that are "on the road less traveled", why not consider taking a box to place. That way, if you don't find the planted box, you can leave one so that the next person has one to find. The recent box planted in Terrell County is very remote and El Lobo went considerably out of his way to place it. Thanks, El Lobo and Star Seeker, for placing those boxes. They live in Midland and so most of their placed boxes tend to be in the western part of the state, so maybe you haven't had the chance to find any, but I can tell you from experience that their boxes and stamps are of very high quality and worth going out of the way to find, should you find yourself out that way.

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2010-01-29 12:46:04 UTC-06:00

Through my bronchitis-hazed sleepless mind, I think I get what you are saying. Now that we're all green, where's the momentum to keep at it? Right?

The green field gives us the mistaken feeling that our job is done. It takes away the challenge of the frontier - of being the first to set foot on the moon, so to speak, or rather, to place in an unmarked county.

One box does not make a county an established letterboxing area, does it? For one thing, one box can go missing far more quickly than ten boxes. For another, it's harder to entice finders to go across one of those big honking huge West Texas counties to go after one box. Sad but true. Heck, folks don't even like to visit a relatively local park unless there are a bunch of boxes to find!

Personally, I don't mind a box sitting out there for months or even years without a finder - in fact, I have a two year old box (almost three years old) that has never been found in Arkansas. I really look forward to the day it is found so I can celebrate the experience of the location with the finder. My secret fear is that it is gone though. But one of these days it will either be found or I'll revisit it and know for sure. Anyway, all of this to say, one box has a lot more difficult time thriving than a few boxes and some placers get discouraged when their boxes aren't found.

On the other hand, my fear is that we overplant. I've said it before, so I won't dwell on it. But I just don't enjoy Easter egg hunting along a trail because part of my enjoyment of letterboxing is enjoying the location as I enjoy the letterboxing. A few well-placed boxes enhances the location - a plethora of boxes tossed along the trail takes away from it. But that's my .02...

I have to admit, I planted one box specifically so I could green a county (I think I greened some others, but they were boxes I wanted to plant specifically). It wasn't a great hide although everyone who has found it has said kind words about it, but I did it on my way to Arkansas in a county that really has no reason to exist. Once other boxes find their way into that county, it won't hurt my feelings for that box to go away - it was sort of a placeholder, rather than a hidden jewel.

It was exciting in the early days to look at that drab brown map and realize the state was wide open, or just about, for planting. And hasn't it been satisfying to watch it fill up? That's one way that I feel badly for new boxers coming along. They didn't get to experience the thrill of watching Texas become a bona fide letterboxing state. HOWEVER, they may not get to green a Texas county, but they can sure follow behind those of us who did and place better boxes than those placeholder boxes that are out there!

Some of you remember the JEN Barnraising - either because you attended or because there was much talk about it. We had a gathering for the specific purpose of planting boxes in Johnson, Ellis, and Navarro counties, just south of Dallas, which all had NO boxes at all. It was a planting gathering, not a finding gathering, although we had fun finding the boxes we had planted and trying to figure out Gryzzled Gryphon's clues for his contribution. We planted them, then left them behind as gifts to future boxers in that area - at that time there was only Delivery Girl, and she was brand new!

The beauty of letterboxing is in its creativity. All the counties may be covered, but that doesn't mean there are no more new and interesting ways to add to our great activity. Just as PI Joe recently changed the game up a bit by introducing Treasure Hiking, and TeamKing introduced the Tour Bus to us and has kept us entertained for a few years now, and just as Ruby Tuesday set out that unbelievably awesome Clue game at the second gathering, and as Dewberry and Mother of 5 have done some over the top gatherings with wickedly interesting twists and turns, and BootsTex and his Murder at the Arboretum, and Silver Eagle's game series, and Puddle Splasher lurks about trying to find ways to turn over the apple cart...the list goes on.

I truly don't feel that letterboxing has any limits as long as we keep our brains moving and our hands carving!

And now, back to my humidifier,

Barefoot Lucy
"Not about footwear, it's about philosophy!"

Anyway, all of this to say, one box has a lot more difficult time thriving than a few boxes and some placers get discouraged when their boxes aren't found.

On the other hand, my fear is that we overplant. I've said it before, so I won't dwell on it. But I just don't enjoy Easter egg hunting along a trail because part of my enjoyment of letterboxing is enjoying the location as I enjoy the letterboxing. A few well-placed boxes enhances the location - a plethora of boxes tossed along the trail takes away from it. But that's my .02...

I have to admit, I planted one box specifically so I could green a county (I think I greened some others, but they were boxes I wanted to plant specifically). It wasn't a great hide although everyone who has found it has said kind words about it, but I did it on my way to Arkansas in a county that really has no reason to exist. Once other boxes find their way into that county, it won't hurt my feelings for that box to go away - it was sort of a placeholder, rather than a hidden jewel.

It was exciting in the early days to look at that drab brown map and realize the state was wide open, or just about, for planting. And hasn't it been satisfying to watch it fill up? That's one way that I feel badly for new boxers coming along. They didn't get to experience the thrill of watching Texas become a bona fide letterboxing state. HOWEVER, they may not get to green a Texas county, but they can sure follow behind those of us who did and place better boxes than those placeholder boxes that are out there!

Some of you remember the JEN Barnraising - either because you attended or because there was much talk about it. We had a gathering for the specific purpose of planting boxes in Johnson, Ellis, and Navarro counties, just south of Dallas, which all had NO boxes at all. It was a planting gathering, not a finding gathering, although we had fun finding the boxes we had planted and trying to figure out Gryzzled Gryphon's clues for his contribution. We planted them, then left them behind as gifts to future boxers in that area - at that time there was only Delivery Girl, and she was brand new!

The beauty of letterboxing is in its creativity. All the counties may be covered, but that doesn't mean there are no more new and interesting ways to add to our great activity. Just as PI Joe recently changed the game up a bit by introducing Treasure Hiking, and TeamKing introduced the Tour Bus to us and has kept us entertained for a few years now, and just as Ruby Tuesday set out that unbelievably awesome Clue game at the second gathering, and as Dewberry and Mother of 5 have done some over the top gatherings with wickedly interesting twists and turns, and BootsTex and his Murder at the Arboretum, and Silver Eagle's game series, and Puddle Splasher lurks about trying to find ways to turn over the apple cart...the list goes on.

I truly don't feel that letterboxing has any limits as long as we keep our brains moving and our hands carving!

And now, back to my humidifier,

Barefoot Lucy
