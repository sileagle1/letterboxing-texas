From: LouC (lou.catozzi@att.net) | Date: 2009-12-30 00:32:46 UTC
I have volunteered to be the Texas state coordinator for Treasure Hiking. This is a program, started in North Carolina, that has letterboxers track their mileage as they plant and find boxes in various states. When you reach a total of 25 miles you are eligible for a PathTag specially designed for the occasion.

I have submitted a design for the Texas 25 Mile PathTag and am awaiting the design to be accepted and the coins struck. This should take about 4-6 weeks. Once the design is approved I will make an announcement that the program has been officially started and is available to anyone who finds or places letterboxes in Texas.

You can find out more about this program at several places:

http://www.treasurehikersusa.com/

http://www.atlasquest.com/about/wiki/browse.html?gCatId=14#q362

http://www.pathtags.com/

State Parks are a wonderful place to get some mileage in and there are some great boxes on State Park trails, but please remember - letterboxing in state parks is controversial at the moment, some parks are amenable and some aren't, some rangers are agreeable, others aren't, and there are precious few park staff that are up to speed on stashing games and not enough boxers that understand the permitting process yet.

It really hasn't been until recently that they began to enforce permitting and we became knowledgeable about it, so many boxers are still either making their minds up about permitting or catching up with permitting their SP boxes. I say all this to remind us all to be mindful that as we look for SP boxes, don't ask park staff to help since we don't know where they stand or what they know, and be sure to rehide well and keep good outdoor practices at the forefront so that undue attention is kept away from box locations. That way, boxes will stay intact until the placer decides how to proceed, and they won't have to fight off problems caused by bad boxing if they do go to get legal with their boxes.

So - yeah - now's a great time to go get that state park pass (I keep mine in my letterboxing backpack), but remember to keep stealth and good practices in hand when letterboxing in SP's!

Barefoot Lucy

Vicki,

Since I came into letterboxing from geocaching I have a GPSr and it keeps a track log of my travels, including a trip odometer. My wife likes to keep track her walks in the neighborhood and so I bought her a pedometer. You can pick them up at REI or Academy - Walmart probably has them as well.

One of the difficulties with long walks into a box is, how do you write up the clues? "850 steps after cross Barton Creek for the third time..." gets to be a little awkward. You have to be on the lookout for VERY prominent landmarks. Have you done the Battlestar Galactica series? If so, I'll bet you remember the fire hydrant in the middle of the stream.

Really, it is the placer of a box that needs to determine and list the total distance walked to get from the start of the clues to the box and back. For any boxes placed in Texas, I will be glad to assist in determining total distance. I have been hiking and mapping all the trails in all the parks and greenbelts I can find for years.

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-12-29 22:11:37 UTC-06:00

I've chosen to permit my boxes; however, I believe planters should have the opportunity to take whatever action they feel appropriate with respect to permitting and they should be given the time to act upon it. My only purpose in making the comments I made is to remind finders that by being sloppy in letterboxing habits and not using stealth, they are forcing the hands of the placers and may jeopardize current or future placements.

Mike, you seem to have the best of it where you are, and I feel it is due in large part to a lot of hard work and diligence on your part to get park staff educated and on board. It ain't that way everywhere - yet, anyway! Folks have stories of trying for months to get boxes permitted, only to get juggled around from ranger to ranger, none of whom are familiar with stashing games and make it a low priority to learn or to stay in contact with placers who are trying to get or stay legal. And some parks take the stance of "don't worry about permits", which is well and good until their head ranger moves on and a new one comes in who wants to be hard-nosed about boxes (and presumably caches too).

I believe that the issues we in this half of the state are running into can be overcome with time and effort, just as you have with the far western parks. In the meantime, good practices by finders makes it a whole lot easier on placers, and really it isn't asking any more than should be asked of finders, even if every box were permitted and completely legal!

That's what I'm sayin...

So - in light of PI Joe's new initiative, I've gotten to wondering. Where ARE significant trails in Texas? Yeah, I know - we can log even short city park type trails for this (and some of them could hardly be considered lightweight!) and they do add up, but I think it would be great to know where the trails are where we can get some distance.

We've touched on State Parks, and most of them have at least a couple miles of trails each, but what about other places? What if everyone throws out the trails they know of? Anybody know good hiking resources that would tell about trails? We've got a book on Texas Hiking Trails that's pretty good - too bad it's buried behind building supplies in my study at the moment!

Here's from my area: Tyler State Park and Mission Tejas SP are good hiking parks. Fairchild State Forest has about 12 miles of trails. There is the 4C trail between Weches and Ratcliff (about 20 miles of trails). Thinking if there are others....hmmm. Oh yeah, the SFA Experimental Forest has a good bit of trail.

One trail I keep trying to find information on is a rail trail I heard something about a few years ago east of DFW. It sounded intriguing, but I can't find anything about it.

Fairchild is by far the largest state forest in the state, so I'm not sure about other state forests and how much trail they have, but I have a fondness for state forests if anyone knows specifics about others. There's one near Mt Enterprise - I can't remember the name of it although I have a box there. It's only about 3/4 mile, but a nice trail full of wildlife.

I'm looking forward to looking more into this. For my part, I would hate to see us take the "If 10 are good, 20 would be better" approach to planting for the sake of getting double benefit out of the trail time (I kind of like the geocaching philosophy to space boxes 1/10th mile apart) but how it goes is how it goes, and I think this will still be a great new twist to the game!

Let us know how we can support you in this, PI Joe!

All the states that have signed up to start a Treasure Hiking program
have agreed to use the same guidelines. These are spelled out at:

http://www.treasurehikersusa.com/

but the short and sweet version is...

All hikes that exceed one mile round trip while following clues to an
existing letterbox or while planting a new letterbox count as a Treasure
Hike. 25 miles of Treasure Hikes qualifies you for a Texas 25 Mile
Treasure Hiking PathTag. These are available from me for $3 and a SASE.
