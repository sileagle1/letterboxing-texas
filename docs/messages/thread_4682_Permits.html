I'm getting in on this discussion a little late and I'm not sure what letterboxing has to do with guns except that I went to a Texas state park a couple of days agk and couldn't get in because they were allowing hunting that day. I wasn't involved in letterboxer back in the day when TPWD had a problem with geocaching, so I don't know that story. I can't say what Fredericksburg has against letterboxing or what prompted the decision to require permits in their parks. I do know that, until a few weeks ago, TPWD didn't have a clue about the existance of a thing called letterboxing. I have corresponding with Chris Holmes there who is in charge of geocaching activities in Texas State Parks and the first time I spoke to him, he told me that he had never heard of letterboxing. I have permitted letterboxes in 7 or 8 state parks and every time, I've had to use the geocache permit application because park personnel didn't know about letterboxing. I am working with Mr. Holmes and he has appointed a ranger from southeast Texas to be in charge of establishing a policy for letterboxing that will be equal to and similar to the geocaching policy. He has notified all park managers about the existence of letterboxing and has agreed to either give us our own separate permit application or include us equally on the existing geocache one. I have been assisted in this matter with a couple of our prominent long time letterboxers, who have given me good ideas and suggestions to present to TPWD. I met with Mr. Holmes's appointee, Robert Owen, last week at Huntsville State Park, along with Chrissy Halpen, the ranger in charge of HSP letterboxing and geocaching. We had a productive meeting and discussed the suggestions and ideas that I brought with me, including the permitting process and holding events in the parks, including the fact that we would probably place a lot of boxes during an event. These two rangers don't have a problem with that in theory as long as the boxes are permitted and placed by their rules. They don't by the way, look upon letterboxes as litter. They think that they are a viable activity for the parks and have asked if we would object to the promotion of the activiy in state parks. This is a work in progress, but all Texas State Parks should now be aware of letterboxing. There is a current policy in place lumped in with geocaching, but I think we will be recognized as an equal activity shortly. As of right now, you are allowed to place boxes in state parks. You should find out from park personnel what areas are off limits and follow their rules. I am still working on a working partnership with Ranger Owen and will let everyone know the result of those meetings when they are finalized. We may not get everything we ask for, but we can demonstrate to them by cooperating that we are willing to follow their rules when placing boxes in their parks. Building their trust in us will help us in our future dealings with them. The fact that nobody in Texas Parks and Wildlife are, or were, familiar with letterboxing, tells me that they haven't had any problems with us. I think it demontrates that we've been doing a good job with our hiding and with our events. They are looking for ways to improve attendence in parks and promote more appeal to younger people and letterboxing is one of the ways they can do that. If you experience a problem with letterboxing in Texas State Parks, lets talk it over and figure out how we can work with them. I don't think they'll ever let us completely police ourselves, because after all, they have bigger fish to fry, like hunting.

Boots Tex
