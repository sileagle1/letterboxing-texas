In a message dated 8/1/2005 10:26:17 AM Central Standard Time, letterboxingtexas@yahoogroups.com writes:
Many Native American women actually incorporate planned mistakes into their
artwork- whether it is a woven rug or a clay pot or beadwork on a dress.
They believe that to attempt to produce a perfect product would be hubris.

JOY
I've always adopted that Navajo philosophy myself - and I think I can safely say I'm avoiding approaching hubris with my stamp carving so far!  Mostly, they're fairly simple up to this point, but still, I've had some very nice comments on them, and I loved doing them, and I'm pretty proud of them (oops!  There's the hubris, after all!).  For me, the fun is in the finding of the LB, discovering the new place, often a really interesting or pretty view (my trail name, you know: Viewfinder), and in actually following the clues successfully to make the discovery!  The interesting logbooks are the next "gift" of letterboxing, and finally, the stamp is almost like the frosting on the cake!  I do love a beautiful, unique, funny, or interesting and well-carved stamp, but then, I also love just a simple one or even a store-bought one!  I love them all, and the fact that someone did all of this to provide for my letterboxing adventures is still amazing to me. 
And ...as to the rehiding issue: yes, for the first time, recently I've been finding my own boxes left exposed, or semi-exposed, and I really take great pains when planting to figure out good hiding places with good cover for them, and even to consider seasonal vegetation changes, possible flood paths, accidental discovery probability, and even where a stamper might comfortably sit to stamp in ... all of which takes me far more time than actually making the logbook, covering the box in camo tape, or even carving the stamp!  So the thought that someone would be careless or thoughtless enough just to leave it out, not replace items securely, or let it be exposed to casual walkers is disturbing!  I prefer to think that squirrels have displaced them, or heavy rains have moved them, or something.  So far, none have been lost...yet!  But I sympathize with those whose boxes have gone missing, because I've already found many of them and know what great boxes they were, and am grateful that they consider replacing them, sometimes several times.  What positive, hopeful, optimistic people Texas Letterboxes have to be to continue this activity in spite of weather, PI, BEA's and FIRE ANTS (they got me again last week! Got to stop wearing flip-flops on the trail!).
