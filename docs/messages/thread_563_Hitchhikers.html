From: celticlions (celticlions@comcast.net) | Date: 2005-01-14 09:41:01 UTC-06:00

A couple more things I should have added:

Letterboxing etiquette suggests that everyone should contact the creator of the HH to let them know where it was last found. That’s your “thank you” to someone who took the time to carve that stamp and add to your letterboxing pleasure with a neat surprise! It is also thoughtful to post to the travelers list or to Wanda&Pete’s Hitchhiker List to help track the HH…but only give the location where you found it – not the location in which you left it. If you read posts about hitchhikers you’ll notice that folks often give a little clue as to where they might have left it – but nothing specific.

 

One of the nicest things for me was Hawk’s recent email which detailed every entry in the Texas Tick HH logbook – thanks, Hawk!!!

A hitchhiker is like a tiny letterbox- with a stamp and logbook – usually they are about the size of a film canister or mint tin. Several of them have logbooks made of rolled up adding machine paper curled tightly around a tiny stamp and placed in a small container or plastic bag.

But don’t despair! Can you get back to that letterbox and add a stamp?

If not, would you like it mailed back to you?

 

Here’s a great site with all the basic info:

http://www.letterboxing.info/articles/

 

take care,

From: (teamking@entouch.net) | Date: 2005-01-14 11:25:56 UTC-05:00
Sounds like you are confusing letterboxing with geocaching. In letterboxing, yes, the hitchhiker would be a stamp. It can be a stamp by itself (with logbook) in a baggie, or in it's own box. Then there variations on hitchhikers - each with it's own name and travel idea. Check out the info on Wanda & Pete's Hitchhiker pages for the basics.

We don't usually leave items in boxes unless it is some sort of first finder prize, or other finder prize. In this case, the finder would keep it.

Hope that helps.

TeamKing

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-01-06 13:29:38 UTC
If you can find the owner of the HH, it's preferable to contact them
and let them know where you picked it up. In general, you don't share
where the HH is going to be placed/was left, although sometimes if I'm
going to take it a long way, I might say something like, "next stop:
Missouri." You generally also don't tell the box owner where you
placed the HH, it's just a nice surprise when they check out the
logbook.

Occasionally, there are HHs that are on a specific mission, like
racing across the US and in those cases, the owners definitely want to
know where you left it.

Many letterboxers are going to smaller containers, so sometimes,
particularly in an urban area, finding a HH-friendly container can be
a challenge!

DB

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2006-01-06 21:25:37 UTC
I usually contact the owner of the HH with all information
I have, but not the owner of the host box. I also try to
report where and when I found it on the Travelers Talklist.
One thing I have noticed that many people forget to do when
stamping in a HH to a new host box is to stamp the
host box stamp into the HH log, in addition to their
personal stamp. The HH log should contain stamp images
of all boxes and boxers it has come in contact with.

Hitchhikers

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-09-22 20:48:26 UTC-05:00

Funny how things go - ya don't hear from a hitchhiker for months, or even years, and suddenly you hear from two!

About five years ago, my daughter, SJZ, and I sat on the barge in the middle of the lake in the summertime, bored to tears while the guys fished. Tired of swatting mosquitoes, we decided to carve for awhile. The result was a handful of bug stamps, the better of which were carved by her.

We decided to release these as hitchhikers, so I set about making logbooks and getting them ready to go. At that time, I was fairly new to the idea of HH's, so I didn't think to put contact information in the logbook.

Off they went, "infesting" boxes around the area. Some of them we have never heard from again, one we hear from more often than others, but most we hear from every few months.

One of them was nearly lost - the finder tried desperately to figure out who it belonged to so they could report that it needed a logbook. Since I hadn't put contact information in the logbook, and since I didn't have that particular one listed (I had actually forgotten some of them), locating the launcher was really tough for her! She finally posted on the Traveller yahoo group and I HAPPENED to see it and I wrote to her. She graciously replaced the logbook, noted it with my contact information and sent it along its way and continued working to locate us. She sent the logbook along to me and I was fascinated to see where all this silent HH had been. It moved along and I have never heard from it again since then.

Time to Spray, on the other hand, communicates very frequently. It's an eerily realistic cockroach carved by SJZ. It moves almost constantly and has criss-crossed the country at least twice, and come back through East Texas at least once. I've sent new logbooks to it on at least three occasions and received the old ones back. I find it ironic that it turns up any and everywhere - proving the point that no one is immune to a roach infestation.

But I digress. I had recently thought how I hadn't heard from any of my HH's in quite a long time. Next thing I knew, I heard from two of them in one week! Flutterby, a neat little butterfly, turned up on the West Coast earlier this week and Time To Spray popped up again on the East Coast.

The thing I love about HH's is the surprise when I find them, but also the surprise of seeing a little note about them in my inbox, and even better, an old logbook in my mailbox.

--
Barefoot Lucy
"Not about footwear, it's about philosophy!"

> About five years ago, my daughter, SJZ, and I sat on the barge in the middle of the lake in the summertime, bored to tears while the guys fished. Tired of swatting mosquitoes, we decided to carve for awhile. The result was a handful of bug stamps, the better of which were carved by her.
>
>
> We decided to release these as hitchhikers, so I set about making logbooks and getting them ready to go. At that time, I was fairly new to the idea of HH's, so I didn't think to put contact information in the logbook.
>
>
> Off they went, "infesting" boxes around the area. Some of them we have never heard from again, one we hear from more often than others, but most we hear from every few months.
>
> One of them was nearly lost - the finder tried desperately to figure out who it belonged to so they could report that it needed a logbook. Since I hadn't put contact information in the logbook, and since I didn't have that particular one listed (I had actually forgotten some of them), locating the launcher was really tough for her! She finally posted on the Traveller yahoo group and I HAPPENED to see it and I wrote to her. She graciously replaced the logbook, noted it with my contact information and sent it along its way and continued working to locate us. She sent the logbook along to me and I was fascinated to see where all this silent HH had been. It moved along and I have never heard from it again since then.
>
>
> Time to Spray, on the other hand, communicates very frequently. It's an eerily realistic cockroach carved by SJZ. It moves almost constantly and has criss-crossed the country at least twice, and come back through East Texas at least once. I've sent new logbooks to it on at least three occasions and received the old ones back. I find it ironic that it turns up any and everywhere - proving the point that no one is immune to a roach infestation.
>
>
> But I digress. I had recently thought how I hadn't heard from any of my HH's in quite a long time. Next thing I knew, I heard from two of them in one week! Flutterby, a neat little butterfly, turned up on the West Coast earlier this week and Time To Spray popped up again on the East Coast.
>
>
> The thing I love about HH's is the surprise when I find them, but also the surprise of seeing a little note about them in my inbox, and even better, an old logbook in my mailbox.
