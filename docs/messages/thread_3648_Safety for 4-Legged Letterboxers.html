Safety for 4-Legged Letterboxers

From: WagTime2007 (WagTime2007@tx.rr.com) | Date: 2008-07-13 13:46:04 UTC-05:00
Many of us like to go boxing with our 4-legged companions, whether for company, safety, or exercise.  I ran across an article recently about heat exhaustion and heat stroke in dogs and wanted to share the information with you all. 
 
Heat illness is not uncommon, since dogs do not have an efficient method of handling heat stress; they don't sweat, and they never seem to figure out that they shouldn't get too excited or work hard in the heat either.  Heat stroke is most common in large breeds, and in dogs with short noses.  You know how quickly the heat can sneak up on you, but it can happen even faster for your dog.
 
Signs of heat exhaustion include heavy panting, hyperventilation, increased salivation followed by dry gums, weakness, confusion or inattention, vomiting or diarrhea.  As the condition progresses toward heat stroke, symptoms include weakness, loss of balance, excessive panting, roaring breathing sounds, decrease in mental awareness, pale or gray gums, shallow breathing, slowed or absent breathing, vomiting and diarrhea that may be bloody, collapse, seizures or coma. Death from heat stroke can occur pretty quickly.
 
If the dog begins showing signs of distress from the heat, it is best to cool him/her immediately with cool (not really cold) water (as in pour it over the dog).  Ice packs can be applied to the "armpits" or neck for heat exhaustion, but may be too intense for heat stroke.  Blowing air over the dog with a fan as you cool him off with water can also be helpful.  Most dogs will not drink water during heat stroke, so offer it but don't waste time trying to talk him into drinking. As soon as the dog seems more comfortable, get to a veterinarian, since delayed complications can occur and the dog needs to be monitored closely.
 
Please keep this info handy and be extra attentive to your furry kids during these hot summer months.
