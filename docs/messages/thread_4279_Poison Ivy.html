Cortaid has a new poison ivy kit that helps. The kit comes with a bottle of cleaner for when you think you may have the oils on you but don't see the effects yet. You use it like soap but it cleans the poison ivy oils from your skin without affecting any other part of you. It also comes with an anti itch spray for those spots its too late to clean, and the spray keeps you from touching it and aggravating the spot which makes it itch more! There is another product you can find in the first aid section that is wipes that you can clean yourself with immediately following poison ivy exposure that will clean you off, but if you weren't paying attention anyway it wont help to have it. I ended up getting poison ivy this morning while letterboxing. darn it. Cream me up and I'm on my way. I also use Calamine lotion once in a while for the really bad spots to dry them out, this may just be an old-wives-tail though.
 
Hope this helps...

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-11-06 15:32:05 UTC-06:00
My daughter is terribly sensitive to PI, and my sensitivity has been going up in recent years.
There are several good products out there for PI, but common sense goes along way too, if you understand what poison ivy is. The Big Three (Poison Ivy, Poison Oak, and Poison Sumac) all have the same active agent - urushiol. It's an oily kind of stuff that gets on your clothes and skin and absorbs to create the rash and itch and all the lovely things that go with it.
So - maybe you can help prevent PI by rubbing on a good lotion to create sort of a skin barrier against the absorption of the oil. Maybe it works, maybe it doesn't, but what's the harm and the logic is there... There are also products out there that claim to prevent absorption, like Ivy Block. I've read mixed reviews on it and have only really tried any of those products once or twice before I lose the tube (my theory is - it isn't any good unless you carry it with you, so by the time I switch backpacks a few times between uses, who knows which one it's in).
I've also found it to be very effective to scrub down with dishwashing liquid after I think I might have been exposed. Dishwashing liquid cuts grease on your dishes - why shouldn't it cut oil on your skin? I've been known to go into the bathroom in a park and strip down to scrub down if I thought I had gotten a really good exposure and wasn't going to get home in time to use one of the other post-exposure products. I carry a small bottle of dishwasing liquid in all my backpacks for just this reason and it's cheap enough to have lots of it around. I truly believe this works - there have been times when I've suddenly looked around and realized I was ankle deep in a patch of the stuff and had no way out without touching it - by all rights I should have gotten the rash, but I didn't, and I believe it's because I scrubbed afterward. HOWEVER, the problem with scrubbing down like this is that you have to put the same clothes back on and if the oil is on your skin, it's on your clothes too, you just cut all the urushiol AND the protective oils in your skin right before you re-exposed it to urushiol. So if you like the idea of scrubbing, you need to also have a change of clothes. I always have a change of clothes anyway myself because I have a habit of getting wet and grody when I'm outdoors.
The best product I have found for post-exposure is Zanfel - a gritty cream that you wet and rub in your hand, then apply to the exposed area. It works well after you see the telltale rash and the itching begins. It's really expensive, but it doesn't take much when you use it so a tube goes a long time. The other product I really like is Technu - there are several different versions of it, but I try to carry a tube of it in my letterboxing backpack because it washes off a little easier than Zanfel and you can apply it after you think you have been exposed.
As far as treating it after you have the rash, the Zanfel works very well for that too, but you can also take allergy medicines like Benadryl or Claritin or Zyrtec to help the itch. The rash needs to be kept dry and clean, with maybe a little moisture to keep it from opening up.
Currently PI free but about to go see about getting exposed,
BarefootLucy (on my way to Camp Pirtle to hang out with 150 of my closest friends)

Poison Ivy

From: Boots Tex (redbarn@eastex.net) | Date: 2010-06-05 19:27:38 UTC
I've been letterboxing for five years, found over a thousand letterboxes, mostly in East Texas, home to poison ivy. I'm certain I have come into contact with PI a few times, but have never experienced any symptoms. I had come to believe that I was immune. However, a week ago I was working around the place, pulling weeds and vines, mowing and weed-eating. I noticed a few red spots on my arms, but attributed them to chiggers, our constant companion in this part of the state. But, a couple of days later, the spots flared up and started itching like no chigger bite I had ever had. I assume this is poison ivy because the itching is pretty much constant. If it is, I have a relatively mild case, for which I'm grateful, but I'll be more careful in the future, knowing now that I am not immune the effects of this dastardly weed. I'd suggest that you be careful as well.

Boots Tex

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2010-06-05 21:40:12 UTC-05:00
The same thing happened to me - I went for years and never got it and then one year (in fact, since I started letterboxing), I got my first case and I get it pretty much every year.
Zanfel is pricey, but you can rub it on the affected area at the first itch (even if it is a few days after exposure) and it will knock that itch down nicely.
If I know I'm about to be exposed (i.e. walking outside), I rub on a coat of Tecnu and it seems to ward off the erushiol oil. You can also scrub down with good ol' soap (I like dish soap because of its grease-cutting power) and water right after I've been tromping about.
Welcome to the world of PI effects...I'm sorry!
BarefootLucy
