West Texas Letterboxer

From: janetsredbarn (janetsredbarn@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-07-17 19:14:04 UTC
I don't know how many of y'all keep up with letterboxing in far West
Texas, but there is a 'boxer out there named El Lobo who is trying to
place a letterbox in every county. So far he has boxes hidden in
Crockett, Dawson, Ector, Jeff Davis, Loving, Midland, Reagan, Reeves,
Upton, and Winkler counties and two in Pecos County. He's the person
who introduced us to letterboxing and isn't one to toot his own horn,
so I thought I needed to toot it for him. His boxes are informative,
fun (check out "The Lesser of Two Weevils), and challenging (have you
ever been to Mentone? see "Everybody's Got to be Somewhere") and his
stamps are awsome. Just thought I'd let you know that when you travel
in the west, you'll have some great boxes to find.

Boots Tex

Re: [letterboxingtexas] Re: West Texas Letterboxer

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-07-18 21:48:40 UTC-05:00
Hi Lore!
 
I would like to caution you about getting publicity from the newspaper.  While it sounds like a quick fix to an obvious problem, it does present its own pitfalls and it doesn't solve the problem as quickly as you might think.
 
A local reporter did an article about letterboxing here in my town, and I firmly believe that article had a direct impact on letterboxing picking up around here.  HOWEVER, those of us who were interviewed by the reporter (Silver Eagle, Baby Bear, and me - because we were the boxers with boxes in my county) were diligent to make it very clear to her that she would be violating the spirit of letterboxing if she were to hand readers information on a platter.  She did a pretty good job of presenting the hobby and respecting the secrecy of it, and she took some time to go with my family to hide a box we had made and to talk to us about finding boxes.  She got a pretty good grip on the spirit of things, and overall we were pleased with the article - I think if one of us had written it, it would've been better, but we didn't.
 
The article was very good, and there WAS an influx of boxers.  Many of them, though, only found one or two boxes and then lost interest because of the scarcity of the boxes and because they were only marginally interested.  Some of them approached it with the wrong attitude and expected that those of us who were already in the game should've handed it up to them and made it easy.  Others just never quite got it (I do believe letterboxers are of above average intelligence).  The long and short was we increased our boxer count by "1".  There were a few others who got involved right around the same time, and perhaps the article reinforced what they had learned elsewhere.  We still have very few boxes, even though we have a lot of boxes now!

The biggest point to be made is this - scope out reporters before you drop the idea in someone's ear.  Find one you like and whose style seems to be compatible to the letterboxing mentality.  Then approach them directly.  Set a very clear framework for what you expect from the article - no secrets revealed, no spoilers, no gimme.  Ask them to broaden the scope of the article and to focus on the benefits of letterboxing rather than on letterboxing itself.
 
But before you go to the paper, I really suggest you plant a good six or eight boxes so they are ready to be found when the article goes out.  As you are probably aware, no matter how intrigued you become by letterboxing, it's really hard to stay interested if there isn't anything around to sink your teeth into.  Once you plant though, realize that by publicizing letterboxing, you may be exposing your boxes to half-hearted care until the new boxers get a feel for good boxing.
 
Before I went to the paper, I might consider going to Barnes and Noble and putting out a flyer to invite folks to a meeting about treasure hunting/letterboxing, or maybe to a local college or learning facility.  Try to think of places that cerebral outdoorsy folk might hang out. 
 
By no means do I say don't publicize, but think broadly enough about it that you are covering the possible unintended consequences that could come of publicity.  It CAN be a good thing, but you have to keep control of the process!
 
Lucy
