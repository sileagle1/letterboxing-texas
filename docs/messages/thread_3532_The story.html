The story

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2008-05-01 21:59:09 UTC-06:00
OK - a few have asked, so I will share. Delete it if you are not interested.
But this may show you what a simple word of thanks may mean to a soldier.

This dude was in the Air Force in Viet Nam. But he wasn't your typical
wing-nut. He was jump-qualified, and spent a whole lot of time in Indian
country setting up navigation beacons for the arc-light missions and other
stuff. He saw the elephant so to speak.

He is now retired, a ham operator in CA. He has adopted a number of soldiers by
daily flying a flag in their honor, and sending them a goodie box every few
weeks. My son is one of them.

Here is the story he shared with my son in a letter inside a package.. He
always sends me a copy. On a recent trip home he was chatting with his wife
about "their soldiers".


In his own words......

On the trip home, we were talking about our troops and how to convince them that
their service is their gift to us, and she suggested my Idaho story. We'll give
it a try and hopefully you'll understand. It too takes place in combat but is
not a combat story. We were about 2 years into the war, we'd finished all the
NVN TACAN missions, and were now either supporting infantry units with comm, or
doing missions along the HCM Trail for the intel guys. One of my guys, Terry,
was from a very small town in Idaho where he had a wife, young son, Mom, Dad,
and a little brother. In fact, he was my only married troop.

When we were on bases prepping for a mission, we wore regular fatigues, didn't
have to carry our rifles, and like everyone else, had baseball caps with our
unit name embroidered on the front. Ours were black, and I'm wearing one in the
photo [our name led to the nickname "1st MOB" which was sort of cool]. Terry
had sent two of them home for his son and brother and the whole town adopted us.
Their first package arrived just as we were starting a 60-day mission in the NW
of RVN on the border with Laos. We were refueled and resupplied weekly by
helicopter, and each time they dropped off a mail sack, there'd be a town
package in it.

Each package would have snacks, candy, games, magazines, the little newspaper
for Terry, and letters for each of us . each time your letter would be written
by a different family in town. Everything was packed in pop corn which we ate
too. Like most of our missions, we were alone on this one, and the packages
really meant a lot to us. The people talked to us like we were theirs, and the
letters almost always would have a family photo, usually decorated by the kids
in the picture. They usually had kids' drawings and the like as well.

About a month into the mission, the refueling A/C arrived and dropped off
another mail sack. It had the usual package from the town which had the normal
stuff and letters. But in addition, there was a large envelope. In the
envelope was an 8x10 photo. It had been taken in front of the bleachers on the
ball field at the school. Terry's wife was seated in the center of the front
row holding their son. His Mom, Dad, and Brother were seated on one side of
her. On her other side was a sign that said "TERRY." The rest of the front row
had signs with our names or nicknames on them. The entire town was seated
behind, and everyone in the photo, including babies and two dogs, were wearing
our black hats.

James, we had probably 30 or more combat missions under our belts by this time.
In addition to the two Airmen I told you about, I had also lost one of my
Soldiers and one of my Marines by this time. We were sort of hardened; I had
been told more than once that we were a bit fatalistic; our humor tended to be
fairly dark; and, with the occasional harassment we were getting, we were fairly
jumpy. Despite all that, the photo put all 23 of us in tears. It was like a
huge hug from across the Pacific. The feeling of "belonging" and that these
people really did care about us was overwhelming.

We had been sort of rotating the "write back" duties. We had nothing material
to send to them, but we did have an AF Polaroid camera and some film. We took a
group photo with the timer, but it was disappointing . the print was fairly
small, you couldn't really see our faces with our gear on, and we didn't really
dare take it off. We didn't have enough film for individual pictures so we took
them in pairs. Everyone wrote a letter to the town, and we sent the whole thing
to our COC who mailed it for us. About the end of the mission, we heard that
they had gotten our package, and had put the photos and our letters into a
little display in the Town Hall where they had some historical things.

Terry was subsequently injured and ended up in the AF hospital at Lackland AFB
in San Antonio. We sort of figured that would end the package stream, but when
the town said we were "adopted," they really meant it and every week to ten
days, we'd get the package. When supporting the infantry, we shared the stuff
of course, and the guys were really stunned at how much support we got. We all
knew by this time that the war was hugely unpopular at home, and I can't believe
that everyone in the town felt good about it, but they really had made the
separation between the war and the warriors. I finally wrote to them and told
them we had received a rotation date and would be coming home soon. We got one
more package from them with an invitation to all of us to visit, and thanking us
for our service. The photo is Terry earlier at a new base being built. Our
Commander had given the mission to us as a stand-down for about a month.
Years later, like 1989 maybe, I was an engineer working for a defense
contractor, and I had occasion to go to Idaho Falls for a couple days of project
meetings with one of our sub-contractors. I realized I was close to the town
and might not get another chance, so I called the office, told them I was taking
another day, and drove up after my meetings. I found the town . it looked
pretty much as I had imagined it from Terry's descriptions. Terry and his
family were long gone of course, but I found the Town Hall, on a town square
which was a park. And inside, in a corner, I found the display. They had
enlarged our group photo to 8x10 [pretty grainy], and it was next to theirs.
Our others were arranged around them, and our letters were in plastic protectors
in a loose leaf binder.

Seeing their photo was an instant transport in time and space for me, and I
didn't hold things together much better than when I first saw it. That same
feeling of belonging came over me and I'm not sure how long I stood there
looking at it. I lost two more of my Airmen after that mission, and seeing
their faces in the photos was both really great and really tough. I leafed
through the binder and found my letter.
About this time, a fairly elderly woman came up behind me and asked if I'd like
to hear the whole story behind the display. She startled me [I was still 10,000
miles and 20 years away], and with my usual social ineptness, I blurted out, "I
know the story ma'am, I'm Skip, that's me with TJ in that photo, and I wrote
this letter." At this point she had her heart attack. Well . not quite, but
she did a pretty good impression of one. She made me promise not to move, and
disappeared through one of the doors.

Very shortly, the 5 or 6 people who worked in the Town Hall came out and met me.
I tried to thank them but it was really difficult to find the right words. News
apparently travels fast in a small town because within minutes, people were
streaming into the building. Some were older than me and were the people who
had written all those letters and packed the packages. Some were younger than
me and were the kids in all those family photos we got. Lots of hugs,
introductions, and me trying to thank them repeatedly. The school was a block
away, it let out, and the kids began streaming through the door. Kodak stock
probably doubled that day with all the pictures being taken. I finally had to
leave to catch my flight. I did my best but I don't think I really succeeded in
explaining to them the impact their support had for me and my guys. At least I
got to try. They all just kept telling me that we had already thanked them with
our service. Two others have made it there, had a similar experience, and also
believe they failed to adequately thank them. They too got the same . "You've
already thanked us with your service."

Terry recovered, remained in the AF, and retired as an E-8. They had another
boy, and finally settled in FL where until recently, he was in real estate
development. They are grandparents to a half-dozen, and, thanks to Brent's kids
[Brent was the kid on his Mom's lap in the photo] will become great grandparents
about the same time we do this summer. Brent enlisted in the AF [after Vietnam,
thank God!], served 22 years and also retired as an E-8. Brent got a degree in
computer science on the GI Bill and is currently working in North Carolina in
the "Research Triangle" area. His brother Mike became a Marine, served four
years, also got a degree on the GI Bill, and is currently in law enforcement in
Alabama. As may be obvious, we are in fairly close contact, now thanks to
email.

So James, take two things away from this story: I know, better than nearly
everyone, what it means to get support from American strangers when you're in
combat; and, just like the folks in that little town, you thank us every day
with your service to our Nation . far more thanks than we deserve.

Stay safe James. You and your comrades are truly awesome! Heroes are hard to
find these days, but we're pretty lucky . we've got a number of them and you're
one.

There you have it. What a wonderful person. Not because he adopted my son who
is also a ham operator. But because my son is one of a number of soldiers he
adopted.

I can think of no other words to add. Hope you enjoyed it.

I have a few adopted soldiers of my one, and I got their names from adoptaussoldier.org   You can register on their site and they will send you a name, address, email, and any personal info that the solder gave them.  There is also information available about what the soldiers need/want, and how to best send your packages.  I got an email from them just the other day saying that they had an influx of soldiers registering for support, and not enough supporters to cover it.   If you want other ideas for goodies to send, email me off-line and I'll be glad to help you out, although Mike and James obviously have the best first-hand knowledge!

Their first package arrived just as we were starting a 60-day mission in the NW
of RVN on the border with Laos. We were refueled and resupplied weekly by
helicopter, and each time they dropped off a mail sack, there'd be a town
package in it.

Each package would have snacks, candy, games, magazines, the little newspaper
for Terry, and letters for each of us . each time your letter would be written
by a different family in town. Everything was packed in pop corn which we ate
too. Like most of our missions, we were alone on this one, and the packages
really meant a lot to us. The people talked to us like we were theirs, and the
letters almost always would have a family photo, usually decorated by the kids
in the picture. They usually had kids' drawings and the like as well.

About a month into the mission, the refueling A/C arrived and dropped off
another mail sack. It had the usual package from the town which had the normal
stuff and letters. But in addition, there was a large envelope. In the
envelope was an 8x10 photo. It had been taken in front of the bleachers on the
ball field at the school. Terry's wife was seated in the center of the front
row holding their son. His Mom, Dad, and Brother were seated on one side of
her. On her other side was a sign that said "TERRY." The rest of the front row
had signs with our names or nicknames on them. The entire town was seated
behind, and everyone in the photo, including babies and two dogs, were wearing
our black hats.

James, we had probably 30 or more combat missions under our belts by this time.
In addition to the two Airmen I told you about, I had also lost one of my
Soldiers and one of my Marines by this time. We were sort of hardened; I had
been told more than once that we were a bit fatalistic; our humor tended to be
fairly dark; and, with the occasional harassment we were getting, we were fairly
jumpy. Despite all that, the photo put all 23 of us in tears. It was like a
huge hug from across the Pacific. The feeling of "belonging" and that these
people really did care about us was overwhelming.

We had been sort of rotating the "write back" duties. We had nothing material
to send to them, but we did have an AF Polaroid camera and some film. We took a
group photo with the timer, but it was disappointing . the print was fairly
small, you couldn't really see our faces with our gear on, and we didn't really
dare take it off. We didn't have enough film for individual pictures so we took
them in pairs. Everyone wrote a letter to the town, and we sent the whole thing
to our COC who mailed it for us. About the end of the mission, we heard that
they had gotten our package, and had put the photos and our letters into a
little display in the Town Hall where they had some historical things.

Terry was subsequently injured and ended up in the AF hospital at Lackland AFB
in San Antonio. We sort of figured that would end the package stream, but when
the town said we were "adopted," they really meant it and every week to ten
days, we'd get the package. When supporting the infantry, we shared the stuff
of course, and the guys were really stunned at how much support we got. We all
knew by this time that the war was hugely unpopular at home, and I can't believe
that everyone in the town felt good about it, but they really had made the
separation between the war and the warriors. I finally wrote to them and told
them we had received a rotation date and would be coming home soon. We got one
more package from them with an invitation to all of us to visit, and thanking us
for our service. The photo is Terry earlier at a new base being built. Our
Commander had given the mission to us as a stand-down for about a month.
Years later, like 1989 maybe, I was an engineer working for a defense
contractor, and I had occasion to go to Idaho Falls for a couple days of project

another day, and drove up after my meetings. I found the town . it looked
pretty much as I had imagined it from Terry's descriptions. Terry and his
family were long gone of course, but I found the Town Hall, on a town square
which was a park. And inside, in a corner, I found the display. They had
enlarged our group photo to 8x10 [pretty grainy], and it was next to theirs.
Our others were arranged around them, and our letters were in plastic protectors
in a loose leaf binder.

Seeing their photo was an instant transport in time and space for me, and I
didn't hold things together much better than when I first saw it. That same
feeling of belonging came over me and I'm not sure how long I stood there

Mike Olbrisch <mike-2007@elp.rr.com> wrote:
OK - a few have asked, so I will share. Delete it if you are not interested.
But this may show you what a simple word of thanks may mean to a soldier.

This dude was in the Air Force in Viet Nam. But he wasn't your typical
wing-nut. He was jump-qualified, and spent a whole lot of time in Indian
country setting up navigation beacons for the arc-light missions and other
stuff. He saw the elephant so to speak.

He is now retired, a ham operator in CA. He has adopted a number of soldiers by
daily flying a flag in their honor, and sending them a goodie box every few
weeks. My son is one of them.

Here is the story he shared with my son in a letter inside a package.. He
always sends me a copy. On a recent trip home he was chatting with his wife
about "their soldiers".

In his own words......

On the trip home, we were talking about our troops and how to convince them that
their service is their gift to us, and she suggested my Idaho story. We'll give
it a try and hopefully you'll understand. It too takes place in combat but is
not a combat story. We were about 2 years into the war, we'd finished all the
NVN TACAN missions, and were now either supporting infantry units with comm, or
doing missions along the HCM Trail for the intel guys. One of my guys, Terry,
was from a very small town in Idaho where he had a wife, young son, Mom, Dad,
and a little brother. In fact, he was my only married troop.

When we were on bases prepping for a mission, we wore regular fatigues, didn't
have to carry our rifles, and like everyone else, had baseball caps with our
unit name embroidered on the front. Ours were black, and I'm wearing one in the
photo [our name led to the nickname "1st MOB" which was sort of cool]. Terry
had sent two of them home for his son and brother and the whole town adopted us.
Their first package arrived just as we were starting a 60-day mission in the NW
of RVN on the border with Laos. We were refueled and resupplied weekly by
helicopter, and each time they dropped off a mail sack, there'd be a town
package in it.

Each package would have snacks, candy, games, magazines, the little newspaper
for Terry, and letters for each of us . each time your letter would be written
by a different family in town. Everything was packed in pop corn which we ate
too. Like most of our missions, we were alone on this one, and the packages
really meant a lot to us. The people talked to us like we were theirs, and the
letters almost always would have a family photo, usually decorated by the kids
in the picture. They usually had kids' drawings and the like as well.

About a month into the mission, the refueling A/C arrived and dropped off
another mail sack. It had the usual package from the town which had the normal
stuff and letters. But in addition, there was a large envelope. In the
envelope was an 8x10 photo. It had been taken in front of the bleachers on the
ball field at the school. Terry's wife was seated in the center of the front
row holding their son. His Mom, Dad, and Brother were seated on one side of
her. On her other side was a sign that said "TERRY." The rest of the front row
had signs with our names or nicknames on them. The entire town was seated
behind, and everyone in the photo, including babies and two dogs, were wearing
our black hats.

James, we had probably 30 or more combat missions under our belts by this time.
In addition to the two Airmen I told you about, I had also lost one of my
Soldiers and one of my Marines by this time. We were sort of hardened; I had
been told more than once that we were a bit fatalistic; our humor tended to be
fairly dark; and, with the occasional harassment we were getting, we were fairly
jumpy. Despite all that, the photo put all 23 of us in tears. It was like a
huge hug from across the Pacific. The feeling of "belonging" and that these
people really did care about us was overwhelming.

We had been sort of rotating the "write back" duties. We had nothing material
to send to them, but we did have an AF Polaroid camera and some film. We took a
group photo with the timer, but it was disappointing . the print was fairly
small, you couldn't really see our faces with our gear on, and we didn't really
dare take it off. We didn't have enough film for individual pictures so we took
them in pairs. Everyone wrote a letter to the town, and we sent the whole thing
to our COC who mailed it for us. About the end of the mission, we heard that
they had gotten our package, and had put the photos and our letters into a
little display in the Town Hall where they had some historical things.

Terry was subsequently injured and ended up in the AF hospital at Lackland AFB
in San Antonio. We sort of figured that would end the package stream, but when
the town said we were "adopted," they really meant it and every week to ten
days, we'd get the package. When supporting the infantry, we shared the stuff
of course, and the guys were really stunned at how much support we got. We all
knew by this time that the war was hugely unpopular at home, and I can't believe
that everyone in the town felt good about it, but they really had made the
separation between the war and the warriors. I finally wrote to them and told
them we had received a rotation date and would be coming home soon. We got one
more package from them with an invitation to all of us to visit, and thanking us
for our service. The photo is Terry earlier at a new base being built. Our
Commander had given the mission to us as a stand-down for about a month.
Years later, like 1989 maybe, I was an engineer working for a defense
contractor, and I had occasion to go to Idaho Falls for a couple days of project
meetings with one of our sub-contractors. I realized I was close to the town
and might not get another chance, so I called the office, told them I was taking
another day, and drove up after my meetings. I found the town . it looked
pretty much as I had imagined it from Terry's descriptions. Terry and his
family were long gone of course, but I found the Town Hall, on a town square
which was a park. And inside, in a corner, I found the display. They had
enlarged our group photo to 8x10 [pretty grainy], and it was next to theirs.
Our others were arranged around them, and our letters were in plastic protectors
in a loose leaf binder.

Seeing their photo was an instant transport in time and space for me, and I
didn't hold things together much better than when I first saw it. That same
feeling of belonging came over me and I'm not sure how long I stood there
looking at it. I lost two more of my Airmen after that mission, and seeing
their faces in the photos was both really great and really tough. I leafed
through the binder and found my letter.
About this time, a fairly elderly woman came up behind me and asked if I'd like
to hear the whole story behind the display. She startled me [I was still 10,000
miles and 20 years away], and with my usual social ineptness, I blurted out, "I
know the story ma'am, I'm Skip, that's me with TJ in that photo, and I wrote
this letter." At this point she had her heart attack. Well . not quite, but
she did a pretty good impression of one. She made me promise not to move, and
disappeared through one of the doors.

Very shortly, the 5 or 6 people who worked in the Town Hall came out and met me.
I tried to thank them but it was really difficult to find the right words. News
apparently travels fast in a small town because within minutes, people were
streaming into the building. Some were older than me and were the people who
had written all those letters and packed the packages. Some were younger than
me and were the kids in all those family photos we got. Lots of hugs,
introductions, and me trying to thank them repeatedly. The school was a block
away, it let out, and the kids began streaming through the door. Kodak stock
probably doubled that day with all the pictures being taken. I finally had to
leave to catch my flight. I did my best but I don't think I really succeeded in
explaining to them the impact their support had for me and my guys. At least I
got to try. They all just kept telling me that we had already thanked them with
our service. Two others have made it there, had a similar experience, and also
believe they failed to adequately thank them. They too got the same . "You've
already thanked us with your service."

Terry recovered, remained in the AF, and retired as an E-8. They had another
boy, and finally settled in FL where until recently, he was in real estate
development. They are grandparents to a half-dozen, and, thanks to Brent's kids
[Brent was the kid on his Mom's lap in the photo] will become great grandparents
about the same time we do this summer. Brent enlisted in the AF [after Vietnam,
thank God!], served 22 years and also retired as an E-8. Brent got a degree in
computer science on the GI Bill and is currently working in North Carolina in
the "Research Triangle" area. His brother Mike became a Marine, served four
years, also got a degree on the GI Bill, and is currently in law enforcement in
Alabama. As may be obvious, we are in fairly close contact, now thanks to
email.

So James, take two things away from this story: I know, better than nearly
everyone, what it means to get support from American strangers when you're in
combat; and, just like the folks in that little town, you thank us every day
with your service to our Nation . far more thanks than we deserve.

Stay safe James. You and your comrades are truly awesome! Heroes are hard to
find these days, but we're pretty lucky . we've got a number of them and you're
one.

There you have it. What a wonderful person. Not because he adopted my son who
is also a ham operator. But because my son is one of a number of soldiers he
adopted.

I can think of no other words to add. Hope you enjoyed it.
