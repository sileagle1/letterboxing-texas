From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2008-10-31 20:53:11 UTC
This brings up a point I've often wondered about: How important is
the logbook? In other words, is stamping into the letterbox logbook
as important to you as getting the stamp image into your logbook?
Life in the letterboxing world would be much simpler if we did not
use physical logbooks since they will always fill up given enough
time, and they usually get wet or damaged, causing us to make long
treks to replace them or depend on the kindness of others. The
stamp, however, usually lasts much longer and could even get wet
without too much damage. Now I admit it is fun to see the logs that
do make it back to me, but many of the boxes I place are so far away
that I don't have any realistic hope of ever seeing the log again.
So in most cases I would prefer to see some kind of online log of a
visit or an email, since that is more immediate and personal. I
guess I'm just curious: how upset would you be if you found a
letterbox with no logbook, but instead prompted you to log your visit
online?

As to why UT generates letterboxers and A&M doesn't, I'm sure there
is an Aggie joke that would explain it!

That's an interesting thought, and one I had never considered. Some
logbooks I have seen are lovely and are works of art in themselves,
and I enjoy finding those kind, although it isn't essential to the
box. For traditionalists, I guess you could say that the logbook
predates the stamp, as the first boxes were simply a record of who
had passed that way. I, too, enjoy flipping through the book to see
which famous letterboxer stood in my place and put his stamp in the
book, and seeing stamps and names that I had never heard of before
and wondering who they were and what brought them to this place. But
gosh, it sure would be easy to stamp and run. No telling how many
more boxes I could get in a day. And, you're right about the fact
that, without logbooks, a box would probably last much longer. I
don't know, I'm still trying to get my mind around this thought. How
many Aggies does it take to hide a letterbox? Well, you need to find
one first.......

Awww BootsTex, you ARE a famous letterboxer!
 
Silver Eagle, I know what you mean about it being easier not having to worry with a box's logbook, but I think omitting the logbook would change the dynamics of the game completely!
 
Let me throw a couple of ideas out:
 
1) Many traditionalist letterboxers don't record their finds online.  They leave a note in the logbook as a surprise for the placer to discover.  I have to confess - I am seriously considering adopting this approach myself, which would mean I would have to get better at writing notes in logbooks.
 
2) When I am letterboxing with someone else who isn't a letterboxer, I often find myself pushing the box's logbook at them to go through, and then wind up telling funny stories about the people behind the stamps in the logbook.  I enjoy thinking about all of y'all while I'm letterboxing, and introducing y'all to them through the logbook.
 
3) One of my personal precepts is leaving a legacy, and I like to think that part of my letterboxing legacy is in the stamps I leave behind in boxes.  There are times I am sorely tempted NOT to write my little ditty because it takes so long and I usually manage not to have a writing pen with me so it's a pain in the rear to dig one out of some remote pocket of my backpack, but then I realize it sort of describes my philosophy toward life, and if someone were to find only one box that I had been to visit, and I had slouched on writing my ditty, they might not know that one little thing about me.
 
4) And what about those boxers who have gone before us?  The logbook is where we now find records of Ruby Tuesday and the Celtic Lions, and Boxer In TRAINing.
 
I know an online logbook would stay nice and clean and it wouldn't get ruined or destroyed and it wouldn't disappear, but you can't touch it and turn the pages and see the difference in ink or see how families tried really hard to stamp together on one page.  It doesn't have an earthy aroma when you open the ziploc because 1) there is no ziploc and 2) there is no earth.  I realize some of those imperfections create more difficulty for the placer, but as far as I am concerned as a placer, it is well worth it.
 
And another reason I would prefer a paper log to an online one - I spend wayyyy too much time on the computer anyway between work and other endeavours, so I try really hard to stay OFF the computer as it relates to letterboxing, which is a big reason I don't use AQ very much - it tempts me too much to sit in my chair instead of slipping into my boots.  I am far less likely to flip through an online logbook because it would seem like a waste of time, whereas I will most definitely take time on the trail to browse through. 
 
Do you remember virtual LB's - in the beginning?  You would solve them, and at the end was a scanned-in stamped image you could print out and put in your logbook?  How often do you find those anymore?  The stamped images are, by and large, a thing of the past with virtual boxing, so that virtual boxing is pretty much just puzzle solving.
 
Or how about relating it to the lost art of storytelling?  When stories had to be passed verbally from one generation to another, the younger generation was diligent to listen so they could in turn pass the stories down and the older generation told stories with pride and care.  When the stories became mass-printed, there was a disconnect between the teller and the learner, and the stories didn't hold their same meaning.  Taking the box logbook away is sort of like saying "I'll tell the story once so it can be written down and printed, and then I'll just hand you a copy of it for you to read for yourself."  Much less personal...Okay, this analogy is working really well in my mind, but I'm having a hard time conveying it. 
 
Anyway, Silver Eagle, I would still go find your boxes if you decided to quit using logbooks, but I wouldn't enjoy them as much, and I would probably not get around to logging online, although I would probably still send you an email.
 
-
 
Barefoot Lucy

Re: Full Logbooks - Are They Needed?

From: Linda (lindak92@msn.com) | Date: 2008-11-04 02:25:20 UTC
I enjoy looking through logbooks, seeing the stamps and reading the
comments. I love to find a different or creative log. Letterboxing
wouldn't be the same without logbooks.

I will say, though, that I did experience a situation where it was
nice not to have them. There is a Sound of Music series in Ohio
(great series). All done by the same person, and all of the boxes
are fairly close together. Most of the boxes were micros and had no
logbooks. The last one was a larger box and had a logbook. For the
planter, all the small boxes made the series easier to hide in that
particular area. Not having 7 micro logs that fill up quicker
should make the series easier to maintain. From my end, I didn't
really need to look at 8 nearly identical books (most people try,
and succeed in finding, most of the series together since they are
closely spaced). Also, I was boxing with another adult and 2 kids.
We don't usual box as a group, so we can't have a group stamp, and
having to all stamp into 8 boxes in quick succession wouldn't have
been very quick, or honestly, very enjoyable. So I was very happy
that only the last box had a log.

On the other hand, what if that last box went missing. And what
about those boxers that don't manage to find the last box...
