From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-09-15 05:08:16 UTC
Hmmmm - well - first - it is a dinner, not a meeting.  Second - it will be with the local WEST Texas area folks - I don't need to prove anything to them.  We already work well here.  The problems are in Austin.  However, if the opportunity to chat with someone above the local level is available, you can be sure I will do my best to represent geocaching and letterboxing to the benefit of all.
 
The problem we have in the park I volunteer in is simple.  The park is 37 square miles of desert mountain range.  Probably 5-% served by roads.  There are 3 rangers to cover that park.  Each ranger works 5 days and has 2 days off.  So on all but one day a week there are really only two rangers covering the park.  Add split-shifts for the extended summer hours and often there is only one ranger covering the park.
 
Volunteers take up the slack.  Mostly I patrol the high places.  I carry a radio and much other gear - for safety, security, and defense.  I wear camouflage - I can be seen if I want to, but sometimes stealth is important.  I write a weekly SITREP and E-mail/FAX it to the local HQ, reporting trail conditions and other matters of interest.  If an event requires immediate attention, I use the radio.  Sitting high on a ridge line talking the rangers in to nab some ATVers is almost as much fun as calling for artillery, but slower.
 
After a while the rangers learn who's judgment they can trust, and who's they cannot.  This is how I got them to give geocaching and letterboxing the freedom we have out here.  That - and I sorta act as a go-between smoothing out any problems.  So far it has worked.  If it gets too demanding I will pull out and the "rules" will apply from that point on.  So far no one has made it that difficult.  That's the thing about being a volunteer.  You can always UN-volunteer too.
 
As has been pointed out, having a "local agreement" is good, but having a mutual understanding at HQ is better.  Unfortunately, it would be easier for me to travel El Paso to Los Angeles than El Paso to Austin as mileage goes.
 
I guess what I am saying is that while I have done and will continue to do what I can from this end, someone closer to Austin needs to ride that horse.
 
Believe it or not, letterboxing and geocaching are not the only groups that I represent with the parks folks.  Being a ham operator, we are very interested in getting permission to add a linked-system in the Diablo Mountains to the park service tower.  This is the weak link that will allow us to tie our radio network in to the rest of Texas.  While most of you don't give a hoot about it, just remember that when there is an emergency, somehow hams continue to communicate even when the phones etc are out.  So if an opportunity does open, that tower site and permission to use it is my first priority this year.
 
Well - time for bed.
 
Mike.
