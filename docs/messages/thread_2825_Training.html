Sorry this is a little off topic, but I guess you could be in
training for a letterboxing marathon and benefit from it :)
Though I have no military background, I have trained for many things
over the years and I agree with Mike's suggestions. For long-term
hydration, I prefer a CamelBak that can be worn as a pack instead of
a fanny pack. This allows hands-free and instantaneous drinking.
Most are easy to refill and you can get any size you want with as
many pockets as desired. If you got one big enough you could
probably put all your letterboxing stuff in it and use it for that,
too, but I use a fanny pack for letterboxing since it is usually of
short duration.

For me, the hardest part of training is keeping my mind occupied so
it doesn't get bored or convince me to stop the discomfort. For
short sessions (running 5 miles or less) I like to just daydream or
think about stuff that is bothering me. I am convinced we do our
best thinking while being physically active. At work, I used to run
at lunch (yes it was tough in the summer) and if I had a problem
driving me nuts I usually solved it during that run. For longer
sessions, music is ok but I prefer audio books. I love to read, so
what better way to do 2 things at once than have a narrator read
while you train. If the book is good, your training session will fly
by and you might even want to keep going until the end of the chapter.
Your local library has many audio books in all kinds of formats: CDs,
Cassettes, and possibly digital that can be downloaded to non-IPOD
devices. I listen to them while driving, too, and am very
disappointed that new cars no longer offer cassette decks (many older
audio books only come that way). The dealer always looks at me funny
when I ask!

Good luck,
Silver Eagle

Since Lightning crossed to Boy Scouts I've been ravenously reading all their material trying to get up to speed on his new program.   Scouting's founder, Robert Baden Powell, is one of my new heroes and I suck down whatever of his writings I can find.
 
One part of the Boy Scout promise says ...to keep myself physically strong, mentally awake and morally straight."  Being overweight, I've always struggled with the fitness aspect of scouts, but I have found it interesting that BP chose to phrase it as physically strong rather than physically fit.  So I was reading just today in the BS Field Book, and it elaborates considerably on what is meant by physically strong.  Fearless Fox has the book with him or I would quote directly, but it says something to the effect that to be physically strong you must be physically able to do all the things you need to do each day, with enoug reserve remaining to do things you WANT to do, and still enough physical reserve to react to and address any emergency needs that may arise. 
 
What that meant to me was that I needed to be able to do my work and family duties for the day, and then be able to head out and go letterboxing or hiking for a couple of hours after work - safely.  However, if my physical reserve is such that I am using up all my energy just doing a good hike, and I don't have the energy to deal with a wild hog in the woods or a compadre who passes out on me, then I'm not physically strong enough for my own standards.
 
And carrying that further, you have to maintain your strength by doing maintenance WHILE you are exerting yourself - snacking as needed and hydrating and resting periodically, as well as wearing proper wear and carrying proper equipment.
 
Reading that really helped me evaluate where I am in the scope of physical ability and where my goals need to be!

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2010-09-27 10:51:08 UTC-05:00

I'm really glad you were there to fill in the gaps and flesh it all out. As I'm sure you would attest, it makes a HUGE difference when an actual letterboxer teaches letterboxing! I had the opportunity to talk to a troop of girls about letterboxing several years ago and it was such a pleasure to pass on to them the ins and outs that you don't know unless you've actually boxed for awhile, to teach them a little about carving, and now to see some of them still at it. So much the better that we've got real letterboxers training leaders who will teach the girls.

When High Tech Hide and Seek first rolled out, it was often (not always) being taught to leaders by instructors who had no clue what either geocaching or letterboxing were really all about, didn't bother trying them out before they tried to teach them, AND didn't at least get in touch with someone who knew anything about it - i.e. with placers through the clue websites. It was very frustrating for many letterboxers to get sappy emails from troop leaders excited about telling the placer they had just stormed through a park with a dozen girls searching for as many boxes as they could find in a couple of hours - too many times it had turned out to be a "search and destroy" mission. It was hard to respond with any sort of enthusiasm about their afternoon of entertainment.. I always sort of thought the interactive nature of the game wasn't getting conveyed to the girls - every placer and every finder has an impact on every box. Instead, they were treating it more like an afternoon at the movie - soak up the entertainment and move on to the next thing and if you happened to leave your popcorn and soda behind, someone was there getting paid to clean it up. I found it particularly frustrating because I've always maintained that scouting and letterboxing could both benefit by having responsible scouts involved in letterboxing - letterboxing teaches outdoor skills to scouts and scouts should make good letterboxers because of the conservation mindset they should have..

I really think you should make it official and volunteer with your council to teach High Tech Hide and Seek (and you are also a gc'er, aren't you?) rather than leave it to chance that someone will be there to take up the slack! I think you'd be good at it!
