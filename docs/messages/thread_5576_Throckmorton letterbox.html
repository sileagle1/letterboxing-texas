Throckmorton letterbox

From: (candj1969@yahoo.com) | Date: 2016-11-25 20:18:15 UTC-08:00
In December, 2008, we took a letterbox planting trip up into north central Texas with the intent to place at least one letterbox in every county we could get to in about a 10 day period.  One of those boxes was called "Catumseh", which we placed near an historical marker which is located on a ranch in Throckmorton County.  A public road runs through the ranch but the marker and the letterbox was and is on private property.  Soon after we got home from that trip, I received an email from the owner of the ranch asking questions about the box, such as what is in the box and what is the purpose of the box.  He had accidentally found the clues listed on LbNA while doing a google search.  I told him about letterboxing and let him know that he could remove the box if he didn't want it on his land.  He lives part of the year in Houston and part of the year on the ranch, so when he was able to get back to the ranch, he found the box and let me know that it had his approval.  He would occasionally let me know that he would take visitors to the box and they would write their remarks in the logbook.  A few days ago, I got this message from him:
[quote]The Catumseh letterbox is located on our property in Throckmorton County. I check it every now and then just to make sure it is still there.  Last week we were there, we live in Houston, and all of the rocks were gone.  I found the letterbox and it is damaged beyond repair.  This was the work of feral hogs in the area. The tablet and everything in the box was undamaged.  However, the tablet is totally used, both sides of the paper.  We appreciate the letterbox being there, and talk to visitors to the site whenever we have the opportunity.  Our house is located about 1/2 mile east of the marker.  My question is, where can I purchase another watertight box such as the one that was there and another tablet like the one that is full of notations?  A lot of the writings in the tablet are by children expressing their interest in the region and letterboxing in general.
I checked the status of the box on AQ and LbNA and found that it has only been found by letterboxers four times in the eight years it has been there.  I answered his note and told him about Lock & Lock boxes and told him that I had made the logbook using card stock and offered to send him a new box and logbook and thanked him for his concern and care.  He answered that if I would send him a new box and logbook, he would pay for the box and postage and put it back in place.  His actual words were "it is imperative that I pay for the box. Nothing is free."  He also said that many people that find it leave autographed dollar bills and other stuff.  I assume that geocachers find it much more often than letterboxers, but they have never taken the stamp.  It probably has the most interesting history of any box I've ever placed.

Boots Tex

I remember when Baby Bear and I found that box back in 2012 it already had several notes from friends of the owners and we thought it was so cool they had embraced it. Even though only 3 or 4 letterboxers have logged the find, who knows how many potential boxers have found it over the years. At a time when most emails about boxes are bad news, this is quite refreshing!

This does bring up an interesting point I have noticed about letterboxing interest declining. It appears not as many people are boxing these days based on logged finds, which I realize is not a true indicator. Not sure if it is because few new people are joining the ranks or seasoned boxers are just too busy. For my own boxes I notice the same few people find everything I place, but other boxes in the same parks get much more visits (e.g. one of my Game boxes at Wild Basin has had only 2 finders since 2012 while other newer boxes have much more visits). Maybe it is because that game is harder to solve or that my clues are only on LBNA, but I am thankful for those of you still out there boxing. I also love hearing stories like this and would love to hear more.
