From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2007-09-28 13:57:41 UTC
My letterbox at Point Blank called "Where's George?", which is one of
my Texas governors series boxes, has gone missing. If I replace it,
it will be moved to a better location. Point Blank is not a bad
location, and it is appropriate for that box, but is off the beaten
path. This box, in its 9 month life, as far as I know was only found
3 times, and never reported found on Atlas Quest. I have a theory
that, as more and more boxes are planted, the out-of-the-way ones are
going to suffer, simply because, when you have a choice, you are more
apt to go for an area that has more boxes. That's human nature, and
I'm guilty of it myself. The idea that "If you plant it, they will
come" is not necessarily valid.

Boots Tex

Hey BT!

 

I'm curious why you think of lonely boxes as suffering! 

 

I'm of the opinion that if the box is "where it should be", then there it should be, regardless of traffic.   It's those sorts of boxes that lead us all to the really cool "wow I never would've been here except for letterboxing" moments!

 

I have two boxes in AR, one of which has had maybe two visitors in two years, and the other has had maybe one in one year.   They are both wayyyyy off the beaten trail, but if you go for them, you are treated to a phenomenal view. 

 

Don't get me wrong, I can appreciate wanting to see more traffic, but sometimes boxes are delightful hidden secrets.

 

I do agree with you though, that "if you plant them, they will come" is far less effective when there is a wealth of boxes such as we have now.   Lots of us are guilty of looking for the areas where we can get more bang for our buck.

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2007-09-28 09:29:29 UTC-05:00
I agree 100% with Dewberry. I recently hid a series of five boxes in an area that is not necessarily populated with letterboxers (just off 380 in Sister Grove Park between Greenville and McKinney at Lake Lavon), and to my knowledge they have not yet been visited. In fact, I knew when I placed them, that they wouldn't be flooded with visitors, but that's okay. My philosophy is, I've found a neat trail which will provide a letterboxer (or letterboxing family) with a nice safe hike on a pretty trail. To me, it really doesn't matter if my boxes are found by two people or fifty two people as long as I offer a nice experience. (Actually, my most visited boxes are in the Botanical Gardens in FW, They've probably had over 80 visitors, and I've already replaced their logbooks twice. I'm surprised that those boxes are still there! I think when you plant a box in an area that potentially has many visitors, you have to be prepared for a lot of maintenance on those boxes.

From: Sandy Milfeld (pugapalooza@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-09-30 22:35:21 UTC-05:00
It's a strange phenomenon, if you ask me. I always thought of letterboxers as being more "active" (hikers, rock-climbers, campers, etc.) and less "take a short-cut" types. I love the drives, the hikes, etc. One of my boxes, ESOT: Brown Pelican, has only two logged finds in 10 months. I just had to hide it at Pelican Bay, but I knew it would suffer loneliness as a result. Witch Hazel is out in Azle, too, but has only 3 finds in a year. And it's practically a drive by. I think because the clues were a poem and not cut and dried clues, it suffered. Strangely enough, those boxes are just a few miles up the road from the Fort Worth Nature Center, and those boxes get hit a lot.

I say "Hooray!" to all of the clever clues, interesting spots, out of the way nooks and crannies!!

And I have to disagree about lonely boxes not suffering. When we go to the trouble to carve, make logbooks, and especially tape up those Lock-N-Locks, we want them to be found! It's fantastic to check on a box and see that the logbook is almost full. It makes ya feel like ya "done good".

And that brings me to my last bit: what's to be done about listed "attempted finds" that weren't actually attempts? In the case of Witch Hazel (if I'm not mistaken) the last attempted find was someone going out and seeing that the place was too crowded for a decent search. I think when folks see that the last log was an attempt, they are more likely to pass that box up on a LB outing. I know I've been guilty of that.

Stargazer







Re: [letterboxingtexas] Missing Letterbox

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2007-09-30 23:16:26 UTC-05:00
My thought on listing a box as an "attempt" is that a box shouldn't be reported as an attempt unless the person looking for it is possitive that they were looking in the right spot and the box is missing. In fact, I think they should make an effort to contact the placer and verify that they were in the right spot before marking it as an "attempt". What does a person have to gain by marking a box as an "attempt" anyway? I especially don't think a box should be marked as attempted if it's a mystery box, because chances are good that you haven't solved the puzzle correctly. Case in point: your (Stargazer's) box: Twisters in Texas. On a recent trip home from Archer City, I looked for this box. I got down to the final couple of clues, and couldn't quite figure it out. The sun was setting and I decided that I'd just have to save that one for another day.  Is that an attempt? Not in my book, because I never quite figured out exactly where it was supposed to be, although I'm sure I had to be within a few feet of it!
Puddle Splasher

From: kerry1194 (k_kellogg@hotmail.com) | Date: 2007-10-01 11:10:10 UTC
Hang in there, Stargazer. The pelican and Witch Hazel are on our
list. As are another 150 boxes in the area and that doesn't even get
us into Dallas... Honestly, if I can get my guest room clean early
this week, Z and I were planning on heading that way on Thursday or
Friday. Don't count on anything but we have plans! Lots of them!
And they involve lots of different boxes. :)

One thing about letterboxing that I've learned is that you really
can't 100% count on the information that you get from the clue. Yes,
you need to follow a clue to the letter. Trust me on this. It can
turn a half hour hike into a 2 hour adventure. But you can't take the
missing and attempt information at face value. I know of 2 boxes that
have been removed from active status because of flooding and major
construction, yet DH rides through this area regularly and we think
the boxes may have survived. The cool part is that he has finally
agreed to take the clues with him on his next ride to investigate. We
still try to find the ones that are listed as unknown or missing.
Why? Because half the fun is knowing that the person before you
wasn't paying enough attention or maybe some random person found the
box down stream, looked it up online, found the clue and then took the
time to put it back where it belongs.

All I know is that I have to return to one spot sometime this week to
see if I can find a box Z didn't see, because his hand was in the
right hole. And Saturday, we returned to a box that we had spent 30
minutes looking for Wednesday only to discover we had been looking
behind the wrong tree. Sometimes, all it takes it a good nights sleep
and a new perspective and then the box kinda jumps out and bites you.
