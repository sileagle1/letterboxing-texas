As y'all know, I've got this mambo stamp project going with Cub Scouts for
our district event at the end of April. Someone came up with this workable
idea, IF I can find an indelible stamp ink.

Rather than having a patch designed for the event (which I had already spent
umpteen hours designing, only to find out it couldn't be done), we are going
to have patches embroidered with the event name and leave them blank so the
boys can stamp the camp stamp and their personal stamp on the patch.
NOW - these patches wind up sewn onto the pockets of scout uniforms
everywhere, so the ink really needs to be indelible. Any thoughts on how to
accomplish this? I plan to make several copies of the camp stamp, and they
will only be used that one day, so that if I have to use an ink that will
damage the stamp, that will be fine as long as the stamps last the day.

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-03-09 15:53:19 UTC-06:00
I haven't tried the Staz-on, but I think that's going to be my first go.  I have some time to figure this out.  I do have a heat gun, but I would like to steer away from having to set the ink unless it clearly will work better than anything else.  Trying to steer 140 boys (and their daddies) is like trying to push a rope, so anything to make it less complicated is better.
 
As to teaching the girls, I have a "thing" about trying to teach cheap.  I'm of the opinion that if I teach someone on cheap material, I risk giving the idea that I don't think it's worth giving them my best, so I want to give them what I would choose to use myself.  Also, if I give them the same material I use, I am familiar enough with it to be able to figure out any problems they may have.  That said, I KNOW that working on erasers works out just fine and I KNOW my "thing" is probably only that - a thing.  But I always have so much extra material just lying around - you know, where I had carved five stamps out of a block of PZKut and had the rest of the block left.  I was able to take the extra stuff, plus another block of PZK, and slice them out a 2'x2' chunk to go after.  And if they finished with that one, I cut 'em another chunk.  I also had lots of extra of the many types of material I've tried, so that if they wanted to experiment with something different they could.  In that extra material, I had good ol' pink pearl knock-off erasers and I did make the point that you can carve anything, even cheap dollar store erasers, and that dollar store erasers are almost always available, whereas standard carving block isn't.  And a couple of them tried erasers and did very well with them.
