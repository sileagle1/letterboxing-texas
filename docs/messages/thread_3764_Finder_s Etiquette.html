Finder's Etiquette

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2008-09-08 15:43:46 UTC
I received a notification through LbNA that read "Your box is missing."
The message was sent anonymously, but referred to my box "Unbroken
Promises" in Fredericksburg. Since the box is so far away that I can't
go check on it anytime soon, I would have liked to have more details,
or at least the (attempted) finder's trail name. If the finder was
someone I knew and trusted, then I could be confident that the box is
indeed missing. If the finder was someone I didn't know, then I could
ask for details to be sure they were looking in the right location and
didn't just miss the box. Instead, there is some doubt in my mind
about the box. I'm not sure why anyone would choose to send a message
like that without signing it, but it almost seems rude. Am I being
sensitive because I may have lost a box (that always hurts a little)?

Boots Tex

The only thing that scares me is the thought that I was observed finding or putting back a box and the observer took the box.  I checked and someone found that one about a month or so after Big Red Coyote and I found it.  Very lovely place.  The anonymous "attempter" must be new and not really sure what (s)he's doing.  The first boxes we looked for here in the town we live in were missing and not only did we contact the owners but we attached pics of where we thought the boxes should have been so they would have an idea of whether we were right or mistaken.  Maybe a pic is too much to expect from other people but at least the person should have tried to describe it.

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2008-09-08 19:13:00 UTC-06:00
Heh heh heh - I am wrong a lot.  Just ask my wife.
 
Sometimes new folks are ashamed to admit that they couldn't find a box.  Like they are STUPID because it is right there and they didn't see it.  Sometimes they really don't have a clue how it works and just get it wrong.  In every area someone has to be the first, then mentor others along.  Maybe this person has no mentor.  Read about letterboxing somewhere, bought a stamp at Wal-Mart, and away we go.  They will learn slowly on their own, or more quickly with help.  But until they are identified there is no way to help them.
 
Hell - I don't like defeat. 
 
I am a suspicious person by experience and nature.  That is why we carry guns.  But I really try to give the benefit of the doubt before I cap someone.  <grin>.  I was boxing for a few years before I learned of this list.
 
Tell you a secret.  There is a GEOCACHE  (GASP!  He said the G word!!!)  in a park called GENE REID park in Tucson.  Every year we went to the Tucson dog show.  And since this cache was in a park, every year we went walking the dogs in the park and I hunted that cache.  FOR FIVE YEARS !!!!!  I finally found it.  And you know what.....  that was a sad moment.  It had become a ritual.  After the dog show we went to Gene Reid Park and let the dogs sniff while I searched a bush for the smallest container painted green and hidden in a bush that is known to man!  One guy found it by running his hand on all the branches.  He was so happy he found it he jumped up and down.  Then he couldn't see it and had to start over!
 
Anyway - sorry you may have lost a box.  Whenever I have a box or cache missing, I try to think of all the fun others had with is before it went missing.  And then it doesn't bother me so bad.
 
My wife's (Dane_Lady) one letterbox is also a Geocache  (GASP!  He said the G word again!!!)  - any kid that finds it alone or with others can take one toy free without trade.  She stocks it with little toys from the 1$ store.  Just her way to try to get kids to go out.
 
Later.
