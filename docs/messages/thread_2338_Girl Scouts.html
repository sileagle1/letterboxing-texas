From: ThimbleCat (inklaura@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2006-12-01 14:11:01 UTC
I do adult trainings for the San Jacinto Girl Scout council on
letterboxing - training the leaders to train their own girls. I have
talked to my own troop of 12 2nd graders as well.
We are really trying to get it going at all of our council camps as a
fun activity for troops to do at camp weekends and give them a little
taste of boxing without endangering public boxes.

I will email you off list the handout I use for the training (or post
it here if anyone else is interested).

In the training class, I give an overview of boxing then pull out my
pack to show them what I take with me. I discuss the "rules" of
boxing and stress the importance of rehiding well, resealing baggies
well, and NOT FILLING UP LOGBOOKS! I show them examples of boxes
they might find out there and types of containers that work well.
We discuss types of logbooks and how the girls can make their own
with cardstock and white paper - I show samples of those. At the
end, we discuss planting boxes, writing clues and carving erasers - I
show examples of carved stamps too.

As part of their class kit they receive a half sheet of cardstock
folded in half filled with about 6 half sheets of white paper folded
inside of the cover. Holes are punched on the edge and brads or yarn
used to tie them together. It's an easy and cheap way for all the
girls to have a logbook. They also get a film canister with a hole
punched on one end, a pony bead, a piece of yarn, a 2x2" square of
funfoam and a small foam mounting square. They thread the yarn
through the hold using the bead to keep it in place (making a
necklace out of the canister). They cut the fun foam into a small
image with kid scissors and attach it to the canister with the
mounting square. Many of the leaders have gotten quite creative
with their fun foam stamps.

When I discussed LBing with my troop, I first got them excited by
showing them my logbook. We talked about the basics of boxing and
how to follow clues. We made and decorated their logbooks at one of
our meetings and then found our first box at a GS camp. They were
HOOKED. I carved us a troop stamp to for the host logbook and then
each girl got to stamp the host stamp into their own logbook. The
nice thing about boxing at the GS camp was that not as much stealth
is needed (good thing too since it was quite chaotic!).

I just kept it very very simple with the girls and will teach them
more as we go along. With a troop whose leaders are new boxers you
would have to give more info up front.

I'm sure it's not much different from the boxing you did with your
cub scouts. You might want to contact the program dept. at the GS
council office and find a liason to get it going (if you haven't
already). Our council just put a blurb in the council newsletter
magazine and I train the class at most of the big training events.

Hope this helps you out a little bit. Getting the scouts started is
where I am spending most of my boxing time lately.

ThimbleCat of The Polka Dots

I would love to have your handout!
 
I gathered up my old logbooks and got out a bunch of stamps I've carved but not done anything with, and I've gathered up some of every kind of carving material I have.  I also downloaded some fairly simple images of things that are appealing to girls.  I've got some boxes and HH's sitting on my desk that I'll take, and some logbook supplies, I've printed off some clues and I've got a copy of GG's first box clues.  And there are always good stories, thanks to everyone on this list!
 
I recently met the membership development person for the local GSUSA council and got to know her a bit, so I asked her to connect me with program people who might want letterboxing information.
 
For reasons I have yet to understand, BSA has opted to get behind geocaching vs letterboxing.  Consequently, councils are seeking grants to buy GPS's.  What happened to teaching map and compass to boys, and isn't letterboxing a better venue for that than geocaching?  Since they are gearing GC'ing toward Boy Scouts, maybe they figure boys should have their basic navigation skills down by the time they cross over from Cub Scouts.  My council has placed some caches on our scout reservation and I'm currently seeking permission to place boxes as well.  So far they have looked at me like I have three eyes!
