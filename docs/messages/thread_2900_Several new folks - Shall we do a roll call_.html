From: Tracy Valdez (gtvaldez@sbcglobal.net) | Date: 2007-07-03 15:22:55 UTC-05:00

Hi all! I am semi-new online here. Been letterboxing for about a year and a half. I live in Fort Worth. My kids love to box with me so I have to be cautious on the boxes we search for. They are 5 and 8 and the 5 year old tires easy on walking routes…don’t want to scare her off of the boxes so we pick and choose many of the ones we go for based on ease of finding.

 

My fav part of the sport is the uniqueness of the stamps carved and the things/people lertterboxers dedicate special boxes to. I also like history based ones.

 

My favorite share is this: After my friend introduced us to lb-ing and helped us carve our signature stamp, we were off to Iowa (from Texas) with printed clues in hand for along the way. Took my father in law with us to locate one (in the dark even) in a city park. It was our first box and we were so jazzed to find it so easily! Opened it up and spread out the stuff in it…oh my…match box cars, carved wooden ball inside a box thing, some cards, a whistle, and about 8-10 other things. No stamp. No logbook. What gives??? So, I thought so innocently that the logbook must have gotten lost and so I grabbed the really neat hand carved wooden ball toy thing and rolled it in my ink pad and logged it in my log book. It made the coolest “bubble” looking stamp. I wrote all the things down in my log book that I found in this cool box. After I put the box all back together again…and noted the word written on the top of it – geocache – (so I could go look it up later!) we re-hid our find and covered it well and drove back to the house.

 

What a delightful discovery of yet another avenue of “the hunt”…geocaching – which is a similar game played without stamps but still has treasures and a hunt. So to all the newbies that may be as ignorant of this other game…geocaching is letterboxing without stamps, with a gps or compass or Lat. and Long. knowledge and a slightly different set of rules. You probably will stumble across one in your letterboxing search. It is expected that if you find one, you can take a treasure from the box but you must leave one of equal or more value in its place. Sometimes there is a log book, sometimes not.

 

I have enjoyed reading the profiles and anecdotes. Thanks to all for sharing and please, everyone – add yours to this line!


Tracy aka gtvaldez

taking my niece geocaching.

About a week before Thanksgiving 2004 I read about geocaching in
Parade magazine and knew I had to try it. I promptly mail-ordered a
GPS receiver, but since I went with cheap postage I didn't expect it
to show up until after the holidays. Since we were heading down to
Orlando and Tampa over the holidays, I wanted to do something on that
trip. On a geocaching forum someone had mentioned letterboxing, so I
looked into that and decided it was something I could start doing
immediately. There were none within 2 hours of my house, but there
were a couple on my way to Central Florida so I printed out some
clues. Then my GPS receiver showed up much quicker than expected the
day before we left. The end result was that I found my first
letterbox and my first geocache on the same day, a letterbox outside
of Gainesville and a geocache on International Drive in Orlando. My
wife and I were both hooked, but she continues as my assistant,
unregistered with no trail name of her own.

Nowadays, letterboxing interests me more than geocaching primarily
because I enjoy carving. Geocaching interests my wife more than
letterboxing because she enjoys looking through the loot (even though
she rarely takes anything), and she'd just as soon we quit
letterboxing. We both enjoy finding new and out-of-the-way sights
and locales, and for that geocaching is better but both are good.

I don't even know my find and place counts. I think my find count is
in the upper 200's somewhere, and my place count is in the 80's not
including stamps carved by me and placed by others or HH's. I have
ten Blue Diamonds. My geocache find count is higher, 400+ I think.

I do enough carving these days that I have a primer up:

http://www.nettally.com/palmk/Rubber_Stamp_Carving_Tips.html

area that I've basically decided to quit to allow others a chance to
plant stuff without tripping over my boxes. I plant on trips -- I
have planted boxes in Georgia, Alabama, Texas, Maine, and New
Hampshire, and had one in Michigan before it went missing -- but
planting takes a lot of time and really eats into a vacation if
you're not careful, so I usually limit my planting so the wife
doesn't get impatient. I planted four in New Hampshire this summer,
the most I've ever planted on such a trip.

I ship stamps to others to plant, and I was the guy who made the
suggestion that resulted in Gillespie Tribe setting up a system to
match up carvers with planters. I have stamps planted by others in
Houston, Massachusetts, California, North Carolina, Kentucky, Maine,
and probably a few other states that I can't recall right now. There
will be boxes with my stamps planted in South Carolina, Connecticut,
and Indiana shortly.

What's surprising to many others: Both my wife and I dislike events
and meets; we've attended a couple, probably won't attend any more.

I do like HH's and have launched a few.

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-07-05 15:38:02 UTC
I don't know how to say this exactly, but I will sometimes make box
recommendations for people out of town, or general ones for people
looking for things like drive-bys or good boxes to do with small children.

What I won't do is recommend one planter over another, as I think
that's just bad form and potentially hurtful to others, even if that's
not what you meant.

Everyone has a different style. One person likes long hikes and
plants on them; another has difficulty getting around so all their
boxes are drive-bys. One person may be a great carver, but pick lousy
locations for their boxes. Another may have great mystery clues;
another interesting custom stamps. All placers are different, but
they tend to have, usually, a certain style to their plants.

If you're from an area and going to be boxing regularly in it, my best
advice is to find a variety of boxes from a variety of planters.
You'll figure out which planter(s) tend to plant the boxes that you
want to find. Not every box is for every finder.

There are lots of components to letterboxing: the carving, the
logbook, the history of the box or the subject, the location (general
park and specific planting spot), and the clues. Collectively, the
infinite variety of combinations makes for a unique experience each
time.

dewberry

dewberrylb <dewberrylb@gmail.com> wrote:
I don't know how to say this exactly, but I will sometimes make box
recommendations for people out of town, or general ones for people
looking for things like drive-bys or good boxes to do with small children.

What I won't do is recommend one planter over another, as I think
that's just bad form and potentially hurtful to others, even if that's
not what you meant.

Everyone has a different style. One person likes long hikes and
plants on them; another has difficulty getting around so all their
boxes are drive-bys. One person may be a great carver, but pick lousy
locations for their boxes. Another may have great mystery clues;
another interesting custom stamps. All placers are different, but
they tend to have, usually, a certain style to their plants.

If you're from an area and going to be boxing regularly in it, my best
advice is to find a variety of boxes from a variety of planters.
You'll figure out which planter(s) tend to plant the boxes that you
want to find. Not every box is for every finder.

There are lots of components to letterboxing: the carving, the
logbook, the history of the box or the subject, the location (general
park and specific planting spot), and the clues. Collectively, the
infinite variety of combinations makes for a unique experience each
time.

dewberry

What you say is very true.  And I might add, it adds an element of "me vs. thee" to it, which I really don't think adds anything to letterboxing. 
 
I've been asked several times from folks who are coming through or to my area to recommend some boxes to them.  Every time I start trying to make a list, I can't help it - I just want to include them all!  So I usually try to pick what I think is the best of each placer's boxes in the area and gear them to the person asking as best I know how to.  But by recommending a wide variety of placers, I know the prospective finder will be pleased by at least a few, if not all, the boxes I suggest. 
 
I think every placer has a signature of their own, whether it is in the type of place they plant, the type of journey it takes to get to that place, the style of box, the story that goes along with, the type of hide they are known for, the sort of stamp they do, the type of cypher they use.  The only time I really DON'T enjoy finding a box is when all of these elements stink - and yes, there are a few boxes out there like that, aren't there?
 
There is truly something for everyone in letterboxing.  I would hate to be the one who voiced her opinion loudly enough that someone took it for authority and missed out on an aspect of letterboxing that would've been their niche.
