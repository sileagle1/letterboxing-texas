From: Kirbert (PalmK@nettally.com) | Date: 2007-06-23 21:56:15 UTC-04:00
Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilt wrote:

> Let me ask you,
> what do the regulations say about letterboxing specifically (not
> geocaching)? Are their letteboxing rules the same for all state
> parks, or does each park decide on its own?

I'm here in Florida, which may be ahead of you guys on this subject.
When the bureaucrats in charge of the state parks, forests, wildlife
management areas, etc., first heard about geocaching and
letterboxing, they came up with a permit process. I initially
accepted this and applied for some permits for some of my existing
boxes. After several months, I eventually actually received the
permits, and spent a couple of days driving around to my caches
applying these little stickers under the lid.

Since then, they've piled on more and more regulations. Each
organization -- there are several separate state organizations
involved -- has its own version of the regulations, but basically all
of them:

A) stipulate that the owner is responsible for any objectionable
materials inside the container. If you own a geocache and some
visitor drops off a pocket knife, it's your fault.

B) clarify that the owner is responsible for any litter in the
vicinity of the hide.

C) require that the owner visit the hide at least once a month. A
couple of them require a visit every two weeks.

D) require that you reapply annually for new permits, and go out and
apply the stickers inside the container.

As much as I appreciate all the good things the park officials do for
us, I eventually decided that such tangles of red tape are not what I
became a geocacher and letterboxer for. I will never again apply for
a permit.

With geocaching, that means I won't be planting any in such areas,
and when my existing permits expire I will be pulling those caches.
Someone else is welcome to hide in those areas and deal with the
permit process. With letterboxes I have the additional option of
hiding a box and not telling the authorities. That works, but
unfortunately very few letterboxers find the clues either so I don't
get many finders.

-- Kirbert

From: Mike - 2007 (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-06-24 03:03:18 UTC
It isn't a park rule - it is a state rule. 
 
It was brought about by a geocacher who put his cache on a cliff.  One of the cache hunters tried for it and decided it was too dangerous.  He reported it to the rangers.  The rangers agreed.
 
The owner was asked to move it to a safer place.  He refused to move it when asked to do so.  He started a smear campaign against the ranger who asked him to move the cache to a safer location.  The complaints were investigated all the way to Austin.  They were found groundless, but suddenly our games were on the radar scope.
 
So they asked him to remove it totally and they banned him from the parks.  He refused.  This went on for 6 months and nearly got all "scavenger" games banned in all Texas parks.  Geocachers lobbied long and hard, and the "form" was the best compromise they could get.  The meeting sorta went - "take the forms or be banned".
 
The form is the same for all 4 state parks in the El Paso area.  I have not investigated parks outside my area - but I am told the form is a state form and is the same all through Texas.
 
I am sorry you seem to think the rules don't need to be followed if you are not a geocacher.  You do as you see fit.  If enough letterboxers just put their boxes in parks and don't bother with the rules, eventually the state will see the wisdom of the placers and just get rid of the form.  Right?  Work with them - or work against them.
 
I've said my piece.  You do as you see fit, you'll hear nothing more about it from me.

From: Mike - 2007 (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-06-24 03:52:07 UTC
The form was written between geocachers and rangers.  But it has come to encompass any hide-and-seek games.  Since my letterboxes are also geocaches, I never gave it a thought.  I have not asked how others deal with it.
 
Many parks are friendly to geocachers and other gamers.  Some are not.  A lot of it has to do with the experience they have in meeting the players.  If you read my geocache listings you will see I point out to treat the rangers nicely to keep them friendly to the game.
 
I have bent over backwards to ensure the rangers in El Paso were friendly.  I have found that working with them is a lot better than working against them. 
 
Other parks are not as nice.
 
The rangers have a job to do also.  Some see it as something they love to do and they are usually easy to get along with provided you play within the park rules.  Other rangers do the job because they need a job.  They hate it but don't have the skills to do other work or whatever.  They are all individuals with all the baggage that goes along with.  If they are not happy in life, it shows.
 
There are four ways to approach this.  Work with the rangers from the start.  Try stealth and switch tactics when caught.  Or try stealth - leaving no contact information in the box - then let the rangers dispose of it when it gets found.  Or just place the boxes outside parks.  I know people doing all four with varying degrees of success.
 
Your box got caught.  What happens now is up to you.  My rangers are very accommodating because I cultivated that relationship.  YMMV.
