As someone who was bitten by a snake in the early spring when snakes are rare, I will say that it can happen.

This will be my last post on the subject.


Lucy Locket


--- In letterboxingtexas@yahoogroups.com, "Mike Olbrisch" wrote:
>
> Well - as a retired soldier with lots of desert experience, I can say that
> snakes are truly rare in the late fall through early spring. Not gone
> completely, but usually. I speak for West Texas, the habits in the east may
> differ somewhat.
>
>
>
> Study up on them - respect them, but do not fear them. And above all, do not
> mess with them. Or kill them.
>
>
>
> Rattle Snakes do not want to encounter you. If given the chance they will warn
> you with a buzz of their tail.
>
>
>
> If forced into a human encounter, they would rather flee than bite.
>
>
>
> If cornered, they will strike. Most snake bites happen in two ways. The snake
> is found suddenly in an unexpected place and startled, or people do not listen
> and try to mess with the snake.
>
>
>
> If a snake bites you, sometimes they do not inject venom. This is more true
> with older and larger snakes. But you cannot count on that. Smaller snakes are
> more dangerous than the larger ones for several reasons. First, they are not as
> frightening, so people will be more likely to mess with them. Second, when they
> strike they nearly always dump all the venom they have into the bite.
>
>
>
> Death from snake bite is a rare thing for normal healthy people. The very
> young, very old, and those with reactionary troubles (like bee stings etc) need
> to be aware of their situation. Keep the bite victim calm, and seek medical aid
> ASAP. Cutting the wound and sucking out the venom is for TV. Study up on
> proper first aid for snake bite.
>
>
>
> Knowledge is power. Most people fear what they do not understand. Kind of like
> hoplophobic people, what they do not understand frightens them. Knowledge and
> experience usually conquers those fears. So it should be with snakes.
> Knowledge and precaution is all you need. Use a stick to poke - not your hand.
> Use a grabber to reach the box, not your arm. If you see a snake, remember it
> belongs there, you are a visitor in its land. Give it the respect it is due by
> yielding the trail to it, and you will be fine. Most snakes cannot quite strike
> as far as they are long, but give it as much room as you can.
>
>
>
> In 20 years I only had to evac one soldier for snake bite. It was just a cute
> little baby snake. He had heard that the baby snakes didn't bite. Heh heh heh
> - just like all guns are loaded, so too are all snakes. Give them their due,
> and all will be well.
>
>
>
> Hope this is helpful.
