Small towns, Big ranches

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2008-12-10 21:11:10 UTC
We had a little break after Thanksgiving, so Lone Star Quilter and I
decided to take a little trip to North Central Texas to fill in some
of those blank counties that didn't have letterboxes. During the
trip, we placed 22 new letterboxes and found a few. We stopped off
in Meridian State Park and placed a little bird box, Chapeau Noir, as
LSQ wants to put a Texas bird in every state park we visit. While we
were there we ran up to Dinosaur Valley SP and put a bird there,
Cheeky Fellow, then up to Weatherford, which needed a Texas governor
box for a very frugal man. Then, we headed west and stayed a couple
of nights at Lake Brownwood SP, where we left Brown Wood Duck and
Honky-Tonk Juke Box. While we were there, we took a trip out west to
Runnels County to see Coppini's Cowboy on the courthouse lawn, then
to Coke County and Fort Chadbourne, where we left Granny Lee. The
fort is on private land, a ranch that has been in the same family
since almost the time it was a fort, and the family is restoring and
protecting the site and the buildings. It's open to the public for
donations. A must-stop if you ever get out that way. We stopped in
Abilene SP to drop off Peter, Peter, Peter (was that a bird I
heard?). While we were in Abilene, IRA Boxer snuck off to Fort
Phantom Hill in Jones County and left the Phantom Cannon. We visited
The Sheriff who wore Petticoats in Coleman County, then started the
drive up the Great Western Cattle Trail with the Wrangler in
Trickham, up to Callahan County for a little Texas Fever, stopping by
Cottonwood to visit The Newton Boys. We left Brownwood and stopped
off at Fort Griffin for about 3 days to visit the land of small towns
and big ranches. The old town of Fort Griffin was a wild and wooly
western town, home of Wyatt Earp and Doc Holliday in its heyday, and
its place on the Great Western Trail provided plenty of cowboy
customers. This place is also on private property, but open to the
public. It's a work-in-progress for the owner, who has hired
archeologists and is replacing the buildings on their original sites,
hoping to make the town a tourist destination. We stopped in at
Shaunissy's Saloon and tasted The Sins of Katie Elder. While there,
we had a bite to eat at the Chuckwagon in Throckmorton and saw some
Tonkawa in Haskell County. Also, Chief Catumseh of the Comanche, in
1856, met with Robert E. Lee near Camp Cooper to discuss a peace
treaty. There's a granite marker 2 miles and 4 cattle guards into a
ranch that begged for a letterbox, so it got one. Knox County
actually got 3 letterboxes: Who Needs Seeds?, Lightnin' Ben and
Coyote. Then, it was back to the cattle trail, where a cowboy could
be heard singing to his pony, "Goodbye, Old Paint". Then, "Up The
Trail" to Wilbarger County and the Red River at Doan's Crossing. We
came back through Stephens and Palo Pinto Counties where we found 3
boxes and left the Carolina Chickadee at Lake Possum Kingdom. Woke
up Tuesday morning and the forecast was for some white fluffy stuff,
so we headed south, aided by the gentle push of a blue norther', to
get ready for Christmas. Y'all have a good one and we'll see you on
the trail. By the way, the clues are not available yet for all those
boxes, but I'm working on it and you should be able to get to them in
a couple of days. If you just can't wait, contact me and I'll share
them with you.

Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter
