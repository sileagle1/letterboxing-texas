Texas History through letterboxing

From: (candj1969@yahoo.com) | Date: 2015-08-15 08:37:41 UTC-07:00

If you've ever found any of my letterboxes, you know that I enjoy the history of our great state.  I share this love with my friend and letterboxer extraordinaire Baby Bear.  We don't make as many trips just to find letterboxes as we used to, but due to BB's recent plants along I-10, all having a lot to do with the history of the area, we decided to plan at least a day trip to find some of them, along with some others that we've missed, and, while we're at it, to take the opportunity to plant a few boxes.  Planting boxes is the hard part for me because I do a lot of research trying to find just the right subject, then finding an image and carving a stamp and making a logbook.  I know you all go through the same process, but I don't do anything ahead of time, so it's always a scramble to get the boxes ready by the date we've set for our trip.  This time, the subject matter has been very difficult because the history of Texas along I-10 has been pretty well covered, by folks like Baby Bear, Silver Eagle, Corazon and many others.  I was pleased to find a subject that was a little different for me than the typical patriot, hero, battle, Native American theme.  I found a nineteenth century German-Texas poet who lived in the no-longer existing village of Black Jack Springs, and who is buried in Black Jack Springs Cemetery and I even found a photograph of him: PERFECT!  I carved a detailed stamp of this long-haired, white-bearded gentleman, made a logbook and wrote up my story and prepared to log in to LbNA to set up the box.  I needed to double check a fact or two so I did a last-minute Google for the man, Johannes Christleb Nathanael Romberg.  The first thing that came up was a letterbox that was planted last March.  By Baby Bear.  Back to the drawing board.  If anyone needs a stamp of an old bearded German poet, I've got one to spare.


Boots Tex
