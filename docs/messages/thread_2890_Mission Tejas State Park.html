Mission Tejas State Park

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2007-06-28 02:54:30 UTC
We just got back home from a couple of days trailer camping with our
son (trail name Fish Tree) and his family (wife and 3 kids 6, 4, 8
mos.). We stayed wet all the time, either from the rain or the
humidity, but had a great time. We didn't take any letterboxes with
us to place because, after all, this is a State Park. This is one
of those parks that hand out a list of geocaches and their gps
coordinates, so I asked the ranger about the park policy on
letterboxing and she said that she had never heard of it. She
called two other rangers in, including the head guy and neither of
them knew what letterboxing was. I explained it to them and they
didn't have a problem with it. The head man did ask where we would
typically hide a box and I told him generally behind trees, under
logs, etc. He said there was nothing wrong with that. They said they
had never had any trouble with geocachers and, in fact, thought they
brought visitors to the park. The first lady was really intrigued
and thought it sounded like a great family hobby. She was impressed
that the kids would actually go with us instead of wanting to stay
in the trailer and watch DVD's. We went back to the trailer and
made 3 letterboxes to place. Mine is called "Nabedache", LSQ's
is "Indian Trails - Quilt Block Series" and Fish Tree carved a stamp
for Princess Aurora and hers is called "Tejas Bell". We took them
to the ranger this morning and showed the boxes to her before
planting them. Looking at the stamp impression on the logbook, she
asked, "How do you get this stamped on, with a potato?". Then I
showed her the stamp and told her that we had just finished carving
them and she was really impressed. I didn't tell her that two
letterboxes had been in that park for over four years (they're both
in fine shape, by the way). Actually, when we were looking for a
place to hide LSQ's box, we found one of Atoy Adventurer's boxes
(Pretty Kitty). She has four there, although we didn't realize it
at the time since they're not listed on LbNA. The ranger gave me
her card and asked if I would email her the website address whenever
I posted the clues. This is a lovely little park, but has some good
trails and a restored 1828 log house,as well as the Mission Tejas,
and is also kept very clean. This is now a letterbox-friendly park
and if we all treat it with respect, we can keep it that way.

Boots Tex

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-06-27 22:40:04 UTC-05:00
Hey BT!
 
I'm so glad you had a good experience there!  I remembered later that MT was, in fact, the park I had found that was treasure-hunt friendly.  I was talking to a weekend ranger when I was there with our pack on a campout in March.  The person I talked to was somewhat familiar with the stashes in the park, although I had forgotten about Atoy Adventurer's also since they weren't on LBNA (or maybe they weren't there then). 
 
I'm not sure if any of them told you, but there is a hike you can take in that park that must be guided by a ranger, because it goes into off-limits areas.  They have an archaeological site where there are even some wagon tracks preserved along the El Camino Real. 
 
I think Mission Tejas is, hands-down, the best State Park I've ever visited.  Long ago we visited there alot and were very familiar with the trails.  It was there on our first day letterboxing that I was astounded to discover Baby Bear's box not 6 feet from where we had gone many, many times.  It has the friendliest staff I've ever run across, and they are also quite knowledgeable about a wide variety of topics.  If you hit them on the weekends, there is a ranger that does a 19th century tools and toys program that will rock your socks - his program lasted an hour and we sat and talked to him for another THREE hours after that, and he could have gone on even longer.  That particular ranger is also very good about catching and properly handling Wampus Cats - he almost always has one he has just captured in his truck and is taking out to release. 
 
It just figures that a park with that kind of track record also sees the wisdom of welcoming visitors who will promote visitation by others and will cooperate with those of us who are willing to responsibly use their facility.  Kudos to them, and kudos to you and LSQ for being ambassadors yet again!

We pretty much had the park to ourselves this time. You're right
about the friendly staff and they do have some excellent events.
One ranger is a natural story-teller and commedian. He came up to
the Mission while we were there and gave the kids bug-catchers, bug
boxes and magnifying glasses. He has a story-telling hour around
the fireplace, but you have to be there at the right time. I knew
that the park had a large area that was off-limits, but didn't know
about the archaeological site. That's cool. I didn't see any
Wampus Cats. Is that the kind with six legs that the Cherokees
brought from Tennessee? I think that's where the term "Catty
Wampus" comes from.

BT
