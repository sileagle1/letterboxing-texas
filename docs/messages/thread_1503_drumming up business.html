From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-12-04 15:50:22 UTC
Of course, we all had to find out about it some way. You'll get some
skepticism from many letterboxers about whether to introduce others to
the hobby, because we've seen the damage that short-termers can do.
You want people who are going to enjoy letterboxing, respect the
environment and others' efforts and in their own way contribute something.

Press publicity can be dangerous to the health of area letterboxers.
I would suggest introducing a few like-minded friends to the hobby and
really stressing to them the importance of few key things like
DISCRETION and REHIDING WELL. To be honest, though, we have sorta had
limited success with this approach--most of our family and friends
think we're weird. But they probably already thought that anyway!

Another approach that I think works well is to always answer your
contact the placer emails. Build bridges with those who show an
interest. Encourage new letterboxers to get more hooked in, through
this list, or AtlasQuest, etc. By doing this, you can encourage the
participation of those who have a natural interest and are in your
area. And perhaps they will become the hiders of great boxes for you
to find. It was directly due to the encouragement of some local
letterboxers that led to our placing our first box and getting hooked
on that part of the game too. Now we have some great friends who we
enjoy letterboxing with!

Dewberry

*****While I see June's point of view, I really relate
to Mike's... When I first began boxing two years ago,
there were precious few boxes within an hour's drive
from me, and every one of them was by Silver Eagle and
Baby Bear. Thank goodness for them! Further, for
over a year I was, to my knowledge, the only
letterboxer anywhere in Northeast Texas (no new boxes
being planted by others and no entries by anyone else
in existing boxes). Thankfully, I heard Silver
Eagle's and Baby Bear's story early on and was
encouraged by the fact that they had stuck with it
when there weren't but a handful of letterboxers and
boxes in the whole state! So I planted...

So how did there come to be more boxes and more boxers
in my area? Interestingly, it came through media
coverage. A local reporter contacted the placers of
boxes in my county and she netted Silver Eagle, Baby
Bear, and me. I can't remember if Baby Bear opted not
to be interviewed or if he and the reporter just
couldn't ever connect, but Silver Eagle and I wound up
talking to her. Before we did though, we talked
between ourselves about what needed to be emphasized
and what needed not to be disclosed. We stressed to
her not to publish clues in her article, but that she
could publish the website. I let her go with us while
we planted a box right in town for the purpose of the
article. The clues were purposely cryptic, so that
anyone who was half-hearted would probably not get it.
We also emphasized the need for discretion and
respect of property, including boxes. She graciously
honored our requests, and while the article had a few
flaws, she respected the points we insisted upon.

The net result of the article was TXLuvBug, among
others. She became a prolific placer and a diligent
finder. Others who flowed either directly or
indirectly from that article are Blue Butterfly and
Lucy Locket. As you probably know, they are also
eager letterboxers who have cast the circle of boxes
out a little wider still. For each of them, I am
thankful.

Do I think news media is the answer? While it sure
helped letterboxing in my area, I have to admit I'm
still very leery. I had an equally bad experience
with media coverage in another group I belong to -
same paper, different reporter, but the reporter was
ham-handed with the information he got from me and the
result was some pretty cruddy new members. If you
pursue media coverage, be very clear about what you
expect to get from it and maintain control over the
process. Research the reporter and figure out if
their style is really what you think will serve your
purpose. And if you find a reporter who bites, don't
get excited and give up the whole ball of wax (you
know, the "auction" reflex). Leave a little on the
table so that anyone interested still has to do a
little research to find us.

While I understand being lonely and wanting more
letterboxers in an area, I still firmly believe that
we are all better off being box-lonely than being in
the company of careless fools. In my opinion,
letterboxers are high level individuals - on the
whole, we have a wide array of interests and we have a
keenly developed sense of a big game. Think about it
- how many folks in the general population have all
the characteristics of a letterboxer: are up for
clue-solving, like the outdoors, have an eye for
detail, are creative thinkers, are technologically
capable, and hopefully have a small amount of artistic
bent?

If I were to look for other ways to promote
letterboxing, I would think of groups that exhibit at
least a couple of those traits, which is why Mike's
plan of planting hybrids may just work. You could
also offer to hold a class at a nearby
park/library/bookstore on boxing (but if I did that, I
would plant a series for the purpose so you aren't
giving up real boxes to people who may not "get it").
Talk to orienteering groups. But even so, be cautious
and don't give away the whole game by revealing real
boxes without at least warning the placer.

Besides that, I really encourage you to take the Baby
Bear/Silver Eagle approach and plant, plant, plant.
That way, when someone starts sniffing around, there
are boxes for them to find and hopefully you can hook
'em before they lose interest.

But I'm not sure if lack of boxes is the motivation of
Sandy's question...

Lucy

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2005-12-04 11:45:05 UTC-06:00
Two things that are close to my heart are letterboxing and the Appalachian Trail. Last year while in CT, I went in search of a box that had been placed on the Trail. After a long, wonderful hike, I came to the area where the box was hidden. Leading away from the main trail, an obvious social trail had developed to the site of the box due to many visits. When I opened the logbook, I found that it had been FILLED with messages from a youth group, telling me that Jesus loves me, quoting scriptures, and messages about who had crushes on whom, along with a couple of store bought stamps. Now that's great and all.... needless to say I was thrilled to read that Jesus loves me, because I was hoping He did, but reading that in a logbook of a letterbox just confirmed it for me. Sorry... my tongue is caught in my cheek a bit. I do feel that this group had good intentions, but they did ruin the logbook by filling it with personal messages. New letterboxers do need instruction on the aspects of letterboxing from how to appropriately stamp into a box, rehide it, as well as respect for the environment.  I do worry that too much general publicity about the sport may attract many "casual" boxers which can be bad for the sport as well as harmful to the boxes. One of the major concerns of the officials in most parks and nature areas is that social trails start to develop to the boxes that eventually harm the environment. To quote Dewberry, we all had to find out about it some way, and I actually did first learn of letterboxing from an article in a newspaper in Connecticut, looked it up the website and ran willy-nilly into the forest. I've introduced family and friends to letterboxing, and they in turn have introduced family and friends and so on. I would conservatively estimate that I am responsible for hundreds of very good letterboxers that have learned of the sport from me or from those that I've introduced to boxing. I do feel that personally introducing people to letterboxing is the best way to cultivate and nurture the sport.

I also agree, that's the best way to interest people appropriately in this activity.  It seems to me (who has been letterboxing now for just over a year) that the number of local boxers has almost exploded - when I first discovered Texas letterboxing (after first doing a little bit of CT boxing with my sister) and checked the LBNA list for Tarrant Co. and Dallas Co. and all the surrounding counties, too, there were very few boxes, and most of them were planted by either Kantexan in Tarrant Co. or Celtic Lions in Dallas Co., and I got to as many of these as quickly as I could and was completely hooked.  Almost immediately I began planning my own boxes and when I received those first emails from finders, I was thrilled!  I've noticed that within this past year, there seem to be many new boxers, or recently relocated experienced boxers, in this vicinity, and most seem willing to travel across the state if necessary to find good boxes.  Since I've visited your boxes and know what good locations and great stamps you've got, I'm sure they'll find many more visitors, but it may take awhile.  There seem to be periods of low activity and spurts of heavy boxing (I've had to replace a few smaller logbooks on about 6 of my boxes in pretty good locations).  I've inspired a boxer or two -
