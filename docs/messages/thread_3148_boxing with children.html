boxing with children

From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-11-01 13:01:59 UTC
In response to Mommyaulait's question, I thought I'd post some of my
thoughts about boxing with children. Everyone, feel free to add on!

1. I've found it's best to set up the outing so that it's a success
whether the box is there or not. If you focus solely on retrieving
the box, you may be disappointed, either because it's not there or
because you couldn't find it. So...find some trees to climb, some
tadpoles to watch, some stones to skip if you're out in the woods. If
you're in the city, choose a park with an unusual playground or
something nearby that will spark their interest. Some people want to
"prefind" to make sure the box is there so their kids won't be
disappointed. I personally think that they're missing a life lesson.
Sometimes you just can't find the box--that's part of it.

2. Snacks. I cannot emphasize this enough. Cool snacks they can
only get when hiking.

3. You will want to do more boxes than they will. Stop when they're
finished or you'll have a harder time getting them out there the next
time.

4. It's fun for them to retrieve the box, but you need to supervise.
And you should probably be the one to open it. Not all box images
are suitable for children, although the vast majority of them are.

5. Teach them not to scream, "WE FOUND IT" at the top of their lungs.
Some families use another code word which to them means the same
thing, but no one else would understand.

6. You should reseal. And you should rehide, at least at first. If
you let your kids do it, check their work. Look at the box from all
angles to make sure it's securely hidden.

7. Most of all, have fun. This is a great opportunity to be outside
with your kids and your family. Letterboxing has been a great vehicle
for us to share our love of hiking and being outdoors with our kids.


I think of our logbook as the scrapbook of our times together. In the
beginning, I focused on getting more finds (of course it was harder to
rack up finds then), but we've found that it works better for us to
focus on quality instead of quantity. We'll never find them all, so I
give up on that as our goal. Instead, we try to enjoy each box as it
comes, on its own terms.

These are great suggestions and I can only add a word of safety to this
one. Make sure whoever retrieves the box uses a stick or something
else to warn any critters that might be lurking around the box. I
don't want to scare you off, but snakes, scorpions, spiders, etc.
sometimes like the same places boxes do. If you are careful, you can
turn a potentially scary situation into a "cool" nature lesson. Have
fun!
