 WARNING!!!!!!!!!!!!
  
We went twice and loved it.  We found you must stand on the bridge above the bayou facing east (downtown) to get the best view of nearly 1/4 of a million bats dispersing toward downtown in approximately 2 minutes. 
 
However I need to alert you to a safety concern.
 
The first time we went we parked three blocks south of the bridge at a Jack-in-the-Box on Waugh.  The walk was nice, but on the way back to the car a young guy, (I'll describe as "surfer-dudish" because of his short bleached spiked hair and speech patterns), verbally threatened my nine-year-old.  He was either out of his mind on drugs or just out of his mind.  Something was definitely wrong with him.  It happened while crossing the very busy street.  Another couple was with us and I felt I could keep my children safe, but now wish I'd had my pepper spray in my pocket.
 
I only had my boys with me that trip.  The week after Thanksgiving, I was arriving near the intersection about one hour before I needed to have my daughter to the Houston Ballet for her classes, so we decided to stop again.  We parked exactly where Angela did and our van was burglarized.  The window was broken, a leather seat punctured by glass and three bags were stolen.  I phoned 911 and was told to vacate the area, a police man would call me.  The report was taken by phone.  The policeman called that area Tinsley Park and said it is known for crime.  Tinsley Park's larger parking lot is actually about 100 yards east of this four car parking space.  The area Angela and I used is right next to the fountain my children affectionately call the daffodil, because when it sprays water, it looks just like the white weed, before it looses its seeds.
 
If I were to go again, which my husband is not supporting, given our first two trips, I would park at the southwest corner of the Allen Parkway/Waugh Intersection.  There is an upscale, two-story strip center which is busy.  Keep all valuables out of sight:  dvds, garage door openers, purses, lunch bags, computers, etc.  The bats take off at dusk.  The night we went a month ago, it was at 5:32.  Three weeks before that, it was at 5:45.  Winter's official start would have been the shortest day of the year and the earliest they would have been departing the bridge.  You want to be there just before sundown.
 
Post Katrina/Rita, we have more homeless and beggars than usual in that area.   I still highly recommend the trip.  The bats were phenomenal.  However, I do not want anyone to endure what we did. 

p.s.  My car alarm did not sound.  The policeman indicated these guys know what they are doing.  If they do not open a door, the alarm is not activated.  They punched the window and grabbed the goods.

From: Michael P. Olbrisch (mike2004@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2005-12-28 23:45:47 UTC
Hmmmm....  I am very glad you escaped without incident!!!!!  While it is true that some places are more prone to such activities than others, everyone should realize that there ARE evil people in the world intent on having their way without regard to the common law abiding citizen.  This could happen anywhere.
 
Your first line of defense should be your alertness and common sense.  If you feel uneasy - there is probably a reason for it.  Avoidance is the best policy.  Most people are afraid of offending strangers, so they act as though everything is normal when their senses tell them there is trouble.  Pay attention to your feelings.  ACT!  Don't slouch and avoid eye contact like a victim.
 
If you are caught off guard, your defensive posture is strictly a personal matter.  I believe in stand-off weapons over contact weapons.  A good pistol or PEPPER FOAM (NOT SPRAY) allow you to defend without contact.  AVOID CONTACT.  If you can touch them - they can touch you.  You do not want to let them close to contact.
 
As a last resort - any object can be pressed into a defensive weapon.  Hold your key ring in the palm of your hand, with the key points protruding out between your fingers.  Punch the key points into the eyes or other sensitive areas.  Engage and escape.  You can call for the police later - escape and survival of you and your party is the first priority.
 
Again - alertness and avoidance are your number one goals.  Overall our state is a 99% safe place.  With a little attention paid to your surroundings, you can ensure your family of that last 1%.
 
Let me share one other thing with you.  This was drilled into me as a young man.  It is called the Combat Color Code.  People say I live more in orange than yellow.  Possibly true.  I can accept it.  I hope everyone reading this avoids white.
 
White
Oblivious to your surroundings. You are waiting to be a victim.  You should never be in white while awake.
 
Yellow
Alert to your surroundings, no particular threat(s) or danger.
 
Orange
Possible threats or danger nearby.  You are alert and ready but action is not eminent.
 
Red
You are in danger.  Take appropriate action to ensure survival for you and your family.
 
Black
Action taken - threat(s) or danger neutralized.  Move to safety and call for help.
 
The NRA offers a great program called REFUSE TO BE A VICTIM.  I recommend it if it is offered in your area.
 
Again, so happy you escaped unharmed.
 
Mike.

This is what I recommend.

http://www.milestonesafety.com/mac10pepfoam2.html

Why foam? The foam squirts out like shaving cream. It is a heavy stream,
not a mist that can blow back in your face if the wind is against you. It
sticks to the attacker like shaving cream too. And it expands to get behind
glasses. The more they try to wipe it away, the more it gets rubbed in.
Last, it contains a UV dye that will identify your attacker for a number of
days.

I n Texas pepper spray (foam) is considered simple assault. If you are
authorized to punch in defense of yourself, you are authorized to use pepper
foam. If you are charged at all, the charges would be the same if you spray
them or hit them. In simpler terms - pepper foam is considered to be the
same level of force as a fist. But it has the stand-off advantage. You
want to keep them from closing to contact if at all possible.

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-12-28 18:25:02 UTC-08:00
My son, Lightning, and I just got home from a long
letterboxing day in Rusk and I find this topic running
on the list. After our experience today, it's
interesting the timing...

We had a full day of boxing planned - I wanted to
check every box in Rusk and I wanted to plant six at
one park and 2 at another. We got to Jim Hogg
Historical Site and sat down at a picnic table to sort
out our stuff and make sure we had everything we
needed. The park was dead empty except for the
groundskeeper when we arrived - empty is a situation
that makes me a little uneasy when I am about to head
off into the woods. Shortly after we got there,
another vehicle pulled in and a guy got out and
started walking around. My son had finished repacking
his pack so he had taken off a little distance away to
play around with the GPS while I finished my own pack.
The guy came over and started up a conversation and
he gave me the creeps. I don't get creepy about folks
too often (in fact, it's a widely known fact that I
have a weirdo magnet, so I often find myself engaged
with folks who are a little - different), but this guy
was sending out the creep vibes, not the oddball
vibes. I spotted my son and wanted to get closer to
him, so I told the guy that I was waiting for my
husband and he would be really mad if he saw me
talking to another man (my teddy bear husband nearly
fell over laughing when I told him this part of the
story), then grabbed both packs and threw them back in
my car and went to my son, who was closer to the road
and to the buildings and so was safer anyway.
Meanwhile, I had memorized the guy's license number
when I walked by his truck and once we were near the
building where the groundskeeper had disappeared, I
made a note of the license plate and a description of
the guy and the truck. I felt really silly since all
I really had to bother me was creepy feelings, but I
went ahead and disturbed the groundskeeper and asked
him if he knew the guy. Sure enough, he apparently
takes his lunch break there at the park every day.
The groundskeeper didn't know his name, but knew his
habit, so I felt better anyway. Still though, I trust
my creepy feelings implicitly and even if the guy
turned out not to be an immediate threat, there was
"something" about him that I didn't like. Although we
had packed a lunch, I decided we would leave for a bit
and grab lunch in town and return when the guy's lunch
hour was over.

It did make me really think about our safety though.
I checked my pepper spray and realized it is expired
(guess I'll be shopping for foam instead). And I
checked my cell phone to see how charged up it was -
and it wasn't. Sheesh. Was I being complacent or
what? I had theories about what I would do in most
given situations, but I sure wasn't prepared to carry
them out on this particular day!

After we returned to the park and the guy was gone, we
sat at a picnic table again to relax and digest lunch
before we hit the trail. While we were sitting there,
we talked about the different safety gear we had with
us and how we could adapt it to different emergency
situations. We talked about why I had my whistle and
my compass tied to the outside of my pack instead of
tucked inside. We played a little what-if. Talked
about how our new GPS could help us in an emergency.
Regretted not bringing our 2-way radios.

As it happened, a couple of different groups of moms
and kids had arrived between the time we left and
returned, and a couple of ladies were sitting at a
table near us, so as we got up and got ready to head
out, one of the ladies came over and asked us about
the length and difficulty of the trail. I was waiting
for Lightning to return from the car, so I told her
the highlights of the trail and what could be easily
reached and what should be avoided. As she was going
back to her table, she commented that she had
overheard my son and I talking about safety and asked
me how I could take him out there if there were so
many things to worry about. It was at that moment
that I realized I have a lot of confidence in him and
me - because we HAVE talked about situations and
identified potential problems and pre-determined
possible courses of action. But more importantly, by
talking out situations, we are training ourselves to
think on our feet. I told her that by actually
talking about potential problems and brainstorming our
way through them, we can go hiking together
worry-free.

Lots of folks allow thinking about bad things to make
them scared and fearful, but I think that's because
they don't think through to the resolution - the
moment when they have successfully handled a situation
using nothing but their own brains and the resources
they will have with them. The likelihood that we can
predict exactly how a situation will happen is pretty
slim, but we can be confident that we can think
quickly to handle whatever may come up.

Lucy
