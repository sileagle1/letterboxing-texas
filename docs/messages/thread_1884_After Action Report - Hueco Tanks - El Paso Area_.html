After Action Report - Hueco Tanks - El Paso Area.

From: Michael P. Olbrisch (mike2004@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2006-05-14 04:42:53 UTC

Good morning Texas letterboxers,

        I promised an After Action Report on the Hueco Tanks Letterboxes.  This is it.  Let me begin by saying none of this would have been possible without the cooperation of the Texas Parks and Wildlife staff at Hueco Tanks State Park.  For a few months I have been dealing with Rangers Danny Lee, (an old friend from another park), and Wanda Olszewski, park superintendent.  They arranged for a park volunteer named Ken Steiner to guide us in the areas where guides were necessary.  Sooo.... Today, 13 May 2006 was the day.

        Moni and I arrived a little bit early for our 0800 appointment.  Wanda and Ken were both ready and waiting.  After a few preliminaries, we were off to the area.  Ken got us permission to drive back into the area.  That saved Moni a lot of walking on her bad knee.  We did a lot of that anyway as you will read.

        We started out with the clues to Hueco Tanks #1.

Hueco Tanks #1
This box is Unclaimed.
Placement date: Jul 11 2000
State: Texas
County: El Paso
Nearest city: El Paso
Number of boxes: 1

Clues
HUECO TANKS LETTERBOX #1

End of paved road on North Mountain. From last covered pick-nick
area 20 NE to large white rock, approx 30 paces. 40 NE to first
large oak, approx 50 paces. From large flat rock due south 8 paces.
Under rock on slab with small tree growing out.


        The directions were RIGHT ON.  Unfortunately, the directions required us to cross some ground that is now closed to the public.  Under Ken's guidance, we carefully pace-counted to the rock at 20 magnetic.  Perfect.  Turn to 40 magnetic and pace-counted to the next point - the tree.  Right on again.  The large flat slab of rock was there.  The excitement builds.  Turn due south - 8 paces.  The "slab" with the small tree growing out of it was flat, but not horizontal.  The crevasse with the tree was about 7 feet above the desert floor.  It was not a problem to step up high enough to see in the crevasse.  So step up I did.  Rocks piled in the crevasse.  I moved the rocks.  Well.......  Did I find it? 

YES!!!!!

        It was in there, just as the clues said it should be.  Very excited now.  Then disappointment.  Friends, this is a PERFECT EXAMPLE of why I have a love for ammo cans.  And it isn't just because I am retired Army.  This container -MAY- have been waterproof under the -BEST- of circumstances.  The sun had eaten away at the plastic on the upper area.  This left a sizeable crack exposed to the top.  This allowed rain to get in.  The stamp was falling apart.  The wood handle was covered in moist green mold.  The logbook was thoroughly soaked.  It may dry out.  Time will tell.  Later back at the park HQ, I was able to wipe the stamp and stamp it in my logbook.  There was no way for me to stamp my stamp in the letterbox logbook.  If this stamp has any meaning to anyone, please fill us in.  I am not even sure it is oriented right.  It could be up-side-down for all I know.  This is the stamp.

    We made our way back to the great white dog show van and put the letterbox in it.  Then after a short discussion, we started out  for Hueco Tanks #2. 

 
Hueco Tanks #2
This box is Unclaimed.
Placement date: Jul 11 2000
State: Texas
County: El Paso
Nearest city: El Paso
Number of boxes: 1
 
Clues
HUECO TANKS LETTERBOX #2
 
If one climbs the south side of North Mountain; follows one of the
easy routes at the end of the paved road (all 5.4 to 5.6 stuff) they
lead to a large amphitheatre that has a huge oak in it. The
amphitheatre is accessible from all directions. In a hueco (natural
hole-as is the area) 14 paces from the old oak is a box covered by a
rock.  Note: access to this area requires some rock climbing.
 
    These clues are a bit imprecise.  However, Ken led us along the back side of North Mountain.  We scouted from the ground and saw a few places that looked promising.  Seeing them is one thing.  GETTING to them is yet another.  The south side is layered in several levels or "steps".  We worked our way up layer by layer, exploring anything that looked like a possibility along the way.  There were several places where you might call it a large amphitheater.  There were several places where there were large oak trees.  There were a number of places where 14 paces was possible.  It was getting hot.  Ken and I were going into places where Moni couldn't follow.  And I was carrying the CamelBak.  Plus we had a LOT of mountain to cover.
 
    While separated from Moni, Ken and I found a place that looked VERY promising.  Ken said the climb in was the 5.4 to 5.6 as stated in the clues.  While the area wasn't what I would call a "LARGE" amphitheater, there was room for 14+ paces.  Best of all, it was accessible from all directions, AND it contained a HUGE oak tree.  I climbed down and did a quick search.  But because Moni was down below in the hot sun, and I had the water, a hurried search was all I did.  I climbed back out, and Ken and I went back out to get Moni and continue up the mountain.
 
    We found a few other places that had possibilities, but nothing that met the clues like that one spot.  After a while, we decided that we had worked our way too far north.  Moni was hot, her knee was starting to give out, and we had a long way to go to get back.  So we decided to stop the search at this point.  Unsuccessful on finding Hueco Tanks #2, Ken led us out on the opposite end from where we parked.  Along the way, Ken led us to some of the nicer pictographs in the park.  I got some really nice photos.  You can see some of the hike photos here:
 
http://www.vonbonehenge.com/news/huegotanks/2006_05_13/index.htm
 
    We got Moni to a covered picnic bench, and Ken and I hiked around to get the vehicles.  Got the van, picked up Moni, and we went to report our movements and findings to Wanda.  At this point, it was well past 1400.  That is 2pm for you civilians.  Over 6 hours on that rough terrain.  No wonder Moni was wore out.
 
    We met with Wanda.  I turned over Hueco Tanks #1 to her.  We discussed options.  They will review the situation, and if it is at all possible, I will be allowed to make a replacement stamp and a replacement letterbox.  The clues may need to be changed.  The location may need to be changed.  It may even be necessary to place it outside the park.  But I feel certain we will get as fair a deal on this as they can accommodate for us.  I hope Wanda dries out the logbook.  I think adding wax paper between each page and letting it dry naturally would be the best bet.  But I am no expert.
 
    I also feel that the place with the huge oak tree still holds the best chance for finding Hueco Tanks #2.  I will be seeking permission for a return soon.  Just as soon as my legs heal from this outing.  <grin>  There were enough places in that spot that two people could spend an hour or more searching.  Since it is on the North Mountain, it is possible that I could be allowed to search that place again without making Ken guide us.  I would like to entice the other "NEW" Letterboxer from El Paso to come along.  We have hiked together before - I trust him.  Hmmmm....  wonder if Brad trusts me???  <grin>  There WAS that trip to the repeater site......  <grin>  I don't want Moni to hurt her knee going in there with me.
 
    So this completes the After Action Report for our Hueco Tanks outing.  We were 50% successful finding #1, and gained a lot of good data on where #2 isn't.
 
END OF REPORT.
 
    I am going to work on a thank-you note to Wanda, and a thank-you to her supervisors.  Maybe a few of you will volunteer to do the same?  If any volunteer, I will get names and addresses to you.
 
NOTE:  If anyone had ANY other information about these two boxes.... ANYTHING AT ALL, please share with me.  It might help.  I will keep the group posted on further events.
 
Mr. Silver Eagle sir... anything???????
