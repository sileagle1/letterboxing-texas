Lena,

As I mentioned in my post - it's an ongoing debate.
Some folks are looking for art and some are looking
for experience. It isn't WRONG to expect art, but
you'll probably be disappointed a lot more often than
not if that's the focus. I have to confess that while
my focus is on the experience, it heightens my
experience to find a very nice stamp. But I also
appreciate the work of anyone who places a hand-carved
stamp, especially knowing the level where my
beginnings were.

Why don't you suggest to your hubby that he carve a
stamp to be planted? I'm betting he'll carve
something that is something less than Dega and he'll
be proud of it! And then you can venture out in the
extreme heat again and plant it....

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-08-01 05:10:53 UTC-07:00
I was carving a stamp at lunch the other day and a
coworker was saying "I could never do that - I'm such
a perfectionist, I would never be happy with my work."

I told her that that is exactly what carving has
taught me - that everything I do doesn't have to be
perfect, and that if I can't be happy with what I've
done, I can recarve it. It's freed me from
perfectionism. There are stamps I've carved 8 or 10
times before I could step back and be proud of them.
But that's okay, because as long as I have more
material to carve and I don't have time constraints or
standards besides my own to meet. I can still see
imperfections in every stamp I've done, but I can
appreciate the character they lend to the piece.

Oddly, I've been really under the gun on a couple of
big projects lately, and yet I've been at my best
creatively in terms of boxing. I think it's become my
pressure valve that keeps me from blowing a gasket -
the thing I can stop and do when I feel like my head
is going to explode.

Lucy
