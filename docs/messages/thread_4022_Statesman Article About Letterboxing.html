Statesman Article About Letterboxing

From: Anna (annabee44@gmail.com) | Date: 2009-03-29 15:18:28 UTC

The following article appeared in the Stateman yesterday. I am sure others share my concern about the specificity with which the jounalist describes certain letterboxes. Baby Bears Fox in Sox, Boots Tex "The General" , Silver Eagles Rough Racerunner and Lone Star Quilters Bluebird of Happiness were specifically mentioned.

Another journalist, also with the Statemen, has been interviewing Austin letterboxers for another upcoming story just about letterboxing. While I am definitely in the "the more the merrier" camp, I also think any discussion of letterboxing in the paper needs to be respectful of the boxes out there and the author should have some understanding of the need for discretion. Any other thoughts?

*************************


RAISING AUSTIN: CAROLYN LINDELL

McKinney Roughs nature program just part of day of adventure
Saturday, March 28, 2009

Sometimes you go to the store to buy apples, but you come home with Dutch apple pie.

That's how it felt after a recent trip to McKinney Roughs Nature Park in Bastrop County. I signed up my two children for a two-hour Junior Naturalists program, for ages 6 to 10 (my 5-year-old was allowed to join).

The program itself is an easy way to get kids looking around outdoors on the lovely grounds of the Lower Colorado River Authority's 1,100-acre park and natural science center. As it turns out, we also had a nice picnic and met a family who showed us all about "letterboxing," which is like a treasure hunt to search for hidden rubber stamps that you imprint in a logbook. Put all that together and our simple morning plans extended into an afternoon of exploration and delight for the children.

Of course, outdoor discovery is the purpose of the educational park, as well as the Junior Naturalists program, which meets 10 a.m. to noon the first Saturday of the month, through May (it should start again in September). Cost is $5 per child (preregistration required).

"We usually teach something of the naturalists' skills, such as journaling, tracking, learning how to research using field guides," said Erin Barker, natural science program coordinator. "The whole point is for them to learn about something in the natural world and get outdoors and introduce them to the park."

We started at the Visitors Center, which houses a gift shop, an exhibit of small native animals (snakes, gecko, etc.) as well as classroom space. The center is spacious and crisply designed.

Only two other kids, Sara and William Perkins were signed up for the Junior Naturalists, though up to 10 kids per teacher can participate. Parents aren't required to stay, so Rayven and Ed Perkins opted to go to the farmers market in Bastrop.

Though topics vary, that day naturalist Louise Ridlon was teaching about camouflage in nature. She started off by having the kids make and decorate journals. Then we headed out on a short hike, until we came to a clearing where Ridlon scattered colored toothpicks. The kids had to find the toothpicks while doing relay races. (Ridlon, a former kindergarten teacher, knows how to get kids to burn off energy.) They did this twice, once over dirt and grass, and again amid dried pine needles. They counted up which colors they found the most/least of and took notes in their journal. Ridlon, who was patient when attentions wandered, discussed how animals that blend in with their surroundings can better elude predators and catch prey.

"Would you want to be a red worm?" easily spotted by predators, she asked. No, they chimed. Then we headed back inside, where the kids designed camouflaged animals, using scissors, markers, etc. Artistic Sara, 7, created a tiger at sunset.

That was it - short and simple. It would have been fine if our day ended there. We had gotten to roam the park and learn a bit. But the Perkinses, RV travelers staying for now in Bastrop, asked if we'd like to join them letterboxing.

That's when our day went from perfectly fine apples to sweet apple pie.

Letterboxing (not a program of the park) is an international hobby where people hide small containers that hold a rubber stamp and a booklet. You can look up www.letterboxing.org to find directions to letterboxes wherever you travel. Rayven Perkins had printed out clues to four letterboxes in McKinney Roughs, so off we went.

The kids ran excitedly ahead along the trails as she called out, "We're looking for a bench on the right" or "Look down the hill straight ahead for a stump."

The letterboxes were hidden in trees and under rocks. We found stamps picturing Texas Gov. Edmund Davis, a bluebird, a fox in socks and a lizardlike critter, which was particularly hard to find because it came in a tiny film-size canister. The kids loved finding these hidden items.

The Perkins family brought their own inkpad and stamp, picturing an RV. They stamped their own logbook and left an imprint of their RV in each letterbox's small logbook. (Letterboxing is similar to geocaching, but without the need for a GPS device.)





Re: Statesman Article About Letterboxing

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2009-03-29 16:18:20 UTC
After reading the article (I read the full article on the Austin American-Statesman web site), my opinion is that it presents letterboxing in a postive light and that the clues are not specific enough for anyone to use them to find the box. Of course, it does provide the location of the web site, so anyone could go there and figure out how to get the clues if they were of a mind to do so, but the web site is available to the public in any case. Now, someone with the park (it's a Lower Colorado River Authority park) could get curious and go find the boxes, but again, that could happen in any place. So, the question is, do we want to advertise letterboxing or try to keep it a secret? I think the article illustrates that letterboxing is a legitimate form of family recreation. I'm not offended by it at all.

Boots Tex

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@windstream.net) | Date: 2009-03-31 01:56:37 UTC
It sounds like the letterboxers did not know the person was a journalist when they invited her to box with them, but I'm not sure. If they had known, they might have warned her of the things Boots mentions here. Either that or we were lucky the journalist just didn't know the names of the boxes. I have to admit I was a little hurt that she called my stamp a "lizard-like creature". I know I'm not the greatest carver in the world, but it stings when somebody doesn't even recognize what you carved :|

The few times I have been interviewed about letterboxing I was hesitant to do it for fear of too much exposure. It is a tough line to walk; wanting more people to know about letterboxing, but not wanting temporary, careless boxers causing problems until they get bored with it all. I usually end up doing the interview because I figure the article will be written anyway, so I might as well get my 2 cents in. I always stress the fun aspects of the hobby, but temper it with cautions about being stealthy and low-key. It is ironic that I have never been interviewed for a TX publication, but I know it is just a matter of time. It is hard to criticize people doing articles about letterboxing when that is how I found out about it, but I still think the less said the better. If somebody really wants to find out more about it, they will do so on there own, and probably be a good, long-lasting boxer because of it.

Silver Eagle
