Randy asked last week while we were discussing clues for some help with some definitions.  Since the clues discussion has died off, I thought we might tackle some definitions for him and others.
 
First of all, a very good glossary of letterboxing terms can be found at Silent Doug's place - www.letterboxing.info.  Silent Doug and his site is heavily relied upon in the letterboxing world for definitions and standards.  This is NOT to say he is the final source in all things, but if you want to get a good solid bit of help, try his site out.
 
With that said, reading the definitions doesn't necessarily give newbies a flavor of what the words refers to.  And to make matters more confusing, we Texas letterboxers have our own few odd phrases. 
Shall we start with Hitchhiker?  Anybody want to kick off a discussion of HH's with a definition, and maybe a bit about the practical side of handling them, and a little etiquette thrown in?

For all this training of how to keep peoples' heads out of their butts, it sure seems like there are still a lot of them around who don't have this particular skill yet!  Is it possible there should be a remedial course, or possibly a corrective course, maybe titled "Cranial-Posterior Separation Education?"
 
Speaking of survival training...
 
I went to a scout meeting this evening - Boy Scouts, not the Cubs I usually hang out with.  They played a game I thought was pretty cool, and now I wish I had brought the paper home with me so I could remember it all.  The game was - you are on a family vacation in Maine in July.  You're in an old van and you've driven many miles into wilderness, crossing over a maze compiled of numerous truck paths but seeing no vehicles for quite a long time.  It's pouring down rain with no prospect of letting up, when your van breaks down.  You are stranded in the middle of nowhere with your parents, two siblings, and the family cat (who the heck travels with their CAT?).  The weather gets down to 40 degrees at night and it IS bug season.  Your family decides that the only option is to strike out walking, and that it is best not to split up.  The boys were then given a list of about 35 items and they had to prioritize them according to what they would want to have with them.  The list included things like a family tent, sleeping bags (not backpacking sleeping bags), 3 lbs of steak, 6 cans of cat food, peanut butter, a 44 magnum, a bowie knife, a map of Maine, a first aid kit, 5 gals of water, peanut butter, breakfast mix of some sort, cigarettes, matches, insect repellant, an alarm clock, a fan, an am/fm radio, and bunches of other things.
 
It became immediately clear which boys had truly done wilderness activities or activities where they had to be wise in what they carried with and which ones had done the easy campouts.   I wish I had read Mike's post BEFORE I went - because I probably taken Army apps with me for some of them! 
 
All but one patrol opted for the steak in their first five items - yet nothing to cook them with and nothing to keep them cold.  The smart ones asked if Maine wild water is potable and if the rainwater was likely safe - to which the answer was yes - so they passed on carrying 5 gals of water.  I was amazed to see that only one patrol chose the peanut butter over every other type of food on the list.  Thankfully, the patrol Lightning hung out with had the overall smartest list, so I'm glad he's my LB cohort!
 
The question I never got answered was who got stuck carrying the stinking cat in the rain?  Seriously - who the heck travels with their CAT?

Well - I was a desert warrior. So I am well adept at desert
survival. Let me share something with you all.



Ten Commandments of Survival in the Desert - When lost or stranded.





1. Hold on to a SURVIVAL ATTITUDE. Your most valuable asset in any
life-threatening situation is a positive mental attitude. If you are not
certain you can live - you will die.



2. Stay where you are - stay calm. If you are driving a vehicle, remain
with it. Relocate only to reach SAFETY and WATER.



3. Move only when absolutely necessary and only at night. If your
position is unendurable, change your location during the cooler night hours.
Move only when you know you can get there safely by doing the following.



a. Leave a clear trail with notes and direction signs.

b. On the note, give your name, date, time, direction, and reason you
are moving.

c. Proceed in a specific direction. Change your line of movement ONLY
AFTER you have left a sign or marker.

d. Go slowly and carefully - beware of overexertion.



4. Conserve your sweat, not your water. Rest by day. Work on shelter
and signals in the cool of the evening or morning. Drink as often as you
need water. Rub your body with urine and other liquids to keep cool.

5. Protect your body. When in the sun and heat remember to:

a. Keep your clothes on - loosen but do not remove.

b. Keep your boots and head gear on.

c. Relax in deep shade - keep your eyes protected from glare.

d. If in the open with no shade nearby, use anything available to make
shade.



6. Make a fuss when you hear or see others nearby:

a. Signal by any means at hand. Use a mirror to reflect the sun. Wave
brightly colored items.

b. Make marks in the sand or lay out rocks large enough to be seen from
the air: SOS or HELP.

c. Start a fire - SMOKE in the daytime and FLAME at night.

d. Get involved in your rescue, but conserve your body water.



7. DO NOT EAT ANYTHING!

a. All food is water demanding. Water is drawn out of your system to
process, digest, and eliminate what you eat.

b. No salt or salt tabs - they will dehydrate you.



8. Keep your mouth closed. Breathe through your nose to minimize
evaporative losses.



9. Think like a searcher. Do the things that will make it easier for
your rescuers to find you:

a. Leave a clear trail with notes and direction signs.

b. On the notes, give your name, date, time, direction, and the reason
you are going.

c. Have a goal in mind - go in one direction with care. AVOID INJURY.



10. Use your head, not your sweat; drink the water you have. Never ration
water! Drink what you have when you need it. Discipline is essential to
survival.

================================================================





Print copies - stick them in your car and pack.

It truly IS a beautiful place.  There is a lot of history - and a lot of restrictions - to that area.
 
Hueco Tanks is an area of low mountains in a high-altitude desert basin between the Franklin Mountains to the west and the Hueco Mountains to the east.
 
Hueco is a Spanish word meaning hollows and refers to the many water-holding depressions in the boulders and rock faces throughout the region. Hueco Tanks is thus a redundant phrase.
