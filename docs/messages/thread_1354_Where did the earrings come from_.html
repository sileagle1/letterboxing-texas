Bunny,

You haven't committed a boxing foul - and this list is
a pretty safe place to ask questions without worrying
about such a thing. I guess the only real offense you
could commit would be spoiling a box by telling the
list exactly where to find it.

Another option is that the box might have been found
by a geocacher who found the box and left a trinket
and probably just signed in, since they don't have
personal stamps unless they box also. It would've
been customary for the cacher to leave a trinket and
also take one, so you have to wonder what they thought
about not having anything to take! If this is what
happened, it sounds like they were savvy enough to
realize the stamp needed to stay in the box, so they
left it as they should have done. There are occasions
of geocachers finding letterboxes and not
understanding them, they take the stamp as the
trinket. Talk about a good way to get a letterboxer
hopping mad! I'm sure cachers have comparable stories
about letterboxers...

You can always log on to geocaching.com (you'll have
to make a user name) and look to see if there are
possibly caches near the box. While boxing leads a
finder to a very specific location, unless both the
placing and the finding geocacher have really, really
good GPS's OR the placer added sufficient clues to the
cache's page to make it possible to identify the
specific location, the finder gets to the right area
and then has to start shuffling around to find the
cache. That's how a lot of boxes get turned up by
cachers accidentally.

I think it behooves us all on both sides of the fence
to be at the very least aware of how the other hobby
operates. Geocaching.com is a good website and has
lots of good information for newbie cachers. And if
you are so inclined, try it out! I found several
caches last year, but it just wasn't my thing.

And if it wasn't a geocacher that left the earrings, I
think we had better re-explore the activities of
BEA's, cause we have a mystery on our hands!

Lucy

*****I'm very glad to know this - the caches in my
area typically had junk in them and I found that
aspect to be less than inviting. Before I went and
found my first cache, I stressed over what I would
take to exchange. Didn't want to do too little, but
didn't want to go overboard either. I got to the
first one and realized I needn't have worried. While
I letterbox for the location and the art, I found
myself geocaching for the curiosity of what was going

because my family LOVES bouncy balls!). A coke tab in
another, and other stuff of equally forgettable
origins in the others. My idea of "value" is
something intriguing or quirky, not necessarily of
monetary value. I had a stock of AFLAC ducks for
awhile (hubby is an insurance agent), so I left those
in three of the caches I found. I personally thought
they were VERY cool!
