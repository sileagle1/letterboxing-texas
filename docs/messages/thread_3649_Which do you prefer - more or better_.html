Which do you prefer - more or better?

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-07-13 19:35:18 UTC-05:00
Funny how things hit you - but I was on a scout outing this weekend - a family trip with Lightning's patrol - when this very question came up.  Three of the four families are well aware of letterboxing and the fact that if we go anywhere, you can bet I'm also taking some clues if there are any boxes in the area to be found.  The fourth family is new to scouting and I only met the mom for the first time this weekend.
 
Last night, after dinner all together (14 of us total), I announced that I was heading out to find a few boxes before sunset (7:30, so I only had time for a scant few at best).  The mom of the new family asked me what I was talking about and I invited her along.  The other two moms didn't want to go, so it was just the two of us - and a great opportunity to get to know one another.  I gave her the clue book and we went over my homemade map and we set out.  She was by far the best navigator I've ever boxed with - hands down, no questions asked.   We got to the site of the first box and she had actually been there before so she was telling me all about the place.  I had never been, but I was really bummed they were closed because listening to her describe it, it sounded like my kind of place - one of those places I probably never would've found, but for letterboxing.  In fact, I might well have spent an hour or two there before I moved along!  We quickly found the box, and we found a gate that was open that allowed us to explore a bit before leaving - needless to say - nervously since we were afraid someone might come along and lock the gate.  It was a cool experience.
 
We went on and found a second one, but it took us longer driving around to find the specific location (not actual road time, but on-site drive time) than actually finding the box, and when we found it, the view from it was - well - uninspiring.  While the subject matter might have been quite fascinating, that wasn't particularly well brought out in the clues and I found myself wishing I had thought to do more research of my own about the location.  Because of the fading light, we decided to call it good for the night.
 
As we were driving away, she said "You know, now I wish we had stopped after the first one."  I was a little shocked to hear her say that, because I've felt that myself sometimes - the next to last box was such a good one - why did I have to go for "one more"?  It reminded me yet again that it is just fine to find even just one box in a day, if it was a really good one - one at a location I can get excited about, a nice stamp, a good search - that sort of thing, rather than boxes I can practically hang my hand out of the car to grab.  What really bugged me about the whole thing with her was - I went and found a few more early this morning, but that second box so turned her off that she wasn't interested in finding anymore, even after she had seemed to exchited about learning about the game.  Hopefully she'll go with me again sometime, but it won't be because she thinks she'll find anything like that second one.
 
So what do y'all think?  To make it easier for you to think about it, here's a scenario - you are in a town with 40 boxes you have never found.  Would you strive to find the 40, choosing not to spend any more time in one place than you have to to find the box?  Or would you strive to find the 40, and plan to come back some other time to explore the cool things you didn't have time to do on your boxing day?  Or would you just go ahead and take the time to explore while you box, and plan to return some other time to find more boxes and do more exploring?  Are you a numbers person or an experiential person?  Needless to say, there is no right or wrong answer, and probably not even very many folks who are strictly one way or the other.  I'm just curious the thoughts of the group...

--
Barefoot Lucy

ok i will put my two cents in on this one...i really enjoy boxes that take me to interesting places with interesting stories, which was one of the reasons that i decided this year to make my boxes have more history and less "trivial" stuff.  i have had 3 knee surgeries, so i really tend to try and avoid long walks, but will walk if there are boxes and the mood/knees are doing ok.  i also know that i tend to be a marathon boxer, trying to get in as many as possible when i go out, but if there is a site that i want to spend some time looking around, i will take the time to do so.  i also think that since a lot of my boxing is either by myself or with wag time or with my mom, that it makes a difference since wag time nor i have kids (2 legged kids) and so we both can go at a pace for us.  there will always be some boxes that are disappointing, but to me those boxes just make you appreciate the good ones even more.  i guess i sort of live by the philosophy that someone has taken the time to place the box for some reason and that i can appreciate.  one more issue with this question is that with gas prices as high as they are and in the region that i live having boxes so spread out, that if i am going to spend the gas money, then i want to make the most out of it and get the most boxes i can in one trip......ok, that is my 2 cents worth, of course i am not even sure if it is worth that...LOL

This will come as no surprise to you, Lucy, because I live in an area where there probably are at least 40 boxes which I haven't found. Even with the knowledge that some will inevitably go missing before I have the opportunity to find them, I still feel no pressure to rush around from box to box gathering as many stamps as possible. To me, a mediocre experience is a real downer, but a wonderfully interesting box (ie: total package) is a rush! I so admire and appreciate planters who put a lot of thought into their boxes, clues and locations. I've definitely been known to only pick up one or two boxes in a town, because I want to enjoy the entire experience - even if it takes all day. A really good box is an inspiration for me to keep striving to make my boxes better. My goal is for people to enjoy the boxes I've placed enough that I can feel I've contributed to a positive and fun experience for whoever finds it. I have several boxes on my worktable that I haven't placed yet because I haven't found a "perfect spot" for them yet.
Still striving....

From: Wanda (wsthm@hotmail.com) | Date: 2008-07-14 14:03:33 UTC
I am somewhere in the middle, but closer to "any letterbox is
wonderful". But I am in Bee County and there are no letterboxes here
except ones I have planted, and no great sites to hide letterboxes,
really.

For me, the 'count' has nothing to do with it. But, in the day, I did
like to get as many as possible, because...well, just because they
are there, and I might not get another chance to get them, I guess.
But still, I did not rush frantically around trying to get every last
one. I am no longer willing to go any distance to letterbox, so that
leaves me in kind of a quandry. I am in the position of hiding and
finding my own boxes. ;)

But a less than stellar letterbox has not ever left a bad taste in my
mouth. Give me a few trees, and I am happy. A historical letterbox is
WONDERFUL, but one just hidden in some random trees is good too. I
love trees or cemeteries or ...even fenceposts, I guess. To me, they
are all interesting to some degree. I love the surprise of what kind
of stamp is in the box. And I love seeing someone else's handiwork in
a handcarved stamp. Again, a great stamp is wonderful, but a very
humble stamp is good too.

Although I haven't made or hidden a letterbox in quite a while, I do
have a couple of stamps carved, and some boxes in the making. They
will be hidden nearby, even though there are no great points of
interest here. That is, provided I can find ANY decent place to hide
them.

Oddly enough, geocaching is really gaining popularity around here.
Now to me, I can't really see a whole lot of fun in that. But since
there are no letterboxes other than mine, here, I have taken my
grandsons out geocaching twice lately - 4 caches one day, and 2 the
second day. The oldest one loves using my GPS. But I'd much rather be
letterboxing.

Oh, and the other day, I was in line at a store, when the lady who
hid the geocaches we found, came up behind me. We are acquainted and
so I struck up a conversation with her. She has found at least one of
my letterboxes and said she takes friends to it every now and then to
show them what letterboxing is. We were amused at our differences in
favoring one over the other. She said that to her, letterboxing just
didn't cut it, because the clues tell you just right where to go, as
in, "Pick up this rock and look under it.". So for her, it is
apparently the puzzle of the hunt that is the thing. For me, besides
the outdoors, it is the artsy quality of letterboxing.

For anyone interested, you can see and read about our geocaching in
my blog, in the July entries. I think the June entries (archives)
have some letterboxing posts, too.
http://www.wanderinglily.blogspot.com

From: (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2008-07-14 19:14:03 UTC-04:00
I'll weigh in on this one, Lucy, because I've done both:  some "marathon" letterboxing a few times, and then some really special finds that took a long time to find, maybe even longer to solve the clues or more than one trip to get the box, and were all the more special for the extra personal investment of time and trouble.   From my point of view, anytime I've tried to look for too many boxes in a single trip (which would be just about anything over 6 or 7 maximum in a single day, even close together), I get a headache and begin to lose interest and the whole thing becomes more of an ordeal I just have to get through than an adventure or pleasant experience.  It's always that "one more box" that undoes me and makes me swear off letterboxing for awhile -- and has almost lost my LSS to letterboxing altogether unless I can practically guarantee a really good location that's worth the trip.  That's just me, of course - others might feel totally energized by more boxes to be found-go for it all!   That's OK for them, but not for me, I've found.   I've also become more discriminating (in a good sense, I hope) about the boxes I'll even look for: I want a good letterboxing experience, an interesting or scenic or previously-undiscovered (by me) location, an interesting or artistic or unique stamp or logbook or clues.  I'm not really after upping my count (it's past 700, but I didn't even notice when that happened until sometime later as I logged the last dozen or so finds into my own personal records).  On long trips, I'll take clues, but only for those that sound like a good hike or a place I want to explore, or maybe a special box by some boxer I've heard about and whose stamps I expect to be really worthwhile.  I'm very happy to bring home 2 or 3 stamps on a two-week trip to a totally different place, and I've got a story to go with every one (which, lucky for most of you, I only share with a few people so you all  don't have to skim past all the trivial details from me).    When I do get inspired to plant new boxes, more and more often it's not because there's a box I want to plant, but because there's a place I want to take finders to discover (well, OK, sometimes it's because there's a box I want to plant and I try to wait until I find that cool location that's just right!  I've got three of those sitting in my letterboxing Box O' Stuff right now waiting until I find that just-right place; one has been waiting 3 years.   Sometime I'll find that place and plant those boxes.  Until then, well, there are enough other boxes out there for finders to enjoy that mine can continue to wait.)
Nice thing about letterboxing:  no rules (but etiquette, yes).   Different people can take different approaches and enjoy the game their own way -- just as long as they consider and respect the environment, the specific locations, and the other planters/finders involved in the game, not just "litterboxing" or trashing other planters' boxes in the process. 
 
Oh, and on that note, I'd like to publicly recognize and thank some letterboxers:  Clueless Blues, I think they are, who were the last finders on a box I was checking on this week while doing a little maintenance and upkeep - they not only had carefully resealed every little baggy and the box itself very carefully, they'd replanted it so carefully and well that I almost couldn't find it myself - taking good care of my letterbox!  Thanks, guys!  I really appreciate your consideration for future finders!

From: WagTime2007 (WagTime2007@tx.rr.com) | Date: 2008-07-14 22:55:57 UTC-05:00
Lucy pondered, "Are you a numbers person or an experiential person?"
 
As the other half of the Texas Cousin Letterboxing Dynamic Duo, I think I am both.  My first exposure to letterboxing was by JBK, so her faster pace seemed normal to me.  We both tend to operate at or above the speed limit a lot of the time, but we are both in it for the overall experience. 
 
I would just as soon avoid longer walks or strenuous hikes (lazy, heat, out of shape, pick your excuse) but I will do it if it means a series of boxes, a particularly skilled carver, or the promise of a pot of gold at the end.  Some things/places/people that are just begging to be commemorated with a letterbox don't always have awe-inspiring locations to go along with them.  However, I can get just as much enjoyment from reading a historical marker and looking at a grave or an old building as I can from a nature hike.  One takes 10 minutes, the other could take hours.  I live in the DFW metroplex, so I have the ability to urban-box pretty much any time, and have gone out on my lunch break several times to pick up boxes that were near where I work.  When I go to East TX or JBK comes here, we try to make the most of our time by planning a whole day of it.  Neither experience tends to feel better or worse than the other...it's all boxing.   If I'm planning a day of boxing in a particular area, I like to see how many finds I can get, getting the biggest bang for my buck (or gallon of gas) as possible.  However, I always leave the option open of lingering in a special location even if it means I don't through my list. 
 
We always describe letterboxing to outsiders as a treasure hunt or a scavenger hunt.  To take that one step further, it's also a bit like a grab-bag...sometimes the contents are disappointing, but sometimes you end up with a heck of a prize.  To me, the not knowing is part of the fun.  I don't feel like my time has been wasted if it's a mediocre drive-by...it's still an experience that I wouldn't have had otherwise. 
 
I'm rambling now, so I'm going to stop before everyone dozes off at their computers!
My two cents...
WagTime
