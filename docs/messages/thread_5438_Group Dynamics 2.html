From: Ashby (ashby@ic.org) | Date: 2015-01-22 00:28:04 UTC
Dear Letterboxing Texas,

I have watched the continued conversation on this topic with only mild interest.

I am actually not especially inclined to join the conversation any further, since I have been content for quite some time to stay around the edges of the group and I'm fine with staying there. I also know that in the online world, any "response" can often come across as defensive (even when not intended) and deteriorate into flame pretty fast. I don't have any urge to contribute to anything like that, none at all.

I only commented in the first place so that the original person who indicated the group seemed unwelcoming would know they were not alone in having that experience in this group. I have had a similar experience, and so have other people who have posted over the course of the past few years, from time to time. This is not the first time this has come up.  It takes a brave heart to post something like that to an online group and I wanted the OP to know they were not the only person out there with that experience here.

However, since the question has been posed directly to me about "how are you helping solve the problem" it seems reasonable that I may as well respond, at least briefly, else it is likely I will risk being identified as being unwilling to be part of the solution, and I don't particularly want that to get that reputation here, either.

I have made a real effort many times in the past to contribute to "make the group feel welcoming" by posting a "hello & welcome" type of reply to new arrivals . . . . when I can . . . .  in the context of the rest of my life.  I try to respond at least briefly as often as I can, but this group is only one (small) part of the rest of my life, after all, and the quantity and quality of my postings here reflects that.

What I could perhaps do to carry more of my share of the "duty" (if there is one?) to help the group feel welcoming is to at least continue doing that much, perhaps adding a second follow-up post to new people? I'm not sure what else I could do in an online group, except perhaps join in the other conversations more often, maybe?

I did attend one TALE and enjoyed it fabulously. I certainly agree that people in-person have been (mostly) friendly and pleasant and fun to spend time with.  In the past, I have also hosted (or maybe co-hosted, I can't remember) one event locally and attended another meetup in New Orleans.....all of those in-person events have been lovely and great. I have offered other meetups from time to time, but had no response, so I mostly don't offer any more. I also continue to do letterboxing on my own, finding clues from various sources, and placing new boxes, and consider myself an "active" boxer, even if I am not "active" very much here in this online group.

But then . . . . . the conversation this time around was not actually about people "in-person" but was specifically about "this yahoo group" (as far as I could tell, at least).

To reply to the question of "what could the group do to be more welcoming?" the main idea that springs to my mind in response to that question is "don't be dismissive of people who indicate the group seems unwelcoming" such as responding with comments along the lines of . . . . .

* the other group is much worse
* the people here are great
* I have always felt welcomed
* If you feel unwelcome, perhaps you should find a different hobby

and similar comments like these that I have heard here recently

All of those may indeed also be true, but by responding with those, you are essentially saying, "the group is not either unwelcoming, because of X reasons it's not."  Discounting someone's experience by saying their experience is not true (when clearly it IS true, for them) is not a "welcoming" behavior.

Nor do those types of responses seem to me to be part of an effective conversation about an issue that several people over an extended period of time have identified as a problem with "this yahoo group" as its own thing, separate from "individual people" or "people in person" or "letterboxing as a hobby" or "events in person" or other manifestations that could be discussed.

And, none of those types of comments are similar to comments that might feel helpful or problem-solving, such as . . . . . . 

"oh my gosh, I'm so sorry to hear you didn't feel welcome, and we *do* want to be welcoming and if people don't feel welcomed, we see that as a problem, so how can we correct it . . . . especially in the future with other new people who might join?"

A second thought about "how could the group seem more welcoming" would be to respond when people post something . . . . . . I have lost track of how many times I have posted to the group about something related to letterboxing and had no response at all. I am not "keeping score" specifically, but I know it's a lot, and it is reasonable and likely that each time a person makes the effort to post, and gets no response at all. . . . . . they do notice . . . . . and it feels unfriendly . . . . . (especially in a relatively small group where it's the same few people posting most of the time) . . . . . and it probably makes the person that much less likely to post again in the future, so any potential involvement or connection starts to spiral down and away. Of course when I have posted something not related to letterboxing, I would not be as likely to expect a reply, but even then, a brief reply or even acknowledgement by at least one person would go a long way to helping someone feel welcome or included or part of the group.

However, for my part, I have come to think that "this yahoo group" as a whole does not really have the goal of "be welcoming" and perhaps that's fine, too. If the group tends to be more of a hangout place for friends who have been together for quite awhile, and less of a place for a larger group of people who are interested in "Letterboxing in Texas" to exchange info and connect, well, that's okay, I suppose. There are other places to do that, after all.

I've made my peace with the whole situation, obviously, or I would not have stayed as long as I have. And I think many other people have taken that same approach, either abandoning the group entirely (while still letterboxing by finding clues from various other sources) or abandoning letterboxing entirely, or simply staying loosely connected to the group, but not actively engaged with it, like I do (and the other lurkers, too, since I know I'm not the only one).

so, those are my thoughts at the moment,

ashby

From: Christine Borgfeld (christine@borgfeld.net) | Date: 2015-01-22 22:44:04 UTC-06:00

Hi Ashby, you raise some good and valid points and I think there are some underlying causes.  I don’t intend for this to be an excuse for the group being less than welcoming, but I believe it will be hard to change without recognizing them.  And my apologies to those who are tired of the discussion!  Feel free to skip this one. 

 

I think that some of the issue (especially online) is that letterboxing creates a surprising tension between experienced boxers (especially those who plant) and new people.  I have seen this play out repeatedly.  Oddly enough, new boxers cause a lot of frustration for planters.  They have trouble finding a box so report it missing, leave boxes exposed, don’t seal the boxes,  put bags on the outside of boxes, plant new boxes on top of existing boxes, post pictures of stamps online, show box locations online, and all sorts of other frustrating things.  On top of this they tend to join the online groups (both here and AQ) and passionately advocate some change to the hobby or rule that we all need to follow. (I did this one myself – insisting that boxes needed better maintenance and status updates on AQ because I was used to that in geocaching)  They often unwittingly offend in the process.  I think over time this leads to some undesirable results from the planters which usually play out online:

 

Lack of patience and less than nice response when you carve a new stamp and drive many miles to replace a box which is still there

People trying to nicely and repeatedly tell someone that this isn’t how things are done, they don’t get it, and finally someone loses patience and spells it out bluntly.  This often results in the new person disappearing from the forum.

New person inadvertently offends a well loved planter – then the rest of the forum goes to great lengths to defend/support the planter to keep them from getting upset enough to stop planting

Restricting boxes and blacklisting people (blacklisting was removed from AQ)

Hostility toward promoting the hobby or insistence that any articles/classes/press stress how to do it responsibly

Mixed feelings about new boxers in general…and a less than welcoming attitude from some

 

A few years ago I know of a planter who blacklisted a new boxer for messing up multiple boxes they visited.  Later I met the new boxer in person who complained bitterly about being treated so rudely when she first started boxing.  Both of them were upset and offended.  There are also boxers who have tried to tell new boxers that they are making mistakes, but this is difficult to do delicately without offending them.  On top of all of this, some of the boxers here do go out of their way to welcome new people, and they are distressed that new people don’t feel welcome. 

 

So hopefully this will lead to some constructive discussion rather than a negative rant.  I’ve thought about this a great deal and would love to hear some ideas on improving the situation.  I have a few of my own, but I’ve already spent WAY to long on this post!

From: (cmcalverley@yahoo.com) | Date: 2015-01-23 11:14:12 UTC-08:00

Attending events is a great way to meet other letterboxers and get a bunch of great pointers all in the same day.  At all of the events that I have gone to, newcomers are always taken under someone's wings and go boxing together.  This is a great way to learn how to read the clues better AND how to properly plant a box, too. 


Also, there are no stupid questions.  We have all been there and had to ask the same questions.  Most boxers would prefer that the questions are asked repeatedly than for new boxers guess and leave a box at risk.

I have only been boxing for about 3 years and still don't feel very comfortable with posting on the boards.  However,  I lurk (a lot) and have gleaned a lot of useful information from them.  I can see both sides (newcomers vs. veterans) of this conversation.  I really think it has more to do with a newcomers lack of confidence than it does with the veterans not being welcoming.  Newcomers cannot reasonably expect all users to wholeheartedly take everyone in AND actively maintain those connections. (I'm saying "all" here.  Individual connections are quite common).  There are just way too many of us and a lot of people don't stay around very long.  Connections will be made at the events or even by trying to find a local person to box with. One reason that newcomers may feel ignored on the boards is that they don't have a lot of original input to the conversations that go on.  Their comments are likely to have been made numerous time before. The AQ "New Members Board" is a great one to get your feet wet on.  The veterans that read this board realize that posters are newbies and will likely have a lot more patience with repeated questions. Eventually, time and experience will allow newcomers to feel more comfortable with posting on the regular boards.

Anyway, that's my two cents.  

Basketcase!

Well I was careful to pick examples that I’ve seen multiple times from different boxers so no one was singled out.  I can provide references if needed J  So I hope no one takes it personally.  And I know you know I CAN keep secrets. 

 

I think it’s easy for everyone to get offended because both new and experienced people are passionate about the hobby.  Anyway, here is an idea to consider.  What if we create a guideline with suggestions to avoid some of the common issues?  This wouldn’t be a repeat of the guide on AQ, but focus it specifically on issues that have caused problems in the past.  For example, one point I’d like to see is this:

 

-          Letterboxers planting the box assume the risk of losing it, and the boxing community respects their judgment on where and how to plant.  If a boxer plants a box in a state park without a permit, in a public location, in boxes without labels, in locations they can’t maintain, in locations which are unlikely to survive, or any other manner you question, this is their choice and they have taken the risk of loss, removal, or having their listing removed from AQ or LBNA.  If you are uncomfortable with any box, don’t get it.  As Mosaic Butterfly says – not every box is for everyone.  

 

I think this is a strong unwritten value in the community that people stumble onto unwittingly and painfully.  It also spells out some very different values from geocaching which is very particular about location approval and maintenance.

 

I’d suggest we post this list to the group every few months so new members don’t have to dig it up or follow a link. 

 

Thoughts?

On your point about secret boxes…secrets and mysteries are part of the fun of the hobby and date back to it’s origins.  I’m sorry you felt isolated by this.  There will always be mystery boxes and secrets so you have to enjoy those you find out about and accept there will be others you miss.  I ran into this at events in the northeast last year where I know I missed out on a ton.  Boxing with different people at the events helps, don’t stay with the same group every time.  Searching the central event location thoroughly is also a good idea.  Perhaps this will as well…
