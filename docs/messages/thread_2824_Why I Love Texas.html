Why I Love Texas

From: carrascoruth2000 (a.carrasco@earthlink.net) | Date: 2007-06-12 02:32:53 UTC
Hey all...I have to get in on this, even if from afar. First, let
me remind you all that you do not have to be born a Texan to love
Texas. Since I am finding myself missing Texas at the moment, I
thought I'd add a "foreigner's" perspective to the great things
about Texas.
-Drivers that wave when you let them into traffic instead of
flipping you the bird.
-COLD air conditioning (it is NEVER cold enough in Michigan)
-SHINER BOCK (can't get it this far north)
-Strangers who actually smile back instead of looking at you as if
you may be crazy
-Neighborhood pools
-The way Texans pronounce the word "can't". I don't know what there
is about that particular word, but I love it.

I think one of the greatest things about Texas for a "non native" is
how surprising it is. Texas in reality is nothing like the pre
conceptions non Texans have of it. I will never forget the first
time I took one of my brothers to Enchanted Rock and he said, "No
way! THIS is Texas?" The very best thing about Texas for me is
that reading all of this has made me HOMESICK, even though I am
currently staying with my parents in the home where I spent the
first 22 years of my life...I would never want to live anywhere but
Texas now. I know how good I have it. Can't wait to get back...MO5

PS add Lyle Lovett, Willie, and Robert Earl Keen to my list of
favorites. Oh yeah, and cheap mangoes in the summer.

From: Mike - 2007 (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-06-12 06:00:37 UTC
Not knowing your abilities, I cannot say any specifics.  But I will tell you a secret.
 
Repetition is the key.
 
Example.  Lift a calf every day, pretty soon you can lift a cow.
 
What this means is that you will get as good as your dedication will allow you to.  If you really want to do this, you can.  But it will take some effort.  You will have to lift that calf every day.  Actually, every other day.
 
Clarify something.  20 miles.  3 days.  Is that 6.666 miles a day over three days?  Or is that 20 miles a day for three days - 60 miles total?  Walking?  Or running?  Or Bicycle?  It isn't clear to me.
 
Do not train for the event.  Start training slowly - but push yourself a little bit.  Some days you will feel like you can do more - so do more.  Some days you will not have it in you - but try to get at least 15-20 minutes of good heart-rate for that day anyway.  Get on a schedule of training.  Like Mon-Wed-Fri or Tue-Thu-Sat.  Do your best to keep to it.  And try to do a little more each day.  It will get easier.  Also - there is only one way to get trained for climate.  That is to train IN that climate.  This is why our soldiers stage in Kuwait before going into Iraq.  They don't just sit around waiting for an assignment.  They train every day - and a little more every day - till they are ready fir the daily duty.
 
Try to do some training in the gear you will be using for the event.  The LAST thing you want to do it put on new shoes just before you walk 20 miles.  My rule of thumb - 50 miles in short walks with a new pair of boots before you do a road-march (military hike) with them.  Lighter shoes might not need as much break-in, but the day of the event isn't the time to learn that there is a glue-spot on the tongue that gives you a sore spot every time you flex your ankle.
 
If you need to carry your own gear (water - lip balm - sun screen - etc) consider a Camel-Bak.  I like the military models - they are rugged.  I hike a lot of rugged places with heavy gear.  Just carried a 55 lb power supply up to a radio site a few days ago.  But they are also heavy.  The civilian models are light-weight.  They won't hold up to my abuse, but this one works for my wife just fine - the Camel-Bak M.U.L.E.  It carries nearly 1 gallon of water, and had enough pockets to carry bug spray etc.....  It was made for biking, but also works out just right for light-weight hiking.
 
Pack your pack a little heavier when you train.  Like carry a big flashlight or something - just a pound or two at first.  Later build up to a 4-pound can of coffee or something.  The days of the event your pack will feel much lighter and you will go farther faster.
 
Gosh - I am writing a book here.
 
Give me more specifics and I will try to dial it in for you.

From: Mike - 2007 (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-06-12 11:39:19 UTC
Hope you get this before you go. 
 
I never liked the fanny-back.  It just doesn't ride right.  But try both and see what you think.  The more weight down low behind your back, the heavier it feels.  The best riding packs are high-up.  They fill pretty easy.
 
This is the one.
http://www.pricepoint.com/detail/14554-130_CAMML6-0-Search--/Camelbak-MULE-2006.htm
 
When new - there IS a slight plastic taste.  It goes away quickly.  One thing I always do is to put ice in it before I fill it.  On our last hike in the Gila I had ice water until the end.  And cold water to me doesn't have as much taste to it.  Of course my camel-baks are years old with miles on them - they lost the plastic taste long ago.
 
If you have an REI near you - they are a good place to start.
 
There is an outfit in Tucson called Summit Hut.  I love the way they fit boots.  They measured my feet standing on both, standing on left, standing on right, and sitting unloaded.  They had an exact representation of my feet before we even started looking at boots. They also had a rock sidewalk and a rocky hill (small) in the store for testing boots.  I had to walk around the store for a while before they even let me decide.
 
Well - gotta go!  More later.

Try to do some training in the gear you will be using for the event.  The LAST thing you want to do it put on new shoes just before you walk 20 miles.  My rule of thumb - 50 miles in short walks with a new pair of boots before you do a road-march (military hike) with them.  Lighter shoes might not need as much break-in, but the day of the event isn't the time to learn that there is a glue-spot on the tongue that gives you a sore spot every time you flex your ankle. 
If you need to carry your own gear (water - lip balm - sun screen - etc) consider a Camel-Bak.  I like the military models - they are rugged.  I hike a lot of rugged places with heavy gear.  Just carried a 55 lb power supply up to a radio site a few days ago.  But they are also heavy.  The civilian models are light-weight.  They won't hold up to my abuse, but this one works for my wife just fine - the Camel-Bak M.U.L.E.  It carries nearly 1 gallon of water, and had enough pockets to carry bug spray etc.....  It was made for biking, but also works out just right for light-weight hiking.
