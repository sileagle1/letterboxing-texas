The problem is that it is their property, and their right to dictate the rules by which their property is used.  When people disregard their rules and play by their own rules, you get what you get.  And it works for a while.  BUT - Speeders eventually get caught.

 

What will happen next - now that someone there has already learned how to get on the web site, and read the clues?  The next step when they can’t get the control they want is an outright ban on all games hide and seek games on their property, and a removal and confiscation of all the boxes they can find.  You need to understand that your game is just abandoned trash to some people.  Right or wrong – it is what it is.  You can decide to be a part of the solution, or a part of the problem.

 

So in that case, the work of a few ruins the game for the rest.

 

If you are Ok with that – then yes…  well said.  For me – I have plenty of hobbies, and if letterboxing were banned totally today, I wouldn’t cry over it.  It would just be another memory in a life rich in memories.

 

As I said earlier, there are two other choices.  Work within the rules, or not box there at all.  There are plenty of other places to put boxes – so I would just archive those boxes, posting that due to the unfriendly nature of the city, the boxes are no longer available and boxing should be avoided within the city limits.  Done.

 

LUCY – REMEMBER the box I placed NEAR Guadalupe peak?  There was NO WAY that it was on park property.  But the rangers raised such a ruckus I pulled it just to shut them up.  It just wasn’t worth ruining it for others.

 

I have seen this before, people still do not realize how close an outright BAN on geocaching and letterboxing was for Texas State Parks.  It took a lot of talking in Austin to keep it, under their rules.  Like it or not – it is what it is.  I know in my heart I will not be a part of the problem.  So – are you a speeder?

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2010-12-04 22:42:15 UTC-07:00

Harsh – but true.  Unfortunately - change is a fact.  Letterboxing is only about 4 years behind geocaching. 

 

A single incident of a very stubborn Geocacher almost had all hide and seek games totally banned from Texas State Parks.  After several contacts and a few meetings, Texas Parks and Wildlife (TPWD) decided to allow “adventure games” to continue, but with some rules and guidelines to better control where caches were placed.  Unfortunately, letterboxing got lumped into that group.

 

Some people think this isn’t right.  That may well be.  But it is what it is, and those who try to run under the radar will eventually bring down those who do play by the rules.  When the powers that be are tired of dealing with the problem of unauthorized boxes in the parks, the answer will be a total ban so that they never have to deal with it again.

 

Geocaching came to grips with the problem a while ago.  I will not post what their solution was – the letterboxing gurus need to think for themselves about it.

 

Because of the lack of popularity, letterboxing got away with it for a while.  As letterboxing grows more and more – (be careful what you wish for) – the problem will become greater and greater.  It has already been mentioned right here on the list that a park used for the annual meeting has a large number of boxes left behind along with the question of just who will maintain them.  Remember – no matter how passionate you are about letterboxing, to the un-initiated it is still abandoned trash.  If someone tossed a box of trash in your yard – how would you feel?  Even if you were promised it was “good trash”?  You still need to “clean it up”.  In addition, this trash was drawing people into a sensitive area where multiple hikers are trampling a rare plant.

 

I worked hard to get the approval of my local rangers.  Because I am others volunteer in the parks, and give back more than we take, we have a great relationship working.  And I try not to plant too far from home, so that I can keep after my “trash”.

 

The best thing to have happen would be for some people to embrace the change, and find a way to make it work for them.  Try to get your face known.  Try to influence the rules the way you would like them to be rather than getting pissed when they throw rules at what they see as a problem.  But that would take work.

 

I don’t have all the answers.  But I do know that if a city wants to put rules on the use of their parks or other property, that is legal.  I have faced the same kind of mindset with owning guns, with ham radio antennas, with geocaching, etc etc…..  If you do not follow the rules, you can be banned from using the parks, or even fined.  What is the fine for littering?  I do not box a lot anymore, so I really don’t care of this hobby becomes such a problem that it isn’t allowed anywhere anymore.

 

QUESTION:  IS THAT HOW YOU WANT PEOPLE TO THINK ABOUT LETTERBOXING?  Or maybe it should be called litterboxing.

 

If you really put on the other guy’s shoes and give it some thought, you can certainly see their side of it.  They already see it as a problem.  So you ignore them and continue to do it your way – and really piss them off.  What would you do?

 

I’m just saying – ya know…..  <grin>.  Times change.  Sad….. but you need to do whatever you think is right, and live with whatever happens.

 

Dunno – you decide.

I am working with Mr. Holmes and he has appointed a ranger from southeast Texas to be in charge of establishing a policy for letterboxing that will be equal to and similar to the geocaching policy. He has notified all park managers about the existence of letterboxing and has agreed to either give us our own separate permit application or include us equally on the existing geocache one. I have been assisted in this matter with a couple of our prominent long time letterboxers, who have given me good ideas and suggestions to present to TPWD. They think that they are a viable activity for the parks and have asked if we would object to the promotion of the activiy in state parks. This is a work in progress, but all Texas State Parks should now be aware of letterboxing. Building their trust in us will help us in our future dealings with them. The fact that nobody in Texas Parks and Wildlife are, or were, familiar with letterboxing, tells me that they haven't had any problems with us. I think it demontrates that we've been doing a good job with our hiding and with our events. They are looking for ways to improve attendence in parks and promote more appeal to younger people and letterboxing is one of the ways they can do that. If you experience a problem with letterboxing in Texas State Parks, lets talk it over and figure out how we can work with them. I don't think they'll ever let us completely police ourselves, because after all, they have bigger fish to fry, like hunting.

letterboxing and geocaching.

geocaches. I do not believe there will ever be a geocache in that park,
geocachers are not as environmentally friendly and self-policing as they might
be. The letterbox was allowed for two reasons. The first is because there was
already one there before they learned of it, so historically it was allowed to
stay. The second reason was because letterboxers are more environmentally
friendly people. The box was not allowed to be returned to its original
position, and it took several days of searching to find a suitable place that
the head ranger would permit it to stay. That is a very sensitive park
environmentally.

aware. When you boil it down, what is the difference between a geocache and a
letterbox? For the rangers, most of whom do have bigger fish to fry, what is
the difference? Someone hides something and someone else seeks something. You
hide it, they find it. Trading stamps, or trading trinkets? Using a compass,
or a GPS? Minor differences really in their minds. While they are aware of the
differences, there was no change to the state forms made locally.
