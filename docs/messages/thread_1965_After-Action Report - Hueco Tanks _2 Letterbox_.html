After Action Report - Hueco Tanks Letterbox #2.

This message is the second part of the continuing story of our search and
recovery efforts for the Letterboxes known as Hueco Tanks #1 and Hueco Tanks
#2.

To recap the previous operation, my wife (Dane Lady) and I applied for and
were granted permission, and enlisted the help of a park guide to search for
both Hueco Tanks #1 and Hueco Tanks #2 Letterboxes. We successfully
recovered Hueco Tanks #1 at N 31 55.195 W 106 02.910. The letterbox was
removed to be given to the park superintendent.

With the clues for Hueco Tanks #2 in hand, the guide and I did a whirlwind
recon of most of Hueco Tanks North Mountain. Dane Lady did her best not to
slow us down too much. We identified and did a fast scan of several
promising areas. We did not recover Hueco Tanks #2 that day, but did
identify and GPS-waypoint several areas to concentrate on. This operation
all but did my wife in. She is a real trooper, but not up to that kind of
strenuous recon activity. I on the other hand loved it!

After discussion through E-mail, the park superintendent reviewed the clues
and I was granted permission to continue to seek recovery of Hueco Tanks #2
on my own, within the park rules of course. At that time I was also offered
the opportunity to repair Hueco Tanks #1 and place it somewhere (To Be
Determined later) in the park. This is great news. It may be difficult to
find a good hiding place that will be approved. But one thing is certain.
It will not happen without trying. My ranger-friend Danny Lee says it
should work out. I am hopeful and will continue to press forward on this as
time permits.

The original Hueco Tanks #1 stamp is not in good shape. The original
logbook is ruined. The plastic container is crumbling. I intend to make a
new stamp in the image of what I believe the original stamp was intended to
be. I also intend to re-mount the original stamp and leave it in the
Letterbox. And I have a good supply of ammo cans. One small one will be
painted to match the approved location. Ammo cans last forever.

Everyone knows I am also a Geocacher. All my letterbox hides to date are
Letterbox Hybrid Geocaches. I have decided to keep Hueco Tanks #1 a
Letterbox exclusively. I am not comfortable having hundreds of Geocachers
searching for the box. A few respectful Letterboxers will be enough. The
park must be protected first and fore-most. I may request to place a
Geocache out there in the future. But that is beyond the scope of this
report.

The search for Hueco Tanks #2.

*************************
Hueco Tanks #2
This box is Unclaimed.
Placement date: Jul 11 2000
State: Texas
County: El Paso
Nearest city: El Paso
Number of boxes: 1

Clues HUECO TANKS LETTERBOX #2

If one climbs the south side of North Mountain; follows one of the easy
routes at the end of the paved road (all 5.4 to 5.6 stuff) they lead to a
large amphitheatre that has a huge oak in it. The amphitheatre is accessible
from all directions. In a hueco (natural hole-as is the area) 14 paces from
the old oak is a box covered by a rock.

Note: access to this area requires some rock climbing.


Brooks / Devere, 12-28-2000
*************************

On Saturday, 24 June 2006, a second attempt was mounted to locate the
Letterbox known as Hueco Tanks #2. This attempt was made with the help of
my son James (Army Scout) who was home on leave from the Army. We hit the
gate as early as was possible. After his orientation briefing, we were on
the trail. My ranger-friend Danny Lee asked us to keep a watch for sign of
some Bighorn Sheep that had moved into the park. We promised to do so.

To get to the locations I GPS-waypointed on the last mission required us to
hike over North Mountain. Although the primary point of interest was lower
on the south end of the mountain, the ground approach on the south side is
closed, open only to guided hiking. We were unguided on this mission, so
our approach was limited. The western approach is nearly a shear cliff.
Not impossible, but certainly more dangerous. Crossing the mountain was the
safest allowed approach. And so we did. I thoroughly enjoyed that hike.
We were out early and only had to share the mountain with a pair of foxes,
some prairie dogs, and an uncountable number of lizards. The sky was
lightly overcast and the temperatures were cool. Cool for summer in El Paso
anyway. The mission was ahead of us. We were optimistic. We had proper
gear and equipment. We were on the hunt. Two warriors headed out to do
what they do best. Life doesn't get better than this! I was loving it.
W.E.T.S.U.

While we were hiking across the mountain we discovered several places with
sheep droppings. I GPS-waypointed and photographed the first few. The
first location was at N 31 55.340 W 106 02.783. But soon it was obvious
that the droppings were not difficult to find. So I stopped marking and
photographing.

James and I arrived at the first and most promising point for Hueco Tanks #2
within an hour of hiking. It is a 30 foot deep steep-walled hole with a
large oak tree on the south west end of the mountain. It is located at N
31 55.242 W 106 02.860. We set out a safety rope, and James went down. I
lowered our gear to James, then followed. James - being a Cavalry scout,
always seems to take point. As I was climbing down he remarked that this
secluded hole was full of sheep droppings. I got to the bottom and we began
our search.

For about 1.5 hours we checked every possible hole, crack and crevice. We
found two very promising places that fit the clues perfectly and had rocks
covering the holes in the east wall north of the tree. One hole was empty.
The second had broken glass in it. Neither had anything resembling a
letterbox inside. In the process of searching for the letterbox we filled
two shopping bags with trash. After exhausting every possibility, I climbed
out, pulled our gear up, and threw the rope down for James to get out. I
enjoyed every minute in that spot. But I was disappointed we came out
without the letterbox.

The search of the other areas went about like the first. None was as
promising as the first. Some places required us to climb in and out
carefully. In order to search many of the likely places we needed to
carefully look behind the plant life. Thorns and scratches were the rule of
the day. At one point we had to remove our gear and crawl through a series
of cracks and crevices with the gear tied to our feet dragging behind to get
to the spot. Each time we went in hopeful, but came out empty. After
nearly 6 hours on this mission we had exhausted every possibility. I was
not ready to give up. But there was nothing left to do. We crawled out and
shouldered up our gear to start back. About 200 yards into the return trip
we realized we had forgotten about 3 bags full of trash. So we returned to
collect up the bags.

On the way back, I took James to see some of the rock paintings. If you
visit Hueco Tanks State Park - be sure to see these paintings. They are
located on North Mountain at N 31 55.422 W 106 02.796. The coordinates
will put you at the base of a rock face to your south. Crawl in the crevice
at your feet and move west to the large room. As your eyes adjust to the
dim light, you will see some of the finest Indian paintings in the park.
Don't forget your camera.

The return trip was uneventful, almost somber. We had failed. Failure is
not a word we accept easily. On the other hand, I had a GREAT outing with
my son. He has grown into a man. The kind of man I was used to working
with when I was a hard-charging Sergeant so long ago. The kind of man you
want to have by your side when everything else around you is going wrong.
Cavalry Scout. Sniper. Paramedic. He can use his hands as weapons, and
his weapons as though they were his hands. His hands can heal or kill with
equal skill. Who wouldn't be proud to stand on line with a warrior like
this?

By the time you read this - and sadly for me - he will be gone. But some of
you will have the opportunity to meet him at Fort Hood. He will be doing
some letterboxing, scouting out the good ones for me when we visit him in
late September. I hope you-all will welcome him to your part of the state.

This concludes the report on Hueco Tanks #2 Letterbox. I will continue to
work with the park staff to rebuild and replace Hueco Tanks #1 in a location
worthy of letterboxing, but fully acceptable to the park staff. This will
not happen quickly. Perhaps by Christmas.

I would like to once again point out that not a word of this report would
have been possible without the cooperation of the staff at Hueco Tanks state
Park. Thank you Rangers! And it is a shining example of why I believe
having an open dialog with the park staff about your letterbox is better
than placing a letterbox secretly and hoping the rangers do not find it.

I also thank Dane Lady and Army scout for participating is parts of this
adventure. Having them along made it all the more enjoyable.

Last - I will send a final report when Hueco Tanks #1 is repaired and
replaced. This has been a great adventure for me. I would also like to
thank Silver Eagle for asking me to take on this mission. I wish the 50-%
successful report I bring to the list was a 100% successful report. I can
only say we did our best.

You can see a slideshow of our adventure. Go to this site:

http://www.photoshow.net/KD9KC/adventure

Select your connection speed and turn on your sound. Sit back and enjoy.
The photos do not do the park justice. Still - what a great adventure!

COMMENTS WELCOME!

Mike - KD9KC.
Desert Warrior.
El Paso, Texas.

After Action Report - Hueco Tanks Letterbox #2.

This message is the second part of the continuing story of our search and
recovery efforts for the Letterboxes known as Hueco Tanks #1 and Hueco Tanks
#2.

To recap the previous operation, my wife (Dane Lady) and I applied for and
were granted permission, and enlisted the help of a park guide to search for
both Hueco Tanks #1 and Hueco Tanks #2 Letterboxes. We successfully
recovered Hueco Tanks #1 at N 31 55.195 W 106 02.910. The letterbox was
removed to be given to the park superintendent.

With the clues for Hueco Tanks #2 in hand, the guide and I did a whirlwind
recon of most of Hueco Tanks North Mountain. Dane Lady did her best not to
slow us down too much. We identified and did a fast scan of several
promising areas. We did not recover Hueco Tanks #2 that day, but did
identify and GPS-waypoint several areas to concentrate on. This operation
all but did my wife in. She is a real trooper, but not up to that kind of
strenuous recon activity. I on the other hand loved it!

After discussion through E-mail, the park superintendent reviewed the clues
and I was granted permission to continue to seek recovery of Hueco Tanks #2
on my own, within the park rules of course. At that time I was also offered
the opportunity to repair Hueco Tanks #1 and place it somewhere (To Be
Determined later) in the park. This is great news. It may be difficult to
find a good hiding place that will be approved. But one thing is certain.
It will not happen without trying. My ranger-friend Danny Lee says it
should work out. I am hopeful and will continue to press forward on this as
time permits.

The original Hueco Tanks #1 stamp is not in good shape. The original
logbook is ruined. The plastic container is crumbling. I intend to make a
new stamp in the image of what I believe the original stamp was intended to
be. I also intend to re-mount the original stamp and leave it in the
Letterbox. And I have a good supply of ammo cans. One small one will be
painted to match the approved location. Ammo cans last forever.

Everyone knows I am also a Geocacher. All my letterbox hides to date are
Letterbox Hybrid Geocaches. I have decided to keep Hueco Tanks #1 a
Letterbox exclusively. I am not comfortable having hundreds of Geocachers
searching for the box. A few respectful Letterboxers will be enough. The
park must be protected first and fore-most. I may request to place a
Geocache out there in the future. But that is beyond the scope of this
report.

The search for Hueco Tanks #2.

Clues HUECO TANKS LETTERBOX #2

If one climbs the south side of North Mountain; follows one of the easy
routes at the end of the paved road (all 5.4 to 5.6 stuff) they lead to a
large amphitheatre that has a huge oak in it. The amphitheatre is accessible
from all directions. In a hueco (natural hole-as is the area) 14 paces from
the old oak is a box covered by a rock.

Note: access to this area requires some rock climbing.

— Brooks / Devere, 12-28-2000

On Saturday, 24 June 2006, a second attempt was mounted to locate the
Letterbox known as Hueco Tanks #2. This attempt was made with the help of
my son James (Army Scout) who was home on leave from the Army. We hit the
gate as early as was possible. After his orientation briefing, we were on
the trail. My ranger-friend Danny Lee asked us to keep a watch for sign of
some Bighorn Sheep that had moved into the park. We promised to do so.

To get to the locations I GPS-waypointed on the last mission required us to
hike over North Mountain. Although the primary point of interest was lower
on the south end of the mountain, the ground approach on the south side is
closed, open only to guided hiking. We were unguided on this mission, so
our approach was limited. The western approach is nearly a shear cliff.
Not impossible, but certainly more dangerous. Crossing the mountain was the
safest allowed approach. And so we did. I thoroughly enjoyed that hike.
We were out early and only had to share the mountain with a pair of foxes,
some prairie dogs, and an uncountable number of lizards. The sky was
lightly overcast and the temperatures were cool. Cool for summer in El Paso
anyway. The mission was ahead of us. We were optimistic. We had proper
gear and equipment. We were on the hunt. Two warriors headed out to do
what they do best. Life doesn't get better than this! I was loving it.
W.E.T.S.U. <grin>

While we were hiking across the mountain we discovered several places with
sheep droppings. I GPS-waypointed and photographed the first few. The
first location was at N 31 55.340 W 106 02.783. But soon it was obvious
that the droppings were not difficult to find. So I stopped marking and
photographing.

James and I arrived at the first and most promising point for Hueco Tanks #2
within an hour of hiking. It is a 30 foot deep steep-walled hole with a
large oak tree on the south west end of the mountain. It is located at N
31 55.242 W 106 02.860. We set out a safety rope, and James went down. I
lowered our gear to James, then followed. James - being a Cavalry scout,
always seems to take point. As I was climbing down he remarked that this
secluded hole was full of sheep droppings. I got to the bottom and we began
our search.

For about 1.5 hours we checked every possible hole, crack and crevice. We
found two very promising places that fit the clues perfectly and had rocks
covering the holes in the east wall north of the tree. One hole was empty.
The second had broken glass in it. Neither had anything resembling a
letterbox inside. In the process of searching for the letterbox we filled
two shopping bags with trash. After exhausting every possibility, I climbed
out, pulled our gear up, and threw the rope down for James to get out. I
enjoyed every minute in that spot. But I was disappointed we came out
without the letterbox.

The search of the other areas went about like the first. None was as
promising as the first. Some places required us to climb in and out
carefully. In order to search many of the likely places we needed to
carefully look behind the plant life. Thorns and scratches were the rule of
the day. At one point we had to remove our gear and crawl through a series
of cracks and crevices with the gear tied to our feet dragging behind to get
to the spot. Each time we went in hopeful, but came out empty. After
nearly 6 hours on this mission we had exhausted every possibility. I was
not ready to give up. But there was nothing left to do. We crawled out and
shouldered up our gear to start back. About 200 yards into the return trip
we realized we had forgotten about 3 bags full of trash. So we returned to
collect up the bags.

On the way back, I took James to see some of the rock paintings. If you
visit Hueco Tanks State Park - be sure to see these paintings. They are
located on North Mountain at N 31 55.422 W 106 02.796. The coordinates
will put you at the base of a rock face to your south. Crawl in the crevice
at your feet and move west to the large room. As your eyes adjust to the
dim light, you will see some of the finest Indian paintings in the park.
Don't forget your camera.

The return trip was uneventful, almost somber. We had failed. Failure is
not a word we accept easily. On the other hand, I had a GREAT outing with
my son. He has grown into a man. The kind of man I was used to working
with when I was a hard-charging Sergeant so long ago. The kind of man you
want to have by your side when everything else around you is going wrong.
Cavalry Scout. Sniper. Paramedic. He can use his hands as weapons, and
his weapons as though they were his hands. His hands can heal or kill with
equal skill. Who wouldn't be proud to stand on line with a warrior like
this?

By the time you read this - and sadly for me - he will be gone. But some of
you will have the opportunity to meet him at Fort Hood. He will be doing
some letterboxing, scouting out the good ones for me when we visit him in
late September. I hope you-all will welcome him to your part of the state.

This concludes the report on Hueco Tanks #2 Letterbox. I will continue to
work with the park staff to rebuild and replace Hueco Tanks #1 in a location
worthy of letterboxing, but fully acceptable to the park staff. This will
not happen quickly. Perhaps by Christmas.

I would like to once again point out that not a word of this report would
have been possible without the cooperation of the staff at Hueco Tanks state
Park. Thank you Rangers! And it is a shining example of why I believe
having an open dialog with the park staff about your letterbox is better
than placing a letterbox secretly and hoping the rangers do not find it.

I also thank Dane Lady and Army scout for participating is parts of this
adventure. Having them along made it all the more enjoyable.

Last - I will send a final report when Hueco Tanks #1 is repaired and
replaced. This has been a great adventure for me. I would also like to
thank Silver Eagle for asking me to take on this mission. I wish the 50-%
successful report I bring to the list was a 100% successful report. I can
only say we did our best.

You can see a slideshow of our adventure. Go to this site:

http://www.photoshow.net/KD9KC/adventure

Select your connection speed and turn on your sound. Sit back and enjoy.
The photos do not do the park justice. Still - what a great adventure!

COMMENTS WELCOME!

Mike - KD9KC.
Desert Warrior.
El Paso, Texas.
