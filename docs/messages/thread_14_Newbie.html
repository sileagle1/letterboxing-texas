Hi Connie,

Welcome to letterboxing!

PFX count is how we keep track of the number of letterboxes we've
found.
P=Placed (the number of boxes we've placed)
F=Found (the number of boxes we've found)
X=eXchanged (the number of personal signature stamps we've received
from other letterboxers) Most of those are from going to
a "gathering", but could also be from meeting another lb'er on the
trail.
E=Event (our family attended the RiverBend gathering in VA last year
and someone had made a wonderful stamp just for that occasion,
usually the stamps are retired after that)

I think there is a better explaination on the letterbox website. This
is how our family keeps track, but everyone does it differently.
There is no right or wrong way, and some don't even keep track. My
type A personality requires me to keep track of such things...:-)

I hope this helps.

That's got to be a good omen for all your future letterboxing adventures, Connie, to find the Texas Tour Bus as your first find!  This is, of course, not a typical letterbox.  There are no clues posted for it on the websites; in fact, it doesn't even officially exist as a letterbox on any of the sites.  Instead of just stamping this one and leaving it for the next finder, as you know, you take it, add a passenger, and hide it somewhere and notify this group and then the race is on for the next finder.  Since its creation, it has pretty well travelled north and south and east all across Texas, but Weatherford is probably about as far west as its been - distances between letterboxers are far out that way!  Hope you'll enjoy letterboxing in the Metroplex, because in the past year there has been a real increase in good letterboxers and letterboxes right around here - plenty of adventures to be had!  -- Viewfinder

Hey, there's plenty of us letterboxers who are retired (or sometimes just tired). That's part of the beauty of this sport. When we get to a gathering, it's like a family reunion. We range in ages from about one to ??? I hope to still be out there on the trails when I'm well into my 80's! I'm not sure I'm going to be letterboxing into my nineties though, because I'll probably be working on my autobiography by then, or perhaps learning to surf the big waves off the African coast. Who knows?
 
That is a great hint Lucy shared with us about rubbing her hands in the dirt. I've had a problem with one of my boxes being dragged out from its hiding place several times by a very persistent armadillo (who was spotted in action by another boxer). Nothing like having an eye-witness to the crime. AND, one of my boxes at Wear Cemetery had chewed marks on the corner when I last checked on it. My guess is it was of the four-legged variety, because it is a Lock n Lock box and creatures with two hands usually don't have to gnaw it open.
 
My suggestion to any letterboxer (new or old) is to click the Visit Your Group in the box below, click on messages, and start at the beginning and skim through the messages. It will give you a great sense of the progression of letterboxing in Texas and introduce you to some of the "characters" who helped make the sport what it is today in the Lone Star State. You will probably be surprised to see that most of the questions that are being asked today, have been discussed over and over again throughout the five year history of the Texas list. If you look closely, you might even be entertained by a few spats here and there!! Who knows?
 
Don't ever get the idea that newbies aren't welcomed on the Texas list. Like Viewfinder said, we were all newbies once. I think what some older members find difficult to deal with is, due in part to a series of published articles such as the one in Family First Magazine, there have been a lot of new people introduced to letterboxing all at one time, and some of those people haven't really taken the time to learn about how to letterbox without putting existing boxes at risk. A problem arises when someone who has planted many boxes cautions someone about a better way to approach a letterboxing situation, and that person takes offense and feels they are being criticized unjustly. Speaking strictly for myself, I have fifty-something boxes planted. The reason I've planted them is for people to enjoy finding. I don't necessarily want to exclude anyone from looking for my boxes. Some are easy, and some are more difficult... something for everyone! However, I have found quite of few of my boxes recently left out in the open rather than being recovered. Whenever possible, I place a rock on top of my boxes to keep them from washing out or being investigated by animals. I can't tell you how many times I've gone to check on a box and found the rock laying beside the box and the box sitting out in plain sight. So now, do I run the risk or insulting someone by trying to explain to the last finder the importance of rehiding the box well so it will be there for other finders, or do I just assume that the boxes I've put so much into will just disappear and there's nothing I can do about it? When we say there are no "rules" in letterboxing, that's not quite accurate. There is certainly a Code of Etiquette and guidelines (posted on AQ and LBNA). It is a matter of respect for what someone has created for your benefit. I know most of the "older" letterboxers on this list, and it's not in the nature of any of them to want to go on this list and complain. We just want all letterboxers to treat our boxes carefully and rehide them so that they will be there for all to find and enjoy.
 
And now.... I am stepping off my soapbox and going outside to finish planting all those daffodil bulbs that I ordered earlier this year when it seemed like such a good idea!
Puddle Splasher
