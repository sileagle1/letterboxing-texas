From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2007-07-22 19:06:56 UTC-05:00
Kristin,
 
The old carve/don't carve/store-bought question is one of the very first questions that pops up for virtually every letterboxer and has seen many discussions on just about any letterboxing list you may join.
 
There are those of us who would say: "Try it!"  and those who would say "You wouldn't catch me carving if my life depended on it". 
 
The two notable "wouldn't carve in a million years" folks that I know of on this list are Mike and Gryzzled Gryphon.  However, rather than going and buying a store-bought, they design their own and have them custom-made.  I haven't had the privilege of seeing one of Mike's in person (although the scanned image of his personal stamp is impressive), I've seen Gryzzled Gryphon's custom stamps and they are very nice.
 
Probably the vast majority of folks on this list do their own carving, and let me assure you, there is a wide range of skill amongst us all.  If y'all are already working on it, I would go with it and see what you come up with.  One of the really cool things about carving your own is that you later can look back and see how much better you've become at carving!
 
BUT - if the carving thing isn't going as you had hoped and you are eager to go looking, just grab a store-bought and use it for a little while until you can get one carved that you like.  Many of us have done that (including my family, and we had the opportunity to re-find our first boxes not too long ago and I had completely forgotten until I looked through the logbooks what store-boughts we had used wayyyyy back then!).
 
I would say avoid using a store-bought when you plant a box though.  But that's just me and I'm sure other folks will say it doesn't matter (and for my part, it wouldn't keep me from looking for a box).

Another thing about the design.  You don't have to hand draw your design.  If you know what you want to be - say, a lizard - you can google images for lizards, pick one, print it, trace it with charcoal pencil, then turn it over onto the rubber and rub it on, then carve it out and you are an artist!

Sooooo - - - I guess you can see how long it will be before I have time to take on yet another thing that takes time. 
 
If you could only read the E-mails I am doing right now regarding gunpowder, primers, and shells in combination to drive a 77gr Moly-coated match bullet out of a 20" barrel at 2700 feet per second.  The powder must be temperature stable so what I reload in January will still work in June.  The powder must be powerful enough to do the job without overfilling the case.  But - it must need enough powder to almost fill the case.  The primer must seat snugly and fire hot enough to ignite the powder.  Then there are little things - like the capacity of the shells.  Not all are the same.  Decisions decisions decisions.....  looking like CCI-400 primers, Winchester shells, Sierra bullets, and maybe Hodgdon H-4895 powder.  Waiting for a friend to send his opinion before I buy a pound and start experimenting.  SLOWLY!
 
Minor mistakes lose matches.  Big mistakes blow up rifles.  Not only do I not want to blow up my rifle - I want to WIN. 
 
Fortunately, pistol shooting is a lot less difficult, and I developed my competition pistol load long ago and I still like it.  5.95 gr of Power Pistol powder in a Winchester 9mm case fired by a CCI-500 primer with a 135gr bullet.  I can win with that combination if I do my part.
 
This fall I am going to learn to shoot skeet - with a combat shotgun.  I am told this should be a challenge.  We will see.  <grin>  If I can do it at all, I should be able to handle that shotgun in any situation.  And that is why I compete.

Kristin, I know by the time I'm writing this there are already several responses, but I am going to give my opinion. Do what makes you happy. I couldn't wait to get on the trails to even begin to think of how to carve a stamp, so I bought some very small asian symbol stamps (I still have them). Then, the next week I bought the carving kit at Michael's, the pink stuff and all and carved a very crude flower (very crude). I know it's not a good carve, but by golly I did it myself. I have never had a bad comment yet. A few fun carvings later and I carved another one, it's better than the first, but still not blue diamond worthy. And that's okay too. I bet in a year or two, I'll be carving with the best of them. Read all you can on the tutorials, believe it or not, they help. If you post a stupid question on AQ, someone will even give you a link to the message threads on how to transfer a copy onto the carving medium. That's how I did my carvings. I use clip art, coloring books and just rub a pencil on the back. But be very careful with letters. . .
 
This was way more than I planned on typing, but that's okay too.
 
Have fun, that's what it's all about,
 
Janet, Blooming Flowers

Then, the next week I bought the carving kit at Michael's, the pink stuff and all and carved a very crude flower (very crude). I know it's not a good carve, but by golly I did it myself. I have never had a bad comment yet. A few fun carvings later and I carved another one, it's better than the first, but still not blue diamond worthy. And that's okay too. I bet in a year or two, I'll be carving with the best of them. Read all you can on the tutorials, believe it or not, they help. If you post a stupid question on AQ, someone will even give you a link to the message threads on how to transfer a copy onto the carving medium. That's how I did my carvings. I use clip art, coloring books and just rub a pencil on the back. But be very careful with letters. . .
 
This was way more than I planned on typing, but that's okay too.
 
Have fun, that's what it's all about,
