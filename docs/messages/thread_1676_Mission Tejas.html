Re: Mission Tejas

From: janetsredbarn (janetsredbarn@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-02-09 03:00:27 UTC
There was a green folded sheet with the usual information about the
park. Inside there were two half-size loose sheets. One was a
schedule of events for the park and the other had the geocache
information on one side and more information about the park (trash
and recycling) on the back. In other words, it looked to be printed
as part of the brochure. It read: Attention all Geocachers!!! Did
you know that Mission Tejas State Park has a cache? In fact,
Mission Tejas now has 5 caches!! See if you can find them...

Pineywoods KM1 (Traditional)
N 31 degrees 32.859 W 095 degrees 14.119

And then the same information for four other caches.

Then there was a note in small print at the bottom of the page that
read:

"All information here is listed on the www.geocaching.com website
and with permission from park manager and cache owners. Please
follow all park rules and regulations. Please stay on or near
trails while caching. Updated 6/23/05"

Boots Tex

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2006-02-08 21:03:04 UTC-08:00
--- janetsredbarn wrote:

> letterboxes in the park

That's an age-old debate in the letterboxing world.
Do we tell or do we hope they don't notice? Better to
ask forgiveness rather than ask permission? How much
disclosure is a good thing?

Because letterboxing is relatively small compared to
geocaching, we've pretty much managed to stay under
the radar (as long as you don't count the bomb squad
incident, but that wasn't in Texas). Official opinion
varies quite a bit about us - from distrust to open
arms. Mike in West Texas is a good example of one who
has actively fostered good relations with park
officials and it sounds as if it has paid off. Of
course, he comes from the GC world, where they have a
lot more public recognition and so have had to deal
with officialdom quite a bit more, so he's been around
this block before.

I don't think any of the more negative incidents have
occurred in Texas, at least not to my knowledge. Here
in Texas, a box will occasionally be discovered by a
park official who might write an email and ask that
the box be removed or tell the placer the box is being
removed, or something like that. I'm only aware of
one incident where the park official got a little
skitchy about it, and I think that was because they
actually caught someone replacing a box and asked
questions and the replacer wasn't ready with a
responsible answer.

Now this is my opinion only (as most of this has
been), but I don't particularly favor going to an
agency as a whole to ask where we fit in - I like
Mike's approach of making friends with the field guys
and letting them see us in action. If the local guys
know that when we come to find a box we also pick up a
bag of trash or do some trail maintenance, then when
the main agency guys start sniffing around, the local
guys are going to say "Hey, no problem, they help us
out!" Boots, your contact with the rangers at Mission
Tejas is another good example of making positive local
contact.

I like having no official sanctions, but I figure the
day is coming when we will have official rules put on
us. I hope that before that happens we can as a group
represent ourselves well as self-policing and as
having responsible practices that we encourage and
spread among ourselves. I think we essentially do
that already, but how do we demonstrate to someone
official that we do more than litter the woods with
tupperware?

Anyway, this is always a very interesting topic of
discussion, no matter what list it comes up on.

Lucy
