Volunteer Hours report. A GREAT ADVENTURE!

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2008-12-21 19:31:02 UTC-07:00

This is a lengthy report - covering my FMSP hike with Writer Joe-Nick Patoski
and Photographer Laurence Parent of Texas Parks and Wildlife Magazine, and their
hike on the Franklin Mountains Ridgeline. The mission was to gather information
and photographs for an article in Texas Parks and Wildlife Magazine. This
mission has been successfully completed.

Let me first express my thanks to my boss Joe Russell and my employer El Paso
Electric Co., for allowing me to take 1-1/2 days off during our busy overhaul
season. Thank you boss. You don't need to read beyond the hours - unless you
wish to.

Volunteer Hours.

15 Dec 2008. Mike - 6 hours. Monika - 3 hours.
16 Dec 2008. Mike - 14 hours. Monika - 4 hours.

15 Dec.

I took 4 hours off to meet Joe-Nick and Laurence at the airport and guide them
to meet John Moses at the TPWD HQ on Clark St. After a false start due to an
aircraft delay it all worked out. I linked up with them just outside El Paso
International Airport. I led them to the TPWD HQ where the meeting with John
Moses lasted about 90 minutes, covering the proposed hike route, safety and
other information. It was decided to take the trail from south to north as I
had recommended. This later proved to be a good idea.

After meeting with John Moses we left TPWD HQ and I led them to the trailhead at
the Thousand Steps Trail / North Stanton St. for photographs. We hiked part-way
up Thousand steps trail and made use of the daylight and the low sun-angle to
photograph the mountains with dramatic shadows showing the rugged spurs and
arroyos. We got back to our vehicles in the dark - which turned out to be good
practice as the following day the hike was extended far longer than was
anticipated.

I next led them to their motel. We made plans to meet for dinner after they got
checked in. Moni and I had a fine dinner With Joe-Nick and Laurence at the
State Line restaurant in Sunland Park NM. We discussed final plans for the hike
the following morning. All was ready.

16 Dec.

I took the whole 8-hour day off to guide the hike. Moni was our safety and
ground contact for this hike. She was also our transportation to the trailhead,
and our pick-up when we were finished in Fussleman Canyon. Thanks Moni.

Shortly before sunrise Moni and I were on the way to pick up Joe-Nick and
Laurence at the motel. All the gear was loaded up in the dog-show van, and we
were on our way to the Thousand Steps trailhead again. After unloading a quick
combat pack-check and a quick radio check, we were on the trail just after
sunrise. Moni went home, but she was monitoring the ham radio either at the
house or from the dog-show van all day for any needs or emergencies we might
have had. Several other ham operators were also monitoring - just in case.
Fortunately, our regular contacts were nothing but progress reports. At
important points along the route Joe-Nick would ask for information from my GPS.
Distance, present elevation, elevation gain, Lat/Lon location, time moving and
time stopped, etc. So the GPS was initialized, cleared and checked just before
departing the trailhead.

The morning started out just perfect for a hike. I had a sweatshirt on, but I
soon discovered I was over-dressed and I pulled it off. Even so, we were
actually warm during the climb-portion of the Thousand Steps trail. There were
frequent pauses for photography. Otherwise the climb was mostly un-eventful,
reaching the WATSP Ridgeline Trail #1 junction in a little over an hour. There
was a gentle breeze of about 10 mph on the west side of the ridge which helped
to keep us somewhat cooled during the climb. At the intersection of Ridgeline
Trail #1 and the Directissimo Trail we got behind the bluff on the east side of
the ridge. This is where we left the official trail and took the trail to the
MRE site. Soon jackets were taken off as we were very warm from the 1300 ft
climb. But just a 1/4 mile along the trail we climbed up the cut and again got
on to the ridgeline. The wind-chill soon had the jackets back on. It was like
that all day long, chilled on the west side and warm on the east side.

Just 1/2 mile later and with permission from Roger O'Dell of MRE, we entered the
TV-station fence through the gate. Joe-Nick and Laurence were instructed that
the story must state that the station area be bypassed to the left around the
fence. This was fully understood. I checked the MRE doors and locks as we
passed by. All was secure Roger, and Thanks.

Since I had the keys to the ham radio room along, we went inside for our one and
only chance to sit and rest out of the sun and wind. We had a short snack and
topped off our water supply from the bottled water I had pre-positioned there
ahead of time. After a short rest and a quick inspection of our radio gear it
was lights-out and door-locked, then back on the trail. Once outside the MRE
fence to the north we were totally off-trail for the next few miles.

The hike continued with lots of scrambling, rock-hopping and cactus-dodging. It
is a rugged trail - if it can be called a "trail" at all. More of a "suggested
path" to follow. My 4 previous exploration trips paid off well as we were able
to traverse the more dangerous areas without difficulty. We were moving slower
than I had anticipated. However, this was not a normal hike, and I was not
really guiding "tourists". The mission was to gather information leading to a
magazine article. Photographs are an important part of that mission. Joe-Nick
and Laurence are both experienced hikers, I was not really concerned. So we
moved a bit slower. The two really rough areas just before the Government Radio
Site slowed us down significantly. During that climb we noticed a helicopter
circling the area. Although out of sight to our north I recognized the sound of
rotor blades unloading - meaning they were landing. Upon clearing the final
climb to the Government Radio Site we saw the helicopter was landing on the
Government Radio Site Helipad. It was the USBP MD-500 (Loach). The winds were
picking up, and the pilot was doing a very good job of landing and taking off in
the winds. This is a guy to remember should you ever need a rescue. I have
seen lots of chopper-pilots. This guy was as good as I have seen in the wind.
I signaled them with my rescue mirror, and we all waved. They did not appear to
see us. Glad we didn't need a "rescue"! This was the point where I had the
Gatorade previously cached, so we picked it up and had a seat on the Government
Radio Site foundation for a short rest. After the difficult climb the Gatorade
tasted great. And it was even well-chilled from the cool nights under the
rocks!

Mike-6, nothing has changed at the Government Radio Site since my previous
report to you and Ft. Bliss.

After a short rest, we continued north. There were two more peaks to pass
before we started the long descent into the McKelligon Canyon saddle. It was
during this part of the hike that the winds increased. Once again my
exploration trips paid off well as we were able to get to the McKelligon Canyon
saddle easily. By the time we hit the saddle I estimate that the wind speeds
were 35-40 mph sustained and 50+ gusts. We stopped in the saddle and discussed
the trail ahead, the weather situation, the incoming cloud cover, and the time.
It was unanimously decided that we would continue along the Ron Coleman Trail to
finish the hike as planned. At this point we saw a few rangers at the
McKelligon Canyon Trailhead. We waved, but did not see an answering wave. We
pressed on.

The climb up the southern end of the Ron Coleman Trail is mostly on the eastern
side of the crest, so the winds were not very bothersome. By this time and with
the worst mostly behind us - we were a bit tired. But the cloud cover was
making it difficult for Laurence to take the kind of photographs he needed. He
did his best and we pressed on. I must say Mother Nature did her best to
accommodate the hike. At places where there were significant features to
photograph, the sun peeked out enough to give the grass a nice golden glow or to
put stark shadows into the deep arroyos while highlighting the spurs. Then the
sun would disappear behind the clouds and we would hustle along the trail again.

The Window was every bit as difficult as I expected it to be. With the wind
blowing it was quite difficult indeed. John Moses, perhaps I was wrong. This
time I wish there had been a safety-chain at the Window. Also, at the next two
climbs above the Window it would have also been nice. I was not really
frightened. But I was certainly glad it was behind us. We stopped above the
worst of the climbs, and Joe-Nick and Laurence both said they were glad we
followed my suggested path starting south instead of north. Going down at the
Window in that wind would have been really difficult.
