A Destroyed Box

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-08-30 16:06:40 UTC-05:00
Because we have some new folks planning to box in my area this weekend, I've been making a push to get the rest of them checked that I or someone else hasn't checked recently. 
 
As y'all know, many of TexasLuvBug's boxes are now missing, but I went at lunch today to check one of the ones presumed to be in place.  I found the remains of it - the stamp and the box were nowhere to be found and there was a mangled baggie around the logbook.  The book itself wreaked heavily of cinnamon, so I'm guessing someone had cinnamon candy or gum or something on their hands while handling the book, which probably explains why the baggie was badly mangled and the box was gone.  The most recent log entry was fairly recent -  August 9th.  This does drive home the fact that we should be cautious in handling boxes after eating and handling smelly stuff (don't forget - baby wipes are even a no-no!).  Animals like letterboxes as much as we do - if they smell like they would taste good!
 
Because the clues are outdated (one of the landmarks is gone) and the stamp was missing, I didn't make any attempt to rescue the box.  I held on to the logbook and I'll mail it to TLB.  It is highly unlikely she will replace it since she is apparently not playing anymore and I don't plan to place a box in that park because they seem to be doing a lot of work there and taking out trees and trimming up the edges of the park area.

Puddle Splasher said:
 
I wonder if we couldn't just make an "Unofficial List of Missing Texas Boxes" and place it on the Texas LB site. 
 
**********************
 
I've wondered about such a listing too, but this is a subject I have a bit of a chip on my shoulder about, so I didn't feel I could objectively look at the possibility.
 
Would it be possible for us to arrive at a suitable definition of "missing"?  And of whose boxes can be listed on that listing?  Would anyone enter boxes on the list, or would someone have to report a box as missing to the "boxer behind the list" who would attempt to verify it?
 
Here are the questions I see as being important:
 
How long must a placer be out of touch to be declared a former letterboxer?
 
What constitutes being out of touch and what constitutes trying hard enough to contact the placer?
 
How many did not finds must a box have before we declare it missing?
 
How long must a box go without being found before being declared missing?
 
Who can declare a box missing?  On this point, I think it is very helpful to have a finder who has actually found the box in question and who goes back and verifies it is not where they found it, but is that always possible?
 
What if the placer is still in the game?  Can the box still be added to the list?
 
I still really liked Silver Eagle's approach of placing emphasis on the placer, and not on the box.  If the placer is still playing and yet they aren't updating, that's a whole different problem I think.

So can we get past those, and I'm sure other, questions?

Re: [letterboxingtexas] A Destroyed Box

From: Maribeth Jagger (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2006-08-30 22:22:02 UTC-05:00
All good questions, Lucy. I think if the placer is still in the game, it really is up to them to declare a box as missing. In a perfect letterboxing world, when a placer gets a report of a missing box, the placer would check on the box personally and as quickly as possible, because they would certainly be the one to verify that it missing. However, there are often reasons why the placer is unable to check their box, so we do have to depend on our friends in the letterboxing community to sometimes help us out. I think if there were an unofficial a list of missing boxes, it should be a short list. I don't think it's necessary to be redundant and include the ones that are officially declared as missing on the LBNA or Atlas sites. The purpose of this list would only be to list those boxes which have been placed by someone who isn't still in the game (is no longer communicating) and whose boxes have been attempted several times in a row without a find, or like the box Lucy just described that is obviously torn up. If a letterboxer finds one of her boxes on the list and checks on it and verifies it is not missing, she could request that it be removed from the list. This list wouldn't have any connection or link to the regular list of clues. It would just be an informal list of boxes that are obviously gone and the placer is not communicating with the list anymore. Perhaps if five people "nominate" a box for the missing list, that would qualify them to be placed on that list until the placer or another finder verifies they aren't missing. Mystery boxes should not be eligible for the unofficial missing list - too hard to verify!
 
Active letterboxers should, as a matter of etiquette, keep up with their boxes to the best of their ability, list them as missing as quickly as they can be verified, and as Dewberry suggested, move them to the archives if they are not going to be replaced.
 
Puddle Splasher

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-08-30 22:38:36 UTC-05:00
Here's what I wonder - how do you declare someone as not playing any more?
 
My thought on that is that you have to try to contact that person by whatever contact method is mentioned in the clues (and I very nearly wrote in the box - and I'm just SURE Mike would've caught me on that and roasted me!), and to try to use Contact Placer functions in LBNA and AQ, and to post a general message on the regional list and on the Big List asking for the placer to contact the poster.
 
Is calling them on the phone going too far?  I mean, I know how to get in touch with one local placer, but I've never been invited to give her a call - I just know her real name and can look it up in the phone book.  Not rocket science.  BUT - that just FEELS like an invasion of privacy to me.  
 
Unless the placer has personally given me their phone number and we have "that kind" of relationship, I think calling them is a bit too much.   I think the definition of "trying to contact" should be limited to using standard letterboxing communication tools.
 
What do you think?

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-08-31 07:03:29 UTC-05:00
While I agree to a certain degree with that, I think what makes me feel a phone call is off-limits is because we've all only met through the internet - that's the long and short of it.  Many of us have met in real life and many more of us will meet at a later time in real life.  But we still control what people know about us through the internet.  While I might go and find someone's boxes, that doesn't give me the right to intrude on their private lives.  If they choose to withdraw from the game, that IS their prerogative and their privacy shouldn't be invaded because of that, even if it does irritate me sometimes that they left their boxes untended.
 
On the other hand, if another letterboxer gives me their phone number, I would expect that to be an invitation to contact them.  And in fact, several letterboxers have MY cell phone and if I were to drop out I wouldn't be at all surprised or alarmed to receive a phone call from them asking me what was going on.
 
You do have a point though about being able to look up a phone number in the phone book, but then, you wouldn't find BarefootLucy in the book, although you would find my legal name (or Fearless Fox's).  However, if you make your name known and you have your number listed, maybe it wouldn't be going too far.

But one point to know that would help you know where I'm coming from - I'm a little freakish about internet safety.  As safe as this list seems, and I believe that for the most part it is, I actually had someone come onto this list in the very early days because they tracked me down here from another interest I have.  I had begun avoiding them because they became too personal.  They wanted to prove to me they meant me no harm, so they did a few internet searches on my trail name, made a couple of good guesses, and joined up here.   And as far as I know, they are still here.  Does that sound harmless?  It doesn't make me FEEL safe, although admittedly no harm has come of it.  But anyway, that experience colors my opinion about this sort of thing, so I admit I may be a little skewed.
