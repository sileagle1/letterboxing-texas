wrote:

> I'm really glad it isn't like that too!
>
> BUT - there is a lot to be gleaned from the Big List. That's where
the
> long-term folks hang out, the ones who were in it from the
beginning, the
> ones that can give some depth to the game for those who are just
coming into
> it. And therein is the rub...
>
> You may or may not have noticed that there is sort of a
generational thing
> in letterboxing. The generations are a lot shorter now than they
are in
> real life. I visualize the scene as a big ol' family farm, with
lots of
> dogs.
>
> The newbies are the puppies. They are very excited and romp
around, yapping
> along and tugging on the tails of the bigger dogs. They think they
> discovered the stick. Every new game is a great idea and they jump
in with
> all four feet to play. Without puppies, the farm will eventually
run out of
> dogs, but goodness, are they ever obnoxious sometimes.
>
> And puppies get older - the young dog is above all that romping
around and
> silliness. He envisions himself much wiser than the puppy, although
> occasionally he can be caught playing with the stick too. But he
knows the
> puppy didn't invent the stick - after all, HE invented the stick.
He knows
> there is a lot he doesn't know, but he wants to hang with the old
dogs, so
> he pretends. If he's a smart dog, he learns not to howl with the
big dogs,
> but to listen to them howling and notice what makes them howl.
Somewhere in
> there, he vows not to become like those old grouchy dogs though.
>
> And the young dog becomes the working dog. Who knows what job he
has, but
> who cares. He doesn't have time for that stick, and he gets really
> irritated watching the younger ones playing with it - after all, he
is now a
> working dog and he has to worry about what must be done and how it
must be
> done and who cares if it gets done. He's gotten it all figured
out, but
> he's too busy working to realize it. Occasionally at the end of
the day, he
> kicks back on his haunches and howls away with the old dogs, but he
tries
> not to howl as loud as them because he feels like he hasn't quite
earned the
> right.
>
> And finally, there are the old dogs. Retired they are. They've
done their
> due and it's time for them to relax on the porch. They sleep most
of the
> day, dreaming of their glory days. They are pretty happy, until
one of
> those young yappers accidentally slaps him with the stick, and
then, oh
> then, he comes alive and all but eats the pup for lunch. By now he
realizes
> the stick has been there all along and dogs long before him had the
stick
> too. He looks at those puppies and realizes how silly they are,
how they
> have all these silly games they play, but he knows they really
don't have a
> point in the end. He has a little respect for the working dogs, he
> tolerates the young dogs, but those puppies? They really, really
get on his
> nerves.
>
> That's how I see the big list and why I think there are so many
flames
> there. It is, after all, the original letterboxing talk list for
the US and
> that's where the first boxers wound up. Now as new folk come along
and
> start trying to use the Big List for a sounding board for their new
ideas or
> thoughts or concepts, the porch dogs get cross and shoo them away
because
> they have already been there and done that.
>
> Just my .02,
> --
> Barefoot Lucy (who occasionally likes to howl on the Big List)
> "It's not about footwear, it's about philosophy"
>
