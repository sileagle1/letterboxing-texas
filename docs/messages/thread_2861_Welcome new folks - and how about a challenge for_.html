My husband said if we got started earlier we could find more
letterboxes in a day than we do.
I've been rethinking my goals in letterboxing, and have about decided to lower my number of boxes I expect to try to find in one outing to maybe no more than 3-5, unless of course there is a longer series in one area or on one long hiking trail. 
 
When I rush from box to box to try to "cover" an area in a single outing, I end up losing track of the whole experience, with only a few really memorable spots that I can recall later when I log those finds (plus, I'm usually too tired and cranky by that time to appreciate those finds as they should be!).  
 
I know on a long trip somewhere, if I stop along the way to search out only one or two or three letterboxes, I feel pretty well satisfied, since each has led me to a new place, often a letterboxer I hadn't yet encountered (unless it's one of Silver Eagle's many far-flung boxes!), and a mini-adventure to add to the trip's experiences.  
 
So why not slow down the whole letterboxing experience when I'm within a reasonable range of home, too?  After all, those are more likely the ones I'll come back for later anyway! 
 
So my new motto is going to be, Go slow!  Take it easy!  Stop at 3, or 4, or just maybe 5 (and we'll see how that goes)

a premature death. So, here's a better idea: Don't believe *ANY* of
these guys, INCLUDING your doctor. Instead, do some research, and
pay attention *only* to legitimate scientific studies, not medical
opinions or any other opinions. And when you think you've got a
handle on what's really going on, adopt some changes in your diet and
watch your blood tests and other indicators for real results. If the
results show no improvement or movement in the wrong direction, you
made a mistake; flush those ideas, regardless of where you got them,
and start over.

Of course, I could tell you what'll happen if you drastically cut
your carbohydrate intake (to less than 50 per day, preferably closer
to 20 per day) and eat mostly proteins and fats instead. Your serum
triglycerides (the most important indicator of impending heart
disease) will drop by 2/3, to something around 50 or 60. Your total
cholesterol (an overrated indicator) will drop slightly. Your HDL
cholesterol (another important indicator -- more is better) will
rise. Your blood pressure will drop. You will feel more energetic.
And if you're overweight, you'll lose some weight. But I agree with
the writer above: you shouldn't believe me on this, you should
confirm it for yourself.

Remember: If you're taking dietary advice from the FDA, you're
taking dietary advice from the likes of Ted Kennedy and Dennis
Hastert. The FDA is a political organization first, with concern for
public health waaaay, waaaay down on its list of priorities. Just
look at its absurd food pyramid and the way it keeps changing
depending on who in Washington manages to influence it.

-- Kirbert

Everybody loves carbs; they're delicious. That's why sugar is the
most common ingredient in everything in the grocery store these days,
including ketchup, peanut butter and salad dressing. But that
doesn't alter the fact that carbohydrates are the cause of rampant
obesity, heart disease and diabetes in this country today. And all
the while the FDA, the medical community, and the media keep harping
that we need to cut back on fats, which are perfectly healthy for one
and all -- including runners.

There has never been a research study that showed any harm whatsoever
from eating non-trans fats, either saturated or unsaturated (it's a
ficticious distinction, really) in any quantity. Eat all you want;
in fact, in general the *more* fats you consume, the healthier you
will be. But steer clear of those carbohydrates, whether you're a
runner or not.
