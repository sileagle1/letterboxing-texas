O-Knots Letterbox Series in Bastrop State Park

From: LouC (lou.catozzi@att.net) | Date: 2010-10-11 02:22:28 UTC
Pull up a chair and I will tell you a story.

Way back in 2001 Eoghan and the Pumpkin Princess were living in Austin and active in geocaching and letterboxing, placing some of the earliest caches and boxes in the state. A few years later they moved to Colorado, got married, and slowly faded away from our notice, leaving all those Texas caches and boxes behind. Several people in the Austin area stepped up and formally adopted the caches but the letterbox clues were on eoghan's personal website so those were less formally looked after locally.

At some point a few years ago Eoghan's website disappeared so the clues were formally lost.

Recently, I found that Silver Eagle had posted the clues to Eoghan's Lost Pines letterbox in the file archives of this mailing list, noting that Lost Pines was the oldest letterbox in Texas. This gave me the idea to try to resurrect the rest of Eoghan's boxes and recreate the clues as best I could.

I started with a visit to Bastrop State Park to try to locate the four O-Knots boxes. I was able to locate #1, #3, and #4 from memory and a partial paper copy of the original clues although the some information was no longer valid. I took #4 home with me as it needed work and it sat in my garage for a while.

Today I returned to Bastrop and my project of resurrection. #4 is back in place near where it was originally but I have not been able to locate #2. I have made two recent visits now and just cannot locate the tree where the box was placed.

Here is where I need guidance. Should I publish the clues to these boxes in the archive files of this mailing list, on Atlas Quest, on LbNA? How should I credit Eoghan as the originator of the series?

PI Joe
Round Rock, Tx

I’m not going to speculate on the best place to publish the clues. I found one of Eoghan's boxes at the first Texas letterboxing event (actually, Silver Eagle's first event was the second event in Texas, but I digress) in Bastrop.

If he isn't reachable, I would follow the path he took. and post the clues as he posted them (they are, by the way, listed in the basement on LBNA). As to crediting him, you could put his name in the title - Eoghan's O-Knots #4. OR - you could credit him in the first line of the clues.

There are some boxes that call for special treatment. Sort of "legacy boxes". I think these are some of those boxes. If it were my decision only, I think they should be kept off the major clue repositories and held as WOM, But that's my .02

Thanks very much for revamping those boxes. They have always had sort of a mystique about them.

First of all, thanks very much for helping to keep our TX Letterboxing heritage alive, and who better than someone who was actually participating during those early days. I did receive an email from Eoghan at least 2 years ago, but I have since cleaned out my archives and don't have it anymore. At one time I did contact him about his clues and received a link to his new website, but now I can only find it in the archives, which I put in the Links section of this List:

http://web.archive.org/web/20040131120839/http://www.sharawadgi.com/letterbox/

It works, but says some of the boxes are missing (e.g. Mayfield and Wraith, both of which I found many years ago), which may not be true. Back before he sent the new link, I asked permission to post his Lost Pines clues in the Files section of this List and he agreed, so posting any other ones there should be ok, too. That way you could make any changes needed, give credit to Eoghan, and they would serve as a backup if the website gets lost again. Maybe the letterboxers in the Austin area could look for them and verify if they still exist. I still have the notes from when I found the O-Knots many years ago, but not detailed enough to tell which tree to look at for box #2. Maybe next time I'm out that way I'll try to find it.

Lou, I see you are signed up for TALE8. I sure hope you can make it and stay for the campfire so we can all here more stories from the early days of TX Letterboxing!
