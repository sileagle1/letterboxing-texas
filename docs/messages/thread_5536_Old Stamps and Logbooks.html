I don't know about you, but I have a hard time retiring old boxes, even when they are apparently missing.  I try to plan trips where I can check on the box and replace or repair it if possible.  Sometimes I never manage to make it to some out-of-the-way place, so I may have boxes that are still listed as active when they are probably missing.  If I get two or three attempts, I will reluctantly retire that box.  However, many times, I have already carved a replacement stamp and made a new log book, which I carry around with me just in case, and it is surprising how many times I actually find a "missing" box, which wasn't actually missing at all.  I have a good-sized plastic storage box full of recarved stamps that I didn't have to use, and full or damaged logbooks that have been replaced over time.  I keep them around in case the original box really does go missing.  Am I alone in this obsession, or does that happen to you as well?  I should learn to let go and just carve new stamps and make new boxes instead of spending time trying to keep old ones active.

I hear ya Boots, and you are not alone in this obsession, though I am not as obsessed as I used to be about replacing missing boxes.  When I first started letterboxing I was devastated whenever one of my boxes would go missing, mainly because there were so few in Texas and I didn't want people driving for miles just to be disappointed when they found no box.  So I replaced almost every one within a few hundred miles.  This lasted for about 3 years until I no longer had time since I was creating lots of new boxes.  My biggest regret was that once archived, the clues would no longer be seen, and that meant all the history and interesting people that were the purpose of the boxes would be lost.  Even now I hate to archive some boxes, like my games, since I want people to still be able to solve the puzzles or play the games even if the box isn't there.  Many times I just put a status of Missing in the clues but don't archive, even though this isn't a great solution.

In your case, replacing boxes is a bigger deal because your stamps are so great and locations so compelling that we would all mourn their loss.  I know losing stamps is more prevalent than ever because of Geocachers, but I hope you continue to replace your precious stamps so future letterboxers will know the joy of finding a "Boots Box".

I agree that it is tough retiring a box.  While it is tempting to replant, I prefer planting a new box for a number of reasons.  From my perspective, if I am going to all the trouble of re-carving and replanting, I feel like I should get credit for the new box.  I know it is "not about the numbers," but it is still fun to see my plant count climb!  From the finder's perspective, there is a new box to find.  Those who have already found my old box have another one to find.  Those who haven't yet found it can find the new one and not even miss the old one.  Now, as for the old lost box, I am not excited about planting something in the same place because there must have been something wrong with it for it to have gone missing.  And finally, I am just too lazy to remember what used to be there most of the time.


That said, I am going to replant one of my boxes in downtown San Antonio just because I like it so well. And I don't mind because it isn't too far away. I just gotta get around to it. And once planted, if you'd like for me to look for it with you, it would be my pleasure!

How timely.  Here's about my day, including one tidbit relevant to the discussion at hand.

        I’m out doing long-delayed maintenance and picking up a couple missed boxes in Jack Brooks Park.  My first “find” of the day is a vandalized plant I missed from the event, reduced to a trail-encrusted felt square and a baggie.  My first maintenance effort is also vandalized.  Ed Gorey’s Cat has the top of the l’n’l intentionally smashed, separated from the box, and tossed on the ground at the hide site next to the baggie.  No stamp or logbook in sight.

          My next maintenance box, also vandalized, was Schrodinger’s Cat, and was even more distressing because I find the idea of a cat in a box (or not) hysterically funny.  This one stings.  The stamp is lying on the ground, but at least they left the felt for me.  I found 3 insect-perfected holes bored clean through the stamp.  Fortunately, 2 are in the negative space.  But one is not.

          In case one tires of venting ire on geocachers, I propose a new common enemy: Frisbee golfers. I personally think the stoners take revenge on the boxes to mitigate the disappointment they feel when they realize the special little prizes they just found next to their flicked-away Marlboro butts in the woods are not, in fact, a drug drop or someone’s dope stash.  ("Aw, man.  WTH?")  The park on the frisbee golf side has more beer cans littering it than a parking lot on prom night.

           Next up, Ziggy Rain, a 2005 Baby Bear box from LbNA.  It seems like a long shot, and that was before I got home to log it and noticed a prior attempt.  Of course, I left the clues at work.  I have no idea how long it’s been between finds, or how much to expect the terrain to have changed.  I’m wondering if I should expect the clues to match.  Since I’m not sure, I figure I’ll just bully my way to the #5 basket as a shortcut, using the clue from Box Radar.  Um, nope.

          Anyway, I finally resort to following the clues, which work just fine.  Imagine that.  I find what I think is the tree, straight-led by the clues to a cedar with multiple branches meeting about 3 feet up. 

          There are muggles playing a hole of Frisbee golf nearby, but no matter.  I have enough cover here, even though they are only 20 feet away.  There is no way the most likely tree could support a pile of sticks the clues say I’m supposed to be putting back,  A stick, maybe.  Two sticks?  Possibly.  But SPOR?  No way. But it’s the only tree that works with the clues, which so far have been accurate. 

     Nonetheless, I poke gingerly around the wooly netherbits of the tree.  And get a promising hollow thump.  Is that a bit of camo duct tape?  The box is seriously wedged, damnit.  It clearly has been awhile since this was planted, my friends, and I find that the tree has grown around the box, trapping it inside its hiding spot.  Just how long has it been? I do a happy dance.  Because I found the box?  Um, sure.  Also, because. . .ants. 

     Usually, they get riled up, then vacate the area and I can get the box.  I am patient that way.  Plus, I am waiting for the muggles to finish their hole, so I won’t have to worry about making noise.  They are taking forever.  I contemplate toughing it out and just pulling and pulling and puling, until I imagine the ants heading for the tenderest regions of my armpits.  I consider that maybe hopping around, stripping off my shirt to slap the ants off while simultaneously screaming might blow my cover.  

        I release the box.  Brush off ants.  Have another go.  Brush off ants.  Reach.  Brush.  Reach.  I really need to cough. The ants can move faster than I can grip.  This continues for awhile until I realize that sucker is seriously wedged in there.  I try another approach.  Literally.  Amazingly, the situation looks exactly the same from the other side.  I think maybe I can grab the branch further up and use some leverage to pull it apart lower down.  Alas, it’s not really a branch, more like a TRUNK.  I take a thin stick and scrape away debris from around the box to try to get some wiggle room.  Brush ants.  Yay!  It now slides up and down! But it can’t be turned toward an opening.  If only I had a SAW in my backpack like the a-hole Frisbee player who is cutting down a tree preventing him from an easier shot at the goal.  I wish I were kidding.        When I finally bullied the box free (oh, yes.  Did you really have doubts that would be the final outcome?) WITHOUT, I might add, resorting to breaking the box.  No, I’m like a fat chick with a cookie trap.  (Which they haven’t invented, yet, but if they had, it would have been just like that.)  I’m still not really sure how it came out.  It was actually squashed from the pressure of the tree growing. When I opened the box, I figure out why the ants just kept coming.  They were INSIDE the box.  They were INSIDE the baggie with the logbook.  They were INSIDE the logbook.  They were UNDER the duct tape camo on the box.         When I got home to log the find, I notice the last find was an attempt logged in 2005.  Coup! I got not a single ant bite.  I am the bomb, diggity.  It definitely made having to retire 2 of my boxes a little less traumatic.
