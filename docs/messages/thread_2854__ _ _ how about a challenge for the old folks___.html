Okay, I got one. It's sort of been touched on, but here goes anyway:
 
Just because a planter (cough-initials PS-cough) says that it is 20 minutes in and 20 minutes out, don't assume that they are of the same physical shape you are. They probably train 5 days a week and can hop skip and jump in and out of there before you get your pack on. So, plan enough time so that you won't accidentally end up in the middle of the woods when it gets dark. One small (but very cute and pink) flashlight is not enough for 3 adults and one child. Have a flashlight for each person, because everyone wants to be the one holding it.

Maribeth Jagger <cjagger405@earthlink.net> wrote:
The one piece of advice I wish I had been given when I first started boxing was to take my time and savor each box! Each box has a unique perspective brought by the person who placed it. Sometimes it's wonderfully written clues, sometimes a magnificently carved stamp or handmade logbook. Sometimes it's an amazing hiding place. Then there are those "blue diamond" type boxes that seem to combine the best of everything. Just remember that whoever placed that box took the time and trouble and offered their creative talents just for your enjoyment - so do exactly that.... take time to enjoy and appreciate each box. Unless you hear the bugle call of an army of mosquitos descending upon you (or a hunger bear), take time to look at the stamps that are already in a logbook and write a brief message.  In other words, take time to smell the roses!
Puddle Splasher

Flashlights?  FLASHLIGHTS????
 
No no no - Wal-Mart had a nice compact LED head-light.  It is on an elastic band.  It runs on 3 AAA batteries.  It has 4 modes - spotlight, floodlight, combined spot/flood lights, and red light.  It is lighter and more compact than the AA mini-mag-lights.  And - it has an on-off switch that isn't likely to be turned on accidentally unless you sit on it just right.  Throw one in your pack (with a set of spare batteries) and it is all you will need.  More than enough light for hiking at night - keeps both hands free - runs 50 hours on one set of batteries - the spotlight is good enough to signal for rescue - and you can use the red light for working when you don't want to be seen.
 
Flashlights have a purpose.  But I think for hiking they have been superseded.  JMHO.

From: Mike - 2007 (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-06-22 11:48:41 UTC
 
My suggestions....  lets see....
 
#1.  Take plenty of water.  If you ask around you will get people to tell you stories from being uncomfortable getting back to the car all the way to getting sick and wondering if they were going to make it back to the car.  You can go a few days without food.  But you may not make a few hours without water.  I would rather dump water at the end of the trip then to wish I had water 1/2 way through the trip.  I have found the easiest way to carry water and gear is a good Camel-Bak.  I started off with the cheap ones from wal-mart.  Out-grew it in about 1 outing.  I eventually ended up getting several true Camel-Baks.  I like the military models.  They are rugged and tough - but a bit heavier than the light-weight civilian models.  But then again - I hike a lot.
 
#2.  Get a cheap GPS - and use it.  It can save you time.  It can save your life!  If you are a techie - get a good GPS.  One with maps in it.  Its a whole other subject, but I wrote an E-mail on selecting a GPS unit - I may still have it if someone wants to see it.
 
#3.  Mark the location of your car in the GPS before you start.  There have been a few times when a letterbox hunt led me in a circle, and I was able to get back to the car in 10 minutes instead of 1.5 hours going back down the trail.  And in bad weather of if you get lost the GPS can get you back to your car.
 
#4.  When planning to hunt letterboxes, use Google Earth.  Read the clues and plot it as much as possible so you know what you are headed into.  In fact, then I am letterboxing in a strange city, I plot all the driving directions to the final parking point, then program it into the vehicle GPS.  Then I just search for the nearest letterbox and let the GPS direct my driving.
 
#5.  STAY ALERT on the trail.  There may be bad animals or bad people out there.  But if you are alert you will not be surprised.  Your brain is your most important weapon.  Use it.
