my Not Letterboxing story

From: Puddle Splasher (cjagger405@earthlink.net) | Date: 2007-12-09 20:27:07 UTC
For those of you who were wondering how my racing adventure went:

Yesterday was my race car ride at Texas Motor Speedway which my
daughter gave me for Christmas last year. At the Speedway, the
outside track is the oval track used for the Nascar and Camel style
cars, and is raked at 30%. The infield track is completely level and
has several severe curves including two hairpins, and believe it or
not, the Corvettes they race on that track go faster than the Nascars
(so they said anyway). At any rate, this is the fastest I've ever
gone in an automobile: five laps at over 170 mph and hour.

When you get the green flag, they floorboard it and you immediately
feel the G's. I thought I was smiling, but then realized it was just
the G-force pulling the skin on my face around to the back of my
head. A couple of times during the race, I had to reach up and pull
my helmet back down over my forehead, and it was so tight when I
originally put it on, I thought it was going to mash my head into a
permanent oblong shape, but after a few curves, it was feeling rather
loose. The scariest part was coming into the turns. I just had to be
completely trusting of my wild, slightly insane driver. We would be
going full speed up to the turns and slam on the brakes shift down
and sling around those hairpin curves on the inner track. When we'd
arrive at a turn at the same time as another car, one driver would be
forced to the outer lane and the other would take the inner. When
we'd take the outer lane, we'd immediately line up right behind the
other car... I mean right on his bumper at that high rate of speed.
Then my driver would always steal the lead back on the straight-of-
way. Un-be-lieve-a-ble! It was an absolutely incredible experience,
but one I NEVER want to do again. I really wasn't scared, and it was
a lot of fun, but after the race, I was in the bathroom throwing up
20 minutes later! At least I didn't get sick while I was in the race
car.

I think letterboxing, even when I'm climbing a mountain and my
muscles are screaming, is much more my speed. At least, I'M in
control.

Puddle Splasher

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2007-12-10 03:22:50 UTC
Depends.
 
A civilian jump has advantages.  You can pick your weather, and your gear, and everything is smooth.  It is somewhat like para-sailing. 
 
A combat jump is usually done with a chute that is designed to get you down quick, not for a nice ride.  Landing isn't nice either, no room for mistakes.  You have nearly 100 pounds of gear strapped on you.  You have your weapon and all the ammo you can carry.  You carry a heavy pack that is what you will have to live out of for several days.  This pack is on a tether and once you are stable under the chute you drop your pack on this tether.  This is so it hits the ground first, and you slow your descent slightly before hitting the ground yourself.  Your jump may be in daylight, but more likely at night.  The weather - well - not your choice either.  If you are lucky, no enemy fire finds you coming down.  Yes - the Geneva Convention says they are not allowed to shoot you in the air.  If they are signatories of the Geneva Convention.
 
Well - enough of that......  I have never done a civilian jump.  Not exciting enough.  <grin>.  But I know some people who have.  If old President Bush can do it, why not give it a try?
 
Mike.

From: tx2chic (blhjrh@aol.com) | Date: 2007-12-10 04:12:38 UTC
There is another option available, and he CAN be behind the wheel.
It's Team Texas Performance Driving School at Texas Motor Speedway,
and that's what I did about 8 or 9 years ago, where you actually do
drive the NASCAR racecars yourself for 10 laps around the real track,
with a professional driver as your passenger. You first go through
about an hour of performance driving school, learning the shape of
the track, etc., and then you ride about 2 or 3 laps at up to 150 mph
with a professional driver to get the feel of the track and the
speed, and then you drive! There are about 10 cars at a time on the
track, all racing and trying to pass and it is the scariest thing
I've ever done, too, and I've done some other scary things, but it
was one heck of an adventure! I didn't do hairpin curves in a
Corvette at 170 mph like PuddleSplasher, but let me tell you, going
about 130-150 mph in a real Nascar, dipping into the curve and coming
up at the wall at those speeds and trying to pass other cars while
you're doing it, well, it's a thrill! If he'd like to drive for
himself, maybe you should check it out for him. But you might want
to be sure his life insurance is all up-to-date first!
Viewfinder
