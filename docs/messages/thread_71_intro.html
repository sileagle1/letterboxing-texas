Hi, Misty, welcome to the wonderful world of lettboxing. I don't
think you mentioned where you are from, and that might be helpful if
someone were interested in speaking to your group of Girl Scouts. As
for the personal stamp, most people do make their own, but some are
store-bought and some folks design their own and have them made. Any
of these methods will work just fine. You might want to start with a
purchased stamp, then later carve your own. This is a good place to
ask questions. Letterboxers tend to be very friendly and willing to
help newcomers.

From: Silver Eagle (sileagle@alltel.net) | Date: 2008-07-03 13:44:16 UTC
Howdy Misty, glad you took my suggestion and joined this group. If I
remember correctly you are from Garland, so I would be surprised if
there weren't some boxes near you. Hopefully some of the Girl Scout
Leaders on this list will chime in with suggestions and maybe some
local letterboxers can find the time to visit your troop. If not, I
have a few suggestions.

Several years ago I talked with a small group of Girl Scouts about
letterboxing by meeting at a park where one of my boxes was hidden.
I started by explaining the concept of letterboxing, supplies needed
and even how to carve if they wanted to try. Then I gave them the
clues to my box and we went looking for it. The challenge with a
group is that it is hard to be stealthy when getting the box, so that
is why I chose one of mine to find, since if it became compromised I
could replace it myself. Probably an even better plan would be to
place a box specifically for your troop to find. This could be
something as simple as a store-bought stamp in a lock-n-lock box with
a notepad. You could choose a local park and hide the box ahead of
time, print the clues, then hand them out when you meet.

As far as basic supplies needed for your troop, you will need
personal stamps, logbooks (unlined is best) and ink pad. As Boots
said, the stamps can be store-bought, and you could even go with just
one stamp and logbook for the whole troop. Most girls will want to
choose there own stamp based on their personality, so I suggest going
to a craft store like Michael's and letting them pick out stamps by
looking at what is available. You could also get your logbooks and
ink pads here (black or other colors). Based on their stamp, they
will then pick a trail name (or vice-versa). When they find the box
using the clues, they will stamp their personal stamp into the box
logbook and write their trail name and date, then stamp the box stamp
into their personal logbook with it's name and date. I would stress
things like "Leave No Trace" and respect for the placer by being
stealthy when retrieving and replacing the box, and make sure the box
is rehidden better than found. Many boxes go missing because of
careless finders and that is a crucial lesson to learn early. But
most of all, keep it fun and make sure everyone has a good time.

Silver Eagle

Hi, Misty

Glad you decided to try letterboxing. I am warning you, though, it is
adective. Silver Eagle gave you some good advice and suggestions. We
