From: dewberrylb (dewberrylb@gmail.com) | Date: 2006-12-31 01:53:51 UTC
As expected, Lucy has laid out a very cogent, measured response.

It is natural that we are going to disagree. Sometimes, that
disagreement feels more personal than it should and certainly more
personal than was ever meant. If you find yourself feeling so
misunderstood, please step back and wait & THINK a bit before you
respond. This is the way major flame wars erupt, which is usually a
big nasty mess and lots of back-and-forth drama and often ends up with
someone stomping off and the whole group divided. Go back and read
the old LBNA archives for some major incidents of this--but it has
happened on every list, including this one, in the past.

That said, I think that those who have been doing this for a while
have a lot to say and a lot to teach those who are newer to the hobby.
And I think new boxers have a lot of energy and enthusiasm and good
ideas that can help reinvigorate those of us who have been around a
while and who have perhaps gotten a little cynical.

We had a wonderful gathering today, but we did have a problem with one
group of game players. They didn't put the boxes back where they had
been hidden; they didn't put the lids on the locknlocks; they didn't
reseal any internal baggies, and often just put the logbooks in not in
baggies. For the function of the game, these were one-day boxes, so
it didn't matter as much as it does in the wild about the baggies and
the box lids. But the boxes being replaced incorrectly caused a major
time problem, as the people following behind had to search extensively
for boxes that should have been a quick grab. And this slowed
everyone up behind them, because then the finders thought that the
clues were wrong, so they replaced the boxes in the wrong spot, and so
on. . .we had big bunching and at least one box that many people
couldn't find, as it was about 5 feet from where it should have been.

How should we have handled this? I really struggled with what to say
that would be helpful, and not come off as overly critical of people
new to the game. What would you have done?

Dewberry

From: Boots Tex and Lone Star Quilter (janet@redbarnretreat.com) | Date: 2006-12-31 04:05:08 UTC
When confronted with a situation, we must all do what we think is
the right thing to do. It's easy to look back and say, "I wish I
had done something different". Given the situation, I think I would
have picked up the pieces of the boxes, just as Kim did, tried to
notify the owners and posted a sitrep on this list. What good were
they doing scattered around the park? The question in my mind
is, "What will I do in the future if confronted with a similar
situation?". First, I need to be prepared to deal with it. I think
I'll make a temporary letterbox to carry with me and, if I think I
have to remove a box temporarily, like overnight to dry out a
logbook (one of those exceptions to the rule which I've done a
couple of times), I'll put my temp box in its place with a note
explaining that the target box is in maintenance and will be
replaced the next morning, just in case someone came looking. This
temp box will be listed on LbNA as a traveling box. It may not be
the box you expected, but at least you'll get a find. If I can't
hang around overnight, I'll do whatever I can and make sure I have a
replacement box, then notify the owner as soon as possible. If the
box is completely destroyed, I don't see anything wrong with taking
the remains, leaving a box with a note explaining the situation (for
those boxers who may be right behind me), then contacting the owner.
All they can do is yell at me, right? That's not going to stop me
from letterboxing, and it shouldn't you either, Kim.

Boots Tex
