Message to FourFitz - Store Bought Stamps

From: barefootlucy (barefootlucy@yahoo.com) | Date: 2005-07-04 18:09:33 UTC
I've been catching up on email again. I read the big list only
sporadically although I stay up with this list diligently for the
most part.

I saw your message about being scared off from planting a store-
bought stamp and being disillusioned by the discussion on that
list. Let me offer you two points of encouragement:

I think I can speak for everyone here, but we in Texas encourage you
to plant what you feel comfortable planting. We don't have an
elitist attitude - we are grateful for every box that gets planted!
Also, I believe (and I think most of us on this list do also) that
as you do more letterboxing, you will find yourself wanting to take
the next step - once you've found a few, you will find you want to
share a locale with everyone else by planting. But maybe you find
carving to be intimidating. So plant store-bought and post your
clues to invite us all out to find somewhere that you want us to
discover - but just plant! So you plant a few store-bought stamps
and you find that you just can't quite find another store-bought
stamp that says what you want it to say and you decide to try to
carve one. So you carve a pink pearl eraser with a pocketknife, and
it doesn't turn out like you want it to - plant it anyway! You then
decide to buy a few tools and a block of real carving material, but
you find you are still clumsy with the tools - it's okay, keeping
working at it and you will get it. BUT, keep finding and keep
planting! You will find that as you grow in the hobby, you will
look back at your early efforts and sometimes laugh and sometimes
maybe cringe a little, but you will see how much you've developed in
carving and clue-writing and seeking and everything else that goes
with letterboxing. We are all on this grand adventure together and
we delight in sharing the journey, so don't be scared away from
doing what feels natural for you to do next - and if it means you
want to plant a store-bought stamp, do it! You ARE among friends
and those in your area will go out and find your store-bought with
fervor.

As to the big list, all of us get discouraged when it gets
rancorous. In fact, I've noticed that at times when that list gets
ugly, this list picks up in traffic. Sometimes I think we are
trying to hum over someone else's argument. In addition to
belonging to this list, let me suggest that you also belong to
groups.yahoo.com/group/newboxers, if you don't already. That's a
really good and encouraging list for newbies. The big list serves
its purpose, but admittedly it is difficult to wade through the crud
sometimes.

Hang in there and stick with us!
Lucy

From: Eibhlin/Aisling (eibhlinm@gmail.com) | Date: 2005-07-04 16:13:09 UTC-05:00
Hi,

I want to add that my question (and my comments) seemed to be very
misunderstood, which is why I've stopped commenting on the big list.
I must be phrasing things very badly.

A few minutes ago, I counted the stamps in my own personal logbook,
and--if I don't count the exchanged stamps that resulted from a
(large) class that I taught in letterbox stamp carving--the stamps are
equally handcarved and store bought.

That's probably a good representation of the balance in actual
letterboxing: 50/50.

I'm thrilled to find any letterbox. I don't think any less of a store
bought stamp than a handcarved one.

When I asked on the list, I was mostly commenting about how often I'll
struggle with truly vague and/or confusing clues if I know that a
hand-carved stamp is the prize.

For example, I once spent over an hour turning over rocks in a heavily
wooded (and very rocky) area in the northeast when the clue was, "turn
left at the two trees and look under the rock!" The stamp was
gorgeous, but the clues were far too vague.

But, in my post to the big list I never intended to make less of any
letterbox, no matter what the stamp is like... For me, every letterbox
is a fabulous opportunity to discover a new trail, a new landscape,
and often a new park.

I hope to meet some of the other letterboxers on this list, but now
I'm wondering if I'm regarded as some horrid woman who disdains store
bought stamps. I'm also suspecting that my comments were confused
with Lady P's, when her sentiments seemed to be the very opposite of
mine.

As an artist, I love handcarved stamps. However, letterboxing is--for
me--about the hikes and the thrill of successfully sorting out clues
to find a box. I also enjoy planting boxes, and reading the log books
later. I get all mushy when I read notes by little kids.

If I was all that keen on carving stamps, I'd be far more active with
the Carving Consortium. I'm not.

I want to make it very clear that I am delighted to find any and all
letterboxes, whether they include handcarved stamps, store bought
stamps, cut-up pink erasers as stamps, or even a piece of a sponge cut
in a cute shape. Heck, if it makes any kind of mark in my logbook,
I'm thrilled.

And, I'm horrified by what was suggested on the big list, and dismayed
that I'm the one who seemed to have started it.

I would never want anyone to give up letterboxing because they're
using store bought stamps. That would limit the hobby to artists and
aspiring artists, and that would be simply awful. This is such a
fabulously fun hobby, I want everyone to enjoy it as much as I do, no
matter how plain or fancy their stamps, logbooks, containers, or
clues.

Sincerely,
Aisling

Hi,

Yes, I stay on the big list, and I've been on it for years. I watch
it mostly for news of gatherings, and what friends are doing.

I was getting active again simply because--since moving to TX from
NH--I'm getting more active with letterboxing again, and I like to be
part of interesting discussions.

However, "interesting" and "flames" are mutually exclusive terms, in
my opinion. So, I'm silent on the big list again, but keep skimming
it for the cool stuff.

I agree about the current climate on the big list. I've lived all
over the U.S. and the gun issue, like politics, can polarize people in
a blink. That seems so weird to me, but I lived for five years in
Utah and eight years in northern California where guns were as routine
in rural areas as they seem to be in Texas.

Then again, I carry pepper spray for dealing with problem wildlife
when I go camping, and I thought that the Canadian border police were
going to have a seizure when I tried to take pepper spray into Quebec.
Needless to say, not one of them had been camping and it was as if I
was speaking a foreign language.

I should have double-checked the laws before trying to cross the
border, of course, but my point is: In some volatile discussions,
opinions vary widely according to the context, and seem to be based on
the individual's experiences. So, I try to bite my tongue on the big
list. In many cases, all that we have in common is an interest in
letterboxing, period.

And, at times like this, it's clear that nerves are frayed and it
doesn't take much for people to snap.

Anyway, I'm nodding in agreement with the posts on this list, and
wanted to add my two cents. I like the big list, and always have.
I'll remain on it, but I'm going "no mail" there again, so that I can
skim the posts at the Yahoo website without reading the flame sessions
in my mailbox.

Cheerfully,
Ais
