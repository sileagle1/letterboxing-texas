From: (kacmbc@aol.com) | Date: 2008-09-16 17:30:12 UTC-04:00

We are still without power, too, and word is that we may be without for a while.  There are two power grids that feed The Woodlands/Conroe area ( Lewis Creek and the Sabine substations) and they were hit pretty hard and suffered some flooding during Ike and apparently were still trying to recover from the storms that hit around Labor Day.  Anyway, other than a bunch of down tree branches and limbs, we are okay.  Our hot tub looks like a swamp because of all of the debris in it, but without power, we can't do anything about it.  We have had post hurricane parties every night to grill all of our meat and last night we had a camp fire with our fire pit and made s'mores. Nothing like marshmallows, chocolate and graham crackers to take the sting out of no power!  What a great way to get to know the neighbors! 

Fortunately, I was able to run yesterday ( after two days of being told to stay put by the husband) and saw the amount of damage all around The Woodlands.  Lots of downed trees- like entire trees just fell over, tons of limbs littering the roadways etc...The Waterway had trees that were leaning over into the water and the Pavillion had it's cloth roof pretty much shredded due to the winds.  I went downtown with some friends a couple of days ago and took pictures of the buildings that lost their windows.  We weren't able to get into downtown because of the bayous flooding the streets that lead into downtown but from the freeways, it was incredible to see the number of windows that were blown out because of the storm.

Runs for Chocolate  :)

From: Barefoot Lucy (barefootlucy@gmail.com) | Date: 2008-09-16 18:03:39 UTC-05:00
I was a little bummed when our power came back on today because we had already gotten permission from the city to burn and we were going to have a nice fire out of our downed limbs, dutch oven dinner, maybe a cobbler with it, and a grand ol' time.  As it has worked out, we strung the hammocks up in the front yard (no trees for them in the back) and have slept in them the last couple of nights, complete with gas lantern and camp clothes and a barrier of Cutter's.  SJZ, RXKaffee, and Trail Whale came over one evening to visit and Trail Whale had a great time playing outside in the dark, swinging in the hammock (after his mama showed him it was okay), and sneaking up on his Papa (Fearless Fox).  I went and worked a shift at the shelter and came back four hours later to find them still hard at it!  Some of our neighbors have ventured by to say hello and - I'm sure - make sure we weren't having any thoughts of making the campout situation more permanent.  I think Fearless Fox may be, but I think I prefer to camp in the woods away from civilization.  Last night alone, some guy thought it was funny to honk his horn in front of our house at 1 a.m. - scared the daylights out of us!   We kept hearing sirens and trains all night long - apparently our little town is a lot busier than we ever knew!  Still though - hammock sleeping just can't be beat!
 
BUT - it was sure nice to come home this afternoon to a well-lit kitchen and whirring ceiling fans.  Electricity is really loud!
 
I wound up working nights at the shelter, where we had power and A/C.  Until this time, I saw staying at the shelter more of an endurance than a nicety.  Not that I don't enjoy the work, I just can't say much for the creature comforts.  I can only imagine now how the evacuees must think when they walk into the shelter.  I know it gets old for them, but it would be so much older if they were stuck in their homes.
 
Like Blue Butterfly, I've been going to my husband's office and wherever else I could steal a little juice or some internet to take care of business.  It has been very odd having to work at finding out whether we have school or whether my office was working or whether Fearless Fox's doctor was open yesterday.  It's also been strange having to conserve cell phone battery since the only place to plug in our phones has been in the car, and our car requires that the key be on to charge the phones and that didn't seem like a good plan. 
 
I can say I have a new appreciation for those of you who weather hurricanes all the time.  I don't care to have to do all the work of preparing, and I sure don't care for the work of cleaning up. 
 
I sure hope everyone else gets back on track really quickly and most of all, I hope this is the LAST big storm of the season!
--

From: Mike Olbrisch (mike-2007@elp.rr.com) | Date: 2008-09-16 19:02:24 UTC-06:00
No - I am talking about a large battery and a solar cell.  Something about the size of a car battery - but deep cycle - battery 500 to 1000 amps - solar cell between 2.5 to 5 amps max charge.  More is better, but more capacity = more $$$$$.
 
The solar cells charge the battery during the day.   The battery can power DC items, or with a small inverter - even AC items like cell phone chargers, laptop computers, radios etc.
 
Depending on how you use it, the battery will eventually go dead.  Figure a 1000 amp battery and a 5 amp solar cell.
 
If the battery were totally dead it would take the 5 amp solar cell 200 hours to produce 1000 amps.  Considering that you can usually expect about 10 hours of good charge, that would be 20 days.
 
BUT - rechargeable batteries need about 125% input to get 100% output.  (this is one of the inefficiencies the hybrid-car dealers don't tell you).  That would mean you need to charge 1250 amps, or about 25 days.
 
IF you are CAREFUL, you do not use more battery every night than you can charge every day.  But that can get expensive.  The other side of that coin is the 10% rule.  Every time you discharge a rechargeable battery more than 10%, you slightly damage the battery.  Over time, it loses capacity.  Everyone has had AA batteries for their camera that just don't hold a charge any more.  That is why.
 
Most people I know go with the bigger battery for about $100, smaller solar panel for about $100, accept the fact that they will be slowly destroying the battery, but it is economical.
 
BTW - I have about 110 amps of solar cells in the bunk house.  That would fully charge that 1000 amp battery in about 14 hours.  But remember, I can only use about 100 amps of the battery before it starts to take damage.  But I can charge that 100 amps back in about 1:15.  Or, MORE BATTERIES.......
 
I put a 400 watt inverter in the Dog Show Van.  It runs on 12VDC, and powers our laptop or other light-load items while we are on the road.  It cost about $40 for the inverter.  Inverters go about $1 for 10 watts - for GOOD units.
 
I have a 1200 watt inverter new in the box - home I never need it - like someone's generator!
 
OH - some of the best deals for disasters is the Bay-Gen series of radios and flashlights.  I guess everyone has copied them now.  I have a LED lantern here.  Crank it for about 2 minutes and get an hour of light.  I have a radio like that, and a cell phone charger too.
 
This company has some really great things.

A battery on the floor will not die because it is on the floor.
 
The old wives tale that concrete draws the charge out of a battery is FALSE. 
 
That said - there is SOME truth to this.  And here it is.
 
If you think about it, batteries do not work well when they are cold.  the chemical action with the lead-zinc-acid slows in the cold.  A battery stored on a cold damp floor will have a reduced capacity until it warms again.  Setting the battery in a place where it can stay warm (NOT HOT) is a good idea.  Keeping it off the floor can only help - but not hurt it.

No - I am talking about a large battery and a solar cell.  Something about the size of a car battery - but deep cycle - battery 500 to 1000 amps - solar cell between 2.5 to 5 amps max charge.  More is better, but more capacity = more $$$$$.
 
The solar cells charge the battery during the day.   The battery can power DC items, or with a small inverter - even AC items like cell phone chargers, laptop computers, radios etc.
 
Depending on how you use it, the battery will eventually go dead.  Figure a 1000 amp battery and a 5 amp solar cell.
 
If the battery were totally dead it would take the 5 amp solar cell 200 hours to produce 1000 amps.  Considering that you can usually expect about 10 hours of good charge, that would be 20 days.
 
BUT - rechargeable batteries need about 125% input to get 100% output.  (this is one of the inefficiencies the hybrid-car dealers don't tell you).  That would mean you need to charge 1250 amps, or about 25 days.
 
IF you are CAREFUL, you do not use more battery every night than you can charge every day.  But that can get expensive.  The other side of that coin is the 10% rule.  Every time you discharge a rechargeable battery more than 10%, you slightly damage the battery.  Over time, it loses capacity.  Everyone has had AA batteries for their camera that just don't hold a charge any more.  That is why.
 
Most people I know go with the bigger battery for about $100, smaller solar panel for about $100, accept the fact that they will be slowly destroying the battery, but it is economical.
 
BTW - I have about 110 amps of solar cells in the bunk house.  That would fully charge that 1000 amp battery in about 14 hours.  But remember, I can only use about 100 amps of the battery before it starts to take damage.  But I can charge that 100 amps back in about 1:15.  Or, MORE BATTERIES.......
 
I put a 400 watt inverter in the Dog Show Van.  It runs on 12VDC, and powers our laptop or other light-load items while we are on the road.  It cost about $40 for the inverter.  Inverters go about $1 for 10 watts - for GOOD units.
